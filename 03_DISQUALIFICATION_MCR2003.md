# PART I: MOTION FOR DISQUALIFICATION

## I. INTRODUCTION

¶ 1. **NOW COMES** Plaintiff, Andrew James Pigors, appearing *in propria persona* (pro se), and respectfully moves this Honorable Court for the disqualification and reassignment of the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill (P-58235) from all proceedings in the above-captioned matter, and in the consolidated matter of Case No. 2023-5907-PP, pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b), and in support thereof states as follows.

¶ 2. This Motion is accompanied by a Brief in Support (Part II), a verified Affidavit of Bias (Part III), and a Proposed Order for reassignment. The Motion is supported by documentary exhibits referenced herein and available for the Court's inspection.

¶ 3. As documented in the accompanying materials and in the court record, Judge McNeill has demonstrated a pervasive pattern of personal bias and prejudice against Plaintiff, including but not limited to: entering ex parte orders without notice or hearing on July 17, 2025 and August 8, 2025; muting Plaintiff three times during the November 15, 2025 remote hearing while defense counsel sat silently; stating on the record "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it" — a categorical refusal to consider Plaintiff's filings; rejecting a favorable HealthWest evaluation; refusing police reports as "hearsay" when they qualify as business records under MRE 803(6); dismissing Plaintiff's documented ADHD disability; imposing a $250 filing deposit as a financial barrier to court access; and incarcerating Plaintiff for a total of 59 days on contempt findings without proper procedural safeguards.

## II. JURISDICTIONAL AND PROCEDURAL BASIS

¶ 4. This Motion is filed pursuant to **MCR 2.003(C)(1)**, which provides that a judge is disqualified when:

> **(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney; or
>
> **(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard as set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

¶ 5. Under **MCR 2.003(D)(1)**, a party seeking disqualification shall file a motion supported by an affidavit of the facts and reasons for disqualification. This Motion is accompanied by the Affidavit of Bias of Andrew James Pigors (Part III), as required.

¶ 6. Plaintiff has standing to bring this Motion as the named Plaintiff in Case No. 2024-001507-DC (Complaint for Custody, Parenting Time, and Child Support filed **April 1, 2024**) and the respondent in Case No. 2023-5907-PP (Personal Protection Order proceedings). Both matters have been consolidated before Judge McNeill.

## III. GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION UNDER MCR 2.003(C)(1)

### A. Personal Bias or Prejudice — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)

¶ 7. Judge McNeill has demonstrated a pervasive pattern of personal bias and prejudice against Plaintiff, as evidenced by the following categories of conduct:

¶ 8. **Ex parte orders without notice or hearing.** Judge McNeill entered ex parte orders on July 17, 2025 and August 8, 2025, among other dates, suspending Plaintiff's parenting time with his minor child, L.D.W. (DOB November 9, 2022), without providing Plaintiff notice, an opportunity to be heard, or the specific factual findings of immediate harm required by MCR 3.207(B).

¶ 9. **Muting Plaintiff during hearing.** During the November 15, 2025 remote hearing (Show Cause #5), Judge McNeill muted Plaintiff's microphone three (3) separate times while Plaintiff was attempting to present his case. Plaintiff's court-appointed attorney, Martini, sat silently throughout the proceeding and failed to object to Plaintiff being silenced. The Court then imposed 14 days of incarceration.

¶ 10. **Categorical refusal to consider filings.** Judge McNeill stated, on the record: *"Do not file anymore, I will not look at it."* This statement constitutes a blanket refusal to consider any future filings from Plaintiff — a direct violation of Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which requires that every person legally interested in a proceeding be accorded the right to be heard according to law.

¶ 11. **Rejection of favorable professional evaluation.** On or about September 5, 2025, the Court received a HealthWest evaluation (reference H0002) that was favorable to Plaintiff. The Court rejected or disregarded this evaluation without explanation, while crediting adverse materials submitted by Defendant.

¶ 12. **Refusal of admissible evidence.** Judge McNeill refused to consider police reports submitted by Plaintiff, characterizing them as "hearsay." Police reports are admissible as business records under **MRE 803(6)** (Records of Regularly Conducted Activity) and as public records under **MRE 803(8)**. The blanket exclusion of an entire category of evidence favorable to Plaintiff, while accepting Defendant's unsworn allegations, demonstrates bias.

¶ 12a. **Specific excluded police reports.** The excluded reports include Norton Shores Police Department Case NSPD-2023-08121 and Case NS2505044 (Officer Ella Randall, Badge #47437). These reports documented exculpatory findings — zero substantiation of Defendant's allegations against Plaintiff. Officer Randall's August 7, 2025 report (NS2505044) recorded Albert Watson's admission that the Watson family sought the police contact specifically "so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son" — direct evidence of coordinated abuse of process that Judge McNeill categorically refused to consider. These records, qualifying as both business records under MRE 803(6) and public records under MRE 803(8), were precisely the type of exculpatory evidence that an impartial tribunal would weigh.

¶ 13.**Dismissal of documented disability.** Plaintiff has a documented ADHD disability. Judge McNeill dismissed this condition with words to the effect that "it's all in my head," demonstrating personal prejudice against Plaintiff's medical condition and a disregard for the Americans with Disabilities Act's requirements for reasonable accommodation.

¶ 14. **Improper delegation of evidence review.** Rather than personally reviewing audio recordings submitted as evidence, Judge McNeill had her staff — including Pamela Rusco, the judge's clerk/secretary — listen to Emily A. Watson's recordings. The delegation of judicial fact-finding to non-judicial staff, particularly on contested evidence, deprived Plaintiff of adjudication by the assigned judicial officer.

¶ 15. **Financial barriers to court access.** Judge McNeill imposed a **$250 filing deposit** requirement on Plaintiff as a condition of filing future motions. This financial barrier, imposed on a pro se litigant who had already lost two jobs and two homes as a direct result of these proceedings, effectively denied Plaintiff access to the court in violation of the Due Process Clause.

¶ 16. **Differential treatment of child support obligations.** The Court forgave Defendant Emily A. Watson's child support arrears while simultaneously charging Plaintiff with arrears and holding him in contempt for nonpayment — applying fundamentally different standards to the same obligation depending on which party owed it.

¶ 17. **Improper daycare payment determination.** The Court zeroed Plaintiff's daycare payment credits, claiming the minor child L.D.W. "didn't attend enough," without an evidentiary hearing on actual attendance records and without applying any established legal standard for daycare payment calculation.

¶ 18. **Incarceration without proper contempt process.** Judge McNeill ordered Plaintiff incarcerated for a total of **59 days** on contempt findings, including:

> (a) **Show Cause #5 (November 15, 2025):** 14 days jail — imposed during a remote hearing where Plaintiff was muted three times and court-appointed counsel Martini sat silently;
>
> (b) **Show Cause #6 and #7 (November 26, 2025):** 45 days jail — imposed for birthday messages Plaintiff sent to his minor child L.D.W. through the AppClose co-parenting application.

¶ 19. These contempt findings were made without the procedural protections required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 95 (1987), including adequate notice of specific charges, the right to counsel under *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480; 460 NW2d 493 (1990), and the opportunity to present a defense or purge the contempt.

### B. Impartiality Reasonably Questioned — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)

¶ 20. A reasonable, objective person, fully informed of all the circumstances set forth herein, would entertain a significant doubt that Judge McNeill would be able to maintain impartiality in this matter, based on:

¶ 21. **Pattern of one-sided adjudication.** The Court has consistently granted Defendant's ex parte motions, including emergency motions to suspend parenting time, while denying, ignoring, or failing to rule on Plaintiff's motions for enforcement, make-up parenting time, and reconsideration.

¶ 22. **Destruction of Plaintiff's livelihood.** The cumulative effect of the Court's orders — 59 days of incarceration, child support set at $1,056.60 per month without an evidentiary hearing on income, and the $250 filing deposit — has caused Plaintiff to lose two (2) jobs and two (2) homes, leaving him financially devastated and unable to effectively participate in these proceedings.

¶ 23. **Extended denial of parenting time.** The ex parte orders and the Court's refusal to enforce its own parenting time orders have resulted in an extended separation between Plaintiff and his minor child L.D.W. This extended deprivation of Plaintiff's constitutionally protected parental rights — without the process mandated by law — is itself evidence that impartiality has been abandoned.

¶ 24. **Tolerance of non-attorney legal practice.** Ronald Berry, Defendant's boyfriend and domestic partner, is not an attorney. Evidence indicates that Mr. Berry may have drafted or substantially contributed to filings submitted to the Court under Defendant's name. The Court has taken no action to investigate or address the unauthorized practice of law, while simultaneously restricting Plaintiff's pro se access to the court.

¶ 24a. **Acknowledgment regarding PPO proceedings.** Plaintiff acknowledges that on October 18, 2025, he entered a guilty plea to PPO violation charges (CC 382a). However, this plea does not diminish or negate the pattern of judicial bias documented herein, for the following reasons: (a) The pattern of ex parte orders, denial of due process, and disparate treatment **predates** the plea by more than a year, beginning with the same-day ex parte PPO issuance of December 3, 2023; (b) A guilty plea to a PPO violation does not retroactively validate ex parte orders entered without notice or hearing, muting of a party during proceedings, categorical refusal to consider filings, or rejection of favorable professional evaluations — each of which constitutes an independent basis for disqualification regardless of the underlying PPO; (c) The contempt proceedings that resulted in 59 days of incarceration were themselves conducted without the procedural safeguards mandated by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987), and *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480 (1990) — deficiencies that are independent of the substantive PPO violations; and (d) The reviewing court should assess whether Judge McNeill's *conduct of the proceedings* — not the underlying merits of the PPO — demonstrates bias requiring disqualification.

## IV. STATEMENT OF FACTS — SPECIFIC INCIDENTS OF BIAS

### A. Procedural History

¶ 25. On **October 15, 2023**, a petition for a Personal Protection Order was filed against Plaintiff in Case No. 2023-5907-PP based on allegations that included claims Plaintiff was "being homeless and couch surfing," "monitoring her with a webcam," and "throwing her off of a table" — allegations contradicted by contemporaneous evidence.

¶ 26. On **November 1, 2023**, the Court issued a PPO (domestic) against Plaintiff without conducting a full evidentiary hearing, depriving Plaintiff of the opportunity to present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, or challenge the allegations.

¶ 27. On **April 1, 2024**, Plaintiff filed a Complaint for Custody, Parenting Time, and Child Support in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. Plaintiff was the party who initiated the custody action.

¶ 28. On **April 11, 2024**, Judge McNeill conducted simultaneous proceedings addressing an ex parte order amending the PPO, an Order After Hearing on Violation of PPO, and related custody matters — consolidating distinct proceedings requiring different legal standards without adequate separation.

¶ 29. On **July 17, 2024**, the Court entered a Final Judgment of Custody and Parenting Time. Subsequently, Defendant repeatedly obstructed Plaintiff's court-ordered parenting time, and the Court took no enforcement action despite Plaintiff's motions and despite MCL 552.511b, which requires enforcement of missed parenting time.

### B. The Ex Parte Orders of July–August 2025

¶ 30. On **July 17, 2025**, Judge McNeill entered an ex parte order affecting Plaintiff's parenting time without notice or hearing.

¶ 31. On **August 8, 2025**, Judge McNeill signed an ex parte "emergency" order suspending **all** of Plaintiff's parenting time with L.D.W. This order was entered without: (a) advance notice to Plaintiff; (b) an opportunity for Plaintiff to respond before the order issued; (c) specific factual findings of immediate and irreparable harm as required by MCR 3.207(B); or (d) a prompt post-deprivation hearing.

¶ 32. The effect of the August 8, 2025 ex parte order was devastating. It completely stopped all parenting time, including regular parenting time, scheduled Labor Day 2025 holiday time, L.D.W.'s third birthday (November 9, 2025), and all special-event and holiday time. Plaintiff had **zero contact** with his child for approximately four months.

¶ 33. Plaintiff timely filed a written objection and motion to modify or rescind the ex parte order and requested a hearing, as instructed by the order itself. Despite Plaintiff's timely compliance, the ex parte order was not promptly addressed and remained in effect for approximately four months (August through November 2025).

### C. The HealthWest Evaluation Rejection

¶ 34. On **September 5, 2025**, the Court received a HealthWest psychological evaluation (reference H0002) that contained findings favorable to Plaintiff. Rather than considering this evaluation on the merits, the Court rejected or disregarded it without explanation, while continuing to credit adverse materials from Defendant. This selective treatment of professional evaluations — accepting those unfavorable to Plaintiff while rejecting those favorable — constitutes evidence of predetermined outcome rather than neutral fact-finding.

### D. The November 15, 2025 Hearing — Muting and Incarceration

¶ 35. On **November 15, 2025**, a Show Cause hearing (#5) was held via remote video. During this hearing:

> (a) Judge McNeill muted Plaintiff's microphone **three separate times** while Plaintiff attempted to present his defense;
>
> (b) Plaintiff's court-appointed attorney, Martini, sat silently throughout and made no objection to Plaintiff being muted or to any procedural irregularity;
>
> (c) The Court imposed **14 days of incarceration** following a proceeding in which Plaintiff was physically prevented from speaking in his own defense.

¶ 36. A hearing in which the accused party is muted — silenced — three times while attempting to speak is not a hearing at all. It is a sentencing. The right to be heard is not merely the right to be present; it is the right to actually speak, present evidence, and make argument. *See* Canon 3(A)(4), Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

### E. The November 26, 2025 Hearing — Birthday Messages as Contempt

¶ 37. On **November 26, 2025**, Show Cause hearings #6 and #7 were conducted. The Court found Plaintiff in contempt and imposed **45 days of incarceration** — bringing the total to 59 days — based in part on birthday messages Plaintiff sent to his minor child L.D.W. through the AppClose co-parenting application.

¶ 38. The AppClose application is a court-sanctioned co-parenting communication platform. Sending birthday wishes to one's own child through a sanctioned platform is an act of parental love and connection — not contempt of court. The imposition of 45 days of jail for this conduct evidences a punitive disposition toward Plaintiff that transcends any legitimate judicial purpose.

### F. The "Do Not File" Directive

¶ 39. At a hearing in these proceedings, Judge McNeill stated to Plaintiff, on the record: **"Do not file anymore, I will not look at it."** This statement was not limited to a specific motion or filing type. It was a categorical directive prohibiting Plaintiff from exercising his constitutional right of access to the courts.

¶ 40. A judge who announces in advance that she will refuse to read or consider a party's future filings has, by definition, prejudged those filings. This statement alone constitutes grounds for disqualification.

### G. Off-Record Evidence Handling and Delegation

¶ 41. During these proceedings, a USB recording was submitted to the Court and handled off-record, without Plaintiff's knowledge or opportunity to challenge its contents, authenticity, or admissibility.

¶ 42. Additionally, rather than personally reviewing audio evidence submitted by Defendant, Judge McNeill delegated this task to her clerk/secretary, Pamela Rusco, and other staff members. The staff listened to Defendant Emily A. Watson's recordings and presumably reported their impressions to the judge. This delegation of judicial fact-finding to non-judicial staff on contested matters violates fundamental principles of due process.

### H. Scanned Evidence of Ex Parte Communications

¶ 43. Plaintiff possesses **nine (9) scanned ex parte documents** (referenced herein as the "SCANNEDexpartescannedandmine" collection) and **sixteen (16) scanned Rusco/Martini emails** (referenced herein as the "SCANNEDruscomartiniemailsscannd" collection) that document ex parte communications and procedural irregularities. These documents are available as exhibits upon the Court's request.

### H-1. The Rusco Warrant Email — Direct Canon 3 Ex Parte Communication (March 3, 2025)

¶ 43a. On **March 3, 2025, at 8:51 AM**, Judge McNeill's judicial secretary **Pamela Rusco** sent an email to **Prosecutor Jamie Hooker** with the subject line regarding Plaintiff's failure to surrender. The email stated:

> "Good Morning Jamie, Andrew Pigors was ordered to turn himself in to the Jail by 5pm on Friday to serve his 14 days in jail for the PPO Violation. I confirmed with the jail that he is not currently lodged. Can you please issue a warrant for him? Attached are both the order after hearing and the jail mittimus."

¶ 43b. This email constitutes a direct Canon 3(B)(2) ex parte communication. The judge's chambers — through Rusco acting at the direction of or on behalf of Judge McNeill — initiated a substantive communication with the prosecution requesting the issuance of a warrant, without contemporaneous notice to Plaintiff or his counsel. Defense attorney Mandi Martini was copied on the email only after the substantive request to the prosecutor had already been made.

¶ 43c. The communication is independently significant for multiple reasons: (a) it demonstrates that the judge's chambers were actively coordinating with the prosecution on enforcement actions against Plaintiff outside the presence of the parties; (b) the request to "issue a warrant" is a substantive prosecutorial action being initiated by judicial staff — an improper delegation of judicial authority in violation of Canon 4; (c) the attachment of the order after hearing and jail mittimus to a prosecution-directed email transforms court orders into prosecution tools, blurring the constitutional separation between judicial and prosecutorial functions; and (d) Rusco's independent verification with the jail that Plaintiff was "not currently lodged" constitutes an independent ex parte investigation by chambers staff into the status of a party.

¶ 43d. A copy of this email is available as an exhibit (Exhibit — Rusco Warrant Email, March 3, 2025).

### H-2. The Martini "Don't Speak" Email — Evidence of Prejudgment and Ineffective Assistance (February 28, 2025)

¶ 43e. On **February 28, 2025, at 1:05 PM** — the day of the Show Cause hearing — Plaintiff's court-appointed attorney **Mandi Martini** sent Plaintiff an email that reveals both ineffective assistance of counsel and the judge's prejudgment of the case. Attorney Martini wrote:

> "You will not interrupt. Got it? I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked. I'm sure you do not want to spend the next week in jail on a contempt charge for speaking out in court when you weren't asked. And yes, she can do that even to lawyers. And no, in no way is that an overreach of her power."

¶ 43f. In the same email, Attorney Martini further stated:

> "All prosecutor is asking for today is that you get a lecture from the judge because she knows there is nothing substantive in the motion."

¶ 43g. This email is devastating evidence of prejudgment. It establishes that: (a) the judge's emotional state ("not in a good mood") and hostility toward Plaintiff were known to counsel **before the hearing began**, demonstrating that the judge's disposition was communicated through channels outside the formal proceeding — an appearance of impropriety under Canon 2; (b) Plaintiff's own attorney instructed him to remain silent rather than exercise his right to be heard, constituting deficient performance under *Strickland v. Washington*, 466 U.S. 668, 687 (1984); (c) the attorney's acknowledgment that "there is nothing substantive in the motion" reveals that counsel knew the prosecution's case was weak, yet her strategy was capitulation rather than advocacy; and (d) the threat of jail for speaking — conveyed by defense counsel as an established expectation of the judge — demonstrates that the judge's pattern of silencing Plaintiff was well-known and anticipated.

¶ 43h. This email corroborates Plaintiff's testimony regarding the muting incidents: a judge whose own courtroom expectations include jailing parties for speaking has abandoned the constitutional guarantee of the right to be heard. A copy of this email is available as an exhibit (Exhibit — Martini Email, February 28, 2025).

### H-3. HealthWest Chart Memo — Off-Record Judicial Coordination with Clinical Witnesses (October 29, 2025)

¶ 43i. A HealthWest clinical chart memo dated **October 29, 2025** documents that:

> "Pamela Rusco, Judicial Secretary for the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill . . . called to request if this clinician could testify . . . will email us the subpoenas."

¶ 43j. This chart memo establishes that: (a) **subpoenas for clinical witness testimony were arranged via email and telephone between the judge's secretary and clinical staff** — not through formal court filings served on the parties as required by MCR 2.506; (b) the arrangement of testimony was conducted **outside the presence of the parties** and was not reflected on the court docket, constituting an ex parte communication in violation of Canon 3(B)(2); (c) the judge's chambers were **predetermining which witnesses would appear** and coordinating the logistics of testimony before any formal hearing had been scheduled or noticed to the parties; and (d) Rusco — a judicial secretary, not a judicial officer — was exercising substantive case management authority, including arranging witness testimony and transmitting subpoenas, in violation of Canon 4's prohibition on improper delegation of judicial authority.

¶ 43k. A copy of this chart memo is available as an exhibit (Exhibit — HealthWest Chart Memo, October 29, 2025).

### H-4. HealthWest Evaluation Inconsistency — Clinically Implausible Reversal

¶ 43l. The two HealthWest evaluations, conducted just **seven (7) calendar days apart**, produced dramatically inconsistent results:

> (a) **First evaluation (September 4, 2025):** Conducted by evaluators Michelle Mitchell (QIDP/QMHP) and Melissa DeAugustine (LBSW). Results: clean baseline, no major concerns, safe environment. All clinical scores at zero or lowest severity.
>
> (b) **Second evaluation (September 11, 2025):** Conducted by evaluator Kassandra Gansen (LMSW). Result: "Rule-out Delusional Disorder." Scores elevated across multiple domains. Critically, the evaluator herself acknowledged the limitation of her own findings: *"It is difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional."*

¶ 43m. A seven-day reversal from a clean baseline to a "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" finding is clinically implausible absent a precipitating traumatic event — none of which is documented. The dramatic divergence is explained by Judge McNeill's intervening biased letter to HealthWest describing Plaintiff's behaviors as "suggestive of a mental health disorder," which was provided without Plaintiff's knowledge and which contaminated the second evaluation.

¶ 43n. Judge McNeill **rejected the first evaluation** — which was favorable to Plaintiff — and **relied on the second evaluation** — which was the product of the judge's own ex parte communication with the evaluator. The second evaluation was directed to chambers via email rather than filed through the Clerk of Court, bypassing MCR 8.119's requirements for public record. This selective reliance on a manufactured evaluation constitutes evidence of predetermined outcome.

## LEGAL ANALYSIS

### I. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

¶ 51. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) requires disqualification when "the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." The standard is objective: whether a reasonable person would perceive a significant risk of bias. *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975). The test is "whether the conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired." *Id.*

¶ 52. The Michigan Court of Appeals has recognized that a pattern of one-sided rulings can cumulatively establish bias. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001). While isolated adverse rulings do not establish bias, a *pattern* of adverse rulings combined with other indicators — such as ex parte adjudication, refusal to consider evidence, and procedural irregularities favoring one party — does.

¶ 53. Here, the evidence of personal bias is overwhelming and goes far beyond adverse rulings:

¶ 54. **The judge silenced Plaintiff during his own hearing.** Muting a party's microphone three times during a remote hearing — the very hearing at which he was sentenced to 14 days in jail — is not an exercise of courtroom management. It is the physical prevention of a party from being heard. No reasonable interpretation of judicial discretion encompasses silencing a party while deciding whether to incarcerate him. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 95 (1987) (contemnor must be afforded the opportunity to present a defense).

¶ 55. **The judge announced she would refuse to consider Plaintiff's future filings.** The statement "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it" is a pre-commitment to ignore Plaintiff's submissions. A judge who has declared in advance that she will not read a party's filings has abandoned the pretense of impartiality. This statement violates Canon 3(A)(4) and constitutes independent grounds for disqualification.

¶ 56. **The judge rejected favorable evidence while crediting unfavorable evidence.** The HealthWest evaluation (H0002, September 5, 2025) was favorable to Plaintiff. The Court rejected it without explanation. The Court simultaneously refused to consider police reports — admissible as business records under MRE 803(6) and public records under MRE 803(8) — while accepting Defendant's unsworn, ex parte filings. This pattern of selective evidence treatment demonstrates outcome-driven adjudication.

¶ 57. **The judge dismissed Plaintiff's documented disability.** Judge McNeill's dismissal of Plaintiff's ADHD diagnosis as something that is "all in my head" reflects personal prejudice against Plaintiff's medical condition. A judge who dismisses a documented disability with contempt cannot be expected to fairly adjudicate matters in which that disability is relevant.

¶ 58. **The judge delegated judicial fact-finding to staff.** Having her clerk/secretary Pamela Rusco and other staff listen to contested audio recordings, rather than reviewing the evidence herself, constitutes an improper delegation of the judicial function. The parties are entitled to have their evidence considered by the judge, not reported on by support staff.

¶ 59. **The judge applied different standards to the same obligations.** Forgiving Defendant's child support arrears while holding Plaintiff in contempt for his own arrears — arising from an amount set without an evidentiary hearing on income — demonstrates a double standard that cannot be reconciled with impartial adjudication.

¶ 60. **59 days of incarceration without proper contempt safeguards.** The United States Supreme Court has recognized that "bias sufficient to violate due process must be so extreme as to display clear inability to render fair judgment." *Liteky v United States*, 510 US 540, 555 (1994). Incarcerating a parent for 59 days — 14 days for speaking during a hearing at which he was muted, and 45 days for sending birthday messages to his own child — while denying the procedural protections of *Dougherty* and *Mead v Batchlor*, meets even this demanding standard.

### II. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — Impartiality Reasonably Questioned

¶ 61. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides for disqualification when "the judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard."

¶ 62. The appearance-of-impropriety standard does not require proof of actual bias. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 671 (2009). "The question is whether a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would harbor doubts about the judge's impartiality." *Id.*

¶ 63. **A reasonable person would harbor more than doubt — they would harbor certainty.** Consider what a reasonable person, fully informed, would observe:

> (a) A judge who entered ex parte orders suspending a father's parenting time without notice or hearing — not once, but repeatedly;
>
> (b) A judge who muted the father three times during a remote hearing, then jailed him for 14 days;
>
> (c) A judge who told the father "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it";
>
> (d) A judge who rejected a favorable professional evaluation (HealthWest) without explanation;
>
> (e) A judge who refused admissible police reports as "hearsay";
>
> (f) A judge who dismissed the father's documented ADHD disability;
>
> (g) A judge who had her staff listen to the mother's recordings instead of listening herself;
>
> (h) A judge who forgave the mother's arrears while jailing the father for his;
>
> (i) A judge who imposed a $250 filing deposit on a father who had already lost two jobs and two homes;
>
> (j) A judge who jailed a father for 45 days for sending birthday messages to his own child through a court-sanctioned application;
>
> (k) A judge whose secretary emailed the prosecutor at 8:51 AM requesting a warrant for the father's arrest — a direct ex parte communication between chambers and prosecution, with defense counsel copied only after the fact;
>
> (l) A judge whose secretary arranged clinical witness testimony via telephone and email without notice to either party — predetermining the logistics of a hearing outside the presence of the parties;
>
> (m) A judge whose court-appointed defense attorney warned the father before a hearing: "she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked" and "I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today" — establishing that the judge's hostility was known and communicated before the proceeding began.

¶ 64. No reasonable person, fully informed of these circumstances, would conclude that this judge could maintain impartiality. The appearance-of-impropriety standard is satisfied many times over.

¶ 65. The Michigan Supreme Court in *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996), emphasized that the appearance-of-impropriety standard serves a prophylactic function — protecting not only the parties but the integrity of the judicial system itself. The public's confidence in the judiciary is undermined when a judge's conduct creates a perception of bias, regardless of whether actual bias exists.

¶ 66. *People v Jackson*, 292 Mich App 583, 597 (2011), recognized that repeated procedural irregularities favoring one party create a reasonable perception of bias even when each individual action might be defensible in isolation. Here, the irregularities are not merely procedural — they include silencing a party, refusing to consider his filings, and jailing him for birthday messages.

¶ 66a. The Michigan Supreme Court's decision in *In re Disqualification of Noe*, 454 Mich 1201 (1997), reinforces that disqualification is warranted when a judge's pattern of conduct creates an objective appearance that the judge has prejudged the matter. The *Noe* standard aligns with the *Caperton* probability-of-actual-bias test: where the cumulative weight of circumstances would lead a reasonable observer to conclude that the judge cannot be impartial, disqualification is required as a prophylactic measure — regardless of whether the judge subjectively believes herself to be fair. The record here far exceeds the threshold identified in *Noe*.

### III. Canon Violations as Evidence of Bias

¶ 67. The Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct establishes the ethical framework governing judicial behavior. Violations of the MCJC, while not automatically requiring disqualification, are "highly relevant" to the MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) inquiry. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App at 672.

¶ 68. Judge McNeill's conduct violates the following Canons:

¶ 69. **Canon 2 — Avoiding Impropriety and the Appearance of Impropriety.** Canon 2 requires that a judge "should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." The pattern of ex parte adjudication, selective evidence treatment, muting of a party, and public statements refusing to consider filings creates precisely the appearance of impropriety Canon 2 forbids.

¶ 70. **Canon 3(A)(4) — Right to Be Heard.** This Canon requires that a judge "shall accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding . . . the right to be heard according to law." Judge McNeill violated this Canon by: (a) entering ex parte orders without hearing; (b) muting Plaintiff three times during a hearing; (c) announcing "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it"; and (d) delegating evidence review to staff rather than personally considering Plaintiff's submissions.

¶ 71. **Canon 3(B)(7) — Ex Parte Communications.** This Canon prohibits a judge from initiating, permitting, or considering ex parte communications. The documented ex parte orders, the off-record handling of the USB recording, and the staff's off-record review of Defendant's recordings all constitute violations of this Canon. Three newly documented incidents dramatically amplify the Canon 3 violation pattern:

> (a) **The Rusco Warrant Email (March 3, 2025).** Judge McNeill's judicial secretary Pamela Rusco emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker at 8:51 AM requesting that a warrant be issued for Plaintiff's arrest, attaching the order after hearing and jail mittimus. This is a direct ex parte communication between chambers and prosecution — initiated by judicial staff, directed to a single party, and concerning a substantive enforcement action against Plaintiff. Defense counsel was copied only after the substantive request was made.
>
> (b) **The HealthWest Chart Memo (October 29, 2025).** A HealthWest clinical chart memo documents that Rusco called HealthWest to arrange clinician testimony and stated she would "email us the subpoenas." Subpoenas were arranged via telephone and email between chambers and a prospective witness — not through formal court filings served on the parties. This constitutes an ex parte communication with a witness and predetermination of testimony logistics outside the presence of the parties.
>
> (c) **The Martini Email (February 28, 2025).** Defense counsel's pre-hearing email reveals that the judge's hostile disposition toward Plaintiff was known before the proceeding commenced — establishing that information about the judge's state of mind was circulating through informal channels. While not itself a judicial ex parte communication, this email corroborates the environment of prejudgment and off-record coordination surrounding these proceedings.

¶ 71a. **Canon 4 — Improper Delegation of Judicial Authority.** Canon 4 and the separation of powers require that judicial functions be performed by judicial officers, not delegated to non-judicial staff for substantive case management. Pamela Rusco — a judicial secretary — has been documented: (a) initiating warrant requests to the prosecution (March 3, 2025); (b) arranging clinical witness testimony and transmitting subpoenas via email (October 29, 2025); (c) conducting independent investigation into Plaintiff's jail status; and (d) serving as the conduit for off-record evaluation submissions. These are not ministerial or clerical functions — they are substantive judicial actions that determine the course of litigation. The delegation of warrant initiation, witness coordination, and evidence routing to a secretary constitutes an improper delegation of judicial authority.

### IV. Due Process Violations Under the 14th Amendment

¶ 72. The Fourteenth Amendment protects a parent's fundamental liberty interest in the "care, custody, and management" of his child. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972). "When a State seeks to deprive a parent of a protected interest in the parent-child relationship, it must afford fundamentally fair procedures." *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753-54 (1982).

¶ 73. Minimum due process requires notice and the opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976). Judge McNeill's conduct has violated these requirements in multiple, independent ways:

¶ 74. **Deprivation without notice.** The ex parte orders of July 17, 2025 and August 8, 2025 suspended Plaintiff's parenting time without notice, depriving Plaintiff of the opportunity to present evidence or argument before his fundamental parental rights were restricted.

¶ 75. **Deprivation without meaningful hearing.** The November 15, 2025 hearing — at which Plaintiff was muted three times, his attorney sat silently, and the Court imposed 14 days of jail — was not a meaningful hearing. A hearing at which a party is physically prevented from speaking does not satisfy due process.

¶ 76. **Incarceration without contempt safeguards.** Judge McNeill imposed 59 days of incarceration on contempt findings without the procedural protections mandated by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 95 (1987), including the right to counsel under *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480; 460 NW2d 493 (1990). *See also Lassiter v Dep't of Social Services*, 452 US 18 (1981) (right to counsel when physical liberty at stake).

¶ 77. **Failure to apply proper legal standards.** Under *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003), custody and parenting-time modifications require a showing of proper cause or change of circumstances. The ex parte orders bypassed this standard entirely. Child support was set at $1,056.60 per month without an evidentiary hearing on actual income, violating basic due process in the determination of financial obligations.

¶ 78. **Financial barriers to access.** The $250 filing deposit, imposed on a pro se litigant rendered indigent by the Court's own orders, created an unconstitutional barrier to court access. The United States Supreme Court held in *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 383 (1971), that "a State may not, consistent with the obligations imposed on it by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, pre-empt the right to dissolve [a legal] relationship without affording all citizens access to the means it has prescribed for doing so." The *Boddie* principle extends to any proceeding in which fundamental rights are at stake: a state cannot condition access to its courts on the payment of fees that a litigant cannot afford when those courts are the exclusive forum for vindicating fundamental constitutional interests. Here, the $250 filing bond — imposed on May 16, 2025 — combined with Judge McNeill's directive to "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it," constitutes a complete denial of Plaintiff's access to the only forum available to protect his fundamental parental rights. The right to petition the government for redress of grievances is not conditioned on ability to pay.
