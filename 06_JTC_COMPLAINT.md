### B1. Scope of This Complaint — Conduct, Not Rulings

Complainant acknowledges that the Judicial Tenure Commission does not act as an appellate court and does not review the legal correctness of a judge's decisions. *See In re Brown*, 461 Mich 1291 (2000); *In re Justin*, 490 Mich 394 (2012). This Complaint is therefore confined to Judge McNeill's **judicial conduct** — specifically: (a) muting a party during hearing while deciding whether to incarcerate him; (b) delegating evidence review to non-judicial staff for ex parte evaluation; (c) stating on the record "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it" — a categorical refusal to perform the judicial function; (d) entering five ex parte orders on a single day without notice; (e) dismissing a documented disability; and (f) maintaining a prior attorney-client relationship with a party without recusal. To the extent any allegation herein also involves a substantive legal ruling, the complaint addresses the *manner* in which the ruling was made (e.g., without hearing, without notice, based on ex parte evidence), not the legal correctness of the ruling itself.

Complainant further acknowledges that on October 18, 2025, he entered a guilty plea to PPO violations (CC 382a) and was subsequently incarcerated for 59 days. This Complaint does not challenge the substantive PPO or its enforcement. Rather, it addresses Judge McNeill's *conduct in presiding over the proceedings* — conduct that would constitute misconduct regardless of the underlying merits of the PPO case.

The pattern of judicial misconduct documented herein — including ex parte communications with court staff, muting of a party during hearings, categorical refusal to consider pro se filings, and reliance on an evaluation procured through the court's own biasing letter — predates the guilty plea by more than a year, beginning with the same-day ex parte PPO issuance of December 3, 2023. A guilty plea to violating a court order does not retroactively validate the judicial officer's misconduct in administering that order.

### Category E: Appearance of Impropriety (Canon 2)

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's cumulative pattern of conduct creates the appearance of impropriety in violation of Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Rule:** Canon 2 requires that a judge "shall avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all of the judge's activities." The standard is objective: whether the conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired." *In re Chrzanowski*, 465 Mich 468, 488 (2001). The appearance of impropriety exists independently of whether actual bias is proven. *In re Brown*, 461 Mich 1291 (2000).

**Application:**

1. **Pattern of ex parte orders uniformly favoring one party.** A review of the docket in Cases 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP reveals numerous orders entered without adequate notice or opportunity to be heard, with those ex parte orders uniformly favoring Mother. This pattern creates in reasonable minds a perception that outcomes are predetermined rather than the product of fair adjudication.

2. **Prior representation of a party.** Judge McNeill stated on the record: "I have represented Party A in the past." This admission of a prior attorney-client relationship with a party, without subsequent recusal, directly undermines the integrity of the proceedings and mandates disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).

3. **Contradictory jail/dismissal order.** On February 28, 2025, McNeill entered an order simultaneously imposing 14 days jail for contempt AND dismissing the underlying matter — a legally incoherent act reflecting a fundamental failure of judicial integrity.

4. **Self-denial of disqualification motion.** On September 25, 2024, McNeill denied her own disqualification motion — "DENIED. No bias shown" — in violation of MCR 2.003(D)(1), which mandatorily requires referral to the chief judge.

5. **Asymmetric enforcement pattern.** Mother committed a documented pattern of violations of court orders — including custody orders and parenting time orders. None of Mother's violations have been enforced, sanctioned, or addressed by Judge McNeill. By contrast, Father was held in contempt and jailed for co-parenting messages asking to see his son. This radical asymmetry demonstrates outcome-determinative bias that a reasonable person would perceive as partiality.

6. **Suspicious evaluation routing.** The second HealthWest evaluation was routed to McNeill's secretary Pamela Rusco rather than through the Clerk of Court, bypassing normal filing procedures and chain-of-custody protections, creating the appearance of evidence manipulation.

7. **Credibility determinations without hearing.** McNeill made adverse credibility findings against Father — including characterizing his statements as untruthful — without conducting any evidentiary hearing, without sworn testimony, and without any opportunity for cross-examination. *See In re Brock*, 442 Mich 101 (1993).

8. **Court-appointed counsel confirmed prejudgment (February 28, 2025).** Defense attorney Mandi Martini emailed Father on the day of the Show Cause hearing: "I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked." That a court-appointed attorney communicated the judge's hostile disposition and the expectation that Father would be silenced — before the hearing began — demonstrates that the appearance of impropriety was so entrenched that even officers of the court anticipated predetermined outcomes.

**Conclusion:** The cumulative pattern — prior representation without recusal, asymmetric enforcement, hostile disposition known to counsel, ex parte order pattern, evaluation routing through chambers — satisfies the *Chrzanowski* standard: reasonable minds would perceive that Judge McNeill's impartiality is impaired.

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's conduct undermines the integrity and independence of the judiciary in violation of Canon 1.

**Conclusion:** Under *In re Justin*, the sustained pattern documented herein warrants greater discipline than any single incident would independently justify.

### Category A: Ex Parte Communications (Canon 3A(4))

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill initiated, permitted, or considered ex parte communications in violation of Canon 3A(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Rule:** Canon 3A(4) provides: "A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding." The Michigan Supreme Court in *In re McCree*, 495 Mich 51, 74 (2014), held that ex parte communications constitute misconduct warranting discipline because they "undermine the fundamental fairness of proceedings and deprive parties of the right to respond to matters considered by the court."

**Application:**

1. **August 8, 2025 — Five ex parte orders in a single day.** McNeill entered five separate orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, all ex parte, all without prior notice to Father, all favoring Mother. Father received no advance notice of any filing, motion, or request that precipitated these orders.

2. **August 5, 2025 — Admitted ex parte evidence review.** McNeill stated on the record: **"Yes, I had my staff listen to it"** — referring to a USB drive containing audio evidence submitted by Mother. This admission establishes that McNeill received and reviewed substantive evidence outside the presence of the parties, in direct violation of Canon 3(A)(4).

3. **November 29, 2024 — Secretary communications.** McNeill's secretary Pamela Rusco transmitted emails regarding the status of a PPO and Father's jail status — substantive case matters communicated outside the presence of the parties and not filed on the record.

4. **October 29, 2025 — Rusco's ex parte call to HealthWest.** Between 1:48 PM and 2:00 PM, Rusco placed a phone call directly to HealthWest to arrange clinician testimony for a November 26, 2025 hearing — outside the presence of the parties. Court staff acting at the judge's direction to contact a prospective witness constitutes imputed ex parte conduct.

5. **Court letter biasing second evaluation.** Prior to the September 11, 2025 second assessment, McNeill provided a letter to HealthWest describing Father's alleged behaviors as "several incidents over the past two years of court proceedings which are suggestive of a mental health disorder." This letter was provided without Father's knowledge or consent, constituting an ex parte communication with a prospective evaluator.

6. **Routing evaluation to secretary.** McNeill directed Father to email his HealthWest assessment to her secretary rather than filing through the Clerk of Court — a procedure that ensured the evaluation never became part of the public record and was never accessible to the opposing party through normal channels, in violation of MCR 8.119.

7. **Statistical pattern.** A comprehensive review of the docket entries in Cases 2023-5907-PP and 2024-001507-DC reveals numerous orders entered without adequate notice or opportunity to be heard, with those ex parte orders uniformly favoring a single party. This pattern is not the product of isolated judicial error but reflects a systematic practice of ex parte adjudication. Under *In re McCree*, such a pattern is independently sanctionable.

8. **March 3, 2025 — Rusco warrant email to prosecutor.** At 8:51 AM on March 3, 2025, McNeill's judicial secretary Pamela Rusco emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker: *"Good Morning Jamie, Andrew Pigors was ordered to turn himself in to the Jail by 5pm on Friday to serve his 14 days in jail for the PPO Violation. I confirmed with the jail that he is not currently lodged. Can you please issue a warrant for him? Attached are both the order after hearing and the jail mittimus."* Defense attorney Mandi Martini was copied only after the substantive request to the prosecutor. This email constitutes a direct Canon 3A(4) ex parte communication — the judge's chambers initiated a substantive prosecutorial action (warrant issuance) outside the presence of the parties, attaching court orders as enforcement tools directed to one side only.

9. **October 29, 2025 — Rusco's off-record coordination with HealthWest (Chart Memo).** A HealthWest clinical chart memo documents that *"Pamela Rusco, Judicial Secretary for the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill . . . called to request if this clinician could testify . . . will email us the subpoenas."* Subpoenas for clinical witness testimony were arranged via telephone and email between chambers staff and the clinical provider — not through formal court filings served on the parties as required by MCR 2.506. This off-record coordination of witness testimony constitutes a Canon 3A(4) ex parte communication and predetermination of hearing logistics without notice to Father.

10. **February 28, 2025 — Martini email corroborating prejudgment environment.** On the day of the Show Cause hearing, Father's court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini emailed him: *"I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked. I'm sure you do not want to spend the next week in jail on a contempt charge for speaking out in court when you weren't asked."* In the same email: *"All prosecutor is asking for today is that you get a lecture from the judge because she knows there is nothing substantive in the motion."* While this is counsel's communication rather than the judge's, it corroborates that the judge's hostile disposition and predetermined approach were known and anticipated before the hearing began — establishing an environment of prejudgment inconsistent with Canon 3B(5)'s requirement of impartial adjudication.

**Conclusion:** The ten documented instances of ex parte communications — including chambers-to-prosecution coordination, admitted off-record evidence review, biased evaluation letters, off-record witness coordination, and a systematic pattern of unilateral orders — constitute a sustained course of Canon 3A(4) violations warranting formal investigation under *In re McCree*.

### Category B: Bias and Prejudice (Canon 3B(5))

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill performed judicial duties with bias or prejudice in violation of Canon 3B(5) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Rule:** Canon 3B(5) provides: "A judge shall perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice." In *In re Brown*, 461 Mich 1291 (2000), the Michigan Supreme Court held that bias and prejudice in judicial proceedings constitute misconduct subject to JTC discipline. A pattern of one-sided rulings, combined with hostile treatment of one party, may establish the requisite bias. *In re Justin*, 490 Mich 394 (2012).

**Application:**

1. **Rejected favorable evaluation she ordered.** McNeill ordered Father to undergo a HealthWest evaluation. Father complied. The evaluation cleared him (Psychosis: 0, Substance Use: 0, Danger to Self/Others: 0). McNeill rejected the evaluation, writing: "None of the above statements are what he testified to in Court. He was evicted, and his trailer is not being remodeled." She rejected a professional assessment because its findings did not match the court's desired narrative.

2. **Manufactured contrary evidence.** McNeill provided a biased letter to the second evaluator instructing that Father's behaviors were "suggestive of a mental health disorder." The result: scores inflated from the first evaluation (Psychosis: 0→2, Depression: 0→2, Anger Control: 0→2). Even the second clinician could not confirm the diagnosis: "It is difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional in the context of a brief screening." The diagnosis was explicitly "Rule Out Delusional Disorder" — NOT confirmed.

3. **Imposed impossible assessment conditions.** McNeill ordered Father to tell the evaluator specific negative facts: "He is to inform the assessor of ALL the information that was presented to the Court at the hearing, including the incident at the library, alleged following by the same individuals, eviction from his housing, his Mother's refusal to talk to him any further, and his lack of employment." No parallel requirement was imposed on Mother.

4. **Used Father's hardships as evidence of unfitness.** McNeill wrote: "The Court has serious concerns regarding the state of the Plaintiff's mental health, which could be a culmination of the job loss, homelessness and family loss he has endured over the past several months." She weaponized hardships caused by the litigation itself against the litigant.

5. **Refused police reports as evidence.** Father submitted police reports (Officer Ella Randall, NSPD Case NS2505044) documenting exculpatory evidence. Seven or more police investigations were initiated based on Watson family reports. Every investigation found NO violation by Father. McNeill never cited this exculpatory record in any order, while accepting at face value a police officer's unverified lay opinion that Father's behavior was "the result of meth use" — despite the HealthWest clinical finding of Substance Use = 0 and no drug test ever ordered.

   5a. **Additional exculpatory records excluded.** Beyond Case NS2505044, additional Norton Shores Police Department reports — including NSPD-2023-08121 — and multiple police contacts resulted in zero findings against Father. The categorical refusal to consider any of these exculpatory public records, while simultaneously crediting unsworn adverse allegations, constitutes a pattern of selective evidence exclusion that is independently sanctionable under Canon 3B(5).

6. **Dismissed ADHD disability.** McNeill dismissed Father's documented ADHD condition, suggesting it was psychosomatic.

7. **Selective evidence admission.** McNeill admitted Lori Watson's USB recording — obtained during a phone call without Father's consent, potentially a felony under MCL 750.539d (Michigan's two-party consent wiretapping statute) — while refusing to admit Father's lawfully made November 2023 audio recording of Albert Watson stating: "I will make sure that you don't see your son."

**Conclusion:** The seven categories of bias documented above — rejection of favorable evidence, manufacture of contrary evidence, imposition of impossible conditions, weaponization of hardships, exclusion of exculpatory records, disability dismissal, and selective evidence admission — collectively satisfy the *In re Brown* standard for bias and prejudice warranting JTC discipline. Under *In re Justin*, the sustained pattern warrants greater discipline than isolated incidents.

### Category C: Failure to Be Patient, Dignified, and Courteous (Canon 3B(4))

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill failed to be patient, dignified, and courteous to a litigant in violation of Canon 3B(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Rule:** Canon 3B(4) provides: "A judge shall be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others with whom the judge deals in an official capacity." In *In re Adams*, 494 Mich 162, 179 (2013), the Michigan Supreme Court held that a failure to be patient and dignified with litigants constitutes misconduct. *In re Ferrara*, 458 Mich 350, 367 (1998), established that demeanor-based misconduct — including hostile, demeaning treatment of litigants — is independently sanctionable.

**Application:**

1. **Called Father "a liar" on the record** — a personal attack, not a finding of fact. Under *In re Ferrara*, hostile characterizations directed at litigants — as opposed to formal findings of credibility — constitute demeanor-based misconduct.

2. **Called Father "crazy" on the record** — hostile, demeaning characterization of a pro se litigant.

3. **Dismissed HealthWest evaluation as a "ghost" mental health evaluation** — a prejudicial characterization without evidentiary basis of a professional clinical assessment.

4. **"Do not file any more. I will not look at it."** McNeill stated on the record this categorical refusal to consider pro se filings, characterizing Father's motions as "harassment to Defendant and to the Court, along with an abuse of the Court system" and threatening sanctions for any future filing. No vexatious-litigant finding was ever made under MCR 2.625.

5. **"The PPO orders you to do things, not her. It orders you."** McNeill endorsed the asymmetric application of the PPO as a one-directional weapon, while telling Father regarding Watson family harassment to "just kind of stay out of it, to just leave it be."

**Conclusion:** The pattern of hostile language, dismissive characterizations, and categorical refusal to hear a litigant satisfies both the *In re Adams* standard (failure to be patient and dignified) and the *In re Ferrara* standard (demeanor-based misconduct). The statement "Do not file any more. I will not look at it" is a categorical abdication of judicial function unprecedented in JTC case law.

### Category D: Failure to Accord Full Right to Be Heard (Canon 3B(7))

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill denied Father the right to be heard according to law in violation of Canon 3B(7) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Rule:** Canon 3B(7) provides: "A judge shall accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law." The right to be heard is a fundamental component of due process. Denying a party the opportunity to present argument or evidence before a decision affecting fundamental rights constitutes misconduct. *In re Hathaway*, 464 Mich 672 (2001).

**Application:**

1. **Muted Father during hearings.** During at least one hearing, McNeill muted Father's audio feed **seven (7) separate times** while he was attempting to present argument or objections. During a separate Zoom hearing, Father was muted **three (3) additional times** while presenting evidence. Muting a party during a hearing is the functional equivalent of removing that party from the courtroom — a denial of the right to be heard that directly violates Canon 3B(7).

2. **During SC#5 (Nov 15, 2024):** Father appeared by remote hearing, was muted repeatedly, and court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini sat completely silent — offering no advocacy whatsoever. Father was sentenced to 14 days jail without meaningful opportunity to be heard.

3. **Six motions denied without individual analysis.** Six motions filed July 16, 2024 (the day before trial) were denied in a single sentence without individual analysis — a blanket refusal to consider arguments violating the right to be heard on each motion.

4. **Zero hearings within MCR 3.207(C)(5) 14-day window.** Throughout the entire case history, not a single hearing was held within the 14-day window mandated by MCR 3.207(C)(5) for contested custody and parenting time motions.

5. **230+ days without evidentiary hearing.** The ex parte order of August 8, 2025 suspending all parenting time became a de facto permanent custody modification without any evidentiary hearing — ongoing for 230+ days and counting. Father has had no opportunity to present evidence or argument challenging the suspension of his parental rights.

6. **Orders entered "without a hearing."** The May 16, 2025 order bears the notation "without a hearing" on its face — an acknowledgment that the court made a determination affecting Father's rights without affording him any opportunity to be heard.

**Conclusion:** The systematic pattern of muting Father during hearings, denying motions without analysis, refusing to hold required hearings, and entering orders without notice collectively constitute a sustained denial of the right to be heard under Canon 3B(7) and *In re Hathaway*.

### Canon 3(C)(1): Failure to Exercise Supervisory Diligence

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill failed to exercise diligence in supervisory responsibilities over court staff in violation of Canon 3(C)(1) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Rule:** Canon 3(C)(1) provides: "A judge should exercise diligence in supervisory responsibilities." A judge must ensure that non-judicial staff do not exercise substantive judicial authority, make case-management decisions, or take actions that materially affect the rights of parties.

**Application:**

1. **Rusco initiated warrant request (March 3, 2025).** Pamela Rusco — a judicial secretary, not a judicial officer — emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker requesting that a warrant be issued for Father's arrest. She independently confirmed with the jail that Father was "not currently lodged" and attached the order after hearing and jail mittimus. Initiating a warrant request to a prosecutor, conducting independent investigation into a party's incarceration status, and transmitting court orders as prosecution tools are not ministerial functions — they are substantive judicial and prosecutorial actions improperly delegated to a secretary.
