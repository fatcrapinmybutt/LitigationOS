# EXHIBIT C: Custody Order — September 28, 2025

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-C
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This is the Custody Order entered by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill on September 28, 2025, awarding 100% sole legal and physical custody to Defendant Emily A. Watson and reducing Plaintiff Andrew Pigors's parenting time to zero. The order was entered while Father was incarcerated and unable to meaningfully participate or object. The court found all twelve (12) best interest factors under MCL 722.23 favored Mother — despite substantial evidence to the contrary that was excluded from the record.

## Legal Significance

This order represents the culmination of a series of procedural violations that began with the August 8–9, 2025, ex parte orders (Exhibit B). The September 28, 2025 order:

1. **Was entered while Father was incarcerated.** Father was serving a contempt sentence imposed by the same judge (see Exhibit G), rendering him unable to attend, object, or present evidence.
2. **Excluded the HealthWest evaluation.** A court-ordered psychological evaluation (Exhibit E) found Father fit to parent, with a LOCUS Score of 12 (Level One — community services only), psychosis score of 0, substance score of 0, and danger score of 0. Judge McNeill excluded this favorable evaluation from the record.
3. **Found all 12 factors favor Mother** without adequate evidentiary basis. Under MCL 722.23, the court must evaluate each factor based on evidence. The systematic exclusion of Father's evidence — including the HealthWest evaluation, police reports clearing Father of all allegations, and AppClose communication records (Exhibit J) showing Father's active parenting — fatally undermines the factual basis for the court's findings.
4. **Changed an established custodial environment** without clear and convincing evidence. MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires clear and convincing evidence to modify an ECE. The ECE established by the Consent Order (Exhibit A) was with both parents. No clear and convincing evidence of harm or unfitness was presented at any hearing where Father could participate.

## Key Evidence

- **Order Date:** September 28, 2025.
- **Father's Status:** Incarcerated on contempt charges imposed by the same judge who entered this order.
- **Custody Award:** 100% sole legal and physical custody to Mother; zero parenting time for Father.
- **HealthWest Evaluation (Excluded):** Court-ordered evaluation dated September 5, 2025, found Father fit. LOCUS Score 12, Psychosis 0, Substance 0, Danger 0. Excluded from evidence by the court.
- **Police Reports (Ignored):** Nine (9) NSPD contacts — zero arrests, zero charges across all contacts. Reports consistently cleared Father of allegations.
- **MCL 722.23 Findings:** All 12 best interest factors found to favor Mother, including Factor (j) (willingness to facilitate close relationship with other parent) — despite Mother's documented pattern of denying parenting time and obstructing communication.

## Constitutional Implications

Entering a final custody order while the noncustodial parent is incarcerated — by the same judge who ordered the incarceration — creates an irreconcilable due process violation. Father could not attend the proceeding, could not present evidence, could not cross-examine witnesses, and could not object to the exclusion of his own court-ordered psychological evaluation.

The exclusion of a favorable court-ordered evaluation, combined with the entry of judgment while Father was imprisoned, transforms a custody proceeding into a proceeding devoid of the hallmarks of fairness. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976) (due process requires meaningful opportunity to be heard). This Court's superintending control is necessary to remedy this fundamental unfairness.
