# EXHIBIT G: Contempt Orders — Show Cause #5, #6, and #7

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-G
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises the contempt orders entered by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill in Show Cause proceedings #5, #6, and #7. In total, Father was sentenced to fifty-nine (59) days of incarceration across these three proceedings. Show Cause #5 (November 15, 2024) resulted in fourteen (14) days' incarceration. Show Cause #6 and #7 (November 26, 2025) resulted in a combined forty-five (45) days' incarceration. The contempt finding in SC#6 and SC#7 was based on birthday messages Father sent to L.D.W. through AppClose — the court-approved communication platform.

## Legal Significance

The contempt power is among the most potent in a court's arsenal. Under MCL 600.1701 and MCR 3.606, it may be exercised to enforce compliance with court orders. However, the contempt power is subject to constitutional constraints, including proportionality, the right to be heard, and the prohibition on using contempt to punish protected speech.

1. **SC#5 (November 15, 2024) — 14 Days.** Father was found in criminal contempt. At the hearing, Father was muted three (3) times during proceedings and was unable to present his full defense. Police Report MU2422249 — which confirmed that Father had NOT violated the PPO during the November 15, 2024 custody exchange — was disregarded by the court.

2. **SC#6 and SC#7 (November 26, 2025) — 45 Days Combined.** Father was found in contempt for sending birthday messages to L.D.W. through AppClose, the court-approved co-parenting communication platform. A father wishing his child a happy birthday through a platform the court itself designated is not contumacious conduct — it is an exercise of constitutionally protected parental communication.

3. **Cumulative Impact.** The combined fifty-nine (59) days of incarceration caused Father to lose two (2) homes and two (2) jobs. These are not collateral consequences — they are the direct, foreseeable result of disproportionate incarceration for conduct that did not endanger the child and, in the case of SC#6 and SC#7, constituted protected parental speech.

4. **Strategic Timing.** Father was incarcerated at the precise times when critical custody proceedings were occurring, preventing him from appearing, objecting, or presenting evidence. The September 28, 2025 custody order (Exhibit C) was entered while Father was incarcerated.

## Key Evidence

- **SC#5 — November 15, 2024:**
  - Finding: Criminal contempt, by proof beyond a reasonable doubt.
  - Sentence: 14 days' incarceration.
  - Police Report MU2422249: Officers confirmed Father did NOT violate the PPO during the November 15, 2024 custody exchange. This exculpatory report was disregarded.
  - Father was muted three (3) times during the hearing.

- **SC#6 and SC#7 — November 26, 2025:**
  - Finding: Contempt for birthday messages sent to L.D.W. via AppClose.
  - Sentence: 45 days' combined incarceration.
  - AppClose is the court-approved platform for parental communication.
  - The messages consisted of birthday wishes — protected parental speech.

- **Cumulative Consequences:**
  - 59 total days of incarceration.
  - Two (2) homes lost due to incarceration.
  - Two (2) jobs lost due to incarceration.
  - Father was incarcerated during entry of the September 28, 2025 custody order.

## Constitutional Implications

- **First Amendment:** A father's birthday messages to his child constitute protected speech. Using the contempt power to punish such speech — particularly when sent through a court-approved platform — violates the First Amendment's prohibition on content-based speech restrictions. *United States v. Alvarez*, 567 U.S. 709, 716 (2012).
- **Fourteenth Amendment (Due Process):** The disproportionate use of incarceration for non-dangerous conduct, resulting in the loss of housing and employment, transforms the contempt power from an enforcement mechanism into a punitive weapon. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich. 81, 96 (1987) (the least possible power adequate to the end proposed should be used in contempt cases).
- **Right of Access to Courts:** Incarcerating Father during critical custody proceedings effectively bars him from participating in his own case, violating Const. 1963, art. 1, § 13.
