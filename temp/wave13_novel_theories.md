## THEORY 1: Void Judgment Doctrine
**Legal Basis:** Mich Const 1963 art 1 §17; MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) (void judgments); MCR 3.207(B)/(C) (ex parte requirements)

**Viability: 8/10**

**Best Case Outcome:** All 24 ex parte orders declared void ab initio — not merely voidable. Entire custody/contempt structure collapses. 59 days incarceration becomes wrongful imprisonment. Parenting time automatically reverts to last valid order (50/50 from May 2024–July 2025).

**Devil's Advocate:** Michigan distinguishes "void" from "voidable." A judge with general subject-matter jurisdiction over custody (MCL 722.23) arguably had jurisdiction to enter even defective orders. *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73 (2009) [VERIFY], holds that circuit courts have inherent jurisdiction over domestic relations — making orders voidable (requiring timely appeal), not void. The October 2025 guilty plea to PPO violation may be deemed a waiver of challenge to derivative orders.

**Rebuttal:** Void judgment doctrine attacks *personal* jurisdiction, not subject matter. A court that deprives a party of notice has no jurisdiction *over that party* for that specific order — Const art 1 §17 ("No person shall be … deprived of … liberty … without due process"). 24 orders without notice = 24 instances where the court never acquired jurisdiction over Andrew for those rulings. The guilty plea addressed the PPO itself, not the ex parte custody orders — they are separate proceedings with separate jurisdictional requirements. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) has no time limit for void judgments.

**Filing Target:** F1 (MCR 2.003 Disqualification — include as supporting argument) + F4 (Federal §1983 — Count II enhancement)

## THEORY 2: Judicial Estoppel
**Legal Basis:** MCR 2.003; *Paschke v Retool Industries*, 445 Mich 502 (1994) [VERIFY]; *Lorencz v Ford Motor Co*, 439 Mich 370 (1992) [VERIFY]

**Viability: 5/10**

**Best Case Outcome:** Court finds McNeill took materially inconsistent positions (forgiving Emily's arrears while charging Andrew; accepting Emily's filings while telling Andrew "don't file"), establishing bias per se. Forces disqualification and demonstrates pattern for federal case.

**Devil's Advocate:** Judicial estoppel traditionally applies to *parties*, not judges. A judge's discretionary rulings on different issues at different times are not "inconsistent positions" — they're case management. The court could argue the arrears situations were factually distinguishable (e.g., Emily's was a one-time forgiveness vs. Andrew's was persistent nonpayment). "Don't file" could be characterized as a reasonable response to repetitive filings.

**Rebuttal:** The estoppel argument works better reframed as *evidence of bias* under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) rather than as a standalone doctrine. The disparate treatment table in the existing disqualification motion (¶39) already captures this — identical obligations treated differently based solely on which parent owed them. The "don't file" + $250 bond combination is worse than inconsistency — it's a denial of access to courts (*Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971)).

**Filing Target:** F1 (MCR 2.003 Disqualification — fold into bias pattern evidence, not standalone theory)

## THEORY 3: Unclean Hands — Unauthorized Practice of Law (Emily/Berry)
**Legal Basis:** MCL 600.916 (unauthorized practice of law); MCL 600.901–946 (regulation of practice of law); *Dressel v Ameribank*, 468 Mich 557 (2003) [VERIFY]

**Best Case Outcome:** Court strikes all Berry-drafted filings as products of UPL. If the ex parte motions that triggered the July 17 and August 8, 2025 orders were Berry-drafted, those orders lose their procedural foundation. Emily's litigation position collapses. Potential criminal referral for Berry under MCL 600.916 (misdemeanor).

**Devil's Advocate:** You need *proof* Berry drafted specific filings — not just suspicion. Emily could claim she wrote everything herself and Berry merely discussed strategy (not UPL). Courts distinguish between a non-lawyer *helping someone understand* the process vs. actually *drafting* legal documents. Without metadata, handwriting analysis, or an admission, attribution is speculative.

The voicemail (Item #6) demonstrates Berry's active involvement in the case outside normal boundaries. Discovery in the federal case can compel document metadata, email communications, and Berry's deposition testimony. Even without airtight proof now, the allegation survives a motion to dismiss and creates powerful discovery leverage. Combined with Berry's ex parte contacts with the court, the pattern of involvement exceeds casual family support.

**Filing Target:** F4 (Federal §1983 — strengthen §1985(3) conspiracy count) + F1 (MCR 2.003 — supporting evidence of the Watson-McNeill-Berry coordination pattern)

## THEORY 4: §1985(3) Conspiracy (Watson Family + Berry)
**Legal Basis:** 42 USC §1985(3); *Griffin v Breckenridge*, 403 U.S. 88 (1971); *Dennis v Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980)

**Viability: 6/10**

**Best Case Outcome:** Emily, Albert, Cody, Lori Watson, and Ronald Berry found jointly and severally liable as co-conspirators. None have judicial immunity. Damages multiply across defendants. Albert Watson's admission to Officer Randall (Case NS2505044) — "so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order" — is direct evidence of conspiracy. Lori Watson's felony recording (MCL 750.539c) is an overt act.

**Devil's Advocate:** §1985(3) requires *class-based discriminatory animus* — not just personal animosity. The Sixth Circuit requires the conspiracy target a recognized class. *Radvansky v City of Olmsted Falls*, 395 F.3d 291, 314 (6th Cir. 2005) [VERIFY]. "Unwed fathers" or "pro se litigants" may not qualify. A family coordinating against an ex-partner is personal animus, not class-based. Additionally, the intracorporate conspiracy doctrine may shield McNeill-court interactions from conspiracy liability.

**Rebuttal:** The existing complaint (Count V, ¶¶78-85) already pleads this with a sex-based/pro se-status discrimination theory. The Watson family members are *private parties* — the intracorporate doctrine doesn't apply to private-judge conspiracies. *Dennis v Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980), explicitly holds private parties conspiring with a judge are liable under §1983. The class-based animus hurdle is real — strengthen by emphasizing sex discrimination (every adverse action against the father, zero sanctions against the mother) and the "class of one" equal protection theory from *Village of Willowbrook v Olech*, 528 U.S. 562 (2000). Albert Watson's recorded statement to police is a smoking gun.

**Filing Target:** F4 (Federal §1983 — already pleaded as Count V; expand to name additional Watson family members as defendants or co-conspirators)

## THEORY 5: Monell Pattern & Practice (County Liability)
**Legal Basis:** *Monell v NYC Dept of Social Services*, 436 U.S. 658 (1978); *City of Canton v Harris*, 489 U.S. 378 (1991); 42 USC §1983

**Viability: 4/10**

**Best Case Outcome:** Muskegon County itself is liable — deep pockets, institutional reform. Court orders training, oversight of ex parte procedures, systemic changes to the 14th Circuit's family division. Injunctive relief protects future litigants.

**Devil's Advocate:** *Monell* requires proof that the constitutional violation resulted from an official county *policy or custom* — not just one rogue judge. 24 ex parte orders by one judge in one case is not a "pattern" sufficient for *Monell*. You need evidence that other judges in the 14th Circuit did the same thing, or that the county had a policy/practice of rubber-stamping ex parte custody orders without notice. *City of Canton* failure-to-train requires "deliberate indifference" — very hard to prove with one judge. The county will argue McNeill acted independently, not pursuant to county policy.

**Rebuttal:** *Monell* liability can arise from a single decision-maker with "final policymaking authority." *Pembaur v City of Cincinnati*, 475 U.S. 469 (1986). A circuit court judge making final custody decisions arguably has final policymaking authority for the county in that domain. However, the Sixth Circuit has been skeptical of this theory for judges. The stronger path: discovery into the 14th Circuit's ex parte order practices — if statistics show a pattern of family division judges routinely entering ex parte orders without notice, that's county custom. Without that data, this theory is speculative.

**Filing Target:** F4 (Federal §1983 — add as amended complaint count ONLY after discovery reveals systemic data. Do NOT plead this in the initial complaint without supporting data — it weakens credibility.)

## THEORY 6: Procedural Due Process Per Se Violation
**Legal Basis:** *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976); *Fuentes v Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67 (1972); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982)

**Viability: 9/10** ⭐ STRONGEST THEORY

**Best Case Outcome:** Court holds that 24 ex parte orders without notice constitutes a *per se* due process violation — no *Mathews* balancing required because the pattern is so extreme it cannot be justified under any weighing. This eliminates the state's ability to argue "well, each individual order was an emergency." The sheer volume proves systemic denial of process.

**Devil's Advocate:** Courts almost always apply *Mathews* balancing — there's no clearly established "per se" exception in the Sixth Circuit for volume of ex parte orders. The state will argue each order must be evaluated individually — some may have been justified emergencies. The October 2025 guilty plea to a PPO violation could retroactively justify some of the protective orders.

**Rebuttal:** The existing complaint (Count II, ¶62) already makes the *a fortiori* argument brilliantly: if *Santosky* requires clear-and-convincing evidence *with* a hearing before terminating parental rights, then terminating those rights *without any hearing at all* is necessarily unconstitutional — no balancing needed. The "per se" framing is not a new legal standard — it's the logical conclusion of applying existing law to extreme facts. 24 of 55 orders (43.6%) ex parte is not "emergency discretion." Five orders in one day is not case-by-case judgment. The guilty plea addressed the PPO, not the custody orders — these are separate proceedings.

**Filing Target:** F4 (Federal §1983 — already the core of Count II; emphasize the per se framing in the brief supporting the complaint)

## THEORY 7: ADA Title II — Disability Discrimination in Court Access
**Legal Basis:** 42 USC §12132 (Title II — public services); 28 CFR §35.130; *Tennessee v Lane*, 541 U.S. 509 (2004)

**Viability: 3/10**

**Best Case Outcome:** Court finds McNeill's dismissal of Andrew's ADHD ("all in my head") constitutes disability discrimination in access to public services (courts). Provides an independent federal claim with its own damages framework and attorney's fees. Strengthens the narrative of systematic bias.

**Devil's Advocate:** ADA Title II claims against courts require showing that a qualified individual with a disability was *excluded from or denied the benefits of* services because of disability. ADHD, while a recognized disability, does not typically impair one's ability to participate in court proceedings. Andrew participated in multiple hearings, filed numerous motions, and engaged fully in the process. The "all in my head" comment, while insensitive, is not an *accommodation denial* — Andrew never formally requested an ADA accommodation. Without a formal request and denial, the claim fails. *Duvall v County of Kitsap*, 260 F.3d 1124, 1138 (9th Cir. 2001) [VERIFY] — requires showing failure to reasonably accommodate.

**Rebuttal:** The comment is evidence of *animus* that strengthens the bias/equal protection claims — but standing alone as an ADA Title II claim, it's weak. Andrew would need to show he requested accommodations (extra time to review ex parte orders, written instead of verbal instructions, etc.) and was denied. The comment is better deployed as evidence of McNeill's dismissive attitude and personal prejudice under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and the §1983 bias claims, not as an independent ADA claim.

**Filing Target:** F1 (MCR 2.003 — use the comment as evidence of personal prejudice, ¶13 of existing motion). Do NOT file as standalone ADA claim without formal accommodation request/denial evidence.

## THEORY 8: Franks v Delaware Applied — Poisonous Tree Doctrine
**Legal Basis:** *Franks v Delaware*, 438 U.S. 154 (1978); *Mapp v Ohio*, 367 U.S. 643 (1961); *Wong Sun v United States*, 371 U.S. 471 (1963); MCL 750.539c (felony eavesdropping); *People v Beavers*, 393 Mich 554 (1975)

**Viability: 7/10**

**Best Case Outcome:** Court finds Emily's PPO affidavit contained knowing falsehoods (contradicted by: 124 cooperative AppClose messages, negative drug screen, all-zeros HealthWest evaluation, zero police findings). Under *Franks*, when an affidavit contains deliberate falsehoods and the remaining content is insufficient to establish probable cause, the warrant/order must be suppressed. All derivative orders (custody modifications, contempt findings, incarceration) are fruit of the poisonous tree and must be vacated.

**Devil's Advocate:** *Franks* is a Fourth Amendment criminal law doctrine — it applies to search warrants, not PPOs or custody orders. No court has applied *Franks* to a civil PPO affidavit in the way proposed. The standard is "knowing falsehoods" or "reckless disregard for truth" — Emily could claim she genuinely believed her allegations even if they were wrong. Subjective fear (even unreasonable) can support a PPO under MCL 600.2950. The October 2025 guilty plea to PPO violation undermines the argument that the PPO was based on false allegations.

**Rebuttal:** The existing complaint (¶¶46D-46F) already establishes the poisonous tree doctrine through Lori Watson's *felony eavesdropping recording* — that's a stronger entry point than the *Franks* analogy because MCL 750.539c makes the recording independently criminal, and *People v Beavers*, 393 Mich 554 (1975), confirms Michigan applies exclusionary principles to such recordings. Don't try to stretch *Franks* to civil PPOs — instead, use the established exclusionary rule via the felony recording. The false affidavit evidence (124 cooperative messages contradicting "danger" claims) is better deployed as evidence of conspiracy (§1985(3)) and fraud on the court rather than through the *Franks* framework.

**Filing Target:** F4 (Federal §1983 — already partially pleaded in ¶¶46D-46H; strengthen with *Beavers* citation and the cooperative-messages contradiction) + State motion to suppress/strike the felony recording and all derivative orders
