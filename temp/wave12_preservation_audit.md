## 2. PRESERVED ISSUES — STRONGEST ON APPEAL

These issues were properly preserved through timely objection, motion, or filing in the lower court. They receive full appellate review under their respective standards.

### 2A. ★★★★★ TIER — LEAD WITH THESE

**ISSUE 1: Ex Parte Suspension Without MCR 3.207(B) Compliance**
- **Preservation mechanism:** Written objection and motion to modify/rescind the ex parte order filed timely per the order's own instructions (F3 ¶33). Hearing requested repeatedly.
- **Standard of review:** De novo — whether the court complied with MCR 3.207(B), MCL 722.27a, and the 2025 amendments is a pure question of law. Fletcher v Fletcher, 447 Mich 871, 877–881 (1994).
- **Why strongest:** The 90-day delay (4.3× the 21-day MCR 3.207(B)(6) limit) is a mathematical fact, not a judgment call. The 2025 amendments to MCR 3.207 and 3.210 (effective Sept. 1, 2025) tightened these requirements mid-case, making the violation even more glaring. No court can reasonably conclude that 90 days complies with a 21-day limit.
- **Risk of waiver:** NONE. Timely objection filed.

**ISSUE 8: Failure to Conduct MCL 722.23 Best-Interest Analysis**
- **Preservation mechanism:** Multiple motions requesting evidentiary hearings with best-interest findings; all denied or ignored.
- **Standard of review:** De novo — whether the court performed the mandatory statutory analysis is a question of law. The word "shall" in MCL 722.23 creates a non-discretionary duty. In re Sanders, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014).
- **Why strongest:** The absence of ANY best-interest findings on ANY of the 12 statutory factors is absolute — not a matter of degree. The COA cannot reasonably uphold a total parenting-time suspension where zero MCL 722.23 analysis appears in the record. Fletcher v Fletcher, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994).
- **Risk of waiver:** NONE.

**ISSUE 9: Failure to Enforce Parenting Time (MCL 552.644)**
- **Preservation mechanism:** Motions to enforce parenting time filed under MCL 552.644; denied/ignored.
- **Standard of review:** De novo — "shall" is mandatory. In re Sanders, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014).
- **Why strongest:** MCL 552.644 provides "the court **shall** do 1 or more of the following" when a custodial parent violates parenting time. Watson's violations are documented (269 total days of withholding). Zero enforcement actions taken. The asymmetry is stark: Father jailed 59 days for alleged PPO violations; Mother faces zero consequences for documented parenting-time violations.
- **Risk of waiver:** NONE.

**ISSUE 6: Judicial Bias / Disqualification**
- **Preservation mechanism:** MCR 2.003 motion for disqualification (F3) filed with supporting affidavit of bias and brief in support — the textbook preservation vehicle.
- **Standard of review:** De novo for constitutional due process (Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009)); abuse of discretion for MCR 2.003 determination (Cain v Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich 470 (1996)).
- **Why strongest:** F3 is a 76-page, comprehensive disqualification filing documenting 14+ specific incidents of bias with documentary evidence (Rusco email, Martini email, HealthWest memo). The $250 bond that blocked adjudication is ITSELF evidence of bias.
- **Risk of waiver:** NONE — filing the MCR 2.003 motion IS preservation.

**ISSUE 7: $250 Bond as Unconstitutional Access Barrier**
- **Preservation mechanism:** The bond was imposed ON the disqualification motion — the error is self-preserving. Petitioner also sought IFP status.
- **Standard of review:** De novo — constitutional question under Boddie v Connecticut, 401 U.S. 371 (1971); M.L.B. v S.L.J., 519 U.S. 102 (1996); Mich Const 1963, art 1, § 13.
- **Why strongest:** Creates a closed loop of unreviewable bias — the biased judge blocks the mechanism for removing biased judges. This is legally indefensible and the COA/MSC must address it.

**ISSUE 3: Suppression of Defense Evidence**

- **Why strongest:** Asymmetry is devastating — court accepted Watson's unsworn/ex parte materials while categorically excluding Father's documentary evidence. Police reports qualify as business records (MRE 803(6)) and public records (MRE 803(8)). Blanket exclusion of an entire evidence category is per se error.

## 3. UNPRESERVED ISSUES — PLAIN ERROR ANALYSIS

Issues not formally raised below but potentially reviewable under MCR 2.613(C) and People v Carines, 460 Mich 750 (1999).

### Plain Error Standard (Three-Part Test):
1. **Clear or obvious error** that is not subject to reasonable dispute
2. **Affected substantial rights** — the error was outcome-determinative
3. **Seriously affected the fairness, integrity, or public reputation** of judicial proceedings

### Pro Se Relaxation:
Under Haines v Kerner, 404 U.S. 519 (1972), pro se filings are held to "less stringent standards than formal pleadings drafted by lawyers." Michigan follows this principle. This relaxes the preservation requirement — imprecise objections may still count.

## 4. STRUCTURAL ERROR CANDIDATES — AUTOMATIC REVERSAL

Structural errors are so fundamental that they defy harmless-error analysis. Per Arizona v Fulminante, 499 U.S. 279 (1991), structural errors require automatic reversal regardless of whether the defendant can show prejudice and regardless of preservation.

### 4A. ★★★★★ MUTING DURING HEARINGS — DENIAL OF RIGHT TO BE HEARD

**Error:** Judge McNeill ordered Father's microphone muted three (3) separate times during the November 15, 2025 Show Cause #5 remote hearing. Father was attempting to present a police report documenting L.D.W.'s poor physical condition. 14 days of incarceration were imposed following a proceeding in which the respondent was physically prevented from speaking.

**Why structural:**
- The right to be heard is the bedrock of due process. Mullane v Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co., 339 U.S. 306, 314 (1950) ("An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process in any proceeding which is to be accorded finality is notice reasonably calculated . . . and an opportunity to present . . . objections.").
- Muting a party's microphone during a hearing is the functional equivalent of removing them from the courtroom. It is a complete denial of the opportunity to be heard — not merely a limitation.
- Per Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, every person legally interested in a proceeding shall be accorded the right to be heard according to law.
- A proceeding that results in incarceration, conducted while the respondent is silenced, is not a "hearing" in any constitutional sense. It is a sentencing.

**Preservation status:** NO PRESERVATION NEEDED. The court physically prevented Father from speaking — he *could not* preserve the issue because the error itself silenced him. This is the quintessential structural error: the mechanism for preservation was destroyed by the error itself.

**Standard:** Automatic reversal. The COA need not conduct harmless-error analysis.

**Strength:** ★★★★★ — This is potentially the single strongest issue for reversal.

---

### 4B. ★★★★☆ CONSTRUCTIVE DENIAL OF COUNSEL (MARTINI)

**Error:** Court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini:
- Sat silently throughout Show Cause #5 while Father was muted 3 times
- Raised zero objections to any procedural irregularity
- Prior to the hearing, emailed Father: "You will not interrupt . . . Judge McNeil is not in a good mood . . . you do not want to spend the next week in jail"
- Effectively threatened her own client with additional incarceration for exercising his right to speak
- Acknowledged the prosecution admitted "nothing substantive in the motion" yet adopted a strategy of capitulation

**Why structural:**
- Under United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648, 659 (1984), a complete denial of counsel — including the constructive denial where counsel is present but entirely fails to function as an advocate — is a structural error requiring automatic reversal without a showing of prejudice.
- Martini's conduct constitutes constructive denial: she was physically present but functionally absent. She did not cross-examine, did not object, did not advocate, and actively discouraged her client from speaking.
- This is distinguishable from Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984) ineffective assistance (which requires prejudice showing). Cronic applies when counsel's failure is so complete that the adversarial process itself was rendered unreliable.

**Preservation status:** NO PRESERVATION NEEDED if Cronic applies. The party cannot be expected to object to their own counsel's failures in real time, especially when counsel has threatened them with jail for speaking.

**Complication:** Michigan courts have been reluctant to apply Cronic outside criminal cases. The argument is strongest for the contempt proceedings (SC#5) that resulted in 14 days of incarceration — these carry criminal-contempt-equivalent procedural protections. Mead v Batchlor, 435 Mich 480 (1990).

**Strength:** ★★★★☆ — Strong under Cronic but faces Strickland counterargument.

### 4C. ★★★★★ BIASED JUDGE WHO SHOULD HAVE BEEN DISQUALIFIED

**Error:** The comprehensive pattern documented in F3 (76 pages) — ex parte orders, muting, "Do not file anymore," evidence suppression, asymmetric enforcement, Rusco warrant email, off-record witness coordination — establishes a constitutionally intolerable probability of actual bias under Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009).

**Why structural:**
- Under Tumey v. Ohio, 274 U.S. 510, 523 (1927), a biased tribunal is a structural defect. "[E]very procedure which would offer a possible temptation to the average man as a judge . . . not to hold the balance nice, clear, and true between the state and the accused denies the latter due process of law."
- The Supreme Court has consistently classified biased-judge errors as structural. Arizona v Fulminante, 499 U.S. 279, 310 (1991) (listing biased judge among structural errors).
- The bias here is documented with extraordinary specificity: Canon 3(B)(7) violations (Rusco warrant email, HealthWest off-record coordination), Canon 3(A)(4) violations (muting, "Do not file" directive), asymmetric enforcement (59 days jail vs. zero enforcement), and the $250 bond that blocked the disqualification remedy itself.

**Preservation status:** PRESERVED through F3 (MCR 2.003 motion). But even if it were unpreserved, biased-tribunal errors are structural and require no preservation.

**Standard:** Automatic reversal of all orders entered by the biased judge. Reassignment to a new judge mandatory.

**Strength:** ★★★★★ — Combined with the F3 filing, this is exceptionally strong.

---

### 4D. ★★★★★ VOID JUDGMENTS (Ex Parte Orders Without Notice/Jurisdiction)

**Error:** 24 of 55 orders (44%) were entered ex parte — without notice to Father and without the emergency circumstances required by MCR 3.207(B). Per Bowie v Arder, 441 Mich 23, 49 (1992), an order entered without the procedural prerequisites mandated by due process is void, not merely voidable.

**Why structural:**
- A void judgment is a nullity — it has no legal effect regardless of when or how it is challenged. It cannot be cured by subsequent proceedings and does not require preservation.
- The August 6–8, 2025 ex parte order: entered without notice, without hearing, without verified child-specific allegations of immediate harm, without best-interest findings, and without the clear-and-convincing standard required by MCL 722.27a(3). Under MCR 3.207(B) and Bowie, this order was void ab initio.
- Serial continuation of a void order for 90 days does not cure the void — it compounds it.

**Preservation status:** NO PRESERVATION NEEDED for void judgments. Father preserved it anyway (timely objection filed).

**Standard:** Vacatur — void orders are vacated as a matter of law.

**Strength:** ★★★★★ — The strongest category. Void orders cannot stand regardless of any other analysis.

---

### 4E. ★★★★☆ "DO NOT FILE ANYMORE" — DENIAL OF ACCESS TO COURTS

**Error:** Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it." This was a blanket directive prohibiting Father from exercising his constitutional right to petition the court.

**Why structural:**
- Combined with the $250 bond, this effectively closed the courthouse doors entirely. When a court both orders a party not to file AND imposes a financial barrier to filing, it has created a total denial of access.
- The right of access to courts is guaranteed by Mich Const 1963, art 1, § 13 ("shall have justice administered without sale, denial or delay") and the Due Process Clause.
- This creates a Catch-22: Father cannot preserve future issues because the court told him not to file, and even if he tries, his filings require a $250 bond.

**Preservation status:** SELF-PRESERVING — the error itself prevents preservation. The directive appears in the record. No further preservation possible or required.

**Standard:** Automatic reversal of any orders entered after this directive, as they were entered in a proceeding where one party was barred from participating.

**Strength:** ★★★★☆ — Very strong, but Watson could argue the directive was limited in scope. The record language ("anymore" + "I will not look at it") is broad enough to support the total-denial reading.

2. **Frame bias/disqualification as the umbrella argument** — if the COA agrees the judge was biased, every other order falls.
