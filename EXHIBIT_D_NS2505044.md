# EXHIBIT D: NSPD Police Report No. NS2505044

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-D
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

## Description

This is Norton Shores Police Department Report No. NS2505044, dated August 7, 2025 — one day before the five ex parte orders were issued. The report documents a call initiated by Albert Watson (Defendant Emily A. Watson's father). The report is critical because it contains Albert Watson's admission that the police call was made as a predicate to obtain an ex parte custody order the following day. This report proves the ex parte orders were not a response to a genuine emergency, but the product of a premeditated plan.

## Legal Significance

MCR 3.207(A) authorizes ex parte orders only when there is evidence of "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage." The legal standard requires a genuine emergency — not a manufactured one.

This police report destroys the emergency predicate for the August 8 ex parte orders (Exhibit B) because:

1. **Albert Watson told the police the purpose of the call.** He stated that the police report was being generated so that Emily could use it to obtain an ex parte order for full custody the next day.
2. **The police found no emergency.** The report documents no arrests, no charges, and no evidence of imminent harm to L.D.W.
3. **Officer Ella Randall's findings undermined the Defendant's narrative.** The officer's report noted observations suggesting *Defendant's* substance use concerns, not Father's.
4. **The timeline proves coordination.** August 5: recording made at Watson home. August 7: Albert Watson calls police to create a paper trail. August 8: five ex parte orders issue. This is not an emergency response — it is a three-day orchestrated campaign.

Judge McNeill signed five ex parte orders within hours of receiving this manufactured police report, without conducting any independent inquiry and without providing Father notice or an opportunity to respond. This Court's superintending control is required to address a trial court that issued the most extreme custody remedy available based on a police report that, on its face, reveals the filing was premeditated.

## Key Evidence

- **Report Date:** August 7, 2025, filed with Norton Shores Police Department.
- **Reporting Party:** Albert Watson, father of Defendant Emily A. Watson.
- **Key Admission:** Albert Watson stated to police that *"They want this documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son."*
- **Police Findings:** No arrest. No criminal charges. No evidence of imminent danger to L.D.W.
- **Officer Ella Randall:** The investigating officer noted indicators suggesting concern about Defendant's conduct. Officer Randall's report referenced that she *"believed this was a result of meth use"* — a finding that implicated Emily A. Watson, not Plaintiff.
- **Defendant's Filing References This Report:** Emily A. Watson's own ex parte motion filed August 8, 2025, referenced the NS2505044 report as the basis for emergency relief, despite the report containing Albert Watson's admission of premeditation.

## Constitutional Implications

When a court acts on a police report that, on its face, reveals the "emergency" was manufactured by the opposing party's family member, the court ceases to function as a neutral arbiter. The right to due process requires that judicial action be based on genuine emergency, not orchestrated filings. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371, 379 (1971) (due process prohibits state action that is fundamentally unfair).

The NSPD report proves the ex parte orders were the product of a coordinated effort — not a judicial response to imminent harm. Father's fundamental parental rights were extinguished based on a manufactured predicate, in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment.
