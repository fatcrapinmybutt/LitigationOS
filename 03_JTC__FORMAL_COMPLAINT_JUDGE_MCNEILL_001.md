## IV. PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

This Formal Complaint is filed pursuant to Michigan Constitution of 1963, Article 6, § 30; MCL 600.5701 *et seq.*; and MCR 9.200–9.220, requesting that the Michigan Judicial Tenure Commission investigate, and upon finding cause, discipline the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill for a sustained pattern of judicial misconduct in the above-captioned cases.

The misconduct documented herein is not the product of isolated judicial error or the exercise of reasonable discretion. Rather, it reflects a **systematic pattern** of due process violations, ex parte communications, denial of fundamental parental rights, hostile treatment of a pro se litigant, abuse of contempt powers, and disregard of mandatory court rules — all of which have caused, and continue to cause, irreparable harm to a minor child (L.D.W., age 2) and his father.

This Complaint is supported by comprehensive review of court records, official transcripts, police reports, clinical evaluations, and docket entries spanning the entirety of the above-captioned proceedings. The evidence establishes clear and convincing proof of violations of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, the Michigan Court Rules, and binding constitutional protections.

### ALLEGATION 3: Failure to Apply Statutory Best Interests Factors

**Case:** No. 2024-001507-DC
**Dates:** Entire case history
**Rule Violations:** MCL 722.27a; MCL 722.27a; *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003)

1. MCL 722.23 requires that all custody and parenting time determinations be based on written findings addressing **twelve (12) enumerated best interests factors** (factors a–l).

2. Throughout the entire history of Case No. 2024-001507-DC, Judge McNeill has made **zero (0) written findings** under MCL 722.23 on any of the twelve statutory factors.

3. Independent analysis of the record demonstrates that **nine (9) of the twelve (12)** best interests factors favor Complainant as the custodial parent.

4. The failure to make required best interests findings is not a matter of judicial discretion — it is a mandatory statutory requirement. *See Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509 (2003) ("The trial court must consider all the best interests factors…and make findings of fact on each one.").

5. No custody modification, change in parenting time, or suspension of parental contact can be lawfully entered without these findings. Under *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 78–87 (2017), when a parenting-time restriction effectively alters a child's established custodial environment — as the total suspension of August 8, 2025 indisputably did — the court must apply the *Pierron* framework, require clear and convincing evidence, and make written MCL 722.23 findings before entering the order. Judge McNeill skipped every step of this mandatory process.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibit G (Docket Sheet — Absence of MCL 722.23 Findings); Exhibit H (best interests factor Analysis — 9 of 12 Favor Father).
