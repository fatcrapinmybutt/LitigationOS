# EXHIBIT E: HealthWest Psychological Evaluation

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-E
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This is the court-ordered psychological evaluation conducted by HealthWest (Muskegon County Community Mental Health), completed on or about September 5, 2025. The evaluation was ordered by the court itself to assess Plaintiff Andrew Pigors's fitness as a parent. The evaluation found Father to be a fit parent with no clinical concerns. Despite these favorable findings, Hon. Jenny L. McNeill excluded this evaluation from the evidentiary record and entered a custody order (Exhibit C) finding all twelve best interest factors favored Mother.

## Legal Significance

The exclusion of a court-ordered evaluation that favored Father is among the most significant indicators of judicial bias in this case:

1. **The court ordered the evaluation.** When a court orders a psychological assessment, it has already determined the evaluation is relevant to its decision-making. Excluding the results of its own order is internally contradictory.
2. **The findings were unambiguous.** The evaluation found Father fit to parent. No clinical basis existed for restricting custody or parenting time.
3. **The exclusion violated evidentiary rules.** Under MRE 702–703, expert evaluations ordered by the court carry substantial evidentiary weight. Excluding a favorable court-ordered evaluation while admitting adverse evidence from the opposing party creates a fundamentally one-sided record.
4. **FOC interference compounded the problem.** Pamela Rusco, the Friend of the Court case manager assigned to this case, contacted the HealthWest clinician on October 29, 2025 — the day before a scheduled hearing. This ex parte contact between the FOC and the evaluating clinician raises serious concerns about improper influence on expert testimony.

This Court's superintending control is necessary because the trial court's exclusion of its own court-ordered evaluation — combined with the FOC's improper ex parte contact with the evaluator — produced a custody determination untethered from the evidence.

## Key Evidence

- **Evaluation Date:** On or about September 5, 2025.
- **Evaluating Agency:** HealthWest (Muskegon County Community Mental Health).
- **Nature:** Court-ordered psychological evaluation of Plaintiff Andrew Pigors.
- **Clinical Findings:**
  - **LOCUS Score: 12** (Level One — community services only; lowest-intensity level, indicating no acute concerns).
  - **Psychosis Score: 0** — No psychotic symptoms detected.
  - **Substance Score: 0** — No substance abuse detected.
  - **Danger Score: 0** — No danger to self or others.
- **Conclusion:** Father is a fit parent. No clinical contraindication to custody or parenting time.
- **Court's Action:** Hon. Jenny L. McNeill excluded this evaluation from the evidentiary record.
- **FOC Ex Parte Contact:** Pamela Rusco (FOC case manager) contacted the HealthWest clinician on October 29, 2025 — the day before a hearing. The nature and content of this contact were not disclosed to Father.

## Constitutional Implications

The systematic exclusion of exculpatory evidence from a custody proceeding violates the fundamental fairness guaranteed by the Due Process Clause. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976). While family courts have broad discretion over evidentiary rulings, that discretion does not extend to excluding a court-ordered evaluation that reaches conclusions favorable to one party while simultaneously relying on the opposing party's unverified allegations.

The FOC's ex parte contact with the evaluating clinician further corrupts the evidentiary process. When the court's own officer contacts an expert witness outside the presence of the parties, the integrity of the evaluation — and of the proceedings themselves — is compromised beyond repair.
