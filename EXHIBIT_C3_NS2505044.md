# EXHIBIT C-3: NSPD Report NS2505044

## Description

This exhibit is the North Shore Police Department (NSPD) incident report bearing case number NS2505044, dated August 7, 2025. The report documents a police contact initiated by Albert Watson — the father of Defendant Emily A. Watson — in which Albert Watson made a critical admission to law enforcement officers regarding the premeditated nature of the ex parte custody proceedings that followed the next day.

According to the police report, Albert Watson told responding officers: "They want this documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son." This statement, made to law enforcement one day before Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill issued five ex parte orders stripping Plaintiff of all custody (Exhibit C-2), constitutes direct evidence that the ex parte proceedings were not a response to any genuine emergency, but rather the culmination of a premeditated plan coordinated among private parties to weaponize the judicial process against Plaintiff.

The NSPD report is an official law enforcement record, created by a neutral third party in the ordinary course of police business, and constitutes powerful evidence of the conspiracy between private and state actors that lies at the heart of this §1983 action.

## Legal Significance

This exhibit is critical to establishing the conspiracy element of the §1983 claim. Under 42 USC §1983, private parties who conspire with state actors to deprive a person of constitutional rights are jointly liable. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 27–28 (1980). Albert Watson's admission demonstrates that the ex parte orders were not the product of independent judicial determination, but rather the execution of a plan devised and coordinated by private actors — specifically, members of Defendant Emily A. Watson's family.

The timing is devastating to any claim of judicial independence in the issuance of the ex parte orders. Albert Watson's statement was made on August 7, 2025. The five ex parte orders were issued on August 8, 2025. The one-day gap between the premeditation admission and the judicial action eliminates any possibility that the orders resulted from an independent assessment of emergency circumstances. This evidence supports a finding that the state court proceedings were corrupted by improper ex parte communications between private parties and the court, in violation of both due process and the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(A)(4).

This exhibit also defeats any claim of judicial immunity. When a judge acts in concert with private parties to deprive a person of constitutional rights, the judge acts outside the scope of judicial authority. *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11–12 (1991) (per curiam) (judicial immunity does not apply to nonjudicial actions or actions taken in the complete absence of jurisdiction).

- **No genuine emergency:** The call to police was to create a paper trail for the next day's court filing — not to report any actual danger

## Constitutional Implications

The NS2505044 report transforms this case from a dispute about procedural irregularity into evidence of a deliberate conspiracy to deprive Plaintiff of his constitutional rights through the misuse of state judicial power. The Fourteenth Amendment prohibits not only the deprivation of liberty without due process, but also the use of state mechanisms as instruments of private vendettas. When private parties successfully co-opt the judicial process to deprive another person of fundamental rights, every participant in the conspiracy — state and private actors alike — is liable under §1983.

The premeditated nature of the deprivation also bears on the question of damages. A planned, coordinated assault on a parent's constitutional rights is qualitatively different from an erroneous judicial decision made in good faith. The evidence of premeditation supports an award of punitive damages against the private-party defendants, as their conduct demonstrates the type of evil motive or callous indifference to constitutional rights that warrants punitive relief. *Smith v. Wade*, 461 U.S. 30, 56 (1983).
