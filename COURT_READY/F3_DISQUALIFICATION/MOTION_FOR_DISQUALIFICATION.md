## IV. LEGAL STANDARD

&para; 37. MCR 2.003(C)(1) establishes three independent grounds for disqualification, each of which is satisfied here:

| Ground | Rule | Standard |
|--------|------|----------|
| Actual Bias | MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) | The judge IS biased or prejudiced for or against a party |
| Serious Risk of Actual Bias | MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) | Objective, reasonable perceptions show serious risk of bias impacting due process (*Caperton*) |
| Appearance of Impropriety | MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii) | Failure to adhere to Canon 2 appearance-of-impropriety standard |

&para; 38. The party seeking disqualification must overcome a "heavy presumption of judicial impartiality." *Cain v Department of Corrections*, 451 Mich. 470, 497; 548 N.W.2d 210 (1996). However, this presumption is rebuttable and is overcome where the evidence demonstrates more than mere adverse rulings — specifically, where it demonstrates extrajudicial source bias, a pattern of conduct making fair judgment impossible, or a systematic denial of procedural rights. *See Liteky v United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 555 (1994).

&para; 39. The constitutional floor is set by *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 884 (2009): due process requires recusal when "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge . . . is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." Michigan adopted this standard directly into MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) in 2009.

&para; 40. Michigan has long recognized this principle. *Crampton v Department of State*, 395 Mich. 347, 351; 235 N.W.2d 352 (1975): "a hearing before an unbiased and impartial decisionmaker is a basic requirement of due process under the Fourteenth Amendment."

&para; 41. The appearance-of-impropriety test does not require proof of actual bias. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich. App. 656, 671; 766 N.W.2d 44 (2009): "The question is whether a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would harbor doubts about the judge's impartiality."

## V. ARGUMENT

### A. IRAC Analysis 1: Ex Parte Orders Without Notice Violating MCR 3.207(B)

**Issue:** Whether the ex parte orders of July 17, 2025, and August 8, 2025, suspending Plaintiff's parenting time without notice, hearing, or required factual findings, constitute grounds for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

**Rule:** MCR 3.207(B) requires that emergency ex parte orders affecting parenting time be supported by specific findings of immediate and irreparable harm. MCR 2.119(B)(1) requires that the movant show notice was given or explain why notice should not be required. The Fourteenth Amendment protects a parent's fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and management of his child. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). "When a State seeks to deprive a parent of a protected interest in the parent-child relationship, it must afford fundamentally fair procedures." *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753-54 (1982). *Liteky v United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 555 (1994), holds that extrajudicial-source bias — bias arising from outside normal judicial proceedings — warrants disqualification, and further holds that even in-court conduct warrants disqualification if it displays "a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible."

**Application:** Judge McNeill entered two ex parte orders suspending Plaintiff's parenting time — on July 17, 2025, and August 8, 2025 — without providing Plaintiff notice or an opportunity to be heard. The August 8 order suspended all parenting time with L.D.W. and contained no findings of fact as required by MCR 3.207(B) or MCL 722.27a(3). Plaintiff timely filed an objection and motion to rescind, but the Court allowed the ex parte order to remain in effect for approximately four months without hearing — resulting in the complete severance of the parent-child relationship for what is now 223 consecutive days. The entry of ex parte orders depriving a parent of his fundamental constitutional rights, without the minimum procedural protections required by Michigan court rules and constitutional due process, is not a routine judicial act — it is an act that, viewed objectively, creates a serious risk of actual bias impacting Plaintiff's due process rights within the meaning of *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 884.

**Conclusion:** The ex parte orders constitute both extrajudicial-source bias under *Liteky* (the deprivation occurred outside any adversarial proceeding) and a failure to adhere to the appearance-of-impropriety standard under Canon 2. These orders independently establish grounds for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

### B. IRAC Analysis 2: Denial of Right to Be Heard — Muting During November 15, 2025 Hearing

**Issue:** Whether physically muting a party's microphone three times during a hearing, then sentencing him to 14 days' incarceration, constitutes grounds for disqualification.

**Rule:** Due process requires notice and the opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides that a judge must accord every person with an interest in a proceeding the right to be heard according to law. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich. 81, 95 (1987), requires that civil contempt proceedings include adequate notice, the right to present a defense, and the opportunity to purge. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Township*, 248 Mich. App. 573, 597 (2001), holds that disqualification is warranted when "the conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality, and competence is impaired."

**Application:** During the November 15, 2025, remote hearing, Judge McNeill muted Plaintiff's microphone three separate times while he was attempting to present his case. Plaintiff's court-appointed attorney, Mandi Martini, sat silently and did not object. The Court then imposed 14 days of incarceration. A hearing at which the respondent is physically prevented from speaking is not a "meaningful" hearing under *Mathews*. It does not satisfy the procedural safeguards required by *Dougherty* for contempt proceedings. The muting was not isolated — it occurred three times in a single hearing, demonstrating a deliberate and systematic effort to silence Plaintiff. Attorney Martini's pre-hearing email warning that the judge "doesn't want to hear from you unless asked" and that Plaintiff risked "a contempt charge for speaking out in court" confirms that the judge's hostility was known and anticipated before the hearing began. No reasonable, objective person would view this conduct as consistent with impartial adjudication.

**Conclusion:** The systematic muting of Plaintiff, combined with immediate incarceration, satisfies the *Armstrong* "reasonable person" test and establishes grounds for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii). It further demonstrates the "deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible" identified in *Liteky*, 510 U.S. at 555.

### C. IRAC Analysis 3: Categorical Refusal to Consider Filings — "Do Not File Anymore"

**Issue:** Whether a judge's on-the-record statement categorically refusing to consider a party's future filings constitutes grounds for disqualification.

**Rule:** The First Amendment and the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment guarantee the right of access to the courts. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371, 383 (1971) (due process requires access to the courts for resolution of fundamental interests). *In re Disqualification of Noe*, 454 Mich. 1201 (1997), holds that disqualification is warranted when a judge's pattern of conduct creates an objective appearance of prejudgment. Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires that a judge avoid "impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities."

**Application:** Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it." This statement is not a routine case management directive — it is a blanket, categorical refusal to consider any future filing by Plaintiff, regardless of its substance, merit, or urgency. A judge who prejudges filings not yet filed has abandoned the appearance of neutrality. This directive, combined with the $250 filing deposit imposed as a precondition to future filings, creates a dual barrier — financial and judicial — to Plaintiff's court access. No other party in these proceedings has been subjected to either restriction. The statement satisfies the *Noe* standard because it creates an objective appearance that the judge has prejudged all future matters involving Plaintiff.

**Conclusion:** The "do not file anymore" directive, reinforced by the $250 filing deposit, constitutes an independent ground for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) (actual prejudice) and MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii) (appearance of impropriety).

### D. IRAC Analysis 4: HealthWest Evaluation Manipulation and Rusco Ex Parte Communications

**Issue:** Whether the Court's rejection of a favorable evaluation, ex parte communication with the evaluating agency, and use of the judicial secretary to coordinate clinical testimony and prosecution action constitute grounds for disqualification.

**Rule:** Canon 3(B)(7) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct prohibits a judge from "initiat[ing], permit[ting], or consider[ing] ex parte communications or other communications made to the judge outside the presence of all parties regarding a pending proceeding." Michigan Ethics Opinion JI-134 extends this prohibition to all court staff, requiring that judges "instruct and train their personnel — including court clerks and FOC workers — not to engage in or permit ex parte communications on substantive issues." *People v Perkins*, 193 Mich. App. 209 (1992), holds that the appearance of impropriety requires disqualification "even without a showing of actual bias or prejudice."

**Application:** The record establishes three distinct ex parte violations flowing from the Court's chambers:

*First*, Judge McNeill sent a communication to HealthWest describing Plaintiff's behaviors as "suggestive of a mental health disorder" — without Plaintiff's knowledge — between the first evaluation (September 4, 2025, clean baseline) and the second evaluation (September 11, 2025, "Rule-out Delusional Disorder"). The Court then rejected the favorable first evaluation and relied upon the contaminated second evaluation. The second evaluation was transmitted to chambers via email rather than filed through the Clerk of Court.

*Second*, on March 3, 2025, Pamela Rusco emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker requesting a warrant for Plaintiff's arrest, independently verifying with the jail that Plaintiff was "not currently lodged," and attaching court orders as prosecution exhibits. This is a judicial secretary initiating law enforcement action against a party — a substantive prosecutorial communication that blurs the separation between judicial and prosecutorial functions.

*Third*, on October 29, 2025, Rusco contacted HealthWest to arrange clinical testimony and offered to transmit subpoenas by email — coordinating witness testimony outside the presence of the parties and outside the court record.

Each of these incidents independently violates Canon 3(B)(7). Together, they demonstrate a pattern in which the Court's chambers actively operated as an adversary to Plaintiff — coordinating prosecution, contaminating evaluations, and arranging testimony — all outside the adversarial process.

**Conclusion:** The ex parte communications constitute extrajudicial-source bias under *Liteky*, violations of Canon 3(B)(7), and a failure to adhere to the appearance-of-impropriety standard under Canon 2. Each violation independently warrants disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). Collectively, they are devastating.

### E. IRAC Analysis 5: Cumulative Pattern of Bias Under *Caperton*

**Issue:** Whether the cumulative effect of the documented pattern of judicial conduct establishes that the probability of actual bias is "too high to be constitutionally tolerable" under *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009).

**Rule:** *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 884, holds that due process requires recusal when, "[u]nder a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness," the probability of actual bias "is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." The test is objective — based on "reasonable perceptions," not proof of actual subjective bias. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Township*, 248 Mich. App. 573, 597 (2001), recognizes that a pattern of conduct can establish bias even where individual incidents might be defensible in isolation. *People v Jackson*, 292 Mich. App. 583, 597 (2011), holds that "repeated procedural irregularities favoring one party create a reasonable perception of bias." *In re MKK*, 286 Mich. App. 546 (2009), reinforces that in family law proceedings, the appearance of partiality is particularly corrosive because the court's role as protector of the child's interests demands the highest standard of neutrality.

**Application:** This is not a case of a litigant dissatisfied with unfavorable rulings. This is a case of systematic, structural bias demonstrated by:

(a) *Ex parte adjudication:* Ex parte orders on July 17 and August 8, 2025, depriving Plaintiff of parenting time without notice or hearing.

(b) *Silencing a party:* Muting Plaintiff three times during the November 15, 2025, hearing, then imposing 14 days' incarceration.

(c) *Prejudging future filings:* Announcing "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it."

(d) *Contaminating evidence:* Sending an ex parte communication to HealthWest that transformed a clean evaluation into a "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" finding, then relying on the contaminated evaluation.

(e) *Ex parte prosecution coordination:* Judicial secretary Rusco requesting warrants from prosecutors and coordinating clinical testimony — all outside the adversarial process.

(f) *Financial barrier:* Imposing a $250 filing deposit on a pro se litigant who had lost four jobs and two homes.

(g) *Disproportionate punishment:* 59 days of incarceration — including 45 days for sending birthday messages to his child through a court-sanctioned application.

(h) *Differential treatment:* Forgiving Defendant's child support arrears while holding Plaintiff in contempt for his; granting Defendant's ex parte motions same-day while ignoring Plaintiff's motions; accepting Defendant's unsworn allegations while rejecting Plaintiff's police reports as "hearsay."

(i) *Disability dismissal:* Dismissing Plaintiff's documented ADHD as "all in my head."

Each individual incident raises a question about impartiality. Together, they establish a pattern that no reasonable, objective person could reconcile with neutral adjudication. Under a "realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness" (*Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 884), the probability of actual bias is not merely high — it is overwhelming.

This case is distinguishable from those in which disqualification was denied on the basis of *Cain*'s "heavy presumption." In *Cain*, the Michigan Supreme Court acknowledged that the presumption exists but emphasized that it is overcome where the evidence goes beyond "adverse rulings" to demonstrate systemic procedural violations. *Cain*, 451 Mich. at 497. The Court further recognized that "in rare cases, the appearance of impropriety can reach a due process violation level." *Id.* This is that rare case.

**Conclusion:** The cumulative pattern satisfies all three prongs of MCR 2.003(C)(1): actual bias under (a), serious risk of actual bias impacting due process under (b)(i), and failure to adhere to the appearance-of-impropriety standard under (b)(ii). Disqualification is constitutionally required under *Caperton*.

### F. Overcoming the Presumption of Impartiality

&para; 42. Plaintiff acknowledges the heavy presumption of judicial impartiality established in *Cain v Department of Corrections*, 451 Mich. 470, 497 (1996). This presumption exists for sound institutional reasons. But it is a presumption, not an irrebuttable rule.

&para; 43. The presumption is overcome here because the evidence does not rest on adverse rulings — the very basis *Cain* held insufficient. Rather, the evidence demonstrates:

(a) **Extrajudicial-source bias.** The ex parte communications through Rusco (warrant request to prosecutors, clinical testimony coordination with HealthWest, contamination of evaluations) originate outside any judicial proceeding. *Liteky*, 510 U.S. at 555, holds that extrajudicial-source bias independently warrants disqualification.

(b) **Deep-seated favoritism making fair judgment impossible.** Muting a party three times, sentencing him to jail for birthday messages, refusing to consider his filings, and imposing financial barriers to court access — while granting opposing party's ex parte motions same-day — constitutes the "deep-seated favoritism or antagonism" that *Liteky* holds warrants disqualification even for in-court conduct. *Liteky*, 510 U.S. at 555.

(c) **Structural violations, not outcome complaints.** The gravamen of this Motion is not that the Court ruled against Plaintiff. It is that the Court adjudicated matters ex parte, silenced Plaintiff, refused to consider his filings, contaminated evaluations, and coordinated prosecution — all structural violations of due process that go to the integrity of the process itself, not the outcome.

Moreover, Plaintiff's continued participation in prior proceedings was compelled — as a party subject to the court's jurisdiction and facing contempt sanctions, Plaintiff had no practical ability to refuse to appear before Judge McNeill.

Only a judge with no professional, personal, or institutional ties to the 14th Circuit bench can provide the appearance and reality of impartial adjudication.
