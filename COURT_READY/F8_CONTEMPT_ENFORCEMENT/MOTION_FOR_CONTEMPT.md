## IV. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### IRAC Block 1: Watson Violated Existing Parenting Time Order

**Issue:** Whether Defendant violated this Court's parenting time order.

**Rule:** To establish civil contempt for parenting time violation, the moving party must

prove by a preponderance of the evidence: (1) the existence of a clear court order; (2) the

respondent's knowledge of the order; (3) the respondent's noncompliance; and (4) the

absence of good cause for noncompliance. *Porter v. Porter*, 285 Mich App 450 (2009).

**Analysis:**

17\. **Element 1 — Clear Order Exists:** This Court entered a specific parenting time

order establishing a 50/50 schedule. The order is clear and unambiguous.

18\. **Element 2 — Knowledge:** Defendant is a party to this action, was present at

proceedings, and was represented by counsel (Jennifer Barnes, P55406, who subsequently

withdrew). Defendant has actual knowledge of the parenting time order.

19\. **Element 3 — Noncompliance:** Defendant has denied Plaintiff all parenting time for

223 consecutive days since August 8, 2025. Prior to that date, Defendant engaged in

sporadic interference including 44 documented AppClose violations, canceled visits, and

blocked communication. The noncompliance is total, sustained, and ongoing.

20\. **Element 4 — No Good Cause:** Defendant has not demonstrated — and cannot

demonstrate — good cause for 223 days of complete denial. "Good cause" under MCL

552.645 requires circumstances genuinely beyond the party's control. Obtaining an

ex parte order through a premeditated, family-coordinated strategy is not an involuntary

circumstance — it is a willful act.

**Conclusion:** All four elements of civil contempt are established by overwhelming

evidence. The burden shifts to Defendant to prove good cause, which she cannot do.

---

### IRAC Block 2: Court Authority to Hold Defendant in Contempt

**Issue:** Whether this Court has authority to hold Defendant in contempt.

**Rule:** MCL 600.1701(c) authorizes courts to exercise contempt powers against any

person guilty of "[d]isobedience or resistance to a lawful writ, process, order, rule, or

command of the court." The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that circuit courts

possess inherent contempt power independent of statute. *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23,

40 (1992) ("Courts have inherent power to punish for contempt of their authority").

**Analysis:**

21\. Defendant's 223-day denial of court-ordered parenting time constitutes

"disobedience or resistance to a lawful... order... of the court" under MCL 600.1701(c).

22\. This Court's authority to enforce its own orders through contempt proceedings is

both statutory and inherent. The failure to enforce such orders would render the Court's

parenting time orders advisory rather than mandatory — an outcome the Legislature

explicitly rejected by enacting the Support and Parenting Time Enforcement Act, MCL

552.601 *et seq.*

**Conclusion:** This Court has clear statutory and inherent authority to hold Defendant in

contempt for her willful and continuing violation of the parenting time order.

---

### IRAC Block 3: MCL 552.644 Mandatory Remedies

**Issue:** What remedies are available and mandatory upon a finding of contempt.

**Rule:** MCL 552.644 enumerates specific remedies the court shall impose when a party

is found in contempt of a parenting time order. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that for each

parenting time occasion denied, the court shall order makeup parenting time of the same

type and duration. The word "shall" makes makeup parenting time mandatory, not

discretionary. *See also* MCL 552.642 (circuits required to establish makeup time policies).

**Analysis:**

23\. Defendant has denied Plaintiff 223 days of parenting time. Under MCL 722.27a(7),

this Court is required to order makeup parenting time "of the same type and duration" for

each occasion denied.

24\. Beyond makeup time, MCL 552.644 authorizes:

   a. Modification of the parenting time order (MCL 552.644(1)(b));

   b. Fines up to $100 per violation (MCL 552.644(1)(d));

   c. Commitment to county jail (MCL 552.644(1)(e));

   d. Work release provisions (MCL 552.644(1)(f));

   e. Conditional suspension of driver's license, occupational license, or recreational license (MCL 552.644(1)(g));

   f. Community corrections program (MCL 552.644(1)(h));

   g. Participation in a parenting program or counseling (MCL 552.644(1)(i)).

**Conclusion:** MCL 552.644 provides a comprehensive enforcement toolkit. The severity

and duration of Defendant's violations warrant the full range of available sanctions.

### IRAC Block 4: Parental Alienation as Basis for Custody Modification

**Issue:** Whether Defendant's conduct constitutes parental alienation warranting custody

modification.

**Rule:** Michigan courts recognize that a custodial parent's systematic interference with

the other parent's relationship constitutes a factor weighing against the interfering parent

under the best-interest analysis. MCL 722.23(j) (willingness to facilitate close relationship

with the other parent). The Michigan Court of Appeals has held that persistent denial of

parenting time and active interference with the parent-child bond are proper grounds for

modification of custody. *See Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31 (2010) (factor (j)

analysis); *See also* MCL 722.27(1)(c) (court may modify custody if in child's best interest).

**Analysis:**

25\. Defendant's conduct constitutes textbook parental alienation:

   a. 223 consecutive days of complete separation — no contact of any kind;

   b. 44 documented AppClose violations — blocking the sole communication channel;

   c. Third-party coordination (Berry, Cody Watson, Albert Watson) to ensure Plaintiff has no access;

   d. Use of the legal system to achieve *de facto* sole custody without meeting statutory modification requirements;

   e. L.D.W. is three years old and has been denied his father for more than seven months during a critical developmental period.

26\. Under MCL 722.23(j), a parent's "willingness and ability to facilitate and encourage

a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent" is

a statutory best-interest factor. Defendant has demonstrated not merely unwillingness but

active hostility toward the father-child relationship.

**Conclusion:** Defendant's sustained parental alienation campaign provides independent

grounds for modification of custody under MCL 722.27(1)(c), in addition to the contempt

remedies sought herein.

---

### IRAC Block 5: Preemption of "Good Cause" Defense

**Issue:** Whether Defendant can establish "good cause" for her denial of parenting time.

**Rule:** MCL 552.645 defines "good cause" for noncompliance with a parenting time

order as requiring circumstances genuinely beyond the party's control. Once the moving

party establishes a violation, the burden shifts to the alleged contemnor to prove good

cause. *Porter v. Porter*, 285 Mich App 450 (2009).

**Analysis:**

27\. Defendant cannot establish good cause for the following reasons:

   a. **The ex parte order was self-manufactured.** The August 8, 2025 suspension of Plaintiff's parenting time resulted from Defendant's own ex parte motion — a motion that was premeditated and coordinated with family members, as documented in the Albert Watson police report admission. A party cannot create the circumstances of her own noncompliance and then invoke those circumstances as "good cause."

   b. **No finding of danger to L.D.W.** There has been no evidentiary finding that L.D.W.'s physical, mental, or emotional health would be endangered by parenting time with Plaintiff. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that the court "shall not deny parenting time" absent such a finding.

   c. **438 days of safe, successful co-parenting.** Plaintiff exercised 50/50 parenting time for 438 consecutive days without any safety incident. This documented history of safe parenting directly contradicts any claim that parenting time poses a danger to L.D.W.

   d. **Pattern establishes willfulness, not good cause.** The 44 AppClose violations, the Berry and Cody Watson threats, the family coordination to obtain the ex parte order, and the 223 days of complete denial collectively establish a willful campaign to deny parenting time — not circumstances beyond Defendant's control.

**Conclusion:** Defendant cannot meet her burden of establishing good cause. The

violations were willful, premeditated, and coordinated.
