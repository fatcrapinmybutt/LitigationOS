### A. Substantive Due Process

63\. The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause includes a substantive component that "provides heightened protection against government interference with certain fundamental rights and liberty interests." *Washington v. Glucksberg*, 521 U.S. 702, 720 (1997). The right of a parent to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

64\. Government action that infringes a fundamental liberty interest must withstand **strict scrutiny** — it must be narrowly tailored to serve a compelling state interest. *Reno v. Flores*, 507 U.S. 292, 302 (1993). The wholesale suspension of all parenting time through ex parte orders, maintained for 223+ consecutive days, without any finding of danger or emergency, is not narrowly tailored to any compelling interest.

65\. The conduct of Defendants, individually and in concert, "shocks the conscience" within the meaning of *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833, 846 (1998). Defendants orchestrated a total deprivation of Plaintiff's parental rights through fabricated evidence, perjured testimony, ex parte adjudication, evaluation tampering, and retaliatory incarceration — without any of the constitutional protections required before the State may sever the parent-child bond.

66\. **Against McNeill:** McNeill entered the critical ex parte orders — including the August 8, 2025 order suspending all parenting time — without notice, hearing, or findings of fact, effecting a de facto termination of Plaintiff's parental rights without strict scrutiny or any constitutional process.

67\. **Against Watson:** Watson initiated and perpetuated the deprivation by filing perjured PPO and custody motions based on fabricated evidence, thereby triggering the chain of ex parte orders that destroyed Plaintiff's parenting relationship. Acting in concert with McNeill, Watson acted under color of state law. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. at 27–28.

68\. **Against Berry:** Berry participated in the deprivation by drafting legal filings as a non-attorney (unauthorized practice of law, MCL 600.916), making ex parte voicemail contacts with the court, and actively assisting Watson in the campaign to sever Plaintiff's parental relationship. Berry's conduct, combined with Albert Watson's recorded threat — "I will make sure you don't see your son" — followed by McNeill's entry of orders accomplishing precisely that, evidences the family-wide conspiracy and the conspiratorial character of McNeill's orders.

69\. **Against Rusco:** Rusco participated in the deprivation by coordinating the biasing communication to the HealthWest evaluator, thereby corrupting the only neutral assessment that could have protected Plaintiff's rights.

76\. The retaliatory timing — the bond was imposed after Plaintiff filed motions challenging the ex parte orders — demonstrates retaliatory motive. *Crawford-El v. Britton*, 523 U.S. 574 (1998).

84\. Rusco's role as the conduit for McNeill's retaliatory evaluation tampering makes Rusco independently liable under § 1983. A state employee who carries out a retaliatory directive is not insulated from liability merely because the directive came from a superior. *Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800 (1982).
