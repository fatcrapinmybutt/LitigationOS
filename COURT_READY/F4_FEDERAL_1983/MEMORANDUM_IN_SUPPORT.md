## I. INTRODUCTION

This action asserts five counts of constitutional violations arising from a systematic pattern of ex parte adjudication, retaliatory incarceration, evaluation tampering, and conspiracy that has deprived Plaintiff of his fundamental parental rights for more than 223 consecutive days. Defendants will predictably raise six threshold defenses: (1) judicial immunity; (2) the domestic relations exception; (3) the Rooker-Feldman doctrine; (4) *Younger* abstention; (5) qualified immunity; and (6) failure to state a claim under *Iqbal/Twombly*. Each is addressed and defeated below.

## II. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY IS PIERCED BY THE CONSPIRACY

### A. The General Rule

Judges are generally entitled to absolute immunity from § 1983 damages for acts performed in their judicial capacity. *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11 (1991); *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356–57 (1978). The doctrine serves the important function of allowing judges to exercise independent judgment without fear of personal liability.

### B. Exception 1 — Non-Judicial Acts

Judicial immunity does not apply to acts "not taken in the judge's judicial capacity." *Mireles*, 502 U.S. at 11–12; *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219 (1988). The following acts by McNeill are non-judicial:

(a) **Ex parte communications with a party** — administrative acts violating MCR 2.003 and Canon 3A(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct;

(b) **Self-ruling on her own disqualification motion** — a function MCR 2.003(D) assigns to the Chief Judge, not the challenged judge;

(c) **Directing that Plaintiff's filings not be reviewed** ("Do not file anymore") — a categorical refusal to adjudicate is the absence of judicial action, not the exercise of it;

(d) **Directing Rusco to transmit biasing communications to the HealthWest evaluator** — ex parte interference with a purportedly neutral evaluation is not a judicial act; it is an extrajudicial act designed to corrupt the evidence upon which the court would later rely;

(e) **Conspiratorial conduct with Watson, Berry, and Rusco** — conspiracy is not a function normally performed by a judge. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 31 (1980).

### C. Exception 2 — Clear Absence of All Jurisdiction

Judicial immunity does not apply to acts "taken in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356–57 (1978). The jurisdiction to act ex parte under MCR 3.207(C)(2) is *conditional* — it requires specific sworn facts showing imminent danger. When McNeill entered the ex parte order of August 8, 2025, without compliance with the mandatory prerequisites that confer jurisdiction to act ex parte, she acted in the **clear absence of the jurisdiction that due process confers**.

### D. Dennis v. Sparks — The Conspiracy Pierces Immunity

The central authority is *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980). In *Dennis*, the Supreme Court held that "a private party who corrupts the judicial process" is liable under § 1983:

> "[P]rivate persons, jointly engaged with state officials in [a] challenged action, are acting 'under color' of law for purposes of § 1983 actions." *Id.* at 27–28.

The Court held that "judicial immunity is not a bar to prospective injunctive relief," and that "the co-conspiring private parties are not combating a mere private wrong but a wrong against the institutions set up to protect and safeguard the public." *Id.* at 28–29.

**Application:** Watson, Berry, and Rusco are private parties who conspired with Judge McNeill. Under *Dennis*, they possess **no judicial immunity whatsoever**. McNeill herself, when acting as a co-conspirator rather than as a neutral adjudicator, is stripped of the immunity that protects judicial — not conspiratorial — acts. The conspiracy theory renders all defendants subject to § 1983 liability regardless of McNeill's judicial status.

## III. THE DOMESTIC RELATIONS EXCEPTION DOES NOT BAR THIS ACTION

### A. The Exception Is Narrow

The Supreme Court in *Ankenbrandt v. Richards*, 504 U.S. 689, 700–04 (1992), definitively held that the domestic relations exception to federal jurisdiction is **narrow** — it applies only to federal courts' issuance of divorce, alimony, and child custody decrees. The exception does **not** bar federal constitutional claims merely because they arise in a family law context.

The *Ankenbrandt* Court was explicit: "the domestic relations exception . . . divests the federal courts of power to issue divorce, alimony, and child custody decrees." *Id.* at 703. It does not extend to "intra-family torts" or constitutional claims. *Id.* at 700–04.

### B. Sixth Circuit Authority Is Dispositive

The Sixth Circuit addressed this precise issue in *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279, 293 (6th Cir. 1998), holding that the domestic relations exception does not bar § 1983 claims arising in a family law context. *Catz* involved constitutional claims arising from domestic relations proceedings — the same posture as this case.

### C. This Complaint Does Not Seek a Custody Decree

This Complaint does not seek a custody decree, a divorce, or an alimony award. It seeks **damages for constitutional violations** — deprivation of due process, First Amendment retaliation, denial of access to courts, and conspiracy under § 1985(3). These are federal constitutional tort claims, not domestic relations matters.

The relief sought — damages, declaratory judgment, and injunctive relief requiring reassignment — does not require this Court to issue any custody decree. The domestic relations exception is therefore inapplicable.

## IV. THE ROOKER-FELDMAN DOCTRINE DOES NOT APPLY

### A. The Governing Standard

The Rooker-Feldman doctrine divests federal district courts of jurisdiction only over cases "brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments rendered before the district court proceedings commenced and inviting district court review and rejection of those judgments." *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp.*, 544 U.S. 280, 284 (2005).

The Sixth Circuit has adopted the "source of the injury" test: Rooker-Feldman applies only where the "source of the injury" is the state court judgment itself. *McCormick v. Braverman*, 451 F.3d 382, 393 (6th Cir. 2006). If the source is conduct by state actors rather than the judgment, the doctrine does not apply.

### B. The Source of Injury Is Conduct, Not Judgment

This Complaint does not ask the Court to reverse, vacate, or modify any state court custody determination. Plaintiff does not argue that the state court reached the wrong substantive result on custody. The "source of injury" in each count is *conduct*, not a *judgment*:

(a) **Count I (Due Process):** The injury is the total deprivation of parental rights without notice, hearing, or strict scrutiny — not the custody ruling itself;

(b) **Count II (First Amendment Retaliation):** The injury is retaliatory incarceration for protected speech and retaliatory evaluation tampering — no state judgment is implicated;

(c) **Count III (Access to Courts):** The injury is the $250 bond, the "do not file" directive, the muting, and the self-ruling on disqualification — systematic denial of access;

(d) **Count IV (§ 1985(3) Conspiracy):** The injury is the coordinated deprivation through a concerted pattern of ex parte adjudication and extrajudicial collaboration;

(e) **Count V (Monell):** The injury is the County's policies and customs that caused the constitutional violations.

None of these claims would be "rendered moot" by any particular custody outcome. They are independent constitutional violations actionable under § 1983. Rooker-Feldman therefore poses no jurisdictional bar.

## V. YOUNGER ABSTENTION DOES NOT APPLY

### A. Sprint v. Jacobs Narrowed Younger to Three Categories

In *Sprint Communications, Inc. v. Jacobs*, 571 U.S. 69, 78 (2013), the Supreme Court dramatically narrowed *Younger v. Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971), holding that *Younger* abstention is confined to only **three narrow categories**:

(a) Ongoing state criminal prosecutions;

(b) Civil enforcement proceedings akin to criminal prosecutions;

(c) Civil proceedings uniquely in furtherance of the state courts' ability to perform their judicial functions.

The Court emphasized: "[T]his Court has never applied *Younger* outside these three categories." *Sprint*, 571 U.S. at 81–82.

### B. A Private Custody Proceeding Does Not Fit

A private custody proceeding between two parents is not a criminal prosecution, not a civil enforcement proceeding akin to a criminal prosecution, and not a proceeding in furtherance of the state courts' judicial functions. It fits none of the three *Sprint* categories.

### C. The Bad Faith Exception Applies

Even if *Younger* were otherwise applicable, the **bad faith exception** squarely applies. *Younger*, 401 U.S. at 54; *Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124 (1975). The facts demonstrating bad faith include:

(a) Numerous ex parte orders entered without notice or hearing;

(b) Muting Plaintiff three times during a hearing;

(c) Refusing to review exculpatory evidence;

(d) Imposing 45 days of incarceration for birthday messages;

(e) The "Do not file anymore" directive;

(f) Evaluation tampering through Rusco;

(g) The documented conspiracy between the judge and private parties.

### D. The State Forum Is Structurally Inadequate

A fundamental premise of *Younger* is that the state forum provides an "adequate opportunity" to raise federal claims. *Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n*, 457 U.S. 423, 432 (1982). Where the federal claim is *against the judge presiding over the state proceeding*, no adequate state remedy exists. A litigant cannot raise a § 1983 claim against the very judge controlling his case. The inherent conflict renders the state forum structurally inadequate. *See Gibson v. Berryhill*, 411 U.S. 564, 577 (1973).

*Younger* abstention therefore does not apply.

## VI. QUALIFIED IMMUNITY DOES NOT SHIELD DEFENDANTS

### A. The Two-Prong Analysis

Under *Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800, 818 (1982), government officials are entitled to qualified immunity unless their conduct violates "clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known." The two-prong analysis requires: (1) whether the facts alleged show the defendant violated a constitutional right; and (2) whether the right was "clearly established" at the time. *Pearson v. Callahan*, 555 U.S. 223, 236 (2009).

### B. Prong One — Constitutional Violations Are Sufficiently Alleged

As set forth in the Complaint (Counts I–IV), Defendants violated Plaintiff's substantive due process, procedural due process, First Amendment, and access-to-courts rights.

### C. Prong Two — The Rights Were Clearly Established

Every right at issue was clearly established **decades** before the 2024–2025 conduct alleged:

| Constitutional Right | Clearly Established Since |
|---|---|
| Right to notice and hearing before deprivation of fundamental liberty | *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) |
| Fundamental right to parent | *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972); *Troxel*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) |
| Right to counsel/safeguards before contempt incarceration | *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011); *Mead v. Batchlor*, 435 Mich. 480 (1990) |
| First Amendment protection of parent-child speech | *Troxel*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) |
| Right of access to courts | *Bounds v. Smith*, 430 U.S. 817 (1977); *Boddie*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971) |
| Equal protection against systematic disparate treatment | *Village of Willowbrook v. Olech*, 528 U.S. 562 (2000) |

No reasonable official could have believed that entering ex parte orders suspending all parenting time without notice or hearing, incarcerating a father for 45 days for birthday messages, muting a litigant, refusing to review exculpatory evidence, or telling a litigant "Do not file anymore" was lawful. These are not close calls on the margins of constitutional law.

### D. Rusco Specifically

Defendant Rusco, as a county employee, might invoke qualified immunity. However, the clearly established right to a fair and unbiased evaluation process — free from ex parte judicial interference — was well established by 2025. *Ward v. Village of Monroeville*, 409 U.S. 57 (1972) (due process requires neutral decision-making); *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009) (due process violated when circumstances create an "unconstitutional potential for bias"). No reasonable county employee would believe that transmitting a biasing communication to a court-ordered evaluator — designed to change the outcome of a neutral assessment — was constitutionally permissible.

### E. Private Parties Have No Qualified Immunity

Defendants Watson and Berry are private parties, not government officials. Private parties cannot invoke qualified immunity. *Wyatt v. Cole*, 504 U.S. 158, 168–69 (1992). They possess no immunity defense whatsoever.

## VII. THE COMPLAINT SATISFIES IQBAL/TWOMBLY PLEADING STANDARDS

### A. The Standard

Under *Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009), and *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007), a complaint must contain "enough facts to state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face." The Court must "draw on its judicial experience and common sense" to determine plausibility. *Iqbal*, 556 U.S. at 679.

**Monell (Count V):**

### C. Pro Se Liberal Construction

This Complaint is entitled to liberal construction under *Haines v. Kerner*, 404 U.S. 519, 520 (1972), and *Erickson v. Pardus*, 551 U.S. 89, 94 (2007). Pro se filings are "held to less stringent standards than formal pleadings drafted by lawyers." Even under the more demanding *Iqbal/Twombly* standard, this Complaint exceeds the plausibility threshold because it provides specific dates, specific conduct by named defendants, specific constitutional provisions, and specific harms — all supported by documentary evidence that will be produced in discovery.

### D. Heck v. Humphrey Does Not Bar These Claims

Plaintiff does not challenge the validity of his PPO guilty plea (October 18, 2025) or the underlying contempt findings. A judgment in Plaintiff's favor would not "necessarily imply the invalidity of his conviction or sentence." *Heck v. Humphrey*, 512 U.S. 477, 486–87 (1994). Plaintiff challenges the *process* — not the outcome. Due process claims and underlying merits are distinct inquiries. *Schreiber v. Moe*, 596 F.3d 323, 334 (6th Cir. 2010).
