## IV. STANDARD OF REVIEW

Under MCL 722.28, this Court applies three standards of review in custody and parenting-time appeals:

**1. Findings of fact** are reviewed under the **great-weight-of-the-evidence** standard. A finding will be sustained unless the evidence clearly preponderates in the opposite direction. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 877–881; 526 NW2d 889 (1994).

**2. Discretionary rulings** — including custody and parenting-time decisions — are reviewed for **palpable abuse of discretion** and will be reversed when the outcome falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Id.*

**3. Questions of law** — including interpretation of court rules and statutes — are reviewed **de novo**. *Id.*

**Due-process violations** — including improper ex parte procedure and deprivation of meaningful opportunity to be heard — are reviewed **de novo**. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404–412; 852 NW2d 524 (2014).

**Judicial disqualification** under MCR 2.003(C) is reviewed **de novo** as a question of law. *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996).

**Unpreserved constitutional issues** may be reviewed under the **plain-error** standard where the error is apparent on the record and failure to review would result in manifest injustice. *People v Carines*, 460 Mich 750; 597 NW2d 130 (1999) (applied by analogy in civil cases).

## V. ARGUMENT

### ISSUE I: THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY SUSPENDING ALL PARENTING TIME FOR 223+ DAYS WITHOUT THE FINDINGS REQUIRED BY MCL 722.27a(3) AND WITHOUT PROPER CAUSE OR CHANGE IN CIRCUMSTANCES UNDER *VODVARKA*

#### A. Governing Law

The Child Custody Act strongly favors ongoing parent-child relationships. Parenting time "shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child" and is presumed beneficial. MCL 722.27a(1). Restriction or denial is permitted only upon **clear and convincing evidence** that parenting time "would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(3).

Before modifying parenting time, a court must find either "proper cause shown" or a "change of circumstances" sufficient to justify revisiting the prior order. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 1, 8; 672 NW2d 198 (2003). The movant bears the threshold burden of demonstrating that the circumstances have materially changed since the last custody or parenting-time order. *Id.* at 9–10.

When a parenting-time restriction effectively alters a child's established custodial environment, the court must apply the *Pierron* framework: determine the established custodial environment, require clear-and-convincing evidence to support changes that alter it, and make best-interest findings on the MCL 722.23 factors. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 85–93; 782 NW2d 480 (2010); *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 28–31; 805 NW2d 1 (2010); *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 78–87; 900 NW2d 130 (2017).

MCR 3.207(B)(1) allows ex parte relief in domestic-relations matters **only** when specific facts in an affidavit or verified pleading show that irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result before notice and a hearing can be held. When such an order is entered, MCR 3.207(B)(6) requires the court to schedule and hold an evidentiary hearing within **21 days**.

Effective September 1, 2025, amended MCR 3.207 and MCR 3.210 further tightened ex parte practice, requiring verified facts about the existing custodial environment and scheduling an evidentiary hearing when the ex parte order "could alter a child's established custodial environment."

#### B. Application to This Case

**1. No threshold showing under *Vodvarka*.** The trial court suspended all parenting time without making any finding of "proper cause" or "change in circumstances" to justify modifying the existing parenting-time arrangement. For 438 days, Father had exercised 50/50 custody safely. No new allegation of harm — supported by verified facts or credible evidence — justified upending that arrangement. The *Vodvarka* threshold was never met.

**2. No sworn, child-specific facts.** Mother's emergency motion was unsworn and unverified. No affidavit laid out concrete, immediate, and irreparable harm to L.D.W. that would result from the short delay necessary to provide notice and hold a hearing. This fails the threshold of MCR 3.207(B)(1).

**3. No evidentiary hearing within 21 days.** MCR 3.207(B)(6) required the trial court to schedule and hold an evidentiary hearing within 21 days of the August 6–8, 2025 ex parte order. The first hearing occurred on **October 29, 2025** — 90 days after the ex parte order, violating the rule by a factor of 4.3×.

**4. The most drastic restriction without clear-and-convincing evidence.** A total suspension of all parenting time is the most extreme remedy available. It is the functional equivalent of awarding sole physical custody to the other parent. Under MCL 722.27a(3), that extreme remedy requires clear-and-convincing evidence that *any* parenting time would endanger L.D.W.'s physical, mental, or emotional health. No such evidence was ever produced. The court-ordered HealthWest evaluation (September 4, 2025) affirmatively found no safety risk. The court ignored it.

**5. No best-interest findings.** The trial court never applied the 12 best-interest factors of MCL 722.23. Under *Pierron* and *Lieberman*, a total suspension that alters the established custodial environment requires findings on each relevant factor, supported by clear-and-convincing evidence. That analysis was never performed.

**6. Inversion of the statutory presumption.** Rather than presuming parenting time is appropriate unless clear-and-convincing evidence shows danger (the statutory framework), the trial court treated *no parenting time* as the default and required Father to disprove untested allegations. This inverts MCL 722.27a(1) and violates the Act's core presumption favoring strong parent-child relationships.

**7. The 2025 amendments independently require reversal.** To the extent the trial court extended the August 2025 ex parte suspension after September 1, 2025, the amended MCR 3.207 and MCR 3.210 expressly forbid changing an established custodial environment without an evidentiary hearing. The continued suspension without a hearing, sworn proof, and best-interest findings violates these additional procedural safeguards.

### ISSUE II: THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED APPELLANT'S DUE PROCESS RIGHTS BY DENYING ANY MEANINGFUL EVIDENTIARY HEARING BEFORE EFFECTIVELY TERMINATING THE PARENT-CHILD RELATIONSHIP

#### A. Governing Law

Parents possess a fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and control of their children — "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). When the state seeks to sever or substantially restrict that interest, heightened procedural protections are constitutionally required. *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–754 (1982).

The *Mathews v Eldridge* three-factor test governs what process is due: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through existing procedures and the probable value of additional safeguards; and (3) the governmental interest, including the fiscal and administrative burden of additional procedures. 424 US 319, 335 (1976).

Michigan's due process clause independently requires "notice and the opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 92; 763 NW2d 587 (2009). Evidence used to restrict fundamental rights must be relevant, authenticated, and — when clinical — grounded in reliable principles and sufficient facts. MRE 401–403, 702–703, 901. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404–412; 852 NW2d 524 (2014).

#### B. Application

**1. The private interest is at its zenith.** Under *Mathews*, the private interest at stake — a parent's relationship with his child — is among the most fundamental interests known to law. Father has been completely separated from his child for 223+ days. Every passing day deepens the harm to both Father and L.D.W.

**2. The risk of erroneous deprivation is extreme.** The existing procedures produced a catastrophically erroneous result:

- The court credited an unauthenticated "second" mental-health evaluation that Father never underwent, while discrediting the court-ordered HealthWest evaluation that found no safety risk. (Settled Statement ¶¶ 23–26.)
- The 7-day diagnostic "flip" from clean baseline (September 4) to "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" (September 11) is clinically implausible for a condition requiring sustained, chronic presentation under the DSM-5.
- The off-record coordination between Rusco and HealthWest clinicians (October 29, 2025) fatally compromised the independence of the clinical evidence.
- Father's exculpatory evidence — the favorable HealthWest evaluation, AppClose logs, police clarifications, and the NSPD letter confirming false attribution of "meth use" — was excluded or ignored.
- Father was muted three times during the November 15, 2025 hearing while attempting to testify. His appointed counsel sat silent.

Additional procedural safeguards — principally, a timely evidentiary hearing within 21 days as required by MCR 3.207(B)(6) — would have had enormous value. The court's failure to hold such a hearing allowed an untested ex parte order to calcify into a 223+ day separation.

**3. The governmental interest does not justify summary deprivation.** No legitimate governmental interest — whether protecting the child or conserving judicial resources — is served by denying a parent any hearing for 90 days while that parent's child is kept from him entirely. The 21-day hearing requirement of MCR 3.207(B)(6) exists precisely because the state's interest in child welfare is served by *prompt, fair adjudication*, not by prolonged deprivation without process.

**4. Reliance on unauthenticated evidence compounded the violation.** The court based ongoing suspension on a purported "second" evaluation that was never authenticated under MRE 901, never subjected to cross-examination, and never tested for reliability under MRE 702–703. See *People v Yost*, 278 Mich App 341, 356; 749 NW2d 753 (2008) (authentication requirements). Using unauthenticated, unexamined materials to sustain a total parenting-time suspension violates both evidentiary rules and the constitutional requirement that restrictions on fundamental rights rest on reliable evidence. *Santosky*, 455 US at 769–770.

#### C. Relief

This Court should:

1. Hold that the trial court violated Appellant's due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment and Mich Const 1963, Art 1, § 17;
2. Vacate all orders entered without a timely evidentiary hearing compliant with MCR 3.207(B)(6);
3. Direct the trial court to admit and consider Father's exculpatory evidence, including the HealthWest evaluation, on remand; and
4. Require that any future parenting-time restriction be based on properly authenticated, cross-examined evidence and accompanied by written findings under MCR 2.517.

### ISSUE III: THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS CONTEMPT POWER BY JAILING APPELLANT FOR 59 DAYS — INCLUDING FOR BIRTHDAY MESSAGES TO HIS CHILD — IN RETALIATION FOR FILING MOTIONS

#### A. Governing Law

When a court uses contempt powers to enforce a PPO, the contempt is criminal in nature and must satisfy criminal-procedure safeguards, including proof beyond a reasonable doubt and the right to be present and heard. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 668; 765 NW2d 44 (2009). MCR 3.708 requires notice, a show-cause hearing, and proof of a *knowing* violation of a valid PPO before criminal contempt sanctions may be imposed.

The contemnor has the constitutional right to be heard before contempt sanctions are imposed. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 94; 413 NW2d 392 (1987). This includes the right to present evidence, call witnesses, and make arguments. Silencing a litigant during a contempt proceeding — particularly by muting during a remote hearing — is antithetical to this right.

Custody and parenting time must be governed by the Child Custody Act, not by parallel or collateral mechanisms that bypass the statutory framework. *Hunter v Hunter*, 484 Mich 247, 265–268; 771 NW2d 694 (2009). PPOs are designed to protect against stalking, harassment, or violence — not to serve as a shortcut to eliminate parenting time.

A court may not use its contempt power to punish a litigant for exercising the constitutional right to petition the court. US Const, Amend I; Mich Const 1963, Art 1, § 13.

#### B. Application

**1. Birthday messages are not contempt-worthy PPO violations.** The November 26, 2025 contempt finding and 45-day sentence were based in part on AppClose birthday messages to L.D.W. AppClose is a court-approved co-parenting communication platform. A father sending birthday wishes to his child through a court-sanctioned platform does not constitute stalking, harassment, or violence under MCL 600.2950. The characterization of birthday messages as a PPO violation warranting 45 days of incarceration epitomizes the weaponization of PPO process.

**2. Escalating contempt without criminal-procedure safeguards.** The record does not show that the court demanded proof beyond a reasonable doubt of each alleged PPO violation, made clear written findings as required by *Henry* and MCR 3.708, or considered less-restrictive remedies before imposing incarceration.

**3. Father was silenced during contempt proceedings.** At the November 15, 2025 hearing (Show Cause #5), Father was muted three times on a remote proceeding while attempting to testify. His appointed counsel remained silent. This directly violates the *Dougherty* right to be heard before contempt sanctions are imposed.

**4. Temporal correlation with filing activity.** The escalation of contempt proceedings corresponds directly with Father's filing of motions seeking redress — motions to modify custody, motions to reconsider, objections to ex parte orders. The contempt apparatus functioned as retaliation against Father's exercise of his right to petition the court.

**5. PPO as a custody weapon — violation of *Hunter*.** The PPO-contempt-custody feedback loop operated as follows: (a) PPO violations (including birthday messages) generated contempt findings; (b) contempt findings were cited as evidence of Father's "instability" or "inability to follow court orders"; (c) that characterization justified further parenting-time restrictions outside the Child Custody Act framework. This is precisely the end-run around the Child Custody Act that *Hunter* forbids.

**6. Asymmetric enforcement.** Mother's documented withholding — 40 days in 2024 and 223+ days ongoing — received no equivalent show-cause scrutiny. Father's family's intimidation at exchanges received no enforcement action. This persistent asymmetry compounds the appearance of bias and constitutes selective enforcement.

**7. Catastrophic collateral consequences.** The 59 days of incarceration caused Father to lose four jobs and two homes. These collateral consequences far exceed any legitimate punitive or coercive purpose of contempt.

#### C. Relief

This Court should:

1. Vacate PPO-based criminal contempt findings and sentences entered without strict compliance with MCR 3.708, *Henry*, and *Dougherty*;
2. Hold that PPO enforcement may not be used to circumvent Child Custody Act standards for modifying custody or parenting time (*Hunter*); and
3. Direct the trial court, on remand, to address parenting-time interference through Child Custody Act mechanisms (MCL 722.31a) with balanced enforcement against both parents.

### ISSUE IV: THE TRIAL COURT DEMONSTRATED ACTUAL JUDICIAL BIAS, OR AT MINIMUM A CONSTITUTIONALLY INTOLERABLE PROBABILITY OF BIAS, REQUIRING DISQUALIFICATION AND REASSIGNMENT

#### A. Governing Law

MCR 2.003(C)(1) requires judicial disqualification where the judge's impartiality "might reasonably be questioned," including where the judge has prejudged the merits or exhibits deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that makes fair judgment impossible. *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347; 235 NW2d 352 (1975) — the objective standard requires disqualification when the judge's conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception of actual bias."

Due process is violated when "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868, 877 (2009); *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996).

### ISSUE V: EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS BY COURT STAFF CONSTITUTE STRUCTURAL ERROR REQUIRING REVERSAL

#### A. Governing Law

Canon 3(B)(7) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct prohibits ex parte communications "concerning a pending or impending proceeding." This prohibition extends to court staff, whose conduct is imputed to the judge under Canon 3(C)(1), which requires judges to "require staff and court officials subject to the judge's direction and control to observe the standards of fidelity and diligence that apply to the judge."

Ex parte communications by court personnel create structural error because they deprive the affected party of the opportunity to know what information reached the decision-maker and to respond to it. *Armstrong v Charter Twp of Ypsilanti*, 248 Mich App 573, 588; 640 NW2d 587 (2001) (decision-maker's ex parte contacts warrant reversal where they may have influenced the outcome).

#### B. Application

**1. The Rusco warrant email — March 3, 2025.** Pamela Rusco, Judge McNeill's secretary, emailed prosecutor Jamie Hooker: "Can you please issue a warrant for him?" (Appendix Ex. 5.) This is a direct ex parte communication between court staff and the prosecution regarding a pending matter. The communication was initiated by the court side. Defense counsel was CC'd only after the substantive request was made to the prosecutor. Canon 3(B)(7) requires that if a court communicates with one party, it must simultaneously provide equal notice and opportunity to respond to the other. That did not happen here.

The nature of the request — asking a prosecutor to issue a warrant — goes beyond mere scheduling or administrative coordination. It reflects the court apparatus actively seeking criminal prosecution of a litigant. Rusco, as court secretary, had no independent authority to request warrants.

**2. Off-record HealthWest coordination — October 29, 2025.** A HealthWest chart memo reveals that Rusco contacted clinicians directly and arranged subpoenas via email — not through formal court filing. (Appendix Ex. 7.) This coordination occurred outside the docket, meaning neither Father nor his counsel had any awareness that the court's staff was managing clinical witnesses who would testify about Father's mental health. The independence of that testimony was fundamentally compromised.

**3. Pattern of extra-docket activity.** These are not isolated incidents. The warrant email (March 2025) and the HealthWest coordination (October 2025) are seven months apart — revealing a sustained pattern of court staff operating outside the adversarial process.

**4. Prejudice.** The prejudice is manifest. The warrant request led to criminal prosecution and incarceration. The HealthWest coordination influenced the clinical testimony that the court used to justify continuing the parenting-time suspension. Both communications directly affected the merits of the proceedings.

#### C. Relief

This Court should:

1. Hold that the ex parte communications by Rusco violated Canon 3(B)(7) and Canon 3(C)(1);
2. Find structural error requiring reversal of orders entered after or in connection with the tainted communications; and
3. Include the ex parte communications as an additional basis for disqualification and reassignment under MCR 2.003(C).

### ISSUE VI: THE $250 FILING DEPOSIT AND "DO NOT FILE ANYMORE" DIRECTIVE CONSTITUTE UNCONSTITUTIONAL PRIOR RESTRAINTS ON COURT ACCESS

#### A. Governing Law

The right of access to the courts is guaranteed by the First Amendment's Petition Clause and by Article 1, § 13 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963. This right is fundamental — it cannot be abridged absent a compelling governmental interest narrowly tailored to achieve that interest.

Michigan law provides a specific procedure for restricting vexatious litigants: a court may impose filing restrictions only after notice, hearing, and specific findings that the litigant has a pattern of filing frivolous, repetitive, or harassing litigation. MCR 2.002. Without such a determination, blanket filing restrictions or financial barriers imposed on a litigant's ability to access the court constitute impermissible prior restraints.

The right of access is especially critical in family law matters where fundamental liberty interests — the parent-child relationship — are at stake. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). A parent seeking to challenge a total suspension of his parenting time has the most compelling need for court access.

Pro se litigants are entitled to have their filings liberally construed. *Haines v Kerner*, 404 US 519, 520 (1972). This principle exists to ensure that substantive rights are not forfeited through procedural unfamiliarity.

#### B. Application

**1. The "do not file anymore" directive.** At an early hearing, Judge McNeill told Father on the record: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." This directive functioned as a blanket prohibition on Father's ability to seek judicial relief — while his child was being withheld and his parenting time was suspended.

**2. The $250 deposit requirement.** The court imposed a $250 bond requirement on Father's motions without a vexatious-litigant determination under MCR 2.002. No notice was provided, no hearing was held, and no findings were made that Father's filings were frivolous, repetitive, or harassing. The deposit requirement was imposed on a parent who had lost four jobs and two homes as a result of the court's own contempt orders — making it an especially effective barrier to court access.

**3. No vexatious-litigant finding.** The essential prerequisite for filing restrictions — a formal vexatious-litigant determination — was never obtained. Without that determination, the court lacked authority to impose financial barriers on Father's motions.

**4. Context amplifies the violation.** The filing restrictions were imposed at the same time the court was: (a) suspending all of Father's parenting time without required findings; (b) refusing to credit Father's exculpatory evidence; (c) holding Father in contempt and jailing him; and (d) coordinating prosecution through court staff. Blocking Father's access to the court in this context effectively foreclosed his only avenue for challenging the cumulative deprivations.

**5. Impact on the parent-child relationship.** The restrictions prevented Father from filing motions to reinstate parenting time, to contest the ex parte suspension, and to present evidence of Mother's withholding. Each day without court access was another day of separation from L.D.W.

#### C. Relief

This Court should:

1. Vacate the $250 deposit requirement and the "do not file anymore" directive as unconstitutional prior restraints on court access;
2. Hold that filing restrictions in family matters may not be imposed absent a formal vexatious-litigant determination under MCR 2.002 with notice, hearing, and findings; and
3. Direct the trial court to accept and rule on Father's properly filed motions without financial barriers.
