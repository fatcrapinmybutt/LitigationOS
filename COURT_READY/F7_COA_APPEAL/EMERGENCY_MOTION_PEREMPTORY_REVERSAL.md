## EMERGENCY MOTION FOR PEREMPTORY REVERSAL

### I. INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff-Appellant Andrew James Pigors moves this Court pursuant to MCR 7.211(C)(4) for peremptory reversal of the trial court's August 6–8, 2025 ex parte order suspending all parenting time and all subsequent orders continuing that suspension. Peremptory reversal is warranted because the trial court's orders are **clearly erroneous as a matter of law** — they violate mandatory provisions of the Child Custody Act, the Michigan Court Rules, and fundamental due process.

### II. FACTUAL SUMMARY

The essential facts are not in dispute:

1. **438 days of safe 50/50 custody.** From May 5, 2024 through July 17, 2025, the parties operated under a temporary court order providing approximately 50/50 custody. Father exercised parenting time safely and consistently for 438 consecutive days. (Settled Statement ¶¶ 11–12.)

2. **Total cutoff.** On July 29, 2025, all contact between Father and L.D.W. ceased entirely. (Settled Statement ¶ 18.)

3. **Ex parte order without required findings.** On August 6–8, 2025, the trial court entered an ex parte order suspending all parenting time. The order was entered without notice to Father, without verified child-specific allegations of immediate and irreparable harm, without an evidentiary hearing, without best-interest findings under MCL 722.23, and without clear-and-convincing evidence of endangerment under MCL 722.27a(3). (Settled Statement ¶¶ 19–21.)

4. **90-day hearing delay.** The trial court did not hold an evidentiary hearing within the 21-day period required by MCR 3.207(B)(6). The first hearing was not held until October 29, 2025 — 90 days after the ex parte order. (Settled Statement ¶ 22.)

5. **Court-ordered evaluation contradicted the suspension.** The HealthWest evaluation of September 4, 2025, ordered by the court itself, found no diagnosis or safety risk justifying supervision or suspension of parenting time. The trial court disregarded this favorable finding. (Settled Statement ¶¶ 23–25.)

6. **223+ days of total separation.** As of the date of this filing, Father has had no contact of any kind with his three-year-old son for 223+ consecutive days.

### III. LEGAL STANDARD FOR PEREMPTORY REVERSAL

MCR 7.211(C)(4) authorizes peremptory reversal where the panel unanimously concludes that the trial court's decision is "clearly erroneous as a matter of law." This mechanism exists precisely for cases where the legal error is so apparent from the record that full briefing and oral argument are unnecessary to expose it.

Peremptory reversal is particularly appropriate where:

- The trial court violated a mandatory statutory or rule requirement;
- The error deprived a party of fundamental rights;
- The harm is ongoing and each day of delay compounds the injury; and
- The correct resolution is clear from the existing record.

All four conditions are satisfied here.

### IV. ARGUMENT

#### A. The Trial Court Violated MCL 722.27a(3) — The Parenting-Time Restriction Statute

MCL 722.27a(3) permits denial or restriction of parenting time only upon **clear and convincing evidence** that parenting time would "endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." The trial court entered a total suspension — the most extreme restriction possible — without any such finding. The court-ordered HealthWest evaluation affirmatively found no safety risk. The statutory standard was not met; it was not even addressed.

#### B. The Trial Court Violated MCR 3.207(B)(6) — The 21-Day Hearing Requirement

MCR 3.207(B)(6) requires that when an ex parte order is entered in a domestic-relations matter, the court must schedule and hold an evidentiary hearing within **21 days**. The trial court waited **90 days** — 4.3× the mandated period. This is not a marginal overshoot requiring a proportionality analysis. The court exceeded the deadline by a factor of more than four.

#### C. The Trial Court Made No Best-Interest Findings Under MCL 722.23

When a parenting-time restriction alters the established custodial environment, the court must make findings on the 12 best-interest factors of MCL 722.23, supported by clear-and-convincing evidence. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81 (2010); *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68 (2017). A total suspension of parenting time is the most extreme alteration of the custodial environment possible. No MCL 722.23 findings were made — at any point.

#### D. The Trial Court Had No "Proper Cause" or "Change in Circumstances" Under *Vodvarka*

Before modifying parenting time, the court must find "proper cause shown" or a "change of circumstances." *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 1, 8 (2003). Father had exercised parenting time safely for 438 days. No new, verified incident of harm justified the modification. The *Vodvarka* threshold was never addressed.

#### E. The 2025 Amendments to MCR 3.207 and 3.210 Independently Require Reversal

Effective September 1, 2025, amended MCR 3.207 and MCR 3.210 require that ex parte orders altering a child's established custodial environment be supported by verified facts and followed by a prompt evidentiary hearing. The trial court continued the August 2025 ex parte suspension after September 1, 2025, without complying with these additional requirements.

#### F. The Ex Parte Communications Require Reversal

The Rusco warrant email (March 3, 2025) and the off-record HealthWest coordination (October 29, 2025) constitute Canon 3(B)(7) violations that taint the proceedings. Orders entered after or in connection with these ex parte communications cannot stand.

### V. IRREPARABLE HARM TO THE CHILD

L.D.W. is three years old. Developmental science is unambiguous: for children aged 2–4, prolonged separation from an attachment figure causes measurable harm to:

- **Secure attachment formation** — The neural pathways governing attachment to caregivers are actively developing at this age. Disruption during this critical window has lasting consequences.
- **Emotional regulation** — Young children learn to regulate emotions through co-regulation with attachment figures. Seven months without that co-regulation impairs developing regulatory capacity.
- **Identity and self-concept** — A child this age is forming his understanding of family, belonging, and self. Complete severance from a parent distorts that formation.

The trial court's orders do not protect L.D.W. — they harm him. The court-ordered HealthWest evaluation found no safety risk. The only "danger" identified was a speculative "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" diagnosed 7 days after a clean baseline, by a different clinician, with the clinician's own caveat that it was "difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional."

Meanwhile, the real, measurable harm — 223+ days of complete parent-child separation — accumulates daily with no judicial review, no hearing, and no pathway to restoration.

### VI. RELIEF REQUESTED

Plaintiff-Appellant respectfully requests that this Court:

**1. Grant peremptory reversal** of the August 6–8, 2025 ex parte order and all subsequent orders continuing the parenting-time suspension, on the basis that the orders are clearly erroneous as a matter of law.

**2. Order immediate reinstatement of parenting time.** Reinstate Appellant's parenting time forthwith, at the 50/50 level that was safely maintained for 438 days, pending further proceedings on remand. Alternatively, reinstate parenting time at the post-trial level (79 overnights) with a schedule to increase toward the prior arrangement.

**3. Order disqualification and reassignment.** Reassign all further proceedings to a different circuit-court judge under MCR 2.003(C), given the ex parte communications, pattern of bias, and predetermined outcomes documented in the record.

**4. Order an evidentiary hearing within 21 days.** If this Court determines that peremptory reversal is not appropriate, order the trial court to hold an evidentiary hearing within 21 days of this Court's order, compliant with MCR 3.207(B)(6), MCR 3.210, MCL 722.23, and MCL 722.27a(3), before a newly assigned judge.

**5. Award make-up parenting time.** Order make-up parenting time equal to all days wrongfully denied — currently 223+ days — to be scheduled in a manner that restores the parent-child relationship while respecting L.D.W.'s routine.
