## SETTLED FACTS AND PROCEDURAL HISTORY

### ¶ 1. The Parties

Andrew James Pigors ("Father" / "Appellant") resides at 1977 Whitehall Road, Lot 17, North Muskegon, MI 49445. Emily A. Watson ("Mother" / "Appellee") resides at 2160 Garland Drive, Norton Shores, MI 49441.

### ¶ 2. The Child

The parties are the parents of L.D.W., born November 9, 2022. Per MCR 8.119(H), only the child's initials are used in this filing. The parties were never married.

### ¶ 3. Pre-Litigation Parenting

Before any court involvement, the parties functioned under a de facto shared-parenting arrangement. Father was a constant caregiver from L.D.W.'s birth. There is no history of criminal convictions, substantiated CPS findings, or confirmed abuse by Father toward the child.

### ¶ 4. Welfare Checks — October–November 2023

In October and November 2023, Mother initiated welfare checks and sought emergency court intervention, alleging Father was mentally unstable and dangerous. Police and medical responders responded to the calls. No evidence supported the allegations. Father was not hospitalized, committed, or diagnosed with any condition justifying parenting restrictions.

### ¶ 5. Police Response — No Findings

Police contacts during this period resulted in no arrests, no charges, no findings of violence, and no involuntary commitments.

### ¶ 6. Medical Response — No Findings

Emergency-room records from the period show no objective findings consistent with the allegations. No toxicology results, forensic testing, or criminal referrals were produced.

### ¶ 7. PPO — December 3, 2023

On December 3, 2023, Mother filed a petition for a Personal Protection Order (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) alleging poisoning with arsenic. The PPO was granted the same day it was filed, ex parte, without notice to Father. Emergency-room records show no objective findings consistent with arsenic poisoning — no toxicology confirmation, no forensic testing. No criminal charges were ever filed based on the arsenic allegation. The PPO was subsequently extended into a third year.

### ¶ 8. First Withholding — March 26 through May 5, 2024

From March 26 through May 5, 2024 — 40 consecutive days — Mother unilaterally withheld L.D.W. from Father. Father repeatedly requested the child's return through available channels.

### ¶ 9. Father's Attempts to Restore Contact

During the 40-day withholding, Father made multiple requests for the return of L.D.W. These requests were ignored or rebuffed.

### ¶ 10. Filing of Custody Case — April 1, 2024

On April 1, 2024, Father filed the custody case (No. 2024-001507-DC), making him the plaintiff. The court later acknowledged Mother's withholding but stated it could not "hold it against her" because the withholding occurred before the formal custody filing.

### ¶ 11. Temporary 50/50 Custody Order — May 2024

On or about May 5, 2024, the court entered a temporary order providing approximately 50/50 custody, equating to approximately 182.5 overnights per year for each parent.

### ¶ 12. The 438-Day Shared Custody Period — May 2024 through July 2025

From May 5, 2024 through July 17, 2025 — 438 consecutive days — the parties operated under the temporary 50/50 custody order. During this 14-month period, Father exercised parenting time safely and consistently. No emergency motions, police interventions, or CPS complaints substantiated any safety concerns during this period. The court itself regarded Father as a fit custodial parent by entering and maintaining this order.

### ¶ 13. Custody Trial — July 17, 2025

A non-jury custody trial was held on July 17, 2025. Mother appeared with retained counsel, Jennifer Barnes (P55406). Father appeared *in propria persona*.

### ¶ 14. Trial — Father's Evidence Limited

During the trial, Father's opportunity to present documentary evidence was limited. He was not permitted to introduce recordings, AppClose communication logs, or police reports that would have contradicted Mother's allegations.

### ¶ 15. Trial — Mother's Evidence Permitted

Mother's counsel was permitted to present Mother's case fully, including witness testimony and documentary evidence.

### ¶ 16. Trial — Excluded Evidence

Father was not permitted to develop the record on: (a) Mother's 40-day withholding (March–May 2024); (b) Mother's family's conduct at parenting-time exchanges; or (c) a recording in which Mother and her father discussed ensuring Father would not see his son.

### ¶ 17. Post-Trial Order — July 17, 2025

After trial, the court entered an order removing Father's joint legal custody and reducing his parenting time from approximately 182.5 overnights to 79 overnights per year. The court did not issue written best-interest findings addressing Mother's documented withholding or analyzing the factors under MCL 722.23.

### ¶ 18. Second Withholding — July 29, 2025 to Present

On July 29, 2025, all contact between Father and L.D.W. ceased entirely. This second withholding has continued for 223+ days as of the date of this filing and is ongoing. Father has had no visitation, no phone calls, no video calls, and no communication with his child during this period.

### ¶ 19. Ex Parte Motion — August 6, 2025

On or about August 6, 2025, Mother filed an emergency ex parte motion to suspend all of Father's remaining parenting time.

### ¶ 20. Ex Parte Order — August 6–8, 2025

On August 6–8, 2025, Judge McNeill issued an ex parte order suspending all of Father's parenting time. The order was entered without notice to Father, without verified child-specific allegations of immediate and irreparable harm, without a full evidentiary hearing, without best-interest findings under MCL 722.23, and without the clear-and-convincing proof of endangerment required by MCL 722.27a(3).

### ¶ 21. No Emergency Basis

At the time of the ex parte order, Father had been exercising parenting time safely under court supervision for 438 days. No new incident of harm, abuse, or endangerment had occurred. Police contacts during the relevant period resulted in no findings against Father.

### ¶ 22. 90-Day Delay Before Hearing

The court did not schedule an evidentiary hearing within the 21-day period required by MCR 3.207(B)(6). The first evidentiary hearing was not held until October 29, 2025 — 90 days after the ex parte order, violating the 21-day rule by a factor of 4.3×.

### ¶ 23. First HealthWest Evaluation — September 4, 2025

At the court's direction, Father underwent a mental-health evaluation at HealthWest. On September 4, 2025, clinicians Mitchell and DeAugustine conducted the evaluation and established a clean baseline. The report found no diagnosis or safety risk that would justify suspension or supervision of parenting time.

### ¶ 24. Second HealthWest Assessment — September 11, 2025

Seven days later, on September 11, 2025, a different clinician (Gansen) issued a "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" assessment. The clinician stated in the report that it was "difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional." Delusional Disorder requires sustained, chronic presentation under the DSM-5 — not a 7-day onset.

### ¶ 25. Court's Treatment of HealthWest Evidence

The court refused to credit or give weight to the September 4, 2025 favorable evaluation. The court relied instead on the September 11, 2025 speculative assessment. The court also began referencing a purported "second" full evaluation that Father never underwent. No authenticated document of such an evaluation was ever produced.

### ¶ 26. "Liar" Characterization

When Father explained on the record that no second evaluation existed, Judge McNeill called Father a "liar" and stated he was "without rational basis."

### ¶ 27. Rusco Warrant Email — March 3, 2025

Pamela Rusco, Judge McNeill's secretary, sent an email on March 3, 2025 to Jamie Hooker, a prosecutor, stating: "Can you please issue a warrant for him?" Defense counsel was CC'd only after the prosecutor.

### ¶ 28. Martini "Don't Speak" Email — February 28, 2025

Father's appointed counsel, Mandi Martini, sent Father an email on February 28, 2025 instructing: "You will not interrupt. Got it? I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil [sic] is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked... All prosecutor is asking for today is that you get a lecture from the judge because she knows there is nothing substantive in the motion."

### ¶ 29. HealthWest Off-Record Coordination — October 29, 2025

A HealthWest chart memo from October 29, 2025 reveals that Pamela Rusco contacted HealthWest clinicians directly and arranged subpoenas via email, bypassing formal court filing. This coordination was not disclosed to Father or entered on the docket.

### ¶ 30. PPO Guilty Plea — October 18, 2025

On October 18, 2025, Father entered a guilty plea on a PPO matter (CC 382a). Father's appointed counsel, Martini, did not present available defenses including the lack of willful intent and the ambiguity of the PPO's scope regarding co-parenting communications.

### ¶ 31. Show Cause #5 — November 15, 2025

On November 15, 2025, Father was sentenced to 14 days in jail following Show Cause #5. The hearing was conducted remotely. During the hearing, Father was muted three times while attempting to testify. His appointed counsel, Martini, remained effectively silent throughout the proceeding and did not object to the muting.

### ¶ 32. Show Cause #6 and #7 — November 26, 2025

On November 26, 2025, Father was sentenced to an additional 45 days in jail following Show Cause #6 and #7. The contempt was based in part on AppClose birthday messages that Father sent to L.D.W.

### ¶ 33. Collateral Consequences — Employment

As a direct result of the 59-day incarceration (14 + 45 days), Father lost four jobs: Metal Arc Welding, IndiGrow Inc., United States Postal Service, and Shape Corporation.

### ¶ 34. Collateral Consequences — Housing

Father also lost two residences, including an illegal lockout from his manufactured home at Shady Oaks Mobile Home Park (the subject of a separate action, Case No. 2025-002760-CZ, Muskegon County Circuit Court).

### ¶ 35. False Attribution to Officer Randall

Mother represented in filings that Norton Shores Police Officer Ella Randall had concluded that Father's "erratic behavior" was caused by "meth use." The police report for Incident NS2505044 shows that Officer Randall was documenting statements made by Mother (Emily Watson) and her father (Albert Watson) — not recording the officer's own conclusions. The Norton Shores Police Department issued a letter confirming that the "meth" statements originated from Emily Watson and Albert Watson, not from Officer Randall. The police report also documented Albert Watson's statement that the family wanted the incident "documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody." When Father raised this correction on the record, Judge McNeill told Father to "quit nitpicking" and refused to correct the record.

### ¶ 36. "Do Not File Anymore" Directive

At a hearing, Judge McNeill told Father on the record: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." This directive was given while Father's parenting time was suspended and while he was attempting to challenge the suspension through proper motion practice.

### ¶ 37. $250 Filing Bond

The court imposed a $250 deposit requirement on Father's motions without a vexatious-litigant determination under MCR 2.002, without notice and hearing, and without specific findings that Father's filings were frivolous, repetitive, or harassing.

## ISSUES PRESERVED FOR APPEAL

The following issues were raised in the lower court proceedings and are preserved for appellate review:

1. Whether the trial court abused its discretion by suspending all parenting time for 223+ days without the findings required by MCL 722.27a(3) and without "proper cause" or "change in circumstances" under *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 1 (2003).

2. Whether the trial court violated Appellant's due process rights by denying any meaningful evidentiary hearing before effectively terminating the parent-child relationship, and by continuing an ex parte order for 90 days without hearing in violation of MCR 3.207(B)(6).

3. Whether the trial court abused its contempt power by jailing Appellant for 59 days — including for birthday messages — in retaliation for protected petitioning activity, in violation of *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987).

4. Whether cumulative instances of bias require disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1) and *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347 (1975).

5. Whether ex parte communications by court staff violated Canon 3(B)(7) and constitute structural error under *Armstrong v Charter Twp of Ypsilanti*, 248 Mich App 573 (2001).

6. Whether the $250 filing deposit and "do not file anymore" directive constituted unconstitutional prior restraints on court access under Mich Const 1963, Art 1, § 13.
