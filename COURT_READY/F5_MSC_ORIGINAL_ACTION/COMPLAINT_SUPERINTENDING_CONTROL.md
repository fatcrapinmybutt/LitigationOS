## I. JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT

1. This Court has original and plenary jurisdiction to issue an Order of Superintending Control over any Michigan court. Const 1963, art 6, § 4 provides:

   > "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts; power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs; and appellate jurisdiction as provided by rules of the supreme court."

2. MCR 7.304(A) authorizes the filing of an original action in this Court, including complaints for superintending control under MCR 7.306.

3. MCR 7.306(A) provides that a complaint for superintending control may be filed when a lower court has failed to perform a duty, has exceeded its jurisdiction, or has otherwise acted in a manner requiring correction by this Court.

4. MCR 7.316(A)(1) authorizes this Court to issue orders of superintending control "to correct or prevent a clear legal error by a lower court or tribunal when the error would otherwise not be subject to adequate appellate review."

5. MCL 600.1701 provides additional statutory authority for superintending control proceedings.

6. This Court's superintending control authority permits it to "correct abuses of judicial power, to compel the performance of judicial duties, and to ensure the orderly administration of justice" when no other remedy is adequate. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–42 (1993).

7. Petitioner has a related appeal pending before the Michigan Court of Appeals, Case No. 366810. That appeal does not provide adequate or timely relief for the extraordinary circumstances presented here. As demonstrated in Section IV.V, infra, the child L.D.W. has been completely separated from Petitioner for 223+ consecutive days and counting. The standard appellate timeline of one to two years is constitutionally intolerable when a three-year-old child's developmental window is closing.

This is not a discretionary act — it is a mandatory legal duty.

21. The *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer* standard, 259 Mich App 499 (2003), requires that before modifying parenting time, the moving party must demonstrate "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances" — and the court must make specific findings supporting any modification. The trial court in this case has never conducted this analysis.

22. The trial court suspended all parenting time — the functional equivalent of awarding sole physical custody to the mother — without any findings on any of the 12 best interest factors. This constitutes a textbook failure to perform a mandatory duty.

23. MCL 552.644 provides that the court "**shall**" enforce parenting time orders. The word "shall" creates a non-discretionary, mandatory duty. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014). Despite documented violations of parenting time by Watson, Judge McNeill has refused to enforce parenting time, effectively allowing Watson to unilaterally sever Petitioner's relationship with his son.

## IV. ARGUMENT

### I. EXTRAORDINARY CIRCUMSTANCES WARRANT SUPERINTENDING CONTROL

38. The Michigan Constitution vests this Court with "general superintending control over all courts." Const 1963, art 6, § 4. This authority is "plenary" and encompasses "the entire range of supervisory authority." *Judges of 74th Judicial Dist v Bay County*, 385 Mich 710, 727 (1971).

39. Superintending control is proper when a lower court has committed "an abuse of judicial power that is tantamount to a failure to perform a clear legal duty, and there is no other adequate remedy." *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993). This Court exercises this power when the lower court has (1) failed to perform a clear legal duty, (2) exceeded its jurisdiction, or (3) the cumulative pattern of error is so severe that it cannot be corrected through normal appellate processes. MCR 7.306(A); MCR 7.316(A)(1).

40. This case satisfies every element:

   a. **Clear legal duties failed.** Judge McNeill has failed to perform mandatory statutory duties: (i) the MCL 722.23 best interest analysis before modifying custody, (ii) the MCL 552.644 mandatory enforcement of parenting time, (iii) the MCR 3.207(C) requirement to hold hearings within 21 days of ex parte orders, and (iv) the MCR 3.210(C) requirement to hold hearings when requested.

   b. **Jurisdiction exceeded.** The trial court entered ex parte orders suspending fundamental parental rights without the findings required by MCL 722.27a(3), without hearings required by MCR 3.207(B)–(C), and without the *Vodvarka* analysis required before any parenting time modification. Orders entered without mandatory procedural prerequisites are void, not merely voidable. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d).

   c. **Normal appellate processes are inadequate.** The appeal pending in the Court of Appeals (No. 366810) addresses specific orders but cannot compel future compliance with mandatory duties, cannot disqualify the judge, and cannot provide relief within a timeframe that prevents irreparable harm to L.D.W.

41. This is not a case of isolated judicial error amenable to standard appellate correction. It is a *systematic breakdown of judicial function* — a pattern of conduct across two consolidated proceedings (2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP) that has resulted in the complete severance of the parent-child relationship for 223+ consecutive days without any adequate judicial process.

42. As this Court observed in *In re Hatcher*, superintending control exists precisely to address situations where "the normal processes of law are inadequate to correct an abuse of judicial power." 443 Mich at 442. When the problem *is* the judge — when the lower court has demonstrated an inability or unwillingness to follow mandatory law — the lower court cannot fix itself. Only this Court can provide the structural remedy required.

### II. McNEILL'S ORDERS ARE VOID FOR LACK OF REQUIRED FINDINGS

43. An order entered without jurisdiction or in violation of mandatory procedural prerequisites is void — not merely erroneous, but a nullity. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) provides that a court may relieve a party from a void judgment "at any time." A void order has no legal effect and confers no rights.

44. MCL 722.27a(3) requires that before a court modifies parenting time, it must make specific findings that the modification is in the best interest of the child. The trial court made no such findings before suspending all of Petitioner's parenting time on August 8, 2025. The order is therefore void.

45. MCL 722.23 requires that the court evaluate all 12 best interest factors before modifying custody. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994). The trial court conducted no such analysis. The custody modification is therefore void.

46. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 512 (2003), established that a party seeking modification of parenting time must demonstrate "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances," and the trial court must make findings supporting any modification. No *Vodvarka* analysis appears in the record. The parenting time suspension is therefore void.

47. When orders are void — not merely erroneous — the normal appellate remedy of reversal is insufficient. Void orders must be vacated. This Court's superintending control power is the appropriate mechanism to declare void, and order vacated, the series of orders entered without mandatory findings.

### III. SYSTEMATIC DEPRIVATION OF DUE PROCESS

48. The Due Process Clause protects the parent-child relationship as "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). When fundamental rights are at stake, procedural protections must be rigorous. *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–54 (1982).

49. The *Mathews v Eldridge* three-factor balancing test, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), confirms the due process violation:

   **(1) The private interest at stake.** The parent-child relationship is the most fundamental liberty interest protected by the Constitution. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972). L.D.W. is three years old — at a critical developmental stage. The reciprocal interest of the child in maintaining a relationship with both parents is equally profound.

   **(2) The risk of erroneous deprivation.** The risk is not theoretical — it has materialized. Petitioner's parenting time was suspended on an ex parte basis without a hearing, without notice, without evidentiary support, without best interest findings, and without the *Vodvarka* analysis. Every procedural safeguard that exists to prevent erroneous deprivation was bypassed.

   **(3) The government's interest.** The state has no legitimate interest in perpetuating proceedings that violate due process. To the contrary, the state has a compelling interest in ensuring that its courts function within constitutional bounds. *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 42 (1992).

50. The pattern of due process violations includes:

   a. **Ex parte orders without hearings.** Critical custody and parenting time orders were entered ex parte — without notice to Petitioner and without any opportunity to be heard.

   b. **Denial of the right to be heard.** Judge McNeill directed Petitioner not to file motions — an explicit suppression of the right to petition the court that violates Canon 3(A)(4) and the First and Fourteenth Amendments.

   c. **No evidentiary hearing despite 223+ days of separation.** MCR 3.207(C) requires that when an ex parte order is entered, the court must hold a hearing within 21 days. No such hearing has been held.

   d. **Contempt without counsel.** Petitioner was jailed for 59 days without court-appointed counsel, in violation of *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480 (1990), and *Turner v Rogers*, 564 US 431, 448 (2011).

   e. **Financial barrier to disqualification.** The $250 bond on the MCR 2.003 motion eliminates the sole internal remedy for judicial bias, violating *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971).

51. The cumulative effect is a proceeding that bears no resemblance to the "fair hearing" demanded by due process. Individual appellate review of individual orders cannot cure the systemic nature of the problem. Superintending control is the appropriate remedy.

### IV. JUDICIAL BIAS PATTERN REQUIRING REMOVAL

52. Judicial disqualification is warranted when "a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would conclude that the judge's impartiality is reasonably questionable." MCR 2.003(C)(1); *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347 (1975). This is an objective standard — actual bias need not be proven.

53. Under *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires recusal when the "probability of actual bias . . . is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 872.

54. The documented pattern of conduct satisfies both the Michigan and federal standards:

   a. **Ex parte communications.** Pamela Rusco, the court's secretary, coordinated with HealthWest regarding Petitioner's case outside the official record — a violation of Canon 3(A)(7).

   b. **Suppression of right to file.** The directive to Petitioner not to file motions violates Canon 3(A)(4), the right to be heard, and the First Amendment. A judge who tells a litigant not to file motions has prejudged the case.

   c. **Asymmetric treatment.** Watson's ex parte filings were promptly granted; Petitioner's motions were denied without hearing or ignored entirely. This pattern of systematically favoring one party over another is the hallmark of partiality.

   d. **Financial barrier to recusal.** The $250 bond on the disqualification motion effectively immunizes the judge from the only internal check on judicial bias — a structural conflict that no reasonable person could view as impartial.

   e. **Disproportionate contempt.** Fifty-nine days of incarceration for a pro se litigant, without counsel, with purge conditions the litigant could not meet, evinces a punitive rather than coercive posture toward Petitioner.

55. This pattern is not the product of a single bad day or a single erroneous ruling. It is a sustained course of conduct spanning two consolidated proceedings over more than a year. The lower court's disqualification mechanism has been rendered unavailable by the $250 bond. The only remedy is this Court's exercise of superintending control to remove Judge McNeill and assign a visiting judge.

### V. THE COA CANNOT PROVIDE ADEQUATE TIMELY RELIEF

56. Petitioner's appeal (COA No. 366810) is pending. However, for the following reasons, the COA cannot provide adequate relief:

   a. **Temporal inadequacy.** Standard COA appellate timelines require one to two years from filing to decision. L.D.W. is three years old. Every day of separation during those one to two years inflicts irreparable developmental harm. The constitutional right at stake — the parent-child relationship — cannot be placed on hold for the duration of an appellate calendar.

   b. **Structural inadequacy.** The COA reviews individual orders for abuse of discretion. It is not structured to conduct the systemic, pattern-based review that these proceedings demand. The problem is not a single erroneous order — it is a systematic failure of judicial function across two consolidated cases.

   c. **Remedial inadequacy.** Even if the COA reverses specific orders, it cannot (i) disqualify Judge McNeill, (ii) assign a visiting judge, (iii) order emergency restoration of parenting time, (iv) impose case management requirements, or (v) refer documented misconduct for investigation. Only this Court's superintending control provides the full range of structural relief required.

   d. **Ongoing harm.** The separation is not a past injury awaiting appellate review — it is an *ongoing constitutional violation* that deepens every day. "Justice delayed is justice denied" is not an aphorism in this context; it is a constitutional reality. A three-year-old child's developmental trajectory does not wait for appellate briefing schedules.

57. This Court has recognized that when lower court error is ongoing and the harm irreparable, the normal appellate process is constitutionally inadequate. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 441–42. The instant case presents precisely such circumstances.
