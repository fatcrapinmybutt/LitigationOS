## INTRODUCTION

Andrew James Pigors has not seen his three-year-old son, L.D.W., in over 223 consecutive days. Not because any court found him to be a danger. Not because any evaluation concluded that contact would be harmful. Not because any hearing produced evidence warranting this result. But because the presiding judge of the 14th Circuit Court, Family Division — Hon. Jenny L. McNeill — entered an ex parte order on August 8, 2025, suspending all parenting time without notice, without a hearing, without findings on the statutory best interest factors, and without the specific findings of harm required by MCL 722.27a(3).

This occurred after 438 consecutive days of successful, court-ordered 50/50 custody.

In the months since that order, the trial court has:

- Refused to hold the evidentiary hearing required by MCR 3.207(C);
- Directed Petitioner not to file motions — explicitly denying the right to petition for redress;
- Imposed a $250 bond on Petitioner's disqualification motion, blocking the sole internal remedy for judicial bias;
- Jailed Petitioner for 59 days on civil contempt without court-appointed counsel, in violation of *Mead v Batchlor* and *Turner v Rogers*;
- Permitted the Real Party in Interest to remove L.D.W. from the country while simultaneously prosecuting Petitioner for contempt.

Every remedy within the lower court system has been pursued and either denied, blocked, or rendered ineffective. The Court of Appeals appeal (No. 366810) is pending but structurally incapable of providing the comprehensive, time-sensitive relief that the situation demands.

This Brief demonstrates that the case satisfies every element required for superintending control: (1) the lower court has failed multiple clear legal duties, (2) no adequate remedy exists at law, and (3) the matter presents constitutional questions of significant importance that this Court alone can resolve. Petitioner respectfully requests that this Court exercise the full scope of its constitutional authority under Const 1963, art 6, § 4.

## STANDARD OF REVIEW

The standard for superintending control is not whether the lower court committed reversible error on a single issue, but whether the cumulative pattern of the lower court's conduct demonstrates "an abuse of judicial power that is tantamount to a failure to perform a clear legal duty, and there is no other adequate remedy." *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993).

This Court reviews the lower court's compliance with mandatory statutory and constitutional duties de novo. Questions of constitutional law — including due process and the scope of fundamental parental rights — are reviewed de novo. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 85 (2010).

## ARGUMENT

### I. THIS CASE QUALIFIES AS "EXTRAORDINARY" UNDER *CHERRYVALE* AND *IN RE HATCHER*

#### A. The Three-Part Test Is Satisfied

The Michigan Supreme Court exercises original superintending control jurisdiction sparingly — only when the case satisfies three elements. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993); MCR 7.306(A).

**Element 1: The lower court has failed clear legal duties.**

Judge McNeill has failed to perform the following mandatory duties — each independently warranting superintending control:

| Mandatory Duty | Authority | McNeill's Failure |
|---|---|---|
| Evaluate all 12 best interest factors before modifying custody | MCL 722.23(a)–(l); *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 882 | No MCL 722.23 analysis conducted |
| Make specific findings before modifying parenting time | MCL 722.27a(3); *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 512 | No findings entered |
| Conduct evidentiary hearing within 21 days of ex parte order | MCR 3.207(C) | No hearing in 223+ days |
| Enforce parenting time violations | MCL 552.644 ("shall") | Zero enforcement actions despite documented violations |
| Allow parties to be heard | Canon 3(A)(4); MCR 3.210(C) | Directed Petitioner not to file; denied hearings |
| Refrain from ex parte communications | Canon 3(A)(7) | Rusco-HealthWest communication documented |

Each of these is a mandatory, ministerial duty — not a discretionary act. When a statute says "shall," the duty is mandatory. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014). A court's refusal to perform a mandatory duty is "an abuse of judicial power" that superintending control is designed to correct. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 441.

**Element 2: No adequate remedy at law exists.**

| Attempted Remedy | Result | Why Inadequate |
|---|---|---|
| Motions in lower court | Denied without hearing; told "do not file anymore" | The judge *is* the problem — she cannot remedy her own failure |
| MCR 2.003 disqualification | Blocked by $250 bond | Petitioner is indigent; bond is a complete bar |
| Court of Appeals (No. 366810) | Pending | 1-2 year timeline; cannot disqualify judge or order emergency relief |
| FOC complaint | Filed, no response | FOC cannot vacate void orders or restore parenting time |
| JTC complaint | Filed, pending | JTC addresses judicial discipline, not custody restoration |

No single remedy, and no combination of remedies, can provide the comprehensive structural relief that the situation requires. Only this Court's superintending control encompasses the "entire range of supervisory authority." *Bay County*, 385 Mich at 727.

**Element 3: The matter raises issues of significant public importance.**

This case presents constitutional questions that transcend the individual dispute: the scope of due process protections before fundamental parenting rights may be suspended; the limits of a trial court's ex parte authority; the constitutionality of financial barriers to judicial disqualification; and the adequacy of existing remedies when a judge systematically fails to follow mandatory law. These questions affect not only Petitioner but every litigant who may appear before the 14th Circuit Court.

#### B. The Systematic Nature Distinguishes This from Routine Error

This Court has consistently distinguished between isolated judicial error — which is properly addressed through normal appeal — and systematic judicial failure, which warrants superintending control. *Lapeer County Clerk v Lapeer Circuit Court*, 469 Mich 146 (2003).

The critical distinction is between a court that makes a wrong decision and a court that *refuses to perform its function*. A wrong decision can be reversed on appeal. A court that refuses to hold hearings, refuses to make mandatory findings, directs litigants not to file motions, and blocks the disqualification remedy cannot be corrected by reversing a single order. The problem is structural, and the remedy must be structural.

Petitioner does not ask this Court to second-guess the exercise of judicial discretion. Petitioner asks this Court to compel the performance of mandatory duties that the lower court has refused to perform. This is the paradigmatic case for superintending control.

### II. PETITIONER HAS EXHAUSTED ALL LOWER COURT REMEDIES

#### A. Every Available Remedy Has Been Pursued and Denied

The exhaustion requirement exists to ensure that this Court is truly the last resort — not a court of first instance for litigants dissatisfied with lower court rulings. In this case, the exhaustion requirement is satisfied with unusual thoroughness.

Petitioner has filed motions for reconsideration, motions for restoration of parenting time, a motion for disqualification under MCR 2.003, an appeal to the Court of Appeals (No. 366810), a complaint with the Friend of the Court, and a complaint with the Judicial Tenure Commission. Each has been either denied, blocked, or is structurally incapable of providing the relief needed.

The most telling evidence of exhaustion is the trial court's own response to Petitioner's efforts: a directive not to file motions. When the lower court *itself* tells a litigant that the court will not hear his claims, the exhaustion requirement is satisfied as a matter of law. There is nothing left to exhaust.

#### B. The COA Appeal Is Pending but Structurally Inadequate

The pending appeal (COA No. 366810) does not preclude this Complaint. Superintending control and appellate review serve different functions. Appellate review examines individual orders for abuse of discretion on the record. Superintending control addresses systemic failures that the appellate process is not structured to correct.

The COA appeal is inadequate for four independent reasons:

1. **Temporal inadequacy.** The standard appellate timeline of one to two years is constitutionally intolerable when a three-year-old child's developmental window is closing. The attachment literature is unambiguous: prolonged separation during the toddler-to-preschool years causes measurable, lasting harm to the parent-child bond. Every day this Court does not act is a day of irreparable constitutional injury.

2. **Structural inadequacy.** The COA reviews the specific orders appealed. It cannot conduct the systemic review of the lower court's pattern of conduct that the situation demands. Reversing one order does not cure a pattern of 223+ days of denied hearings, denied filings, and denied due process.

3. **Remedial inadequacy.** The COA cannot disqualify Judge McNeill, assign a visiting judge, restore parenting time on an emergency basis, or impose structural compliance requirements. Only superintending control provides this full remedial range.

4. **The lower court cannot self-correct.** The fundamental problem is that the judge *is* the source of the constitutional violation. Remanding to the same judge for compliance with the same rules she has systematically violated would be futile.

### III. THE CONSTITUTIONAL DIMENSION ELEVATES THIS BEYOND ROUTINE CUSTODY DISPUTE

#### A. The Fundamental Liberty Interest at Stake

The parent-child relationship is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This is not dicta — it is the foundational principle of a century of Supreme Court jurisprudence.

The state may not sever the parent-child relationship without the most rigorous procedural protections. *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–54 (1982) (clear and convincing evidence required before terminating parental rights). When even *termination* requires clear and convincing evidence and full procedural protections, it follows a fortiori that the functional equivalent — a complete suspension of all parenting time for 223+ days without a hearing — cannot be accomplished by ex parte order, without evidence, and without findings.

The child's interest is equally profound. *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972) ("The rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed essential, basic civil rights of man, and rights far more precious than property rights."). L.D.W. is three years old. He is at a critical stage of development. The involuntary severance of his relationship with his father for 223+ days — following 438 days of successful shared custody — is not a minor inconvenience. It is a constitutional injury of the highest order.

#### B. The *Mathews v Eldridge* Framework

The *Mathews v Eldridge* three-factor balancing test, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), confirms that due process requires far more than the lower court provided:

**(1) The private interest.** Maximum — the parent-child relationship is the most fundamental liberty interest the Constitution protects. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65.

**(2) The risk of erroneous deprivation.** The risk is not theoretical — it has materialized. Every procedural safeguard was bypassed: no hearing, no notice, no best interest findings, no *Vodvarka* analysis, no opportunity to present evidence or cross-examine witnesses. The probability of erroneous deprivation under these "procedures" approaches certainty.

**(3) The government's interest.** The government has no legitimate interest in denying due process. *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 42 (1992). To the contrary, the state has a compelling interest in ensuring that its courts comply with mandatory constitutional and statutory requirements.

All three factors weigh decisively in favor of Petitioner. The process afforded was constitutionally deficient as a matter of law.

#### C. The *Caperton* Due Process Standard

Under *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), the Due Process Clause requires recusal when the "probability of actual bias . . . is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 872. The question is objective: whether a reasonable observer would question the judge's impartiality.

The *Caperton* standard is not limited to cases involving financial conflicts of interest. It applies whenever the totality of the circumstances creates an unacceptable probability of bias. *Liteky v United States*, 510 US 540, 551 (1994) (extrajudicial source doctrine — bias arising from outside the judicial process warrants recusal).

The pattern documented in this case — ex parte communications through a secretary, suppression of filings, disproportionate contempt, asymmetric treatment of the parties, and financial barriers to disqualification — satisfies the *Caperton* standard. A reasonable observer, knowing all the circumstances, would question Judge McNeill's impartiality. The Due Process Clause requires recusal.

### IV. PEREMPTORY ACTION IS WARRANTED

#### A. Irreparable Harm Is Ongoing and Accelerating

This is not a case where the harm occurred in the past and appellate review can make the petitioner whole. The constitutional injury is ongoing — it deepens every day that Petitioner is separated from L.D.W.

The developmental science is unambiguous. Prolonged separation during the ages of two to four — the precise developmental window at issue here — disrupts secure attachment, elevates cortisol levels, and produces behavioral and emotional consequences that may persist into adulthood. These harms are not speculative. They are the predictable consequence of the lower court's ex parte order, compounding daily.

L.D.W. was 2 years and 9 months old when the separation began. He is now over 3 years old. He has spent more than 15% of his entire life separated from his father — after 438 consecutive days of successful shared custody. The urgency is not a rhetorical device. It is a developmental reality.

#### B. The Balance of Equities Favors Immediate Relief

The balance of equities overwhelmingly favors Petitioner:

| Factor | Petitioner | Respondent |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature of injury** | Complete severance of parent-child relationship for 223+ days | Administrative inconvenience of judicial reassignment |
| **Irreversibility** | Developmental harm to three-year-old is irreversible | Reassignment to visiting judge is procedurally routine |
| **Constitutional weight** | Fundamental liberty interest (Troxel) | No constitutional right to preside over a particular case |
| **Record of compliance** | 438 days of successful 50/50 custody | Pattern of mandatory duty failures |

No legitimate interest of the Respondents is harmed by granting the relief requested. A visiting judge assignment is a routine administrative matter. An evidentiary hearing with mandatory findings is what the law already requires. Restoring parenting time pending rehearing merely reinstates what functioned successfully for over fourteen months.

#### C. The Public Interest Supports This Court's Intervention

The public has a compelling interest in a functioning judiciary that follows mandatory law. When a trial court systematically fails to conduct required hearings, systematically fails to make required findings, and systematically denies a litigant's right to be heard, public confidence in the judiciary is undermined. This Court's exercise of superintending control reinforces the rule of law and protects the integrity of the judicial system.
