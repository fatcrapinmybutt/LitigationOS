## IV. LEGAL STANDARD

20. **Statutory Authority.** MCL 600.2950(12) provides that a PPO "shall remain in effect until modified or rescinded by the court." Either party may petition for modification or rescission. MCR 3.707(A) establishes the procedural mechanism.

21. **Termination Standard.** A court may terminate a PPO when the movant demonstrates that the circumstances justifying the PPO have materially changed, such that the PPO is no longer necessary for the protection of the petitioner. *Hayford v. Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 (2008).

22. **Credibility of Basis.** A PPO must be supported by credible evidence establishing a reasonable apprehension of violence or threatening behavior. *Pickering v. Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694 (2002); *Kampf v. Kampf*, 237 Mich App 377 (1999). Where the factual predicate for the PPO has been disproven, termination is warranted.

23. **Fraud on the Court.** A judgment or order obtained through "fraud, misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party" is subject to relief under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c). A PPO procured through perjured testimony is void *ab initio*. See *Fruit of the Poisonous Tree* doctrine.

24. **Constitutional Framework.** The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972). A PPO that effectively terminates all parental contact must satisfy strict scrutiny and employ the least restrictive means. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976).

### C. Excessive Collateral Consequences — Weaponization of PPO

#### Issue

37. Whether the PPO has been converted from a protective instrument into a weapon for custody advantage, constituting abuse of process warranting termination.

#### Rule

38. A PPO cannot serve as a substitute for custody modification proceedings. *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247 (2009). Michigan law requires specific findings under MCL 722.27 and the best-interest factors of MCL 722.23 before custody may be modified. When a PPO is used to achieve a *de facto* custody modification without meeting these statutory requirements, the PPO exceeds its lawful purpose and must be terminated.

39. Abuse of process requires (a) an ulterior purpose and (b) a willful act in the use of process not proper in the regular conduct of the proceeding. *Friedman v. Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1, 30 (1981). Serial, evidence-free show-cause filings weaponize the PPO framework and constitute abuse of process sanctionable under MCR 1.109(E).

#### Application

40. The PPO has been systematically weaponized as a custody tool:

   a. **Every show-cause motion relates to communication about L.D.W. — not to genuine safety threats.** No documented show-cause alleged physical proximity, stalking, threats, or any behavior constituting danger to Petitioner's person. Every allegation involved Respondent's attempts to communicate with or about his child.

   b. **The PPO created a deliberate Catch-22.** The PPO was drafted so broadly that it prohibited Respondent from communicating through AppClose — the very platform the Court ordered the parties to use for co-parenting communication. Petitioner then filed show-cause motions when Respondent used the court-ordered platform for its intended purpose. This is a textbook trap: comply with the custody order and violate the PPO, or comply with the PPO and abandon your child.

   c. **The PPO functions as an end-run around custody procedures.** By obtaining a PPO that effectively eliminated Respondent's parenting time, Petitioner achieved a *de facto* custody modification without meeting any of the statutory requirements of MCL 722.27 — exactly the abuse that *Hunter* prohibits.

   d. **Strategic timing confirms tactical purpose.** The PPO was filed on December 3, 2023, amid an active custody dispute. Show-cause motions were timed to coincide with critical custody proceedings. The PPO extension was triggered by a strategically-timed fourth violation filing on November 19, 2024. On April 11, 2024, PPO Amendment, PPO Violation Hearing, and Watson's Custody Complaint all occurred on the same day. This is not the behavior of a person seeking protection — it is the behavior of a litigant seeking tactical advantage.

   e. **Asymmetric enforcement.** While Respondent received 59 days of incarceration for birthday messages, Petitioner's family members committed far more serious conduct — Albert Watson threw PPO papers through Respondent's car window and physically removed L.D.W. from Respondent's arms; Cody Watson engaged in documented threats — with zero sanctions. A PPO enforced exclusively against one party while the other party's agents act with impunity is not a protective order. It is a weapon.

   f. **59 days of jail for birthday messages.** The disproportionality speaks for itself. A father was incarcerated for nearly two months for attempting to wish his child a happy birthday through a court-approved communication application. This is not the protection of a domestic violence victim — it is the weaponization of judicial process.

#### Conclusion

41. Under *Hunter v. Hunter*, this Court is required to evaluate whether the PPO serves a legitimate protective function or has been repurposed as an unauthorized custody tool. The record compels the conclusion that this PPO has been perverted from its statutory purpose. It must be terminated.

### D. Constitutional Violations — First and Fourteenth Amendments

#### Issue

42. Whether the PPO, as applied, unconstitutionally restricts Respondent's fundamental parenting rights and right to communicate with his child, in violation of the First and Fourteenth Amendments.

#### Rule

43. **Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process.** The parent-child relationship is among the oldest of fundamental liberty interests recognized by the Supreme Court. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982) ("fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child"). A State may not deprive a parent of this interest without due process of law, which requires, at minimum, adequate notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976).

44. **First Amendment — Prior Restraint.** A PPO that prohibits all communication between a parent and child operates as a prior restraint on protected expression. While PPOs may restrict threatening or harassing speech, a blanket prohibition on all parent-child communication — including birthday greetings through a court-approved application — exceeds what the First Amendment permits. The least restrictive means must be employed. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656 (2009).

45. **Michigan Constitution.** Const 1963, art 1, § 2 (equal protection), § 17 (due process), § 5 (free speech) provide independent protections. MCL 722.27a provides that a child has a RIGHT to parenting time with both parents absent clear and convincing evidence of endangerment.

#### Application

46. The PPO as currently enforced violates these constitutional guarantees:

   a. **Due process in issuance was inadequate.** The PPO was issued ex parte on December 3, 2023, without notice to Respondent and without an evidentiary hearing. While ex parte issuance is permitted in emergency circumstances under MCR 3.706, the *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test requires evaluation of: (1) the private interest affected — here, a fundamental parenting right; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation — here, extremely high given the fabricated factual basis; and (3) the government's interest — here, no legitimate interest in maintaining a PPO based on disproven allegations.

   b. **223+ days of total separation without adequate process.** Respondent has been completely separated from his child for 223+ consecutive days. No evidentiary hearing has produced findings, based on competent evidence, that Respondent's parenting poses a risk to L.D.W. The continuation of this separation without such findings violates substantive due process. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982).

   c. **First Amendment — birthday messages are protected speech.** The conduct underlying Respondent's incarceration — sending birthday messages to his child through a court-approved application — constitutes protected parent-child communication. A PPO that criminalizes birthday greetings to one's own child operates as an unconstitutional prior restraint that is not narrowly tailored to any legitimate protective purpose.

   d. **Least restrictive means not employed.** Even assuming arguendo that some restriction on contact between Respondent and Petitioner were justified, the PPO's blanket prohibition on all communication — including parent-child communication through court-approved channels — is not the least restrictive means available. Modification to permit parenting communication while maintaining a no-contact provision between the parties would adequately serve any legitimate protective interest.

   e. **MCL 722.27a — the child's right to parenting time.** L.D.W. has a statutory right to a relationship with both parents. The PPO as enforced deprives L.D.W. of this right without the clear and convincing evidence of endangerment required by Michigan law. The child is not the petitioner's property — L.D.W. is a person with independent rights that this PPO violates.

#### Conclusion

47. The PPO as currently enforced violates the First and Fourteenth Amendments to the United States Constitution and the corresponding provisions of the Michigan Constitution. At minimum, the PPO must be modified to permit parent-child communication. Termination is the constitutionally required remedy given the complete absence of credible evidence supporting continued restriction.
