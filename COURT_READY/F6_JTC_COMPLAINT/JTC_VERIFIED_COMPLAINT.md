## I. INTRODUCTION

1. Andrew James Pigors ("Complainant" or "Father") respectfully submits this Request for Investigation to the Michigan Judicial Tenure Commission ("Commission") pursuant to Michigan Constitution of 1963, Article VI, § 30, and MCR 9.200–9.220, requesting that the Commission investigate the judicial conduct of the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill, Judge of the 14th Circuit Court, Family Division, Muskegon County, Michigan.

2. This Complaint alleges a sustained pattern of judicial misconduct including: (a) ex parte communications through judicial staff; (b) entry of ex parte orders without notice; (c) bias and prejudice against a party-litigant; (d) denial of due process in contempt proceedings resulting in 59 days of incarceration; (e) denial of court access through financial barriers and refusal to consider filings; (f) disability discrimination; and (g) evidence manipulation through rejection of a favorable court-ordered evaluation and procurement of a manufactured contrary evaluation.

3. Complainant acknowledges that the Commission does not function as an appellate court and does not review the legal correctness of judicial decisions. *See In re Brown*, 461 Mich 1291 (2000). This Complaint is confined exclusively to Judge McNeill's **judicial conduct** — specifically, the *manner* in which she conducted proceedings, not the substantive correctness of any ruling. Where a ruling is referenced, the complaint addresses the procedural circumstances surrounding the ruling (absence of notice, absence of hearing, reliance on ex parte evidence), not its legal merit.

## III. SPECIFIC COUNTS OF MISCONDUCT

### COUNT I: EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS
#### Violations of Canon 3(A)(4), Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct

7. Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides: "A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

8. Ethics Opinion JI-134 establishes that a judge is responsible for improper ex parte contacts by staff and has an affirmative duty to instruct staff regarding the prohibition on ex parte communications.

**A. Five Ex Parte Orders — August 8, 2025**

9. On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five separate ex parte orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, all without prior notice to Father, all suspending his parenting time, and all favoring Mother. Father received no advance notice of any filing, motion, or request that precipitated these orders.

10. The day before — August 7, 2025 — Albert Watson (Mother's father) told North Shores Police Department Officer Ella Randall (Badge #47437, Case NS2505044) that "they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son." The temporal and substantive connection between this admission and the next-day entry of five ex parte orders indicates coordination outside the judicial process.

**B. Admitted Ex Parte Evidence Review — August 5, 2025**

11. On August 5, 2025, Mother submitted a USB recording to the clerk's office. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Yes, I had my staff listen to it." This admission establishes that the judge received and considered substantive evidence — an audio recording — outside the presence of the parties, in direct violation of Canon 3(A)(4). The recording was made by Lori Watson (Mother's mother) without Father's consent, potentially a felony under MCL 750.539c. No authentication under MRE 901 was established.

**C. Rusco Warrant Email — March 3, 2025**

12. At 8:51 AM on March 3, 2025, Judge McNeill's judicial secretary Pamela Rusco emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker: "Good Morning Jamie, Andrew Pigors was ordered to turn himself in to the Jail by 5pm on Friday to serve his 14 days in jail for the PPO Violation. I confirmed with the jail that he is not currently lodged. Can you please issue a warrant for him? Attached are both the order after hearing and the jail mittimus."

13. This email constitutes a Canon 3(A)(4) ex parte communication. The judge's chambers initiated a substantive prosecutorial action — a warrant request — with the prosecution, outside the presence of the parties, and attached court orders as enforcement instruments directed to one side only. Defense attorney Mandi Martini was copied only after the substantive request was made. Additionally, Rusco independently investigated Father's incarceration status by confirming with the jail — a substantive investigative function, not a ministerial act.

**D. Rusco Off-Record Witness Coordination — October 29, 2025**

14. A HealthWest clinical chart memo documents that "Pamela Rusco, Judicial Secretary for the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill . . . called to request if this clinician could testify . . . will email us the subpoenas." Subpoenas for clinical witness testimony were arranged via telephone and email between chambers staff and the clinical provider — not through formal court filings served on the parties as required by MCR 2.506. Neither Father nor Mother received notice of this coordination.

**E. Biased Letter to Evaluator**

15. Prior to the September 11, 2025 HealthWest assessment, Judge McNeill provided a letter to HealthWest describing Father's behaviors as "several incidents over the past two years of court proceedings which are suggestive of a mental health disorder." This letter was sent without Father's knowledge or consent, constituting an ex parte communication with a prospective evaluator that biased the evaluation before it began.

**F. Routing Evidence Through Secretary**

16. Judge McNeill directed Father to email his HealthWest evaluation to her secretary Pamela Rusco rather than filing through the Clerk of Court. This procedure ensured the evaluation bypassed MCR 8.119 filing requirements, never became part of the public record, and was never accessible to the opposing party through normal channels.

**G. Rusco Email — November 29, 2025**

17. On November 29, 2025, Pamela Rusco transmitted emails regarding the status of a PPO and Father's jail status — substantive case matters communicated outside the presence of the parties and not filed on the record.

**Harm Caused by Count I:**

18. The cumulative effect of these ex parte communications resulted in: (a) total suspension of Father's parenting time without adversarial process; (b) a warrant issued through off-record coordination between chambers and prosecution; (c) clinical witness testimony arranged without party notice; (d) a biased evaluation that replaced a favorable one; and (e) the complete severing of the father-child relationship for 223+ days.

19. *In re Justin*, 490 Mich 394 (2012), holds that even a single significant ex parte violation warrants censure. The pattern documented here — involving the judge, her secretary, prosecutors, and evaluators — far exceeds any single incident.

### COUNT II: BIAS AND PREJUDICE
#### Violations of Canon 2 (Appearance of Impropriety), Canon 3(A)(3) (Patient, Dignified Treatment)

20. Canon 2 requires that "a judge shall avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." Canon 3(A)(3) requires that a judge "be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants."

**A. Muting Father During Hearings**

21. During the Show Cause #5 hearing on November 15, 2025, Father appeared remotely and was muted repeatedly while attempting to present argument and objections. Court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini sat completely silent — offering no advocacy, no objections, and no defense. During a separate hearing, Father was muted seven documented times in a single proceeding. During a Zoom hearing, Father was muted three additional times while presenting evidence. Muting a party during a hearing is the functional equivalent of removing that party from the courtroom.

**B. "Do Not File Anymore, I Will Not Look at It"**

22. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." She further characterized Father's motions as "harassment to Defendant and to the Court, along with an abuse of the Court system" and threatened sanctions for any future filing. No vexatious-litigant finding was ever made under MCL 600.2591. This constitutes a categorical refusal to perform the judicial function and an abdication of the duty to adjudicate matters properly before the court.

**C. Hostile On-Record Statements**

23. Judge McNeill called Father "a liar" and "without rational basis" on the record — personal attacks directed at a litigant, not findings of fact supported by evidence. She characterized the favorable HealthWest evaluation as a "ghost" mental health evaluation — a prejudicial characterization without evidentiary basis.

**D. Asymmetric Enforcement**

24. Mother committed a documented pattern of violations of court orders throughout these proceedings. Zero of Mother's violations have been enforced — no show-cause orders, no contempt findings, no sanctions. By contrast, Father was held in contempt, jailed for a cumulative 59 days, and subjected to escalating sanctions for sending co-parenting messages through a court-sanctioned application. Mother's arrears were forgiven while Father was simultaneously charged. This radical asymmetry — zero enforcement against one party, maximum punishment against the other — demonstrates outcome-determinative bias.

**E. Court-Appointed Counsel Confirmed Prejudgment**

25. On February 28, 2025, Father's court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini emailed him before the hearing: "I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked. I'm sure you do not want to spend the next week in jail on a contempt charge for speaking out in court when you weren't asked." That a court-appointed attorney communicated the judge's hostile disposition and the expectation that Father would be silenced — before the hearing began — demonstrates that the appearance of impropriety was so entrenched that officers of the court anticipated predetermined outcomes.

**F. Prior Representation Without Recusal**

26. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "I have represented Party A in the past." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge has been counsel or adviser to a party. Despite this admission of a prior attorney-client relationship with a party, Judge McNeill did not recuse herself and continued to preside over both cases.

**Harm Caused by Count II:**

27. The cumulative pattern of bias resulted in Father being unable to meaningfully participate in proceedings affecting his parental rights, his child's welfare, and his own liberty. The appearance of impartiality — essential to public confidence in the judiciary — was destroyed.

28. *In re Haley*, 476 Mich 180 (2006), holds that a pattern of bias justifies removal. *In re Brown*, 461 Mich 1291 (2000), holds that courtroom demeanor misconduct constitutes sanctionable conduct.

### COUNT III: FAILURE TO AFFORD DUE PROCESS
#### Violation of Const. 1963, Art. I, §§ 17, 20; Canon 3(A)(5); Canon 3(A)(7)

29. Canon 3(A)(7) requires that "a judge shall accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law." Canon 3(A)(5) requires prompt, efficient, and fair disposition.

**A. 59 Days Incarceration Without Proper Safeguards**

30. Father was incarcerated for a cumulative 59 days arising from custody and PPO proceedings: 14 days from the November 15, 2025 hearing (SC#5) and 45 days from the November 26, 2025 hearing (SC#6 and SC#7). The 45-day sentence was imposed for sending birthday messages to his child through the AppClose co-parenting application.

31. On February 28, 2025, Judge McNeill entered a legally incoherent order simultaneously imposing 14 days of jail for contempt AND dismissing the underlying matter. A dismissal eliminates the court's authority to enforce contempt — these dispositions are mutually exclusive.

**B. Suspended Parenting Time Without MCR 3.207(B) Findings**

32. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders suspended all of Father's parenting time without any written findings of endangerment required by MCL 722.27a(3). On November 26, 2025, Judge McNeill quoted the correct legal standard — "clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child" — but then failed to apply it: the suspension was continued without written findings. Throughout the entire case history, no documented hearing was held within the 14-day window mandated by MCR 3.207(C)(5) for contested custody and parenting time motions.

**C. Self-Denial of Disqualification Motion**

33. On September 25, 2024, Father filed a Motion to Disqualify under MCR 2.003. Judge McNeill denied the motion herself: "DENIED. No bias shown." MCR 2.003(D)(1) mandatorily requires that when a judge does not disqualify herself, the motion "shall" be referred to the chief judge for determination. No such referral was made — a direct violation of the mandatory referral procedure that denied Father the independent review MCR 2.003(D)(1) was designed to ensure.

**Harm Caused by Count III:**

34. Father was deprived of liberty for 59 days, separated from his child for 223+ consecutive days without an evidentiary hearing, and denied the procedural protections mandated by Michigan Court Rules and the Michigan Constitution.

### COUNT IV: DENIAL OF COURT ACCESS
#### Violation of Canon 3(A)(5); Const. 1963, Art. I, § 13 (Right to Petition)

35. The right of access to courts is fundamental. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971), holds that financial barriers cannot deny access to courts in cases involving fundamental rights.

**A. $250 Filing Deposit**

36. Judge McNeill imposed a $250 bond/deposit requirement as a condition for Father to proceed with filings. This financial barrier was imposed without statutory authority, without a vexatious-litigant finding under MCL 600.2591, and without any finding that Father had the ability to pay. Father is a pro se litigant of limited means who lost four jobs during the course of this litigation.

**B. Blanket Refusal to Consider Filings**

37. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." Six motions filed July 16, 2024 (the day before trial) were denied in a single sentence without individual analysis. This blanket refusal to consider filings constitutes a denial of the right to petition the court for redress — without any formal vexatious-litigant finding.

**Harm Caused by Count IV:**

38. Father was effectively barred from seeking judicial relief on matters affecting custody, parenting time, and his child's welfare. The combination of financial barriers and blanket refusal to review filings created an impenetrable wall between Father and the court.

---

### COUNT V: DISABILITY DISCRIMINATION
#### Violation of Canon 3(A)(7); ADA Title II; MCR 1.105

39. Canon 3(A)(7) requires that a judge afford every person the right to be heard. The Americans with Disabilities Act, Title II, prohibits discrimination by public entities, including courts. MCR 1.105 requires courts to provide reasonable accommodations for persons with disabilities.

40. Father has a documented diagnosis of Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). Judge McNeill dismissed this documented disability, suggesting it was psychosomatic rather than recognizing it as a condition that may affect Father's ability to participate in proceedings, organize filings, and comply with complex procedural requirements.

41. No inquiry was made regarding reasonable accommodations. No accommodations were offered or provided. Father's ADHD was treated as evidence of unreliability rather than as a disability requiring accommodation.

**Harm Caused by Count V:**

42. Father, a pro se litigant with a documented disability, was denied the accommodations necessary to meaningfully participate in proceedings that determined his parental rights and his child's welfare.

### COUNT VI: EVIDENCE MANIPULATION
#### Violation of Canon 2(A); Canon 3(A)(4); Canon 3(A)(5)

**A. Rejection of Favorable Court-Ordered Evaluation**

43. Judge McNeill ordered Father to undergo a HealthWest evaluation. Father complied. The evaluation (Case #02508341, September 4–5, 2025, clinicians Michelle Mitchell, QIDP/QMHP, and Melissa DeAugustine, LBSW) cleared Father entirely: Psychosis 0, Substance Use 0, Danger to Self 0, Danger to Others 0, Depression 0, Impulse Control 0, Anger Control 0. LOCUS Score: 12 (Level One — lowest severity). Diagnosis: F43.22 Adjustment Disorder with Anxiety — RULE OUT only.

44. Four days later, Judge McNeill rejected these findings, writing: "None of the above statements are what he testified to in Court. He was evicted, and his trailer is not being remodeled." The court rejected a professional clinical assessment because its findings did not match the court's desired narrative.

**B. Manufactured Contrary Evaluation**

45. Judge McNeill then sent a biased letter to HealthWest describing Father's behaviors as "suggestive of a mental health disorder." The second evaluation (September 11, 2025, clinician Kassandra M. Gansen, LMSW) — conducted just seven days after the first — produced a "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" finding with inflated scores (Psychosis: 0→2, Depression: 0→2, Anger Control: 0→2). Even the second evaluator acknowledged the limitation: "It is difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional in the context of a brief screening."

46. The second evaluation was directed to chambers via email rather than filed through the Clerk of Court, bypassing MCR 8.119. Father was never provided a copy, never informed of its existence, and never afforded an opportunity to challenge it. This constitutes a violation of the principles established in *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 US 83 (1963).

**C. Exclusion of Exculpatory Police Reports**

47. Father submitted police reports (Officer Ella Randall, NSPD Case NS2505044; and additional reports) documenting exculpatory evidence. Seven or more police investigations were initiated based on Watson family reports. Every investigation found no violation by Father. Judge McNeill never cited this exculpatory record in any order, while accepting at face value a police officer's unverified lay opinion that Father's behavior was "the result of meth use" — despite the HealthWest clinical finding of Substance Use = 0 and no drug test ever ordered.

**D. Selective Evidence Admission**

48. Judge McNeill admitted Lori Watson's USB recording — obtained during a phone call without Father's consent, potentially a felony under MCL 750.539c (Michigan's two-party consent wiretapping statute) — while refusing to admit Father's lawfully made November 2023 audio recording of Albert Watson stating: "I will make sure that you don't see your son." The asymmetric admission of evidence uniformly favoring one party demonstrates bias.

**Harm Caused by Count VI:**

49. The manipulation of the evaluation process — ordering an evaluation, rejecting the favorable result, biasing the replacement evaluation, and concealing the second evaluation from Father — constitutes a systematic effort to manufacture evidence supporting a predetermined outcome. Combined with the exclusion of exculpatory evidence and selective admission of potentially illegally obtained evidence, this pattern goes beyond judicial error into deliberate misconduct.

### COUNT VII: CUMULATIVE EFFECT — CONDUCT CLEARLY PREJUDICIAL TO THE ADMINISTRATION OF JUSTICE
#### Michigan Constitution, Article VI, § 30(2)

50. Article VI, § 30(2) of the Michigan Constitution authorizes discipline for "misconduct in office," "persistent failure to perform duties," and "conduct clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice."

51. *In re Brennan*, 504 Mich 80 (2019), established that the "cumulative effect" of multiple violations — even if some individually might warrant only lesser sanctions — can justify the most severe discipline including removal and a ban on future judicial service. In *Brennan*, 17 counts of misconduct warranted removal.

52. The pattern documented in this Complaint — ex parte communications through a judicial secretary, entry of orders without notice, admitted review of ex parte evidence, rejection of favorable evaluations, manufacture of contrary evaluations, hostile courtroom demeanor, muting a litigant, refusal to consider filings, imposition of financial barriers, incarceration without proper safeguards, disability discrimination, and asymmetric enforcement — constitutes a sustained course of conduct that is clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice.

53. This pattern is not the product of isolated judicial errors or legitimate exercises of discretion. It reflects a systematic practice in which:

    a. Every ex parte order favored a single party;

    b. Every discretionary ruling was adverse to a single litigant;

    c. Every procedural irregularity operated to the detriment of a single father's relationship with his child;

    d. The judicial secretary functioned as an extension of the court's authority, initiating warrant requests, coordinating witness testimony, routing evidence, and transmitting subpoenas — all outside the presence of the parties and off the record.

54. The trajectory of Father's parenting time tells the story: 182.5 overnights per year (50/50 custody) → 79 overnights → **0 overnights** — a 100% elimination without a single written best-interests finding under MCL 722.23. A child who had two active parents now has one. L.D.W. was approximately two years old when all contact with Father was severed. The child is now in a critical developmental period without any relationship with his father — a harm that compounds daily.

## IV. FAILURE TO EXERCISE SUPERVISORY DILIGENCE
#### Canon 3(C)(1)

55. Canon 3(C)(1) requires that "a judge should exercise diligence in supervisory responsibilities." A judge must ensure that non-judicial staff do not exercise substantive judicial authority.

56. The documented activities of Pamela Rusco — initiating warrant requests to prosecutors, coordinating clinical witness testimony via telephone and email, independently investigating Father's incarceration status, routing substantive evidence through her personal email, and transmitting subpoenas — collectively establish that Judge McNeill improperly delegated substantive judicial authority to her secretary. Rusco functioned not as a ministerial aide but as a case manager making decisions that materially affected Father's liberty and parental rights.

57. Under Ethics Opinion JI-134, Judge McNeill bears direct responsibility for these actions by her staff.
