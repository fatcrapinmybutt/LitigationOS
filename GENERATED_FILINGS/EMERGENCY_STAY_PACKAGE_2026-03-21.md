#### B. Irreparable Harm to the Parent-Child Bond

33. **Issue:** Whether continued proceedings under a potentially conflicted judge cause irreparable harm sufficient to warrant a stay.

34. **Rule:** Irreparable harm exists where the injury cannot be adequately remedied by a later legal or equitable remedy. In the context of parental rights, the Supreme Court has recognized that "[t]he fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents." *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982). The passage of time in child custody matters causes unique, irreversible harm.

35. **Application:** Plaintiff has been separated from his child for more than 235 days. Research in developmental psychology uniformly recognizes that prolonged parent-child separation during early developmental years (L.D.W. is approximately three years old) causes lasting psychological harm. Each additional day of proceedings under a judge whose impartiality is reasonably in question extends this harm. Unlike monetary damages, the destruction of a parent-child bond during these critical developmental years cannot be remedied retroactively.

36. **Conclusion:** The ongoing, compounding nature of the harm to the parent-child relationship constitutes irreparable injury warranting a stay.

40. **Conclusion:** Due process requires that proceedings be stayed until the disqualification question is resolved by an impartial authority.

### II. CONTROLLING AUTHORITY

#### A. Mandatory Disqualification Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) establishes mandatory disqualification when the judge or the judge's spouse is "related within the third degree" to a party, a party's attorney, or the spouse of either. Unlike discretionary disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a), mandatory disqualification under subsection (b) does not require a showing of actual bias — the relationship itself compels disqualification as a matter of law.

The Michigan Supreme Court in *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich. 347 (1975), established the foundational principle that judicial disqualification serves not only to ensure actual impartiality but to preserve the *appearance* of impartiality essential to public confidence in the judiciary. The Court quoted the venerable maxim: "Not only must justice be done; it must also be seen to be done." *Id.* at 351.

#### B. The Constitutional Requirement of an Impartial Tribunal

The right to an impartial tribunal is rooted in the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The Supreme Court of the United States has repeatedly held that this right is among the most fundamental protections of the Constitution:

> "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process. This applies to administrative agencies which adjudicate as well as to courts."

*In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955).

The *Caperton* decision extended this principle, holding that due process mandates recusal not only where actual bias is demonstrated but where "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 872. The Court in *Caperton* applied an objective standard, asking "whether, under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness, the interest poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden." *Id.* at 883-84 (internal quotation marks omitted).

#### C. Stay of Proceedings Under MCR 2.614

MCR 2.614 provides broad authority for a court to stay proceedings in the interest of justice. Michigan courts have recognized that a stay is appropriate where continuing proceedings would be futile, harmful, or inconsistent with principles of judicial economy. *See Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp.*, 248 Mich. App. 573, 588-89 (2001) (analyzing factors relevant to stay determinations).

In the context of a pending disqualification motion, the logic for a stay is compelling: if the disqualification motion is granted, all orders entered during the pendency of that motion may be subject to challenge as void or voidable. Continuing proceedings under these circumstances wastes judicial resources and inflicts further harm on the parties.

### III. ARGUMENT

#### A. The Berry-McNeill Nexus Mandates Disqualification and a Stay

The factual predicate for this motion is straightforward: Judge McNeill is married to Cavan John Berry, a family law attorney practicing in the Muskegon jurisdiction. Ronald Berry, a relative of Cavan Berry, is the domestic partner of the Defendant. This relationship places the matter squarely within the mandatory disqualification provisions of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).

The failure to rule on the disqualification motion for nearly six months — in direct violation of the 14-day requirement of MCR 2.003(D)(2) — itself demonstrates the need for a stay. A judge who cannot or will not rule on a disqualification motion within the prescribed timeframe should not simultaneously be exercising judicial authority over the very party who has challenged that authority.

The Supreme Court's admonition in *Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510, 532 (1927), is instructive: "Every procedure which would offer a possible temptation to the average man as a judge to forget the burden of proof required to convict the defendant, or which might lead him not to hold the balance nice, clear, and true between the state and the accused, denies the latter due process of law."

#### B. The Three-Judge Structural Conflict Requires Transfer Outside the Circuit

Ordinary disqualification contemplates reassignment to another judge within the same court. Here, however, the structural conflict extends beyond Judge McNeill.

Chief Judge Kenneth Hoopes and Judge Maria Ladas-Hoopes both practiced at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill Law Offices — the same firm where Judge McNeill practiced before ascending to the bench. Chief Judge Hoopes is the judicial officer ordinarily responsible for reassignment following a disqualification. His prior professional partnership with Judge McNeill creates a reasonable question about whether he can perform this reassignment function impartially.

Further, Judge Ladas-Hoopes presided over a separate eviction proceeding against Plaintiff in the 60th District Court, entering judgment against him. Whether or not that ruling was legally correct, the fact that Plaintiff has been subjected to adverse rulings from three judges who all practiced at the same law firm creates a cumulative appearance of institutional bias that cannot be remedied by intra-circuit reassignment.

This structural compromise is precisely the type of systemic failure that warrants superintending control by the Michigan Supreme Court. Pending such review, a stay is necessary to preserve the status quo.

#### C. Irreparable Harm Is Ongoing and Compounding

The calculus of irreparable harm in this case is not speculative — it is mathematical. Every day that passes without contact between Father and L.D.W. is a day that cannot be recovered. L.D.W. is approximately three years old. At this critical stage of development, prolonged separation from a parent has documented, lasting effects on a child's attachment, emotional development, and sense of security.

The *Vodvarka* court recognized that changes to established custodial environments require clear and convincing evidence because of the harm inherent in disrupting parent-child relationships. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich. App. 499, 509-10 (2003). Here, the custodial disruption has been imposed by judicial order, not by parental agreement, and it has persisted for more than 235 days under a judge whose impartiality is in question.

The favorable evaluation from HealthWest (Reference H0002, September 5, 2025) further undermines the factual basis for the continued suspension. The Court's refusal to restore parenting time despite favorable professional evaluation deepens the irreparable harm analysis.

#### D. Anticipated Counterarguments

**1. "The stay will prejudice Defendant."**

A stay preserves the status quo. Defendant retains physical custody of L.D.W. under the existing operative orders. The only "prejudice" Defendant might claim is the inability to obtain further orders from a judge whose impartiality is in question — which is precisely the outcome disqualification law is designed to prevent.

**2. "The disqualification motion lacks merit."**

This argument is premature. The disqualification motion has not been ruled upon. If the motion lacks merit, the Court could have denied it within the 14-day window prescribed by MCR 2.003(D)(2). The failure to rule for nearly six months undermines any argument that the motion is frivolous.

**3. "A stay harms the child's best interest."**

The child's best interest is not served by proceedings conducted before a conflicted judge. Moreover, the child's best interest requires a relationship with both parents. MCL 722.27a establishes a presumption in favor of parenting time, recognizing that it is "in the best interest of a child for the child to have a strong relationship with both of the child's parents." More than 235 days of enforced separation from a parent — based on unsworn speculation and maintained despite a favorable professional evaluation — is antithetical to the child's best interest.

**4. "Transfer outside the circuit is unnecessary."**

The three-judge structural conflict renders intra-circuit transfer inadequate. If Chief Judge Hoopes reassigns to himself or to Judge Ladas-Hoopes, the appearance of institutional bias persists. Transfer to an adjacent circuit is the minimum remedy consistent with due process.

The confluence of mandatory disqualification grounds, a six-month failure to rule on the disqualification motion, a three-judge structural conflict, a pattern of ex parte proceedings, and more than 235 days of enforced parent-child separation creates an urgent need for judicial intervention. A stay of all proceedings preserves the status quo, prevents further irreparable harm, and allows for orderly review of the threshold disqualification question by an impartial authority.

Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court grant the emergency stay and, in the alternative, transfer these cases to a judge outside the 14th Circuit.
