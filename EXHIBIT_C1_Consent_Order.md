# EXHIBIT C-1: April 29, 2024 Consent Order

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-1
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC §1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit is the Consent Order entered on April 29, 2024, by the Fourteenth Circuit Court, Muskegon County, Michigan, Case No. 2024-001507-DC. The order established joint legal and joint physical custody of the minor child, L.D.W., with a parenting time schedule providing equal (50/50) time between both parents. The order was entered by agreement of both parties and reflected the court's determination at that time that shared custody served the best interests of the child.

The Consent Order represents the baseline constitutional right at issue in this federal civil rights action. Under clearly established law, a parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his or her child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. This order memorializes the existence and judicial recognition of that fundamental right.

Prior to the events giving rise to this complaint, Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors was an actively involved, equal custodial parent exercising his constitutionally protected parental rights on a daily basis pursuant to this court order.

## Legal Significance

The Consent Order is the foundational exhibit in this §1983 action because it establishes the precise constitutional interest that was subsequently destroyed without due process of law. The United States Supreme Court has long recognized that a parent's right to the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children is a liberty interest far more precious than any property right. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 758–59 (1982). This order is the documentary proof that Plaintiff possessed that protected interest.

The significance of this exhibit is amplified by what followed. Between April 29, 2024 — the date this order was entered — and August 8, 2025, Plaintiff exercised his parental rights without interruption. Then, on August 8–9, 2025, Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill issued five ex parte orders that completely eliminated Plaintiff's custody and parenting time without any prior notice, opportunity to be heard, or adversarial proceeding. The deprivation of the rights established by this Consent Order, accomplished through ex parte judicial action, is the gravamen of this federal civil rights complaint.

This exhibit further demonstrates that no court has ever found Plaintiff to be an unfit parent through any proper adjudicatory process. The joint custody arrangement reflected the court's own finding that Plaintiff was a suitable custodial parent — a finding that was never reversed through constitutionally adequate procedures.

## Key Evidence

- **April 29, 2024:** Consent Order entered granting joint legal custody and joint physical custody of L.D.W.
- **Parenting time schedule:** Equal 50/50 division of parenting time between Plaintiff and Defendant Emily A. Watson
- **Voluntary agreement:** Order entered by consent of both parties, not as a result of contested litigation
- **No findings of unfitness:** No adverse findings regarding Plaintiff's parenting capacity at any time prior to the ex parte orders
- **Active parental relationship:** Plaintiff exercised continuous, uninterrupted parenting time from April 29, 2024 through July 29, 2025

## Constitutional Implications

The Consent Order establishes beyond dispute that Plaintiff possessed a judicially recognized, constitutionally protected fundamental liberty interest in the custody and companionship of his child, L.D.W. The Fourteenth Amendment provides that no State shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

The complete deprivation of this established custody right — accomplished through ex parte orders issued without notice or hearing — constitutes a textbook violation of procedural due process. Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), the fundamental requirement of due process is the opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. No such opportunity was provided before or after the issuance of the ex parte orders that destroyed the parental rights established by this Consent Order. Plaintiff has now been separated from his child for over 255 days and counting.
