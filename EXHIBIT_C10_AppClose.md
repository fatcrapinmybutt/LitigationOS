# EXHIBIT C-10: AppClose Communication Records

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-10
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC 1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises communication records from AppClose, a court-approved co-parenting communication platform used by Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors and Defendant Emily A. Watson in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. AppClose is a monitored platform specifically designed for co-parenting communication, and its use was sanctioned by the Fourteenth Circuit Court as the designated means of communication between the parties regarding their minor child, L.D.W.

The AppClose records document Plaintiff's communications, including the birthday messages to L.D.W. that became the basis for Show Cause #6 and #7 (Exhibit C-5), resulting in 45 days of incarceration. The records demonstrate that Plaintiff's messages were normal parental communications: birthday wishes, expressions of love, and routine co-parenting coordination. The messages contain no threats, no harassment, no intimidation, and no content that could reasonably be construed as contemptuous conduct under any standard.

These records are generated and maintained by AppClose's platform, providing a complete, unalterable, timestamped log of all communications. They constitute an objective, third-party record of the actual content of the messages that were characterized by the state court as contemptuous conduct warranting 45 days of incarceration.

## Legal Significance

The AppClose records are critical to the First Amendment retaliation claim. The records establish that the communications punished as contempt were, in substance, nothing more than a father's birthday wishes to his child. The First Amendment protects the right of parents to communicate with their children. When a court punishes a parent with 45 days of incarceration for sending birthday messages through a court-approved platform, the punitive action is not a response to contemptuous conduct but rather retaliation for the exercise of protected expression.

The records also demonstrate the absence of any legitimate basis for the contempt findings. Contempt of court requires willful disobedience of a court order. The AppClose records show that Plaintiff used the very communication platform approved by the court, for the very purpose it was designed to serve: communicating with his child. If the court intended to prohibit all communication, including birthday wishes, such a prohibition would itself raise serious constitutional concerns as an unconstitutional prior restraint on protected speech.

Furthermore, the contrast between the innocuous content of the messages and the severity of the punishment (45 days incarceration) supports the inference of retaliatory intent and establishes the gross disproportionality relevant to both the Eighth Amendment and the punitive damages analysis. No rational, unbiased judicial officer would sentence a parent to 45 days in jail for wishing his child a happy birthday.

## Key Evidence

- **AppClose platform:** Court-approved co-parenting communication tool, specifically sanctioned for use in Case No. 2024-001507-DC
- **Birthday messages to L.D.W.:** Normal parental expressions of love and birthday wishes
- **Message content:** Contains no threats, no harassment, no intimidation, no profanity, and no contemptuous language
- **Timestamped records:** AppClose generates unalterable, timestamped logs of all communications
- **Basis for SC#6 and SC#7:** These birthday messages were cited as the grounds for 45 days of incarceration
- **Court-approved platform:** Plaintiff used the exact communication method directed by the court
- **Normal co-parenting communication:** Messages reflect routine parental interaction, indistinguishable from millions of co-parenting messages sent daily
- **No prior warning:** Plaintiff was not warned that birthday messages would be treated as contemptuous conduct

## Constitutional Implications

The AppClose records present a stark First Amendment issue. The freedom of speech protected by the First Amendment encompasses a parent's right to communicate with his child, particularly through channels expressly approved by the court for that purpose. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) (recognizing the constitutional dimension of the parent-child relationship). The punishment of protected parental speech through the contempt power constitutes both a content-based restriction on speech and a retaliatory exercise of governmental authority.

The records also implicate the Due Process Clause in a fundamental way. When a court approves a communication platform, a litigant is entitled to rely on that approval. The subsequent punishment of communications made through the approved platform, without any prior notice that such communications would be deemed contemptuous, violates the notice requirement inherent in due process. A person of ordinary intelligence would not understand that sending birthday wishes through a court-approved platform could result in 45 days of incarceration. The vagueness and unpredictability of the contempt standard as applied here offends basic due process principles. *Grayned v. City of Rockford*, 408 U.S. 104, 108 (1972).
