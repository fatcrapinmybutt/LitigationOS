# EXHIBIT C-6: Law Partnership Records

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-6
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC §1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises public records, Michigan State Bar records, and business filings documenting the prior law partnership among three judicial officers who presided over separate aspects of the litigation affecting Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors: Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, Hon. Kenneth Hoopes (Chief Judge, Fourteenth Circuit Court), and Hon. Maria Ladas-Hoopes (Sixtieth District Court). These three judicial officers were formerly law partners at the firm of Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill, located at 435 Whitehall Road, Muskegon, Michigan.

The partnership records establish that the three judges who collectively controlled the trajectory of Plaintiff's legal proceedings across multiple courts shared a prior professional relationship of the closest possible kind — a law partnership, which under Michigan law creates fiduciary obligations and shared financial interests. This prior relationship was never disclosed to Plaintiff and never the subject of recusal consideration by any of the three judges.

Across these three former law partners' courts, Plaintiff lost custody of his child (McNeill, Fourteenth Circuit), lost his housing case (Hoopes, Fourteenth Circuit), and faced criminal charges (Ladas-Hoopes, Sixtieth District). The statistical improbability of one pro se litigant appearing before three former law partners across three separate courts — and losing in all three — warrants the inference of structural bias and coordinated judicial action.

## Legal Significance

The law partnership records are essential to establishing the conspiracy and structural due process claims in this §1983 action. A law partnership creates the type of intimate professional relationship that, under the Code of Judicial Conduct, requires disclosure and may require recusal. Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(C)(1) provides that a judge shall disqualify himself or herself in any proceeding in which the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned. The existence of a former law partnership among three judges presiding over related cases involving the same litigant is precisely the type of circumstance that would cause a reasonable person to question impartiality.

The failure of all three judges to disclose their prior relationship, combined with the consistently adverse outcomes for Plaintiff across all three courts, supports an inference of concerted action. Under §1983, when state actors conspire to deprive a person of constitutional rights, each conspirator is jointly and severally liable. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 27–28 (1980). The law partnership records establish the relationship foundation necessary to support this conspiracy allegation.

Furthermore, the concentration of judicial authority over one litigant's cases among former law partners raises a structural due process concern that transcends individual bias. Even if no explicit agreement to coordinate existed, the structural reality — three former partners presiding over the same litigant's cases — creates an appearance of impropriety so severe that it deprived Plaintiff of the fair tribunal guaranteed by the Due Process Clause. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 872 (2009) (due process may require recusal based on objective circumstances, without proof of actual bias).

## Constitutional Implications

The concentration of judicial power over one litigant's cases among three former law partners strikes at the heart of the Due Process Clause's guarantee of a fair and impartial tribunal. The Supreme Court has recognized that due process requires not only the absence of actual bias, but also the absence of circumstances that create an unacceptable probability of bias. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 877 (2009). Three former law partners presiding over the same litigant's custody, housing, and criminal proceedings — all resulting in adverse outcomes — creates an objective probability of bias that no reasonable person could ignore.

This structural arrangement also implicates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. When the judicial system itself is structured in a way that systematically disadvantages a particular litigant — through undisclosed relationships among presiding judges — it denies that litigant the equal protection of the laws. Plaintiff was not similarly situated to other litigants in the Fourteenth Circuit and Sixtieth District Courts; he faced a uniquely hostile judicial environment shaped by prior professional alliances that were never disclosed and never addressed.
