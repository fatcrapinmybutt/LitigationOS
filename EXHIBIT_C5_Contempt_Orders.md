# EXHIBIT C-5: Contempt Orders (SC#5, SC#6, SC#7)

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-5
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC §1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises the contempt orders entered against Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors in Case No. 2024-001507-DC: Show Cause #5 (SC#5), entered on or about November 15, 2024, sentencing Plaintiff to 14 days of incarceration; and Show Cause #6 and #7 (SC#6 and SC#7), entered on or about November 26, 2025, sentencing Plaintiff to a combined 45 days of incarceration. In total, these three contempt findings resulted in 59 days of incarceration.

The grounds for these contempt findings reveal a pattern of First Amendment retaliation and abuse of the contempt power. SC#5 resulted from Plaintiff's exercise of his right to file motions with the court — a constitutionally protected activity. Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill had previously stated on the record: "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it." SC#5 thus punished Plaintiff for exercising his right of access to the courts. SC#6 and SC#7 resulted from Plaintiff sending birthday messages to his minor child, L.D.W., via the AppClose platform — a court-approved communication tool. During the hearing on SC#6 and SC#7, Plaintiff was also held in contempt for objecting on the record when the judge and Defendant Emily A. Watson discussed requiring Plaintiff to take prescription medication as a condition of parenting time. The judge told Plaintiff to "shut my mouth" — and his objection became part of the basis for the contempt finding.

The cumulative 59 days of incarceration caused catastrophic collateral consequences: Plaintiff lost two jobs and two homes as a direct result of the imprisonments.

## Legal Significance

The contempt orders are central to multiple counts in this §1983 complaint. First, they establish a pattern of First Amendment retaliation. The right to petition the government for redress of grievances — including the right to file motions with a court — is expressly protected by the First Amendment. *California Motor Transport Co. v. Trucking Unlimited*, 404 U.S. 508, 510 (1972). A judge who imprisons a litigant for filing motions has committed a per se First Amendment violation.

Second, the imprisonment for sending birthday messages to one's own child via a court-approved platform constitutes punishment for protected speech. A parent's communication with his child is protected expression under the First Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) (recognizing the constitutional dimension of the parent-child relationship). The use of contempt power to punish a father for wishing his child a happy birthday is the type of arbitrary and oppressive government action that the Bill of Rights was designed to prevent.

Third, the contempt proceedings themselves violated procedural due process. Plaintiff was denied meaningful opportunity to present his defense, was silenced by the court when he attempted to object to the discussion of forced medication, and was sentenced to incarceration in proceedings that lacked the procedural safeguards required for criminal contempt. *International Union, UMW v. Bagwell*, 512 U.S. 821, 826–27 (1994) (criminal contempt requires procedural protections including notice, right to counsel, and proof beyond reasonable doubt).

## Key Evidence

- **SC#5 (November 15, 2024):** 14 days incarceration — Plaintiff punished for filing motions with the court
- **Judge's statement:** "Do not file anymore, I will not look at it" — direct denial of access to courts
- **SC#6 and SC#7 (November 26, 2025):** 45 days incarceration — Plaintiff punished for sending birthday messages to L.D.W. via AppClose
- **AppClose:** Court-approved communication platform — the very tool Plaintiff was directed to use
- **Medication coercion:** During hearing, judge and Emily A. Watson discussed requiring prescription medication as a condition of parenting time
- **Contempt for objecting:** Plaintiff held in contempt for stating that neither the judge nor opposing party was qualified or legally authorized to mandate medication
- **Judge's response:** Told Plaintiff to "shut my mouth"
- **Total incarceration:** 59 days across three contempt findings
- **Collateral consequences:** Two jobs lost; two homes lost — directly caused by incarceration periods
- **Plaintiff was muted three times** during the SC#5 hearing; police report evidence was ignored

## Constitutional Implications

The contempt orders implicate the First Amendment, the Fourteenth Amendment, and the Eighth Amendment. The First Amendment protects both the right to petition courts and the right to communicate with one's child. The use of contempt to punish both activities constitutes viewpoint-based retaliation — the court punished Plaintiff not for any disruptive or defiant conduct, but for expressing views and exercising rights that the court found inconvenient.

The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause requires that contempt proceedings resulting in incarceration comply with the procedural safeguards applicable to criminal proceedings. The proceedings here fell far short of constitutional adequacy. Plaintiff was silenced, denied the opportunity to present a defense, and sentenced to incarceration for conduct that does not constitute contempt under any reasonable interpretation of the law.

The cumulative 59 days of incarceration — resulting in the loss of two jobs and two homes — also raises serious concerns under the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment. The punishment was grossly disproportionate to the alleged misconduct: filing court motions and sending birthday messages. This disproportionality further supports the inference of retaliatory intent and entitles Plaintiff to compensatory and punitive damages under §1983.
