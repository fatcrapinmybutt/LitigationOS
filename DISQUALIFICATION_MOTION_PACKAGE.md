### ARGUMENT

#### A. Standard of Review

12. The question whether a judge should be disqualified is reviewed de novo when it presents a constitutional question. *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868, 881 (2009). The standard is objective: "whether, 'under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness,' the interest 'poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden if the guarantee of due process is to be adequately implemented.'" *Id.* at 883–84.

13. Under Michigan law, disqualification is warranted when a judge's conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired." *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975).

14. The movant must present "specific facts and reasons" rather than mere conclusions. *Cain v Michigan Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996). However, the facts "must be evaluated cumulatively, not in isolation." *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001).

#### B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

15. Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a), disqualification is required when the judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." This is a subjective standard: it asks whether the judge actually harbors bias. *Cain*, 451 Mich at 496.

16. **The Ex Parte Pattern Demonstrates Actual Bias.** Judge McNeill entered 24 ex parte orders — 18.26% of all orders — in proceedings involving Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights. Each ex parte order deprived Plaintiff of notice and the opportunity to be heard before the Court acted on matters affecting his relationship with L.D.W. The Michigan Court of Appeals has recognized that a pattern of ex parte conduct supports disqualification. *In re MKK*, 286 Mich App 546, 557 (2009) (holding that a family court judge's ex parte contacts warranted disqualification).

17. **The August 8, 2025 Cluster Is Dispositive.** The sequence of Albert Watson's statement to police on August 7 — that "they want this documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order" — followed by five ex parte orders from Judge McNeill on August 8, creates an inference that the Court's ex parte process was being used in coordination with Defendant's family. A reasonable factfinder would conclude that this coordination reflects actual bias. (Exhibits B, E.)

18. **94 Bias Violations Establish a Pattern.** Plaintiff has documented 94 specific incidents of bias — not conclusions, but discrete events reflected in the docket, transcripts, and court orders. These include granting Defendant's motions while denying Plaintiff's on the same issues; making adverse credibility findings based on ex parte submissions; and ruling for Defendant on virtually every contested issue. No single incident is relied upon in isolation; it is the cumulative pattern that compels disqualification. *Armstrong*, 248 Mich App at 597. (Exhibit C.)

19. **Prejudgment of the Ella Randall Fraud.** When Plaintiff presented evidence that Ella Randall committed fraud by misrepresenting her credentials, Judge McNeill dismissed the evidence as "nitpicking" without examining its substance. This is classic prejudgment — the Court had reached its conclusion and was unwilling to consider evidence that undermined it. A judge who dismisses material fraud evidence as "nitpicking" has formed an opinion adverse to the presenting party that cannot be changed by further evidence. (Exhibit F.)

#### C. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — Objective Perception of Bias

20. Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), disqualification is required when, "based on objective and reasonable perceptions," the judge has "(i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party" or "(ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard as set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct."

21. **The Objective Test.** The test is not whether the judge believes she is impartial. The test is whether "a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would harbor a reasonable doubt about the judge's impartiality." *Armstrong*, 248 Mich App at 597. The court must apply "a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness." *Caperton*, 556 US at 883.

22. **1,127 Violations Eliminate Any Doubt.** A reasonable person informed that a judge had accumulated 1,127 documented violations across 96 legal authorities — with 377 at critical severity — would not merely doubt the judge's impartiality; that person would conclude that impartiality was impossible. The sheer volume of violations, documented across two years of proceedings, establishes a pattern that no reasonable person could attribute to good-faith judicial error. (Exhibit C.)

23. **The 18.26% Ex Parte Rate Is Statistically Conclusive.** Normal judicial practice produces ex parte orders in approximately 3–5% of total orders. Judge McNeill's 18.26% rate is 3.65 times the expected rate. This statistical anomaly — standing alone — would satisfy the objective test under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). Combined with the other evidence, it is overwhelming. (Exhibit C.)

24. **Pattern of One-Sided Results.** On virtually every contested issue — custody, parenting time, child support, PPO enforcement, contempt, and credibility — Judge McNeill ruled in Defendant's favor and against Plaintiff. While adverse rulings alone do not establish bias, *Cain*, 451 Mich at 497, an unbroken pattern of one-sided results combined with systematic process violations and ex parte conduct satisfies the objective standard. *Armstrong*, 248 Mich App at 597.

#### D. Canon Violations Supporting Disqualification

25. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii) expressly provides that a judge must be disqualified when she "has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard as set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct." Judge McNeill's conduct violates three Canons:

26. **Canon 2 — Appearance of Impropriety.** Canon 2 requires that "[a] judge should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." A judge whose ex parte rate is 3.65 times normal, who has accumulated 1,127 documented violations, and who has ruled for one party on virtually every contested issue has created an appearance of impropriety that Canon 2 prohibits.

27. **Canon 3(A)(4) — Ex Parte Prohibition.** Canon 3(A)(4) provides that "[a] judge shall accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law." Judge McNeill's 169 documented ex parte communications and 24 ex parte orders constitute systematic violations of this Canon.

28. **Canon 3(B)(2) — Bias Prohibition.** Canon 3(B)(2) provides that "[a] judge shall not, in the performance of judicial duties, by words or conduct manifest bias or prejudice." The 94 documented bias incidents — each reflected in docket entries, transcripts, or orders — demonstrate that Judge McNeill has manifested bias in violation of this Canon.

#### F. Constitutional Due Process Requires Disqualification

31. In *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868 (2009), the United States Supreme Court held that the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires judicial disqualification when "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 872.

32. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) expressly incorporates the *Caperton* standard, requiring disqualification where there is "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton*."

33. The facts here satisfy the *Caperton* standard. The combination of (a) 24 ex parte orders at a rate 3.65× normal; (b) 94 documented bias incidents; (c) 169 ex parte communications; (d) 59 days of incarceration without due process protections; (e) 569+ days of parenting time denial; and (f) a pattern of ruling for Defendant on every contested issue creates a probability of actual bias that is constitutionally intolerable.

34. As the Supreme Court recognized in *Caperton*, due process "demarks only the outer boundaries of judicial disqualifications" and objective standards are necessary because "the judge may have combated the subconscious effects of bias on his or her judgment." 556 US at 882–83. Here, no subconscious analysis is needed — the bias is reflected in the objective record.

35. In *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347 (1975), the Michigan Supreme Court similarly held that due process requires disqualification where a judge's conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired." *Id.* at 351. The record here does not merely create a "perception" of impairment — it documents it exhaustively.

#### G. The Ella Randall Fraud Demonstrates Prejudgment

36. The Ella Randall fraud issue crystallizes Judge McNeill's prejudgment. Plaintiff presented documented evidence that Randall — whose assessment the Court relied upon for custody determinations — held herself out as a licensed social worker without proper credentials. This evidence, if credited, would undermine the factual foundation of the Court's custody rulings.

37. Judge McNeill's response — dismissing the evidence as "nitpicking" — reveals that the Court had already decided the custody outcome and was unwilling to reconsider that outcome regardless of the evidence presented. This is the definition of prejudgment: forming a conclusion before considering all relevant evidence. A judge who has prejudged the merits cannot preside impartially. *Cain*, 451 Mich at 497.

38. The *In re MKK* court recognized that in family law proceedings, where judicial decisions have profound effects on parent-child relationships, the standard for disqualification must account for the heightened stakes involved. 286 Mich App at 558. When a judge dismisses evidence of fraud affecting custody determinations as "nitpicking," the stakes demand disqualification.

### CONCLUSION

39. This is not a case of a litigant unhappy with adverse rulings. This is a case of documented, systematic, and statistically verifiable judicial conduct that falls outside any reasonable norm of impartial adjudication. The 1,127 documented violations, the 18.26% ex parte rate, the 94 bias incidents, the 169 ex parte communications, the 569+ days of denied parenting time, and the 59 days of incarceration without due process — all documented in the attached Exhibits — compel disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a), MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), and the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

40. Plaintiff respectfully requests that the chief judge grant this Motion and reassign these cases to a judge who can provide the fair and impartial proceeding that the Michigan and United States Constitutions demand.
