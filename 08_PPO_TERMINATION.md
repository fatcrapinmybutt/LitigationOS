### D. Addressing the Judicial Estoppel Argument

Respondent anticipates that Petitioner may argue judicial estoppel — that the guilty plea to PPO violations constitutes an admission that the PPO was valid and necessary, precluding this motion. This argument fails for three independent reasons:

1. **Different legal questions.** The guilty plea addressed whether Respondent *violated the terms* of the PPO. This motion addresses whether *continuing the PPO is necessary* in light of changed circumstances. These are distinct legal inquiries. A person may acknowledge violating an order while simultaneously arguing that the order is no longer necessary. *See People v. Likine*, 492 Mich 367 (2012) (guilty plea addresses elements of offense, not collateral consequences).

2. **MCL 600.2950(5) expressly provides for modification/termination.** The Legislature created a mechanism for PPO respondents to seek termination based on changed circumstances. If a guilty plea foreclosed all subsequent challenges, the statutory termination mechanism would be nullified — an absurd result. *See In re Certified Question (Bankey v. Storer Broadcasting)*, 432 Mich 438, 455 (1989) (courts should avoid statutory interpretations producing absurd results).

3. **Changed circumstances since the plea.** Even accepting the guilty plea at face value, circumstances have materially changed: 230+ days of zero contact, no new allegations, favorable professional evaluation, and demonstrated compliance. The plea reflects past conduct; this motion addresses present circumstances.

### E. Legal Standard for Termination

Michigan law establishes that a PPO is appropriate only where the petitioner demonstrates a reasonable apprehension of violence or threatening behavior. *Pickering v Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694 (2002); *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 (2008).

Where, as here, the petitioner cannot demonstrate any reasonable apprehension based on objective evidence — and where the sole basis for the PPO consists of fabricated allegations and innocuous communications through a court-approved application — termination is required.

MCR 3.707(B) further requires that a respondent receive notice and opportunity to be heard before a PPO is extended. The December 4, 2024 extension occurred without prior notice to Respondent, who was notified only on December 5, 2024 — after the extension was already entered.

3. **The PPO functions as an end-run around custody modification procedures.** Michigan law requires specific findings under MCL 722.27 and the best-interest factors of MCL 722.23 before custody may be modified. ## 4. PART II: MOTION TO EXPUNGE PPO FROM RECORD

### A. Statutory Authority

MCL 600.2950(5) authorizes the court to modify or rescind a PPO. Where a PPO was obtained through fraud or misrepresentation, expungement is the appropriate remedy to prevent continuing injustice to the respondent.

Additionally, **MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c)** provides independent authority for relief from judgment where the judgment was obtained through "fraud (whether heretofore denominated intrinsic or extrinsic), misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party." As demonstrated in Part I, Section B, every foundational allegation supporting this PPO has been affirmatively disproven. The PPO was procured through fraud and is subject to vacatur under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c), independent of the changed-circumstances analysis under MCL 600.2950(5).

### A-1. Access to Courts — The $250 Filing Bond

To the extent this Court or Petitioner argues that Respondent's challenge is procedurally barred or conditioned on payment of a filing bond, the United States Supreme Court has held that financial barriers to court access in cases involving fundamental rights violate due process. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971). A $250 filing bond requirement — applied to an indigent *pro se* litigant seeking to challenge a PPO that was obtained through fraud and has resulted in 59 days of incarceration and 230+ days of separation from his child — would constitute an unconstitutional barrier to court access. Respondent's fundamental parental rights under *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000), cannot be conditioned on the ability to pay.

### B. Grounds for Expungement

Expungement of the PPO is warranted because:

1. **The PPO was obtained through fraud and misrepresentation.** The three foundational allegations — arsenic poisoning, physical violence, and surveillance — were fabricated. No credible evidence supported any of them at the time of issuance, and none has emerged since.

2. **The PPO was issued without due process.** The order was issued ex parte on the same day as the petition, without notice to Respondent and without an evidentiary hearing. This violated Respondent's due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment and Const 1963, art 1, § 17.

3. **The PPO has been maintained through abuse of process.** Petitioner filed six or more show cause motions, not to protect her safety, but to leverage the PPO as a tactical weapon in the custody dispute. The escalating sentences — from zero days to 14 days to 45 days — reflect not increasing danger but increasing judicial punishment for a father attempting to communicate with his child.

4. **126 documented procedural violations.** The litigation record documents 126 judicial violations specific to PPO procedure under MCL 600.2950 and MCL 600.2950a, forming part of a broader pattern of procedural failures documented across both cases. These procedural failures render the orders void or voidable.

5. **Continuing prejudice.** The PPO on Respondent's record causes ongoing harm to his reputation, employment prospects, housing opportunities, and — most critically — his custody case. So long as the PPO exists, it creates a false narrative of dangerousness that is unsupported by fact.

### C. The PPO Is Void Ab Initio

An ex parte PPO entered without compliance with the mandatory procedural requirements of MCR 3.708 is void *ab initio*. *See* MCR 3.708(B) (requiring verified petition with specific acts, dates, and evidence). A void order has no legal force or effect and cannot serve as lawful authority for restricting parental contact.

The 126 documented procedural violations demonstrate a systematic pattern of non-compliance that renders these orders unenforceable as a matter of law. Respondent is entitled to expungement as a matter of right.
