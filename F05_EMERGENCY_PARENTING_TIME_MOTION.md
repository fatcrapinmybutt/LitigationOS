## IV. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. The Fundamental Right to Parent

15. The United States Supreme Court has recognized the right of parents to the care, custody, and control of their children as "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

16. This fundamental right is protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and may not be infringed without, at minimum, notice, a hearing, and specific factual findings by a neutral arbiter. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753–54 (1982).

17. The complete suspension of all parenting time constitutes a *de facto* termination of parental rights. Such an extreme deprivation demands the highest procedural protections. *See M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102, 116–17 (1996) (state may not condition fundamental right on ability to pay).

### B. The Ex Parte Orders Violated Mandatory Procedures

18. MCR 3.207(C)(5) mandates that contested custody and parenting time motions be heard "within 14 days of filing." Throughout this case, **zero** hearings were held within this mandatory timeframe.

19. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that parenting time shall be granted "in accordance with the best interests of the child" and may be restricted only upon specific findings that parenting time "would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." No such findings were made.

20. When a modification effectively alters an established custodial environment — as the complete suspension of parenting time indisputably did — the court must apply the clear-and-convincing evidence standard and make written findings on all twelve MCL 722.23 factors. *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich. 81, 92 (2010); *Lieberman v. Orr*, 319 Mich. App. 68, 78–87 (2017). Judge McNeill made no such findings.

### C. Superintending Control Is the Only Adequate Remedy

21. MCR 7.306(B) authorizes this Court to exercise superintending control where a lower court has "fail[ed] to act" or has "act[ed] outside its authority." Both conditions are met here:

 a. **Failure to act**: Judge McNeill has refused to schedule hearings on Plaintiff's motions, refused to consider the HealthWest evaluation, and imposed a blanket filing bar.

 b. **Action outside authority**: Ex parte orders entered without notice, hearing, or mandatory findings exceed the court's authority under MCR 3.207 and MCL 722.27a.

22. There is no adequate remedy at law. The presiding judge is the violator. The chief judge is conflicted. The entire circuit is compromised. Only this Court's plenary authority over all Michigan courts can provide the relief Plaintiff and his child require.

### D. Irreparable Harm Demands Immediate Action

23. Every day of continued separation constitutes a new and distinct constitutional injury. At **248** days and counting, the harm to both Plaintiff and L.D.W. is severe and accelerating:

 a. L.D.W. is at the most critical stage of attachment development (ages 2-4). Prolonged separation from a bonded parent causes lasting psychological harm that no later reconciliation can fully repair.
