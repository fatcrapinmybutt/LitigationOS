# EXHIBIT C-9: Emily Watson Recantation

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-9
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC 1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit is the NSPD police report from Case No. 2023-08121, documenting a statement made by Defendant Emily A. Watson to law enforcement officers on October 13, 2023. In this recorded statement, Emily A. Watson told police that "nothing was physical" with respect to her interactions with Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors. This admission directly contradicts the allegations of physical violence that formed the basis of the Personal Protection Order (PPO) she filed just two days later, on October 15, 2023, in Case No. 2023-5907-PP.

The two-day timeline is critical. On October 13, 2023, Emily A. Watson told law enforcement, under circumstances where she had every incentive to report any physical violence, that nothing physical had occurred. Forty-eight hours later, she filed a PPO petition alleging conduct sufficiently threatening to warrant emergency judicial intervention. The recantation, documented in an official police report, establishes that the PPO petition was filed based on allegations that the petitioner herself had contradicted to law enforcement just two days prior.

The PPO became a foundational instrument in the subsequent deprivation of Plaintiff's parental rights. It was used to restrict Plaintiff's contact with his child, to justify increased scrutiny and adverse assumptions in the custody proceedings, and ultimately to support the ex parte orders that eliminated Plaintiff's custody entirely. The recantation thus undermines the entire evidentiary chain that led to the deprivation of Plaintiff's constitutional rights.

## Legal Significance

The recantation is significant to the Section 1983 claim in multiple respects. First, it establishes that the PPO, which became a cornerstone of the state court proceedings against Plaintiff, was obtained through misrepresentation. When a court order is obtained through fraud or material misrepresentation, the order lacks legitimacy, and actions taken in reliance on that order are tainted by the underlying fraud. The PPO cannot serve as a legitimate basis for the deprivation of constitutional rights when the petitioner herself told police, just 48 hours before filing, that the factual predicate for the PPO did not exist.

Second, the recantation supports the conspiracy claim under Section 1983. The filing of a PPO based on allegations the petitioner knew to be false, followed by the use of that PPO as leverage in custody proceedings, constitutes a scheme to deprive Plaintiff of his constitutional rights through the abuse of judicial process. When private parties use false allegations to obtain court orders that deprive another person of fundamental rights, and when state actors enforce those orders without independent inquiry, all participants in the scheme are liable under Section 1983. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980).

Third, the recantation is powerful impeachment evidence that goes to the credibility of Defendant Emily A. Watson across all proceedings. A party who makes contradictory statements to police and to the court within a 48-hour window demonstrates the type of willingness to manipulate official proceedings that undermines the reliability of all her representations to the court.

## Key Evidence

- **October 13, 2023:** Emily A. Watson tells NSPD officers: "nothing was physical"
- **NSPD Case No. 2023-08121:** Official police report documenting the statement
- **October 15, 2023:** Two days later, Emily A. Watson files PPO petition in Case No. 2023-5907-PP
- **48-hour contradiction:** Statement to police directly contradicts allegations in PPO petition filed 48 hours later
- **PPO as custody weapon:** The PPO was subsequently used as a basis for restricting Plaintiff's parental rights
- **Chain of deprivation:** False PPO allegations led to restricted contact, which led to adverse custody findings, which led to ex parte orders, which led to complete elimination of parental rights
- **No physical evidence:** Consistent with the nine NSPD contacts (Exhibit C-8) producing zero arrests and zero charges

## Constitutional Implications

The recantation evidence directly implicates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. When a court order that forms the foundation of subsequent deprivation proceedings is obtained through statements that the petitioner herself contradicted to law enforcement, the entire chain of proceedings built upon that order is constitutionally infirm. Due process requires that judicial proceedings be based on reliable evidence; proceedings built on a foundation of admitted falsehoods cannot satisfy this requirement.

The recantation also supports a substantive due process claim. The use of knowingly false allegations to obtain court orders that deprive a parent of fundamental rights is the type of conduct that "shocks the conscience" and violates substantive due process. *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833, 846 (1998). A scheme to manipulate the judicial system through contradictory statements, made to police one day and to the court the next, demonstrates a deliberate intent to use governmental power to achieve an unconstitutional objective: the destruction of a parent-child relationship through fraud.
