## 1. STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

### 42 USC §1983 — Text
> "Every person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State... subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen of the United States... to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured in an action at law, suit in equity, or other proper proceeding for redress..."

### Elements Required (4 elements):
1. **Person** — defendant must be a "person" (individuals yes; state entities in official capacity — 11th Amendment issues)
2. **Under color of state law** — acting pursuant to state authority
3. **Subjects or causes to be subjected** — causation
4. **Deprivation of constitutional right** — specific right identified

### Companion Statutes:
- **42 USC §1985(3)** — Conspiracy to interfere with civil rights
- **42 USC §1986** — Neglect to prevent conspiracy
- **42 USC §1988** — Attorney fees for prevailing party (pro se can recover costs but not "attorney fees")
- **28 USC §1343** — Federal jurisdiction for civil rights claims
- **28 USC §1331** — Federal question jurisdiction

## 3. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY ANALYSIS

### Judge Jenny L. McNeill — Immunity Framework

**General Rule:** Judges have absolute immunity for judicial acts within their jurisdiction.

**Two Exceptions (Stump v. Sparkman / Mireles v. Waco):**

| Exception | Test | Application to McNeill |
|-----------|------|----------------------|
| **Non-judicial acts** | Was the act judicial in nature? (Factors: normal judicial function, occurred in courtroom, arose from judge-litigant relationship, involved exercise of judicial authority) | McNeill's **ex parte communications** with Watson/Barnes, **off-record evidence handling** (USB recording), and **administrative scheduling manipulation** may qualify as non-judicial |
| **Clear absence of all jurisdiction** | Did the judge have ANY jurisdiction over the matter? (This is an extremely high bar — "complete absence," not just "excess") | This is harder — McNeill has general jurisdiction over family cases. However, actions taken **after disqualification was properly raised** may approach this threshold |

### Key Strategy Points:
1. **Focus on conspiracy theory** — Per *Dennis v. Sparks*, even if McNeill retains immunity, **the private co-conspirators (Watson, Berry, Barnes) lose theirs** if conspiracy is established
2. **Seek declaratory/injunctive relief** — Per *Pulliam v. Allen*, injunctive relief is available against judges (subject to 1996 amendment requiring violated declaratory decree or unavailability of declaratory relief)
3. **Plead non-judicial acts specifically** — Ex parte communications, off-record evidence handling, refusal to review exculpatory evidence = arguably non-judicial
4. **Plead in individual capacity only** — Avoids 11th Amendment sovereign immunity

### Analysis of McNeill's Conduct as Non-Judicial:
- **Ex parte order suspending parenting time** without verified pleading or specific facts (MCR 3.207 violation) → arguably without proper procedural foundation
- **Off-record handling of USB recording and mental health evaluation** → administrative, not judicial
- **Refusal to review exculpatory photographs** → arguably abdication of judicial function
- **Acceptance of hearsay over sworn testimony** → while this is judicial, the *pattern* combined with ex parte conduct supports conspiracy inference

## 4. DOMESTIC RELATIONS EXCEPTION

### Why It Does NOT Bar This Action

**The Law:**
- *Ankenbrandt v. Richards*, 504 U.S. 689 (1992): DRE limits federal jurisdiction ONLY in **diversity cases** involving issuance of divorce, alimony, or child custody decrees
- It does **NOT** preclude federal question jurisdiction (§1983)

**Sixth Circuit Confirmation:**
- *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279 (6th Cir. 1998): **"The domestic relations exception is not a bar to Section 1983 claims."** Due process and equal protection claims connected to family law matters arise independently of any request for a new decree.
- *Alexander v. Rosen*, 804 F.3d 1203 (6th Cir. 2015): DRE applies only to a "narrow range" of cases — does not deprive federal courts of jurisdiction unless plaintiff "positively sues in federal court for divorce, alimony, or child custody."
- *Doe v. Claiborne County*, 103 F.3d 495 (6th Cir. 1996): Federal courts can hear §1983 claims arising from family court proceedings.

**Application to Our Case:**
- We are NOT seeking a custody decree, divorce, or alimony
- We ARE seeking damages and declaratory relief for **independent constitutional violations** (due process, equal protection, First Amendment)
- The §1983 claims arise from **how** the proceedings were conducted (process failures), not from the substance of custody decisions
- This is precisely the type of claim *Catz* held is actionable

**Anticipated Defense Language to Counter:**
> "Defendants will argue the DRE bars this action. This argument fails. The Supreme Court in *Ankenbrandt*, 504 U.S. at 693-94, and the Sixth Circuit in *Catz*, 142 F.3d at 291, have squarely held that the DRE does not bar federal question jurisdiction under §1983. Plaintiff does not seek a custody determination — Plaintiff seeks redress for constitutional violations in how proceedings were conducted."

## 5. ROOKER-FELDMAN DOCTRINE

### Why It Does NOT Apply

**The Law:**
- *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries*, 544 U.S. 280 (2005): Rooker-Feldman applies ONLY when "the losing party in state court filed suit in federal court after the state proceedings ended, complaining of an injury caused by the state court judgment and seeking its review and rejection."

**Key Limitations:**
1. We are NOT seeking review or reversal of a state court judgment
2. We are raising **independent constitutional claims** about the *process* used
3. The §1983 claims allege violations of federal rights that exist independently of any state court decision
4. Per *Evans v. Cordray* (6th Cir.), independent constitutional challenges are not barred even when related to state litigation

**Application:**
- Our claims challenge the **procedures** (ex parte orders, denial of hearings, refusal to consider evidence), NOT the custody outcome itself
- Even if the state court proceedings concluded, our federal claims are **independent** — they would exist regardless of who won custody
- Per *Exxon Mobil*, the doctrine is "confined to... cases brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments"

**Complaint Language:**
> "This action does not seek review or reversal of any state court judgment. Plaintiff's claims arise from independent constitutional violations — deprivation of due process, ex parte proceedings, denial of meaningful opportunity to be heard, and conspiracy to deprive civil rights — that exist independently of any state court custody determination."

## 6. YOUNGER ABSTENTION

### Why It Does NOT Apply

**Three Exceptions to Younger:**
1. **Bad faith or harassment** — state proceedings conducted in bad faith
2. **Patently unconstitutional statute** — underlying law is flagrantly unconstitutional
3. **Inadequate state forum** — no adequate opportunity to raise federal claims

**Application:**
- *Sprint Communications v. Jacobs*, 571 U.S. 69 (2013): Younger abstention is limited to three categories: (1) ongoing state criminal prosecutions, (2) certain civil enforcement proceedings, and (3) civil proceedings involving orders uniquely in furtherance of state courts' ability to perform judicial functions
- Family custody proceedings **do not fit** any of these three categories per *Sprint*
- Even if Younger applied, the **bad faith exception** is available where proceedings are conducted through ex parte orders, denial of hearings, and conspiracy

**Key Sixth Circuit Point:**
- If state proceedings are concluded, Younger does not apply at all (it only applies to *ongoing* proceedings)
- Our claims relate to completed proceedings — damages action, not injunction against ongoing case

## 7. CONSPIRACY UNDER §1983 AND §1985

### §1983 Conspiracy (Dennis v. Sparks Framework)

**Elements:**
1. **Agreement** — a "meeting of the minds" between private parties and state actor to violate federal rights
2. **State action** — at least one conspirator acted under color of state law
3. **Overt acts** — specific acts in furtherance of the conspiracy
4. **Deprivation** — actual deprivation of a constitutional right resulted

**Critical Holding — Dennis v. Sparks, 449 U.S. 24 (1980):**
> "Private persons who conspire with state officials to deprive others of federally protected rights can be held liable under §1983. Judicial immunity for the state actor does NOT extend to private co-conspirators."

**Application to Our Defendants:**

| Conspirator | Role | Overt Acts | State Action Link |
|-------------|------|------------|-------------------|
| **McNeill** (state actor) | Judge issuing orders | Ex parte orders, denial of hearings, refusal of evidence | Under color of judicial authority |
| **Watson** (private party) | Primary beneficiary | Filing false/misleading ex parte motions, coordinating with Barnes | Conspiracy with McNeill converts to state action per *Dennis* |
| **Berry** (private party) | Watson's partner, non-attorney acting in litigation | Presence at hearings, coordination with Watson's strategy | Conspiracy with McNeill converts to state action |
| **Barnes** (private party, attorney) | Watson's attorney who coordinated ex parte submissions | Filing ex parte motions, potentially coordinating with McNeill off-record | Attorney as conduit for conspiracy; *Dennis* applies |
| **Rusco** (state actor, FOC) | FOC officer executing biased recommendations | Biased recommendations without investigation, failure to follow FOC procedures | Under color of FOC authority |

### §1985(3) Conspiracy

**Additional Elements Beyond §1983:**
1. All §1983 conspiracy elements PLUS
2. **Class-based animus** — the conspiracy must be motivated by discriminatory animus based on race, gender, or other protected class
3. **Note:** Some circuits have limited §1985(3) to race-based animus; Sixth Circuit recognizes gender-based animus

**Application:**
- §1985(3) is viable if we can plead gender-based animus (father discriminated against based on sex in custody proceedings)
- Alternatively, plead as §1983 conspiracy (no class-based animus required) which is the stronger claim

### Conspiracy Pleading Requirements (Post-Iqbal):
- **DO:** Allege specific dates, communications, and coordination
- **DO:** Identify specific overt acts by each conspirator
- **DO:** Show how the private parties' actions were intertwined with state action
- **DON'T:** Make conclusory statements like "defendants conspired"
- **DON'T:** Rely on parallel conduct alone without plus factors

## 8. FIRST AMENDMENT RETALIATION

### Prima Facie Elements (Sixth Circuit — *Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*)

1. **Protected activity** — plaintiff engaged in constitutionally protected conduct
2. **Adverse action** — government took action that would deter a person of ordinary firmness
3. **Causation** — adverse action was motivated at least in part by the protected activity

### Application to Our Case:

| Element | Facts |
|---------|-------|
| **Protected Activity** | Andrew filed complaints against Judge McNeill with the Judicial Tenure Commission; filed motions for disqualification; publicly criticized court proceedings; exercised right to petition government for redress |
| **Adverse Action** | After JTC complaint and disqualification motion: (1) increased restrictions on parenting time, (2) ex parte orders, (3) refusal to consider exculpatory evidence, (4) hostile treatment in proceedings |
| **Causation** | Temporal proximity between filing complaints and adverse orders; pattern of increasing hostility correlating with protected activity; departure from normal judicial procedures |

### Key Timing Evidence:
- Document specific dates of JTC complaints/disqualification motions
- Document specific dates of adverse orders that followed
- Close temporal proximity creates inference of causation

### Judicial Immunity Issue for Retaliation:
- Judges are typically immune for judicial acts even if retaliatory
- However, if retaliation manifests through **non-judicial acts** or **conspiracy with private parties**, immunity may not apply
- Strategy: Frame retaliation through the conspiracy theory — private defendants are not immune even if McNeill is

## 10. IQBAL/TWOMBLY PLEADING STANDARDS

### What the Complaint MUST Contain:

**Step 1 — Strip out legal conclusions:**
- Court identifies and disregards conclusory allegations
- "Defendants violated plaintiff's due process rights" = conclusory (INSUFFICIENT)

**Step 2 — Evaluate remaining factual allegations for plausibility:**
- Remaining facts must make the claim "plausible on its face"
- Plausible = more than possible, less than probable
- Court uses "judicial experience and common sense"

### Practical Pleading Rules for Our Complaint:

| DO | DON'T |
|----|-------|
| Allege **specific dates** of each constitutional violation | Use vague time references ("on or about") |
| Name **each defendant** and their specific conduct | Use group pleading ("defendants acted") |
| Describe **what** each defendant did and **how** it violated rights | State bare legal conclusions |
| Cite **specific constitutional provisions** violated | Just say "civil rights" generally |
| Include **specific evidence** references | Make unsupported factual claims |
| Show **personal involvement** of each defendant | Rely on respondeat superior |
| Allege **specific harm** suffered | Make general "injury" allegations |

### Pro Se Liberal Construction:
- *Haines v. Kerner*, 404 U.S. 519 (1972): Pro se pleadings held to less stringent standards
- *Erickson v. Pardus*, 551 U.S. 89 (2007): Need not comply with formal rules
- **BUT** — still must meet Iqbal/Twombly plausibility threshold
- Court should construe pro se complaint liberally and give opportunity to amend before dismissal

## 12. DAMAGES FRAMEWORK

### A. Compensatory Damages
| Category | Description | Basis |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Emotional distress | Anxiety, depression, trauma from parental separation | Well-established in §1983 |
| Loss of parental relationship | Interference with parent-child bond | Constitutional injury |
| Economic losses | Legal fees, lost income, travel costs | Out-of-pocket damages |
| Reputational harm | False implications from ex parte proceedings | Stigma-plus doctrine |

### B. Punitive Damages
- Available against **individual defendants** (not municipalities)
- Standard: "reckless or callous indifference to federally protected rights"
- McNeill: Pattern of ex parte orders, refusal of evidence = reckless disregard
- Watson/Berry/Barnes: Active conspiracy = callous indifference
- Rusco: Biased recommendations without investigation = reckless disregard

### C. Declaratory Relief
- Declare that defendants' conduct violated Plaintiff's 1st and 14th Amendment rights
- Declare that the ex parte proceedings were constitutionally deficient

### D. Injunctive Relief (Limited for Judges)
- Per 1996 amendment to §1983: injunctive relief against judges available ONLY if declaratory decree was violated or declaratory relief was unavailable
- Strategy: Seek declaratory relief first, then injunctive if violated

### E. Attorney Fees (§1988)
- Pro se litigants generally CANNOT recover attorney fees under §1988
- **BUT** can recover costs and reasonable litigation expenses
- If pro bono counsel is later retained, fees become recoverable

### Strategic Recommendations:

1. **Lead with conspiracy count** — This is the strongest theory because it:
   - Makes private parties (Watson, Berry, Barnes) liable under §1983
   - Circumvents McNeill's judicial immunity for the co-conspirators
   - Creates fullest damages exposure
   
2. **Plead detailed facts** — Every paragraph should contain who, what, when, where
   
3. **Front-load the DRE/Rooker-Feldman rebuttal** — Address in the jurisdictional section to prevent early dismissal motion

4. **Emphasize pro se status** — *Haines v. Kerner* and *Erickson v. Pardus* require liberal construction

5. **Request jury trial** — Damages claims are triable to jury; juries are more sympathetic to pro se civil rights plaintiffs than judges

6. **Include verification** — Verified complaint carries more weight and can serve as evidence
