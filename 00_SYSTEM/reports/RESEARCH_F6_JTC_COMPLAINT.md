## 2. JTC COMPLAINT PROCEDURE

### 2.1 Jurisdictional Scope

The JTC has jurisdiction over **Michigan state court judges, magistrates, and referees only**. It does not have jurisdiction over federal judges, administrative law judges, or attorneys acting in non-judicial capacity. Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, as a sitting judge of the 14th Circuit Court (Family Division), falls squarely within JTC jurisdiction.

### 2.2 Who May File

**Any person** may file a complaint (called a "Request for Investigation") with the JTC. The complainant need not be an attorney or party to the case. The JTC may also initiate investigations on its own based on media reports, court records, or referrals from other agencies.

**Andrew James Pigors** has standing as a party-litigant in the underlying case (2024-001507-DC) with direct, first-hand knowledge of the alleged judicial misconduct.

### 2.3 The "Request for Investigation" Form

The official form is the **Request for Investigation (RFI)**, available as a downloadable PDF from the JTC website. The form contains four mandatory sections:

#### Section I — Grievant Information
- Full legal name of complainant
- Complete mailing address
- Daytime and evening telephone numbers
- A second grievant may be included (maximum 2 per form), each requiring separate notarization

#### Section II — Judge/Judicial Officer Information
- Full name and title of the respondent judge: **Hon. Jenny L. McNeill**
- Court or tribunal: **14th Circuit Court, Family Division**
- County: **Muskegon County, Michigan**
- Case name and number: **Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC** (also 2023-5907-PP for PPO matters)
- Date(s) and time(s) of alleged misconduct
- Complainant's relationship to the case (party-litigant/father)

#### Section III — Allegations
- Detailed, specific factual statement describing each act of misconduct
- Additional pages may be attached
- **Supporting documents** — copies only (never originals): orders, transcripts, exhibits
- If transcripts are included, include a copy of the front page of each relevant volume

#### Section IV — Notarization
- Complainant's signature executed **in the presence of a notary public**
- Notary must attest to the truthfulness of statements
- If two grievants, each must sign and be separately notarized

### 2.5 Investigation Timeline

| Phase | Timeline | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Receipt** | Immediate | JTC acknowledges receipt in writing |
| **Staff Review** | Several weeks | Staff screens for completeness, jurisdiction, and facial merit |
| **Preliminary Investigation** | Weeks to months | Staff investigates: collects documents, reviews court records, contacts witnesses |
| **Commission Consideration** | Quarterly meetings | Staff reports to full Commission, which votes on further action |
| **Formal Complaint** | If cause found | JTC issues formal complaint; respondent judge must answer |
| **Hearing** | Months | Evidentiary hearing before appointed master |
| **Master's Report** | After hearing | Findings of fact and conclusions of law |
| **Commission Recommendation** | After report | Commission recommends sanction to Supreme Court |
| **Supreme Court Decision** | Final | Supreme Court issues final order |

**Total estimated timeline:** 6 months to 2+ years for complex cases. There is no statutory deadline. The JTC states it acts "as expeditiously as possible."

### 2.6 Confidentiality

- All complaints and investigations are **confidential** until:
  - A formal complaint is filed by the JTC, OR
  - The Commission makes a public recommendation to the Supreme Court
- The complainant is notified in writing of the disposition
- The JTC does not provide updates during the investigation
- **MCR 9.221** grants absolute immunity from civil suit to complainants and witnesses

### 2.7 What the JTC CANNOT Do

- **Cannot reverse or modify court orders** — the JTC addresses conduct, not legal error
- **Cannot provide legal advice** to complainants
- **Cannot serve as an appellate remedy** — the complaint must allege ethical misconduct, not mere disagreement with rulings

## 3. EVIDENCE STANDARDS

### 3.1 Burden of Proof

The **JTC's disciplinary counsel** bears the burden of proof at hearing. Per **MCR 9.200 et seq.**, the standard is:

> **Preponderance of the Evidence** — The evidence must show that the alleged misconduct is **more likely than not** to have occurred.

This is the **lowest civil standard** — significantly lower than "clear and convincing evidence" (used in termination of parental rights) and far lower than "beyond a reasonable doubt" (criminal cases).

| Standard | Threshold | Used by JTC? |
|----------|-----------|:------------:|
| Preponderance of Evidence | >50% likelihood | **YES** |
| Clear and Convincing | Highly probable | No |
| Beyond Reasonable Doubt | No reasonable doubt | No |

**Strategic significance:** Our case has **1,127 documented violations** across multiple categories, many with documentary evidence (orders, transcripts, filings). A preponderance standard makes this case exceptionally strong. Even if some incidents are disputed, the sheer volume and documentary trail overwhelmingly meets this threshold.

### 3.2 Evidence Types Accepted

| Evidence Type | Accepted? | Notes |
|---------------|:---------:|-------|
| Court orders and docket entries | ✅ | Primary documentary evidence |
| Hearing transcripts | ✅ | Include cover page of each volume |
| Written filings and motions | ✅ | All parties' filings relevant |
| Correspondence | ✅ | Letters, emails related to judicial conduct |
| Witness statements | ✅ | May be notarized for credibility |
| Audio/video recordings | ✅ | Submit copies (USB drives, CDs) |
| Court records and registers of actions | ✅ | From clerk's office |
| Expert declarations | ✅ | Regarding judicial standards |
| Prior JTC complaints against same judge | ✅ | JTC can review its own files |
| Database/analytical summaries | ⚠️ | Supplementary — must be tied to primary documents |
| Hearsay | ⚠️ | Formal rules of evidence apply at hearing stage; admissible at investigation stage |

### 3.3 Evidence Authentication

All documentary evidence should be authenticated where possible:
- Court orders: certified copies from the clerk
- Transcripts: official court reporter copies
- Filings: file-stamped copies from the clerk
- FOC records: FOIA requests if needed

### 3.4 Cumulative Evidence Doctrine

**In re Brennan, 504 Mich 80 (2019)** established that the **"cumulative effect"** of multiple violations, even if some individually might warrant only lesser sanctions, can justify the most severe discipline including removal. This doctrine is directly applicable to our case with 1,127 documented violations.

## 5. EX PARTE VIOLATIONS ANALYSIS

### 5.1 The Ex Parte Prohibition

Canon 3A(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct **categorically prohibits** a judge from:
1. **Initiating** ex parte communications about pending cases
2. **Permitting** such communications to occur
3. **Considering** information received ex parte

The prohibition extends to communications through intermediaries, including court staff, FOC personnel (when acting as advocates rather than neutral referees), and any other third parties.

### 5.2 Limited Exceptions

The only exceptions to the ex parte prohibition are:
- Scheduling or administrative matters that do not affect substantial rights
- Communications expressly authorized by law (e.g., emergency ex parte orders under specific statutory authority with immediate post-order notice)
- Communications with court personnel whose function requires such contact (e.g., law clerks, but NOT parties or their proxies)

### 5.3 FOC Communications — Critical Distinction

Per **Ethics Opinion JI-159 (2025)**, communications between a judge and court-employed referees (FOC) are **not considered ex parte** only when:
- The referee acts as **neutral court personnel**, not as an advocate
- The communication maintains proper **neutrality and disclosure to both parties**
- The process ensures both parties receive copies of all recommendations

**However**, if FOC communications:
- Convey one party's position without the other party's knowledge (**VIOLATION**)
- Contain substantive ex parte recommendations without disclosure (**VIOLATION**)
- Are used to circumvent adversarial process requirements (**VIOLATION**)

Per **Ethics Opinion JI-134**, a judge has an affirmative duty to instruct staff about the ex parte prohibition and is **responsible for improper ex parte contacts by staff**.

## 6. PATTERN OF BIAS ANALYSIS

### 6.1 Why "Pattern" Matters

Under Michigan JTC jurisprudence, establishing a **pattern of misconduct** transforms the analysis in several critical ways:

1. **Elevated sanctions** — A pattern justifies more severe discipline than isolated incidents (*In re Brennan*: 17 counts → removal)
2. **Cumulative effect doctrine** — Even individually minor violations, when part of a pattern, can constitute grounds for removal (*In re Ferrara*, 458 Mich 350)
3. **Rebuttal of "judicial discretion" defense** — A pattern negates the argument that individual rulings reflect legitimate judicial discretion
4. **Demonstration of unfitness** — Per Art. VI, § 30(2), persistent failure to perform duties and conduct "clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice" are constitutional grounds for removal

### 6.2 Presenting 1,127 Violations as a Pattern

The documented violations should be organized to demonstrate pattern through:

#### A. Temporal Distribution
- Map violations across the timeline of proceedings
- Show consistent, ongoing misconduct rather than isolated incidents
- Demonstrate escalation over time (if applicable)

#### B. Category Clustering
- **6 major violation categories** with double-digit or triple-digit counts:
  - MCR 2.003 Disqualification: 167 (70 critical)
  - Procedural Misconduct: 161 (59 critical)
  - Ex Parte Violations: 150 (126 critical)
  - Credibility Failures: 51 (17 critical)
  - PPO Weaponization: 27 (9 critical)
  - Canon Violations: 23 combined (3 critical)

#### C. Actor Violation Analysis
- **5,242 total actor violations** attributed to McNeill
- Top categories demonstrate systemic nature:
  - Child welfare impact: 1,747 critical
  - Ex parte: 1,015 critical
  - Judicial bias: 395 critical

#### D. Harm Concentration

## 9. WINNING STRATEGIES FROM SUCCESSFUL JTC CASES

### 9.1 Lessons from *In re Brennan* (2019)

The most instructive modern Michigan JTC case provides a blueprint:

| Brennan Factor | Our Case Parallel |
|----------------|-------------------|
| 17 counts of misconduct | 1,127 documented violations across 6+ categories |
| Failure to disqualify | 167 MCR 2.003 disqualification violations |
| Ex parte improprieties | 150 ex parte violations (126 critical) |
| Destruction of evidence | Off-record evidence handling (USB, mental health eval) |
| False statements under oath | Credibility failures: 51 violations (17 critical) |
| Mistreatment of litigants | Procedural misconduct: 161 violations (59 critical) |
| Misuse of judicial authority | PPO weaponization: 27 violations (9 critical) |
| "Cumulative effect" justified removal | 1,127 cumulative violations — far exceeds Brennan's 17 counts |

### 9.2 Key Strategies That Lead to Discipline

Based on analysis of JTC annual reports and successful complaints:

**Strategy 1: Lead with Conduct, Not Rulings**
- Frame every allegation as a **conduct violation**, not a complaint about the legal outcome
- Focus on **how** the judge acted, not **what** the judge decided
- Example: "Judge entered order without notice or hearing" (conduct) rather than "Judge's custody order was wrong" (ruling)

**Strategy 2: Document Everything with Primary Sources**
- Every allegation backed by a court order, transcript, or official record
- No unsourced statistics or unverified claims
- Certified copies carry more weight than personal statements

**Strategy 3: Present a Pattern, Not Isolated Incidents**
- Organize chronologically to show escalation
- Cross-reference violations across categories to show interconnection
- Use the cumulative effect doctrine explicitly
- Cite *In re Brennan* for pattern-based removal authority

**Strategy 4: Be Specific and Factual**
- Date, time, courtroom, docket entry for every incident
- Name the specific Canon or Rule violated
- Quote the relevant standard, then describe the violating conduct
- Avoid emotional language — let the facts speak

**Strategy 5: Demonstrate Harm**
- Show concrete harm to litigant (loss of parenting time)
- Show harm to child welfare (separation from parent)
- Show harm to public confidence in judiciary
- Show harm to administration of justice

**Strategy 6: Professional Presentation**
- Typed, professionally formatted documents
- Clear exhibit indexing
- Logical organization
- Concise but thorough

**Strategy 7: Anticipate Defenses**
- Address "judicial discretion" defense proactively
- Distinguish between legitimate judicial decisions and ethical violations
- Show that the pattern transcends any single discretionary call

### 9.3 What Does NOT Work

| Approach | Why It Fails |
|----------|-------------|
| "Appeal-style" complaints | JTC is not an appellate court; challenging legal merit is dismissed at intake |
| Emotional, unspecific accusations | Without documentary support, complaints are not investigated |
| Complaints about a single ruling | Isolated incidents rarely result in discipline unless egregious |
| Unsupported allegations of conspiracy | Require specific, provable facts tied to Canon violations |
| Complaints about attorneys (not judges) | JTC has no jurisdiction; file with Attorney Grievance Commission instead |
| Excessive length without organization | Overwhelming volume without clear structure is counterproductive |

## 10. RISK ASSESSMENT

### 10.1 Potential Obstacles

| Obstacle | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|----------|:----------:|:------:|------------|
| Dismissed at intake as "appeal-style" | **MEDIUM** | High | Frame as conduct violations; lead with ex parte and procedural categories, not substantive rulings |
| Judicial discretion defense | **HIGH** | Medium | Counter with volume (1,127), pattern, and procedural (not substantive) nature of violations |
| Confidential resolution (private admonishment) | **MEDIUM** | Medium | Cannot control this; strongest possible complaint increases likelihood of public action |
| JTC resource constraints | **LOW** | Medium | Well-organized complaint reduces JTC workload, increasing likelihood of action |
| Retaliation concern | **LOW** | High | MCR 9.221 provides absolute immunity from civil liability for complainants |
| Long timeline | **HIGH** | Medium | File early; continue appellate and other remedies in parallel |
| JTC views violations as inflated | **MEDIUM** | High | Each violation must be tied to specific, verifiable incident with documentary support; avoid aggregate statistics without specific backing |

### 10.2 Potential Defenses by Respondent Judge

| Defense | Strength | Counter-Argument |
|---------|:--------:|-----------------|
| "Legitimate exercise of judicial discretion" | Strong for individual rulings | Overcome by pattern evidence — 1,127 violations across 6+ categories is not "discretion" |
| "Complainant is a disgruntled litigant" | Moderate | Counter with documentary evidence independent of complainant's characterization; transcripts and orders speak for themselves |
| "Ex parte communications were with neutral FOC staff" | Moderate | Distinguish between neutral referee communications (JI-159) and substantive ex parte contacts; show FOC acted as advocate |
| "Procedural requirements were followed" | Moderate | Point to specific instances where procedures were not followed: off-record evidence, lack of notice, refusal to review evidence |
| "No actual bias — rulings were based on evidence" | Moderate | Counter with pattern of one-directional rulings, refusal to consider exculpatory evidence, and off-record evidence handling |
| "Complainant failed to use appellate remedies" | Weak | JTC complaint and appellate remedies are independent; no exhaustion requirement |

### 10.3 Risks Specific to This Complaint

**Risk 1: Database Metrics May Appear Inflated**
- The 1,127 violation count and 5,242 actor violations come from a litigation database that may count related incidents separately
- **Mitigation:** Present a curated subset of the most serious, independently verifiable violations rather than raw database totals
- Focus on the top 20-50 most well-documented incidents with certified court records

**Risk 2: "Conspiracy" Category May Undermine Credibility**
- The 337 critical "conspiracy" actor violations may appear speculative to the JTC
- **Mitigation:** Reframe as "coordinated procedural irregularities" with specific documentary support; avoid the word "conspiracy" in the complaint

**Risk 3: Volume May Overwhelm JTC Staff**
- 1,127 violations presented en masse may appear unfocused
- **Mitigation:** Use the narrative to highlight 15-25 strongest incidents in detail; include the full database summary only as a supplementary exhibit

**Risk 4: Ongoing Litigation**
- Filing during active litigation may appear tactical
- **Mitigation:** Emphasize that the complaint addresses conduct violations, not litigation strategy; note that delay would allow continued harm

### 10.4 Risk Mitigation Framework

| Priority | Action |
|:--------:|--------|
| 1 | Lead with the 150 ex parte violations — strongest category, hardest to defend |
| 2 | Present the 167 disqualification violations as evidence of underlying bias |
| 3 | Use procedural misconduct (161 violations) to show systemic due process failures |
| 4 | Include PPO weaponization (27 violations) to show misuse of judicial authority |
| 5 | Cite cumulative Canon 2 and Canon 3 violations to establish ethical framework |
| 6 | Reserve the full 1,127-violation database as a supplementary exhibit, not the lead argument |

### 11.4 DB Query Templates for Exhibit Preparation

The following queries can extract the evidence foundation from `litigation_context.db`:

SELECT * FROM judicial_violations
WHERE violation_type LIKE '%ex_parte%' AND severity = 'critical'
ORDER BY date_observed;

SELECT * FROM judicial_violations
WHERE violation_type LIKE '%disqualif%' AND severity = 'critical'
ORDER BY date_observed;

-- Actor violations summary for McNeill
SELECT violation_category, severity, COUNT(*) as count
FROM actor_violations
WHERE actor_name LIKE '%McNeill%'
GROUP BY violation_category, severity
ORDER BY count DESC;

FROM judicial_violations
WHERE severity = 'critical'
ORDER BY date_observed;
