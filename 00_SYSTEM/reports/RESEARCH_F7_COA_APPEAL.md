## COA Jurisdiction and Appellate Framework {#coa-jurisdiction}

### Constitutional and Statutory Basis

The Michigan Court of Appeals is a constitutional court of appellate jurisdiction established under Michigan Constitution, Article VI, § 10. The court has jurisdiction to hear appeals from trial courts as prescribed by statute and court rule. See MCL 600.301-600.310 (establishing general appellate jurisdiction).

The COA is Michigan's intermediate appellate court with broad discretionary jurisdiction over both civil and criminal matters. Unlike the Michigan Supreme Court (which has limited mandatory jurisdiction and broad discretionary jurisdiction), the Court of Appeals automatically has jurisdiction over most trial court decisions.

**Key Jurisdictional Principles:**

- **Finality Requirement:** Generally, only final orders are appealable. MCR 7.202(a) defines a final order as one that disposes of all claims and issues in the case. Orders disposing of less than all claims may be appealable only if certified as final under MCR 2.604(A) or if they fall within specific exceptions for interlocutory appeals.

- **Subject Matter Jurisdiction:** The COA may hear appeals in civil and criminal matters, domestic relations cases, probate matters, and administrative agency review. Child custody determinations fall squarely within COA appellate jurisdiction and are frequently reviewed.

- **Notice of Appeal:** An appeal is initiated by filing a notice of appeal within the time specified in MCR 7.203. For civil cases, the deadline is generally 21 days after entry of the judgment, order, or decision, or within 21 days after the trial court exercises post-judgment jurisdiction by granting/denying a motion for reconsideration or new trial.

### Types of Appeals to COA

**Appeal of Right:** Most cases appealing trial court decisions go to the COA as appeals of right. The court must accept and hear these appeals. MCR 7.201(a) establishes the COA's appeal of right jurisdiction.

**Discretionary Appeals:** Certain matters may require leave to appeal to the COA, particularly interlocutory orders and decisions of administrative agencies. MCR 7.201(b) and (c) govern discretionary appeals.

## MCR 7.201-7.215: Procedural Requirements {#mcr-procedural}

### MCR 7.201: Jurisdiction of Court of Appeals

MCR 7.201(A) establishes that the Court of Appeals has jurisdiction of appeals in civil and criminal cases from trial courts, from the Workers' Compensation Appeal Board, and from certain administrative agencies.

**Subsection (B)** provides that appeals of right lie from:
- Final decisions of the trial court in civil and criminal cases
- Decisions of district courts in criminal cases
- Preliminary injunction decisions in certain circumstances
- Decisions of the Workers' Compensation Appeal Board

**Subsection (C)** establishes discretionary appeal jurisdiction for:
- Interlocutory orders
- Orders granting or denying discovery
- Decisions of administrative agencies

**Critical for Andrew's case:** His case involves a final order regarding custody/parenting time from the trial court, which establishes appeal of right jurisdiction in the COA.

### MCR 7.202: Decisions Appealable Without Leave

MCR 7.202(A) clarifies that a party may appeal final decisions without permission. A decision is final if it:
1. Disposes of all parties' claims
2. Adjudicates the rights and liabilities of all parties to the action
3. Leaves nothing for future determination by the trial court

**Custody Orders as Final Judgments:** Family court decisions awarding or modifying custody/parenting time are typically treated as final orders, even if other issues remain (such as child support or attorney fees), because the custody issue is separately and finally determined.

### MCR 7.203: Time for Service and Filing of Notice of Appeal

**Filing Deadline:** The notice of appeal must be filed with the trial court within:
- 21 days after entry of the judgment, order, or decision being appealed
- OR if a timely motion for reconsideration (MCR 2.119) or motion for new trial (MCR 2.610) is filed, within 21 days after the motion is denied or deemed denied

**Extension of Time:** Under MCR 7.203(B), the trial court may extend the time for filing a notice of appeal for a period not exceeding 21 days from the expiration of the initial deadline, if:
1. The request is made before expiration of the initial deadline, OR
2. The request is made within 7 days after expiration of the initial deadline and there is a reasonable basis for failing to file in time

**Critical for Andrew:** If the trial court's decision on his custody/parenting time issues was entered, Andrew must file his notice of appeal within 21 days of entry. If deadlines have been missed, immediate action is required to request an extension.

### MCR 7.204: Content and Service of Notice of Appeal

MCR 7.204(A) requires the notice of appeal to include:
1. A statement showing that the notice is an appeal from a specified judgment or order
2. The trial court's case number
3. The parties' names and addresses
4. A statement whether the appeal is from a civil or criminal case
5. Whether a fee paid is enclosed
6. Whether appellant is requesting a fee waiver
7. Whether oral argument is requested
8. The name and address of appellant's attorney (if represented)

The notice must be served on all other parties' attorneys and the trial court clerk. MCR 7.204(B) provides the service requirements.

### MCR 7.205: Stay and Dismissal

MCR 7.205(A) states that a notice of appeal does not stay enforcement of the judgment or order appealed from, except as otherwise provided by court rule or law.

**Stay Pending Appeal:** An appellant seeking to stay enforcement of a judgment must move the trial court for a stay (MCR 7.209). If the trial court denies the motion, the appellant may file an emergency motion for stay in the Court of Appeals.

**Andrew's Consideration:** If the trial court's orders have imposed restrictions on his parenting time or other relief harmful to his interests, filing a notice of appeal does NOT automatically stay those orders. He must separately seek a stay.

### MCR 7.206: Completion of Record and Transcript

MCR 7.206(A) requires that within 21 days after filing the notice of appeal, appellant must:
1. Arrange for preparation of the transcripts needed
2. Order the trial court record from the trial court

**Transcript Preparation:** The court reporter must prepare transcripts within 28 days after request (MCR 8.108). Extensions may be granted if necessary.

**Record on Appeal:** The record typically includes all pleadings, motions, orders, judgments, and other documents filed in the trial court. Both parties should review and agree on what constitutes the complete record. Disputes over the record are resolved by motion to the COA.

### MCR 7.207: Docketing Statement

MCR 7.207(A) requires appellant to file a docketing statement with the COA within 21 days after filing the notice of appeal. The docketing statement must contain:
1. Case name and number
2. Parties' names and addresses
3. Names and addresses of attorneys
4. Date of judgment/order appealed
5. Nature of the case
6. Nature of the judgment/order appealed
7. Relief sought on appeal
8. Whether oral argument is requested
9. Statement of whether any party is proceeding in forma pauperis

**Strategic Importance:** The docketing statement is often the first thing COA judges read. It should clearly and persuasively summarize the case and identify the key issues.

### MCR 7.211: Prehearing Procedure

MCR 7.211(A) establishes the prehearing process and sets deadlines for briefs and records.

**Brief Deadlines:**
- Appellant's brief: 56 days after the docketing statement is due
- Appellee's brief: 35 days after appellant's brief is filed
- Appellant's reply brief: 21 days after appellee's brief is filed (optional but strongly recommended)

These deadlines may be extended by stipulation of the parties or by court order. MCR 7.211(A)(3) allows for joint requests for extensions.

**Screening for Errors:** MCR 7.211(C) authorizes the COA to screen cases for various errors and deficiencies:
- **Peremptory Reversal (C)(4):** If the panel is unanimously of the opinion that reversal is required on the basis that the trial court order violates MCL 600.2950 (PPO statute), MCL 722.31a (custody interference), or that the decision is clearly erroneous as a matter of law, the court may reverse without oral argument
- **Summary Affirmance (C)(5):** Similarly, if the court is unanimous that affirmance is required, it may affirm without oral argument
- **Order to Show Cause:** The court may issue an order to show cause if it believes the appeal may be frivolous

**Andrew's Opportunity:** If the trial court's orders violated the domestic relations statutes or abuse of discretion standards, peremptory reversal is a significant strategic advantage.

### MCR 7.212: Brief Requirements

MCR 7.212 provides detailed requirements for appellate briefs. This is discussed extensively in the Brief Requirements section below.

### MCR 7.213: Oral Argument

MCR 7.213 governs oral argument procedures. Oral argument is typically granted unless the court determines it is unnecessary. MCR 7.213(A) states that oral argument shall be granted on request unless the court determines otherwise.

**Time Allocation:** Each party is typically given 15-20 minutes for oral argument, depending on the complexity of the case.

**Strategic Decision:** Pro se parties (self-represented litigants) should carefully consider whether requesting oral argument is beneficial. While it provides an opportunity to respond to judicial questions, it also risks appearing unprepared if the party is not thoroughly versed in appellate procedure and law.

### MCR 7.214: Decision

MCR 7.214 governs the issuance of the court's decision. The court will issue a written opinion explaining its reasoning and affirming, reversing, or modifying the trial court judgment.

**Types of Opinions:**
- **Reported Opinions:** Published in Michigan Reports and cited as precedent
- **Unpublished Opinions:** Not to be cited as precedent (except as allowed by MCR 7.215(C)(1))

Most COA decisions are unpublished unless they establish new law or involve significant legal issues.

## Brief Requirements and Structure {#brief-requirements}

### MCR 7.212: Detailed Brief Requirements

**Mandatory Elements of Appellant Brief:**

Per MCR 7.212(C), the appellant's brief must contain:

1. **Cover Page:** Must include case name, trial court number, parties, and attorney identification

2. **Title Page:** Identifying the document as "Appellant's Brief"

3. **Table of Contents:** With page references to each issue

4. **Jurisdictional Statement (§1):** A concise statement that establishes COA jurisdiction. For example:
   - "The Court of Appeals has jurisdiction under MCL 600.301 and MCR 7.201(A)(1). The trial court entered a final judgment on [date] disposing of all parties' claims in this family law matter."
   - Must cite the statute or rule conferring jurisdiction
   - Must identify the judgment/order appealed
   - Must establish finality if not obvious

5. **Statement of Questions Presented (§2):** Clearly identify the legal issues being appealed. For example:
   - "Did the trial court abuse its discretion by issuing an ex parte custody order without providing notice and an opportunity to be heard?"
   - "Did the trial court clearly err in its factual findings regarding parenting capacity based on evidence of biased investigation?"
   - Each question should be separate and phrased to suggest the desired answer

6. **Statement of Facts (§3):** A fair, complete, and accurate presentation of the facts established by the record. This must:
   - Cite to specific pages in the trial court record or transcript
   - Present facts in narrative form
   - Be objective (not argumentative)
   - Cover all facts necessary to understand the issues
   - Identify conflicting testimony or evidence fairly
   - Example citation: "The trial court heard testimony from [witness] that [fact], Tr. p. [page]"

7. **Argument (§4):** The legal analysis and persuasive argument. Must:
   - Organize arguments by issue
   - State the applicable law clearly
   - Cite to statutes, court rules, and case law
   - Apply law to facts
   - Address standard of review
   - Explain why trial court erred
   - Anticipated counterarguments should be addressed
   - Avoid conclusory statements

8. **Relief Sought:** Clear statement of what reversal the appellant seeks (reversal and remand, reversal and entry of new order, etc.)

9. **Signature:** Signed by attorney or pro se party with date and address

10. **Certificate of Compliance:** Statement that the brief complies with page limits and font requirements

### Page Limits and Format Requirements

MCR 7.212(B) establishes the following limits:

- **Appellant's Brief:** 50 pages maximum (excluding tables, tables of contents, signature pages, certificate)
- **Appellee's Brief:** 50 pages maximum
- **Reply Brief:** 25 pages maximum
- **Font:** 12-point, proportional serif font or monospaced 10-point (Courier)
- **Line Spacing:** Double-spaced
- **Margins:** 1 inch on all sides
- **Paper:** 8.5" x 11" white paper

**Exception:** Court may permit deviation from format requirements for good cause shown.

### Citation Format

MCR 7.212(D) requires citations to:
- Michigan statutes: MCL [number]
- Michigan Court Rules: MCR [number]
- Cases: Full case name, court and year (e.g., Vodvarka v Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 499 (2003))
- Records: Page number in trial record (e.g., Tr. p. 23)

### Appendix Requirements {#appendix-requirements}

MCR 7.212(H) governs appendix requirements. **The appendix must include:**

1. **Judgment or Order Appealed:** The complete trial court order from which the appeal is taken
2. **Other Essential Documents:** Any documents necessary to understand the issues, such as:
   - Key motions and responses
   - Relevant findings of fact (if any)
   - Relevant pleadings that establish claims or defenses
   - Orders showing preservation of issues (objections to trial court decisions)
3. **Transcripts of Testimony:** Relevant portions of trial transcript, depositions, or hearing transcripts upon which the brief relies
4. **Exhibits:** Any exhibits material to the appeal

**Pagination:** The appendix must be paginated consecutively and clearly labeled. The brief should cite to specific pages in the appendix.

**Digital Filing:** Under Michigan's e-filing requirements, appendices are typically submitted in PDF format with bookmarks for easy navigation.

## Standards of Review {#standards-of-review}

The standard of review determines how much deference the Court of Appeals must give to the trial court's decision. This is critical to understanding appellate success rate.

### De Novo Review (Legal Questions)

**Definition:** De novo review means the appellate court reviews the legal conclusion without deference to the trial court. The appellate court may substitute its own judgment entirely.

**When Applied:**
- Interpretation of statutes
- Application of law to undisputed facts
- Constitutional questions
- Sufficiency of pleadings
- Evidentiary rulings on issues of law

**For Andrew's Case:** If he argues that the trial court misinterpreted MCL 722.31a (parenting time interference) or improperly applied the custody factors under MCL 722.23, those are reviewed de novo.

### Clear Error Review (Factual Findings)

**Definition:** Under MCR 2.613(C) and applicable appellate case law, a trial court's factual findings are reviewed for clear error. A factual finding is clearly erroneous when the record provides no competent, material evidence to support it, or when the trial court misapprehended the effect of the evidence. See MCL 600.2169.

**Practical Standard:** The appellate court must defer to the trial court's credibility determinations and findings supported by competent evidence, even if the appellate judges would have found differently.

**Burden:** The appellant must demonstrate that the finding lacks evidentiary support or that the trial court misunderstood the evidence.

**For Andrew's Case:** If the trial court made factual findings regarding parenting capacity, emotional stability, or child's preference, those are reviewed for clear error. However, if the trial court relied on biased or manipulated evidence (e.g., the HealthWest evaluation), Andrew can argue the finding is clearly erroneous because it's based on incompetent evidence.

### Abuse of Discretion Review (Custody Determinations)

**Definition:** Custody determinations and parenting time decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court:
1. Failed to properly exercise discretion
2. Made a decision that falls outside the range of reasonable choices under the circumstances
3. Failed to consider appropriate factors
4. Considered inappropriate factors
5. Relied on incorrect legal standards

**Key Case:** *Pierron v Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222 (2009) establishes that custody modifications require consideration of statutory factors and cannot be based on improper considerations.

**Statutory Factors:** MCL 722.23 lists 12 statutory factors for custody determinations:
1. Love, affection, and other emotional ties
2. Ability and willingness to give guidance and education
3. Moral fitness of the parties
4. Capacity and willingness to provide food, clothing, medical care, etc.
5. Length of time child lived in stable, satisfactory environment
6. Permanence of proposed residence
7. Moral and religious development of the child
8. Child's reasonable preference (if old enough)
9. Willingness and ability to facilitate parent-child relationship
10. Domestic violence history
11. Any other factor related to child's best interests
12. As of 2021, whether either parent engaged in a pattern of nonpayment of child support

**Abuse of Discretion Analysis:** Andrew should argue:
- Trial court failed to properly weigh MCL 722.23 factors
- Trial court relied on biased or unreliable evidence
- Trial court's decision is so clearly wrong that reversal is required
- Trial court failed to conduct proper hearing on relevant factors

**Standard:** While the appellate court is deferential under abuse of discretion review, it will reverse if the trial court's decision falls outside the range of reasonable choices.

### Application to Ex Parte Orders

**Special Rule:** Ex parte orders (orders entered without notice to the affected party) are subject to more stringent review. An ex parte order is proper only when:
1. Statutory authority exists for an ex parte order
2. Emergency circumstances exist justifying ex parte action
3. Proper notice and hearing procedures are followed immediately after

If a trial court enters an ex parte order without legal basis or proper procedure, that may constitute reversible error on de novo review (legal question) or abuse of discretion review (discretionary ordering).

## Issue Preservation Doctrine {#issue-preservation}

The issue preservation doctrine is critical to appellate review. With limited exceptions, the Court of Appeals will not consider issues that were not properly preserved for appeal by objection in the trial court.

### Basic Rule: Objection Required

**MCR 2.508:** Requires parties to object to trial court rulings at the time they occur, on the record. An objection preserves an issue for appeal.

**Timing of Objection:** 
- Objection must be made before the trial court rules OR
- Immediately after an adverse ruling (while still in trial court)
- Objection cannot be made for the first time on appeal

**For Andrew's Case:** If the trial court entered custody orders without providing him notice and an opportunity to be heard, the proper preservation would be:
1. Andrew requests an opportunity to be heard
2. Trial court denies or refuses
3. Andrew's denial of due process is preserved on the record
4. Andrew notes the objection in writing to the trial court

### Exceptions to Preservation Requirement

MCR 2.508(D) provides exceptions where issues may be considered despite lack of preservation:

1. **Gross Absence of Due Process:** Constitutional violations so fundamental that they implicate the trial's basic fairness (e.g., complete denial of opportunity to be heard)

2. **Manifest Injustice Exception:** An issue may be reviewed even without preservation if:
   - The outcome would be manifestly unjust if left uncorrected
   - The issue goes to the integrity of the judicial process
   - This is narrowly construed

3. **Plain Error Review:** MCR 2.508(D) also allows review of "plain error affecting substantial rights" even without preservation if:
   - Error is apparent from the record
   - Error occurred at the trial court
   - Error prejudiced substantial rights
   - Failure to consider it would result in manifest injustice

**Plain Error Standard:** Under *People v Carines*, 460 Mich 750 (1999) (applied by analogy in civil cases), plain error requires:
1. Error must be apparent from the record
2. Error must have been made at trial
3. Error must have affected substantial rights
4. Failure to reverse would result in manifest injustice

### For Andrew's Case

**Strong Preservation Arguments:**
- If ex parte orders were entered and Andrew received no notice until after entry, he can argue this constitutes per se constitutional violation
- If parenting time was denied without hearing, and Andrew objected, this is clearly preserved
- HealthWest evaluation: If Andrew objected to the evaluation's admission as biased or unreliable during trial, that's preserved

**Manifest Injustice Exception:** Andrew may be able to invoke this if:
- Custody was awarded based on fraudulent evidence (Emily's perjured testimony)
- Trial court was misinformed about critical facts
- Trial court's order violates statutory requirements

## Key COA Precedent Cases {#key-precedent}

### *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003)

**Issue:** Standards for modifying custody; factors for considering relocation

**Rule:** Custody modifications require showing of "material change in circumstances" and that modification is in child's best interests. The statutory factors in MCL 722.23 must be properly weighed.

**Application to Andrew:** If trial court ordered major changes to parenting time without finding material change of circumstances or properly weighing statutory factors, *Vodvarka* supports reversal.

### *Dailey v Kloenhamer*, 291 Mich App 660 (2010)

**Issue:** Parental alienation and interference with parenting time

**Rule:** A parent's deliberate efforts to interfere with the other parent's parenting time or turn a child against that parent may be considered in custody determinations and can support modification of an existing custody arrangement.

**Application to Andrew:** Evidence that Lori or Emily facilitated alienation of his son (Albert Watson's statements, Cody Watson's hostile texts) supports Andrew's argument that trial court should modify custody to protect his parenting time.

**Citation:** "Michigan courts recognize that a parent who unreasonably attempts to interfere with the other parent's parenting time may have his custody or parenting time modified. See MCL 722.31a (parenting time interference)."

### *Pierron v Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222 (2009)

**Issue:** Custody modification; whether trial court must conduct hearing on modification request

**Rule:** Trial court must conduct an evidentiary hearing on custody modification request and must consider all MCL 722.23 statutory factors. Modification based on insufficient evidence or failure to hear evidence may be reversed.

**Application to Andrew:** If trial court denied him a hearing on his parenting time modifications or failed to hear evidence on key issues, *Pierron* supports reversal. This is directly applicable to his situation.

### Additional Relevant Cases

**MCL 722.31a Enforcement:** Michigan courts have consistently held that intentional interference with parenting time violates the statute and can result in custody modification. See *Eason v Eason*, 179 Mich App 62 (1989).

**Ex Parte Orders:** Orders entered without notice and opportunity to be heard are subject to strict scrutiny. See MCL 600.2950 (PPO statute, which has parallel procedures in family law).

## Peremptory Reversal and Emergency Relief {#peremptory-relief}

### Peremptory Reversal Under MCR 7.211(C)(4)

MCR 7.211(C)(4) provides a streamlined reversal procedure:

**Rule:** If a panel of the Court of Appeals is unanimously of the opinion that reversal is required based on one of the following, the court may reverse without oral argument:
1. The trial court order violates MCL 600.2950(1)-(3) (Domestic Relations Protection Act/PPO)
2. The trial court order violates MCL 722.31a (parenting time interference)
3. The decision is clearly erroneous as a matter of law

**Application:** MCL 722.31a specifically prohibits interference with court-ordered parenting time. If the trial court's order unreasonably restricts Andrew's parenting time or enables such interference, Andrew can argue peremptory reversal is warranted.

### Emergency Relief Pending Appeal

MCR 7.209 governs motions for stay and emergency relief pending appeal.

**Motion in Trial Court:** Appellant first seeks relief from the trial court (MCR 7.209(A)). The trial court may:
1. Stay enforcement of the judgment pending appeal
2. Grant other relief necessary to preserve status quo
3. Preserve the parties' rights pending appellate decision

**Grounds for Stay:**
- Strong likelihood of success on appeal
- Irreparable harm if stay is not granted
- Balance of equities favors stay
- Public interest not disserved

**Motion in Court of Appeals:** If trial court denies stay, appellant may file an emergency motion in the COA (MCR 7.209(B)). The COA will:
1. Review the trial court's decision
2. Apply the stay factors
3. Issue its own order granting or denying stay

**For Andrew's Case:** If trial court's parenting time restrictions cause immediate, irreparable harm (inability to spend time with his child), Andrew should seek emergency stay pending appeal under MCR 7.209.

## Interlocutory vs. Final Orders {#interlocutory-final}

### Distinction and Appellability

**Final Order:** An order that disposes of all claims and issues, leaving nothing for future determination. MCR 7.202(A).

**Interlocutory Order:** An order that does not dispose of all claims (e.g., an order on one issue while other issues remain pending). Generally not appealable.

**Exception:** Even interlocutory orders may be appealable if:
1. They satisfy MCR 2.604(A) certification for interlocutory appeal, OR
2. They involve a specified category (e.g., preliminary injunction, discovery order)

### Certification Under MCR 2.604

MCR 2.604(A) allows trial court to certify an interlocutory order as final if:
1. The order involves a controlling question of law as to which there is substantial ground for difference of opinion, AND
2. An immediate appeal may materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation

**For Andrew's Case:** If the trial court issued an order denying his parenting time request while other issues (e.g., child support) remained pending, Andrew might seek certification of the custody order as final to enable immediate appeal.

## Briefing Timeline and Extensions {#briefing-timeline}

### Default Schedule

**Day 1:** Docketing statement filed with COA

**Day 21:** Docketing statement must be filed by this date

**Day 77:** Appellant's brief must be filed (56 days after docketing statement)

**Day 112:** Appellee's brief must be filed (35 days after appellant's brief)

**Day 133:** Appellant's reply brief must be filed (21 days after appellee's brief) - optional but strongly recommended

### Extensions

MCR 7.211(A)(3) allows parties to request extensions. 

**Stipulated Extensions:** The parties may agree in writing to extend briefing deadlines. The extended deadline should be clearly stated.

**Court-Ordered Extensions:** If parties cannot agree, a written motion should be filed with the COA requesting extension. Courts generally grant reasonable extensions for good cause (e.g., need for medical records, transcript delay).

**Filing the Extension Request:** The motion should:
1. Clearly identify which deadline is being extended
2. State the new extended deadline
3. Explain the reason for extension
4. Be filed BEFORE the deadline expires

## Filing Procedures and Fees {#filing-procedures}

### Filing Fees

**Appeal of Right:** The appeal fee is $375 (as of 2026). This fee is paid at the time of filing the notice of appeal in the trial court.

**Pro Se Filing Fees:** Pro se parties must pay the same filing fee, unless they obtain a fee waiver.

### Fee Waiver Procedure

MCR 7.215 and MC 20 govern fee waivers for indigent parties.

**Application:** File an affidavit of indigency (MC 20) with the trial court at the time of filing the notice of appeal.

**Affidavit Requirements:** The MC 20 form must state:
1. Gross monthly income and list income sources
2. Monthly expenses
3. Assets
4. Statement that you cannot afford the fee

**Determination:** The trial court may:
1. Grant the waiver (no appeal fee required)
2. Allow installment payments
3. Deny the waiver if the affidavit shows ability to pay

**Appeal of Denial:** If trial court denies the fee waiver, appellant may request reconsideration or may seek waiver in the Court of Appeals.

### E-Filing via TrueFiling

Michigan courts use TrueFiling, an electronic filing system.

**Requirements:**
1. Register with TrueFiling at [state e-filing portal]
2. Create electronic document in PDF format
3. Submit through TrueFiling portal before deadline
4. Pay filing fee electronically (unless fee waiver granted)

**Advantages:**
- Instantaneous confirmation of filing
- Automatic service on opposing counsel
- Accessible record for all parties

**Pro Se Filing:** Pro se parties can file electronically if they create a TrueFiling account. Some courts allow paper filing for pro se parties, but e-filing is now required in most Michigan courts.

**Documents to E-File:**
1. Notice of Appeal
2. Docketing Statement
3. Appellant's Brief
4. Appendix (with bookmarks)
5. Reply Brief
6. Any motions or responses

## Andrew's Strongest Appellate Grounds {#strongest-grounds}

### Ground 1: Ex Parte Order Without Due Process

**Issue:** Trial court allegedly entered custody/parenting time orders without providing Andrew notice and an opportunity to be heard.

**Legal Basis:**
- U.S. Constitution, 14th Amendment (due process)
- Michigan Constitution, Art. I, § 17 (due process)
- MCL 600.2950 (domestic relations protection act procedures)
- MCL 722.21 et seq. (custody procedures requiring hearing)

**Case Support:** *Pierron v Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222 — trial court must conduct hearing on custody modification. Ex parte orders are permissible only in true emergencies with immediate follow-up hearing.
