### 🔴 CRITICAL (could cause dismissal or sanctions)

**C1. Catz v. Chalker Overcitation (Lines 63, 557, 563, 319, 852)**
The complaint cites *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279 (6th Cir. 1998), at least 5 times for the proposition that "§ 1983 is a viable remedy for state interference with parental custody rights." **The actual holding went the OTHER direction** — the 6th Circuit DISMISSED most of Catz's § 1983 claims on preclusion/abstention grounds, finding them "inextricably intertwined" with state court decisions. The case *discussed* § 1983 viability but *applied* abstention to reject those claims. Opposing counsel will cite the same case against you. **FIX: Supplement with stronger authority or accurately represent the holding. Consider citing *Duchesne v. Sugarman*, 566 F.2d 817 (2d Cir. 1977) or other circuits that squarely hold § 1983 is available.**

**C3. §1985(3) Class-Based Animus Not Adequately Pled (¶82, Line 463)**
The complaint alleges the conspiracy was "motivated by class-based animus against Plaintiff as an unwed father and pro se litigant." Under *Griffin v. Breckenridge* and subsequent authority (especially *Bray v. Alexandria Women's Health Clinic*, 506 U.S. 263 (1993)), §1985(3) requires **racial or other recognized class-based invidious discriminatory animus**. "Unwed father" and "pro se litigant" are NOT well-established classes under §1985(3). The 6th Circuit has been strict about this. **This count is highly vulnerable to dismissal. FIX: Either (a) strengthen the class-based argument with sex/gender animus authority, or (b) rely primarily on §1983 conspiracy and treat §1985(3) as alternative.**

### 🟠 HIGH (weakens the complaint significantly)

**H1. Judicial Immunity Argument Is Extremely Difficult (Lines 593–607)**
The "clear absence of all jurisdiction" exception to *Stump v. Sparkman* is almost never found when a judge acts within their general subject-matter jurisdiction (family law). A family court judge ruling on custody and contempt IS acting within her jurisdiction, even if procedurally defective. The complaint's argument that MCR 3.207(C)(2) prerequisites are "jurisdictional" is creative but courts consistently treat procedural defects as *error*, not *absence of jurisdiction*. **The non-judicial act theory (ex parte communications, self-ruling on disqualification, conspiracy) is the stronger path. Consider reducing emphasis on the jurisdictional exception.**

**H2. Kovacic v. Cuyahoga — Mischaracterized Holding (Line 361)**
Cited for: "removal of child without procedurally adequate due process violates substantive due process." **Actual holding**: Mother's claims were time-barred; children's claims survived Rooker-Feldman. The case didn't reach the merits of the substantive due process claim. **FIX: Replace with a case that actually held on the merits, such as *Brokaw v. Mercer County*, 235 F.3d 1000 (7th Cir. 2000).**

**H3. Bartell v. Lohiser — Overclaimed Holding (Line 361)**
Cited for: "strict scrutiny required for parental rights interference." **Actual holding**: The court acknowledged parental rights are fundamental but applied QUALIFIED IMMUNITY and found no clearly established violation. The court did NOT apply strict scrutiny. **FIX: Cite *Troxel* plus *Glucksberg* for the strict scrutiny proposition instead. Bartell can stay as supporting but qualify the citation.**

**H4. Crawford v. Washington — Wrong Constitutional Context (Line 403)**
The complaint cites *Crawford v. Washington*, 541 U.S. 36 (2004) for "the fundamental right to confront and cross-examine evidence" in a CIVIL proceeding. *Crawford* is a **Sixth Amendment Confrontation Clause** case that applies exclusively to CRIMINAL prosecutions. **FIX: Replace with *Goldberg v. Kelly* (already cited) or *Mathews v. Eldridge* for the civil due process right to respond to evidence.**

**H5. Exclusionary Rule in Civil Context (Lines 207–211)**
*Mapp v. Ohio* and *Wong Sun* apply the exclusionary rule in **criminal** proceedings. There is no established right to exclude illegally obtained evidence in a civil family court case. While a creative argument, a federal court is likely to reject this. **FIX: Frame the argument as "due process was violated by relying on evidence obtained through criminal conduct" rather than invoking the exclusionary rule by name.**

**H6. Tower v. Glover — Wrong Type of Attorney (Line 473)**
Cited for: attorneys who conspire with state actors are liable under § 1983. **But** *Tower v. Glover* is specifically about **public defenders** (state-appointed). Jennifer Barnes was a **private attorney**. The better citation for private attorneys is *Dennis v. Sparks* itself (already cited) plus *Adickes v. Kress*. The court may distinguish *Tower* as inapplicable. **FIX: Remove the Tower citation for Barnes; Dennis v. Sparks alone is sufficient.**

**H7. Pittman v. Cuyahoga — Actually Adverse Authority (Authority table, line 855)**
Cited in the authority table for "§ 1983 viability in child welfare context." **Actual holding**: Social workers get **absolute immunity** for actions related to judicial proceedings. This case could be used AGAINST you by defendants to argue immunity. **FIX: Remove from the authority table entirely.**
