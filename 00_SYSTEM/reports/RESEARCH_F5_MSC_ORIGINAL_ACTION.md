## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This filing invokes the Michigan Supreme Court's original jurisdiction under MCR 7.306 to seek superintending control or mandamus over the 14th Circuit Court (Judge McNeill). The basis is a pattern of systematic due process violations, ex parte abuse, judicial bias, and denial of fundamental parenting rights that cannot be adequately remedied through normal appellate channels. The MSC exercises original jurisdiction sparingly — only when (1) there is no adequate remedy at law, (2) a lower court has failed a clear legal duty, and (3) the matter presents an issue of significant public importance.

## III. THRESHOLD: WHEN DOES MSC EXERCISE ORIGINAL JURISDICTION?

The MSC exercises original jurisdiction **extremely rarely**. Understanding the threshold is critical to framing the complaint effectively.

### The Three-Part Test

1. **No Adequate Remedy at Law**
   - Normal appeal is either unavailable or would be too slow/inadequate
   - Irreparable harm is occurring NOW (212+ days of separation)
   - Standard appellate process takes 1-2 years — child suffers ongoing harm
   - COA appeal already filed (366810) but cannot provide immediate relief

2. **Lower Court Failed Clear Legal Duty / Acted Outside Authority**
   - McNeill's documented pattern:
     - 44% ex parte rate (24 of 55 orders)
     - Canon 3(A)(4) violation: "do not file anymore, I will not look at it"
     - Canon 3(A)(7) violation: ex parte communication with prosecutor
     - No evidentiary hearing within 21 days as required by MCR 3.207(C)
     - Best interest factors not properly analyzed (MCL 722.23)

3. **Issue of Significant Public Importance** (strengthens but not always required)
   - Systematic due process violations by a sitting judge
   - Pattern affecting not just this case but potentially all litigants before McNeill
   - Pro se litigant discrimination
   - Parental alienation / PPO weaponization systemic issue

### How to Show "No Adequate Remedy at Law"

This is the hardest element. You must demonstrate:

| Argument | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| **Appeal too slow** | Child has been separated 212+ days; appellate timeline is 1-2 years; irreparable harm every day |
| **Appeal cannot undo harm** | Developmental harm to child from prolonged separation cannot be reversed |
| **Lower court unresponsive** | McNeill told Andrew "do not file anymore" — normal motions will not be heard |
| **COA appeal pending but inadequate** | COA 366810 addresses past errors but cannot compel future compliance |
| **Habeas-like urgency** | Functional deprivation of custody is equivalent to restraint requiring immediate remedy |
