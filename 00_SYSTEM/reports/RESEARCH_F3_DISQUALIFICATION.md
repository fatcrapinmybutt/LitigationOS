## 1. MCR 2.003 — Statutory Text & Grounds

### MCR 2.003(C) Grounds for Disqualification

> **(1) Disqualification of a judge is warranted for reasons that include, but are not limited to, the following:**
>
> **(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
>
> **(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either:
>
> > **(i)** a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868 (2009), or
> >
> > **(ii)** has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Source:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)-(b), Michigan Court Rules (current through 2025 amendments)  
**URL:** https://voiceofdetroit.net/wp-content/uploads/2011/05/MCR-2.003-and-Code-of-Judicial-Conduct.pdf

### Additional Statutory Grounds (MCR 2.003(C))

- **(C)(1)(c):** The judge has personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding.
- **(C)(1)(d):** The judge has been a material witness concerning the matter in controversy.
- **(C)(1)(e):** The judge or the judge's spouse, or a person within the third degree of relationship, is a party, officer/director/trustee of a party, or is known to have an interest that could be substantially affected.
- **(C)(1)(f):** The judge or the judge's spouse, or their close relative, is acting as lawyer in the proceeding or is known to have an interest that could be substantially affected.

### Two Independent Tests — BOTH Apply to Our Case

| Test | Rule | Standard | Burden |
|------|------|----------|--------|
| **Actual Bias** | MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) | Judge IS biased or prejudiced for/against a party | Must show personal bias — heavy presumption of impartiality |
| **Objective/Appearance** | MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i)-(ii) | Reasonable person would question impartiality; OR failure to adhere to Canon 2 appearance of impropriety standard | Objective, reasonable perceptions — does NOT require proving actual bias |

**STRATEGIC NOTE:** The (C)(1)(b) test is significantly easier to meet than (C)(1)(a). Our motion should lead with (C)(1)(b)(ii) — appearance of impropriety — and bolster with (C)(1)(b)(i) — serious risk of actual bias — while also arguing (C)(1)(a) actual bias as the strongest ground.

## 2. Leading Authorities

### A. FEDERAL / U.S. SUPREME COURT

#### Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009)
- **Court:** U.S. Supreme Court
- **Holding:** Due process under the 14th Amendment requires judicial recusal when the "probability of actual bias on the part of the judge is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." The test is objective — based on "reasonable perceptions," not actual proof of bias.
- **Key Quote:** "Under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness, the probability of actual bias... is too high to be constitutionally tolerable."
- **Relevance to F3:** Michigan adopted this standard directly into MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) in 2009. This is the constitutional floor — if we meet Caperton's standard, disqualification is constitutionally required regardless of state court rule interpretation.
- **Verified in DB:** ✓ (FED-023)

#### Liteky v. United States, 510 U.S. 540 (1994)
- **Court:** U.S. Supreme Court
- **Holding:** Established the "extrajudicial source doctrine" — bias arising from outside judicial proceedings (extrajudicial sources) warrants disqualification. However, opinions formed during judicial proceedings can ALSO warrant disqualification if they "display a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible."
- **Key Quote:** "[O]pinions formed by the judge on the basis of facts introduced or events occurring in the course of the current proceedings... do not constitute a basis for a bias or partiality motion unless they display a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible."
- **Relevance to F3:** Our case has BOTH extrajudicial source bias (ex parte communications with FOC) AND in-court conduct showing deep-seated favoritism (pattern of one-sided rulings, proceedings without father present). Liteky supports disqualification on either ground.
- **Verified in DB:** ✓ (FED-024)

### B. MICHIGAN SUPREME COURT

#### Cain v. Department of Corrections, 451 Mich. 470; 548 N.W.2d 210 (1996)
- **Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
- **Holding:** Party seeking disqualification must overcome a "heavy presumption of judicial impartiality." Must show "actual bias or prejudice" under MCR 2.003(B)(1) (now (C)(1)(a)). Adverse rulings alone are insufficient. BUT the Court recognized that "in rare cases, the appearance of impropriety can reach a due process violation level."
- **Key Quote:** "[T]he party who challenges a judge on the basis of bias or prejudice must overcome a heavy presumption of judicial impartiality."
- **Relevance to F3:** This is both the standard we must meet AND our strongest authority. Cain requires us to present evidence beyond mere adverse rulings — we have ex parte orders, proceedings without notice, FOC ex parte communications, and a documented pattern of 1,127 judicial violations. This is the "rare case" Cain contemplated.
- **IMPORTANT WARNING:** Cain is often cited by courts DENYING disqualification. We must distinguish our case by showing the evidence goes far beyond "adverse rulings" — it demonstrates systemic bias from extrajudicial sources and patterns that make fair judgment impossible.

#### Crampton v. Department of State, 395 Mich. 347; 235 N.W.2d 352 (1975)
- **Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
- **Holding:** A hearing before an unbiased and impartial decisionmaker is a "basic requirement of due process" under the 14th Amendment. Actual bias need not be proven — disqualification is required where "the probability of actual bias... is too high to be constitutionally tolerable."
- **Key Quote:** "[Experience] teaches that such risk of actual bias may be intolerable in certain circumstances, even if actual bias is not established."
- **Relevance to F3:** Crampton is the Michigan predecessor to Caperton. It establishes that Michigan has long recognized the constitutional due process basis for disqualification — predating the U.S. Supreme Court's Caperton decision by 34 years. Strong support for the proposition that even the PROBABILITY of bias requires disqualification.

#### People v. Wells, 238 Mich. App. 383 (1999)
- **Court:** Michigan Court of Appeals
- **Holding:** Appellate review of disqualification decisions uses the abuse-of-discretion standard on factual findings.
- **Relevance to F3:** If our motion is denied, this case governs the standard on appeal. Preserving a clear factual record is essential.
- **Verified in DB:** ✓ (MICH-002)

### C. MICHIGAN COURT OF APPEALS

#### Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Township (2001)
- **Court:** Michigan Court of Appeals
- **Holding:** Applied the "reasonable person" test: disqualification is warranted when "the conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality, and competence is impaired." Court found insufficient evidence in that case.
- **Key Quote:** "[W]hether, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."
- **Relevance to F3:** Armstrong defines the test we must meet under (C)(1)(b). Our evidence (documented pattern of 1,127 violations, ex parte orders, proceedings without father present) should easily satisfy this reasonable-person standard.

#### People v. Perkins, 193 Mich. App. 209 (1992)
- **Court:** Michigan Court of Appeals
- **Holding:** The "appearance of impropriety" — such as financial ties between judge and attorney — requires judicial disqualification, "even without a showing of actual bias or prejudice."
- **Relevance to F3:** Perkins establishes that even the APPEARANCE is sufficient. We don't need to prove actual bias if the appearance of impropriety is clear.

### D. MICHIGAN CODE OF JUDICIAL CONDUCT

#### Canon 2 — Appearance of Impropriety
> "A judge should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities."

#### Canon 3(A)(4) — Ex Parte Communications
> "A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications or other communications made to the judge outside the presence of all parties regarding a pending proceeding."

- **Ethics Opinion JI-134:** Judges must instruct and train ALL staff (including FOC) not to engage in ex parte communications on substantive matters.
- **Canon 3 (2025 Amendment, eff. Jan 1, 2026):** Judges are explicitly barred from "intentionally or recklessly allowing bias, prejudice" — strengthening grounds.

### Timing Rules

- **14-day discovery rule:** Motion must be filed within 14 days of discovering the grounds for disqualification.
- **Good cause exception:** Late motions MAY be granted for good cause shown, but untimeliness weighs against granting.
- **Ongoing/cumulative bias:** Where grounds are based on a PATTERN of conduct (our case), the 14-day clock arguably resets with each new violation. The most recent violations are within 14 days.
- **Waiver risk:** Failure to timely file can be deemed a waiver of the right to seek disqualification.

### Verification Requirement

The motion **must be verified** — meaning:
- Signed under oath, OR
- Includes a declaration under penalty of perjury: *"I declare under the penalties of perjury that the statements in this motion are true to the best of my information, knowledge, and belief."*
- If facts are known only to the attorney, a verified statement by counsel is acceptable.
- **For pro se parties:** Andrew must personally verify the motion with the declaration language.

## 5. Winning Strategies from Successful Cases

### Strategy 1: Lead with the Objective Test (C)(1)(b), Not Just Actual Bias

The (C)(1)(b) "objective and reasonable perceptions" test is EASIER to meet than the (C)(1)(a) actual bias test. Structure the motion to present:
- **Primary:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii) — Appearance of impropriety (Canon 2 violation)
- **Secondary:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) — Serious risk of actual bias (Caperton standard)
- **Strongest:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Actual bias supported by overwhelming evidence

### Strategy 2: Document the Pattern with Specificity

Courts require specific facts, not conclusory allegations (*Cain*). Our DB contains:
- **1,127 total judicial violations** (167 specifically under MCR 2.003)
- **276 evidence quotes** supporting bias/ex parte claims
- **20 docket events** showing ex parte orders and proceedings
- **4 complete authority chains** for the disqualification filing

Each factual allegation must cite specific dates, case numbers, and record references.

### Strategy 3: Establish Extrajudicial Source Bias

Per *Liteky*, extrajudicial source bias is the strongest ground. Present evidence of:
- **Ex parte communications between judge and FOC** (violates Canon 3(A)(4))
- **Ex parte orders issued without notice to father** (9 documented events)
- **Proceedings held without father present or notified** (denial of due process)

### Strategy 4: Frame as Constitutional Due Process Violation

Per *Caperton* and *Crampton*, frame the motion not just as a court-rule violation but as a **constitutional due process violation** under the 14th Amendment. This:
- Elevates the standard of review
- Creates grounds for federal court relief (§1983) if state courts deny
- Makes the appeal stronger

### Strategy 5: Use the "Cumulative Effect" Doctrine

Individual incidents may not suffice. But the **cumulative effect** of:
- 1,127 documented violations
- Multiple ex parte orders
- Pattern of one-sided rulings
- Proceedings without father present
- FOC ex parte communications with judge

...creates an overwhelming showing that "a reasonable, objective observer would question the judge's impartiality" (*Armstrong*).

### Strategy 6: Include Proposed Order (MC 264)

Filing a proposed MC 264 Order of Disqualification/Reassignment with the motion shows professionalism and makes it easy for the court to grant the motion.

## 6. Pitfalls from Failed Cases — What to AVOID

### Pitfall 1: Relying Solely on Adverse Rulings
**Risk:** *Cain* explicitly holds that "adverse rulings, even if erroneous, do not constitute bias."
**Solution:** Focus on CONDUCT (ex parte communications, proceedings without notice) not just OUTCOMES (unfavorable rulings). Frame rulings as evidence of the pattern, not as independent grounds.

### Pitfall 2: Conclusory Allegations Without Specific Facts
**Risk:** Courts routinely deny motions that state "the judge is biased" without specific factual support.
**Solution:** Every allegation must cite a specific date, case number, document, and page. Our DB has this data — use it.

### Pitfall 3: Untimely Filing
**Risk:** Filing beyond 14 days of discovery = waiver argument.
**Solution:** Identify the most recent violations (within 14 days) as the triggering events. Argue the pattern is ONGOING and each new act restarts the clock.

### Pitfall 4: Appearing to Judge-Shop
**Risk:** Courts are suspicious of litigants who seek disqualification simply because they are losing.
**Solution:** Frame the motion around DUE PROCESS and PROCEDURAL FAIRNESS, not around dissatisfaction with outcomes. Emphasize ex parte communications and proceedings without notice — these are structural violations, not outcome complaints.

### Pitfall 5: Subjective Perceptions Instead of Objective Evidence
**Risk:** "I feel the judge is biased" is insufficient. *Armstrong* requires "objective and reasonable perceptions."
**Solution:** Present documented, verifiable facts. Transcripts, orders, docket entries — not opinions or feelings.

### Pitfall 6: Failing to Verify the Motion
**Risk:** An unverified motion is procedurally defective and can be summarily denied.
**Solution:** Include the verification language under penalty of perjury. Andrew must sign.

### Pitfall 7: Not Requesting Chief Judge Review if Denied
**Risk:** If McNeill denies the motion and Andrew doesn't request Chief Judge review, the issue may be waived.
**Solution:** If denied, immediately file a written request for de novo review by the Chief Judge under MCR 2.003(D)(3)(b).

## 9. Key Quotes for Brief

### On the Standard

> "Disqualification of a judge is warranted... [when] the judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party... or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2."
> — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)

> "[W]hether, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."
> — *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp.* (2001)

### On Due Process

> "[A] hearing before an unbiased and impartial decisionmaker is a basic requirement of due process under the Fourteenth Amendment."
> — *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich. 347 (1975)

> "Under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness, the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge... is too high to be constitutionally tolerable."
> — *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009)

### On Overcoming the Presumption

> "[T]he party who challenges a judge on the basis of bias or prejudice must overcome a heavy presumption of judicial impartiality."
> — *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich. 470 (1996)

> *Our response:* The presumption is overcome where the evidence shows a pattern of 1,127 documented judicial violations, including ex parte orders, proceedings without notice, and ex parte communications — not mere "adverse rulings."

### On Extrajudicial Source Bias

> "[O]pinions formed by the judge on the basis of facts introduced or events occurring in the course of the current proceedings... do not constitute a basis for a bias or partiality motion unless they display a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible."
> — *Liteky v. United States*, 510 U.S. 540 (1994)

> *Our response:* The bias arises from EXTRAJUDICIAL sources (ex parte communications with FOC, ex parte proceedings without father present) AND the in-court conduct displays "deep-seated favoritism... that would make fair judgment impossible."

### On Ex Parte Communications

> "A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications or other communications made to the judge outside the presence of all parties regarding a pending proceeding."
> — Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(A)(4)

> "[J]udges must instruct and train their personnel—including court clerks and FOC workers—not to engage in or permit ex parte communications on substantive issues."
> — Michigan Ethics Opinion JI-134

### On Appearance of Impropriety

> "The appearance of impropriety — such as financial ties between the trial judge and a party's attorney — requires judicial disqualification, even without a showing of actual bias or prejudice."
> — *People v. Perkins*, 193 Mich. App. 209 (1992)

## 10. Appeal Path if Denied

### Step-by-Step Appeal Process

| Step | Action | Rule | Timeline |
|------|--------|------|----------|
| 1 | Motion heard by Judge McNeill | MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a) | Initial hearing |
| 2 | If denied → Request Chief Judge review (de novo) | MCR 2.003(D)(3)(b) | Immediately after denial |
| 3 | If Chief Judge denies (or is unavailable/disqualified) → SCAO assigns another judge | MCR 2.003(D)(3)(c) | Upon request |
| 4 | If all deny → Application for Leave to Appeal (interlocutory) | MCR 7.203(B), 7.205 | Must show substantial harm from waiting |
| 5 | Alternative → Petition for Writ of Mandamus | Extraordinary remedy | Only where clear legal duty to recuse and no adequate remedy |
| 6 | Preserve for appeal after final judgment | MCR 7.203(A) | After final order entered |

### Key Notes on Appeal

- **Not immediately appealable as of right.** Denial of disqualification is NOT a final order — must seek leave to appeal under MCR 7.203(B)/7.205.
- **Standard of review:** Abuse of discretion on factual findings (*People v. Wells*).
- **Mandamus is rare** but available where judge has clear legal duty to recuse. Requires showing: (1) clear legal duty to recuse, (2) no other adequate legal remedy.
- **Always preserve the issue** by requesting Chief Judge review. Failure to do so may be treated as waiver.
- **Federal backup:** If all state avenues fail, the constitutional due process basis (Caperton/Crampton) preserves the issue for potential federal habeas or §1983 claim.
