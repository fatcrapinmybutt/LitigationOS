## Overview: Criminal vs. Civil Remedies {#overview}

### Distinction Between Civil and Criminal

**Civil Remedies:** Private lawsuit for damages (Andrew v. Family members/Lori/Emily)

**Criminal Remedies:** Government prosecution of criminal conduct (Prosecutor v. Offender)

**Key differences:**

| Aspect | Civil | Criminal |
|---|---|---|
| **Plaintiff/Prosecutor** | Private party (Andrew) | State/Federal government |
| **Burden of proof** | Preponderance of evidence (50%+) | Beyond reasonable doubt (95%+) |
| **Remedy** | Money damages, injunctions | Imprisonment, fines, probation |
| **Private benefit** | Direct payment | Deterrent effect, justice |
| **Consequences** | Financial | Loss of freedom, felony record |

### Strategic Rationale for Criminal Referral

While Andrew's primary remedy is civil litigation for custody and housing, **criminal referrals serve strategic purposes:**

1. **Pressure on adversaries:** Prospect of criminal prosecution motivates settlement
2. **Corroboration:** Criminal investigation may gather evidence useful in civil case
3. **Deterrence:** Increases cost of continuing hostile conduct
4. **Truth-finding:** Criminal investigator (police, prosecutor) may uncover additional evidence
5. **Public safety:** Some conduct (child abuse, obstruction) affects broader public interest
6. **Justice accountability:** Criminal sanctions hold actors accountable for serious wrongdoing

## Child Abuse Statutes (MCL 750.136b) {#child-abuse}

### Classification and Degrees

**MCL 750.136b** defines child abuse and establishes four degrees of offense.

### First-Degree Child Abuse (MCL 750.136b(2))

**Definition:** A person commits first-degree child abuse if:

1. The person:
   - Knowingly or intentionally causes serious physical injury to a child
   - OR knowingly or intentionally causes serious mental injury to a child (including by inflicting severe psychological abuse)

2. AND acts with intent to cause injury or with knowledge the conduct will cause injury

**Punishment:** Felony, up to 10 years imprisonment

### Second-Degree Child Abuse (MCL 750.136b(3))

**Definition:** A person commits second-degree child abuse if:

1. The person:
   - Intentionally causes physical injury to a child (not necessarily serious)
   - OR engages in child endangerment (reckless conduct likely to cause injury)

2. OR negligently causes serious physical injury

**Punishment:** Felony, up to 4 years imprisonment

### Third-Degree Child Abuse (MCL 750.136b(4))

**Definition:** A person commits third-degree child abuse if:

1. The person:
   - Recklessly causes physical injury to a child
   - OR negligently causes physical injury while violating another law

2. OR uses child in drug manufacturing, drug trafficking, or drug possession

**Punishment:** Misdemeanor or felony (0-2 years)

### Fourth-Degree Child Abuse (MCL 750.136b(5))

**Definition:** A person commits fourth-degree child abuse if:

1. The person:
   - Knowingly or intentionally acts toward a child in a manner that harms, threatens, or endangers child's health or safety
   - Includes harsh corporal punishment, confinement, deprivation of necessities

**Punishment:** Misdemeanor, up to 1 year imprisonment

2. **Impact:** Results in documented psychological harm (therapist reports, behavioral changes, etc.)

## Perjury and Subornation of Perjury {#perjury}

### Perjury Statute (MCL 750.423)

**MCL 750.423** defines perjury:

"A person who, being lawfully sworn or affirmed as a witness or required to take an oath or affirmation, willfully, deliberately, and contrary to the oath or affirmation:
1. States as true any material fact knowing it to be false, or
2. States as untrue any material fact knowing it to be true,

is guilty of perjury and shall be punished by imprisonment up to 15 years."

### Elements of Perjury

To prove perjury, must establish:

1. **Oath/Affirmation:** Witness was lawfully sworn or affirmed

2. **Statement:** Witness made specific statement of fact (not opinion)

3. **Materiality:** Statement was material to proceeding (relevant to outcome)

4. **Falsity:** Statement was false

5. **Knowledge:** Witness knew statement was false OR knew it was true but stated it was false

6. **Willfulness:** False statement was deliberately made (not mistaken)

### Application to Family Court Testimony

**Emily's testimony in Andrew's case:**

If Emily testified to facts that are:
1. Demonstrably false (contradicted by documentary evidence)
2. Knowingly false (she knew truth and testified otherwise)
3. Material (relevant to custody determination)
4. Made under oath

Then perjury charge is appropriate.

**Example perjury:**
- Emily testified: "Andrew never showed up for scheduled parenting time"
- Documentary evidence shows: Text messages, emails, calendar records proving Andrew did show up
- Conclusion: Perjury (if Emily knew truth and deliberately lied)

### Subornation of Perjury (MCL 750.424)

**MCL 750.424** defines subornation of perjury:

"A person who willfully, deliberately, and corruptly:
1. Procures, induces, counsels, or commands another person to commit perjury, or
2. Procures, induces, or counsels another to make false statements

is guilty of subornation of perjury and shall be punished by imprisonment up to 15 years."

### Application: Lori Facilitating Emily's Perjury

**If evidence shows:**
1. Lori coached Emily on false testimony
2. Lori provided false information for Emily to testify to
3. Lori pressured Emily to make false statements
4. Emily's perjury resulted from Lori's inducement

**Then Lori is guilty of subornation of perjury.**

### Burden of Proof

Prosecutor must prove **beyond reasonable doubt:**
- Testimony was false
- Witness knew it was false
- Statement was material
- False statement was deliberate

**Evidentiary support:**
- Contradictory documentary evidence (emails, texts, records)
- Testimony from other witnesses contradicting perjured testimony
- Communications showing witness was coached
- Admissions by witness

## Assault and Battery (MCL 750.81) {#assault-battery}

### Statute Definition (MCL 750.81)

**MCL 750.81** provides:

"A person who assaults or beats another person is guilty of assault and battery."

### Assault (Threat of Harm)

**Assault** is an act that places another in reasonable apprehension of immediate battery (harmful contact).

**Elements:**
1. Act by defendant
2. That causes victim to reasonably fear immediate harmful contact
3. Victim's fear is reasonable (objective standard)

**Examples:**
- Raising fist in threatening manner
- Advancing menacingly toward person
- Threatening statement coupled with ability to carry out threat

### Battery (Harmful Contact)

**Battery** is:
1. Intentional physical contact
2. That is harmful or offensive
3. Without consent

## Stalking (MCL 750.411h) {#stalking}

### Stalking Statute (MCL 750.411h)

**MCL 750.411h** defines stalking:

"An individual shall not engage in stalking.

Stalking means a course of conduct involving repeated or continuing harassment of another individual that would cause a reasonable person to feel molested, intimidated, threatened, harassed, or frightened."

### Elements of Stalking

1. **Course of conduct:** Multiple acts, not isolated incident

2. **Repeated or continuing:** Pattern over time

3. **Harassment:** Conduct that is harassing, threatening, or intimidating

4. **Objective reasonableness:** Reasonable person would feel molested, intimidated, threatened, or frightened

### Stalking Examples

- Repeated unwanted contact (calls, texts, emails)
- Following or surveilling person
- Threatening communications
- Property damage or threats against property
- Interference with person's relationships or activities
- Posting threatening or denigrating messages

### Punishment

**Stalking:** Misdemeanor, up to 1 year or $1,000 fine

**Aggravated stalking** (with explicit threat or violation of restraining order): Felony, up to 5 years

### Application to Andrew's Case

**Potential stalking conduct:**
- Albert Watson or Lori making repeated threatening statements
- Coordinated interference with parenting time (pattern)
- Threatening communications from Cody Watson (if pattern)
- Repeated attempts to poison child against Andrew
- Interference with relationship despite court orders

**Elements analysis:**
- Is there course of conduct? (Multiple incidents over time)
- Is conduct harassing/threatening? (Yes—interference with custody, threats)
- Would reasonable person be intimidated? (Likely—losing parenting time with child)

**If pattern established, stalking charge may be appropriate.**

## PPO Violations as Criminal Contempt (MCL 600.2950a) {#ppo-contempt}

### Protection Order Violations (MCL 600.2950a)

**MCL 600.2950a** makes violation of court-ordered personal protection order (PPO) a crime:

"A person who violates a protective order issued under this act is guilty of contempt of court."

### Types of Violations

**Common PPO violations:**
1. Contact (personal, by phone, email, through third party)
2. Physical proximity (within specified distance)
3. Threat or harassment
4. Interference with residence or property
5. Obstruction of movement or travel

### Punishment

**First offense:** Misdemeanor, up to 93 days or $500 fine

**Subsequent offense:** Felony, up to 5 years imprisonment or $5,000 fine

### Application to Andrew's Case

- Any contact despite PPO

- Photograph/video of violations if possible
- Witness statements

**Criminal referral:** Report each violation to law enforcement and prosecutor.

**Additional remedy:** File motion with trial court for contempt sanctions (potential jail time, additional fines).

**Foundation:**
- Intentional, systematic alienation causes serious psychological/emotional injury to child
- Damage includes loss of relationship with parent, identity confusion, emotional trauma
- Deliberately depriving child of parent-child relationship constitutes abuse

### Case Support

- Courts have found parental alienation relevant to custody modification
- Evidence of deliberate alienation can support change in custody
- Severe alienation can constitute emotional abuse under MCL 750.136b

### Criminal Application

**Prosecutorial theory:**
- Intentional, systematic alienation of child from parent = deliberate infliction of serious mental injury
- Meets elements of first-degree child abuse (MCL 750.136b(2)) or second-degree (MCL 750.136b(3))

3. **Neglect of relationship:** Willful obstruction of parent-child relationship

### CPS Investigation Process

1. **Report received:** DHHS logs complaint
2. **Priority assessment:** Determines if immediate safety risk
3. **Investigation:** CPS caseworker investigates within 24 hours (emergency) or 5 days
4. **Interviews:** Caseworker interviews child, parents, witnesses
5. **Determination:** Findings of substantiated abuse/neglect or unsubstantiated
6. **Safety plan:** If substantiated, creates safety plan to protect child

### CPS as Pressure Tool

**Strategic value of CPS report:**
- Investigation by government entity (more credible than civil litigation)
- If substantiated, creates record of abuse
- May result in court-ordered services (therapy, parenting classes)
- Can support custody modification in family court
- Creates additional leverage for settlement

## Federal Civil Rights Violations (18 USC 241-242) {#federal-civil-rights}

### Federal Civil Rights Act (18 USC 241)

**18 USC 241** prohibits conspiracy to deprive person of constitutional rights:

"If two or more persons conspire to injure, oppress, threaten, or intimidate any person...in the free exercise or enjoyment of any right or privilege secured to him by the Constitution or laws of the United States...they shall be fined or imprisoned up to 10 years."

### Application to Custody Interference

**Federal theory:**
- Andrew has constitutional right to parent his child (protected liberty interest)
- Lori, Emily, Albert Watson, Cody Watson conspired to deprive Andrew of this right
- Conspiracy evidenced by coordinated alienation efforts
- Conduct is intentional and willful

**Federal civil rights violation if:**
1. Defendants acted under color of law (unlikely here—they're private parties)
2. OR defendants conspired to violate federal rights (conspiracy with state official such as HealthWest evaluator)

### 18 USC 242: Acting Under Color of Law

**18 USC 242** targets conduct by government officials:

"Whoever, under color of any law, statute, ordinance, regulation, or custom, willfully subjects any person to deprivation of any rights secured by Constitution shall be fined or imprisoned up to 10 years."

### Application to Trial Court/Evaluator Misconduct

**If evidence shows:**
1. Trial court knowingly relied on fraudulent evidence
2. HealthWest evaluator deliberately provided biased evaluation
3. These officials were coordinating with Lori/Emily
4. Result was deprivation of Andrew's constitutional parenting rights

**Then 18 USC 242 violation may apply.**

## Obstruction of Justice and False Reporting {#obstruction}

### False Reporting to Law Enforcement (MCL 750.411)

**MCL 750.411** prohibits making false reports to police:

"A person who makes a written or oral report to a police officer or state police officer knowing that the report is false is guilty of making a false report and shall be punished by:
- Misdemeanor: up to 1 year or $1,000 fine
- Felony: up to 4 years if report causes evacuation or emergency response"

### Application to Andrew's Case

**If evidence shows:**
1. Emily or Lori made false reports to police claiming Andrew threatened, harassed, or abused child
2. Reports were knowing false accusations
3. Reports triggered police investigation/response

**Then false reporting charge is appropriate.**

- Emily called 911 claiming Andrew was threatening her/child
- Police responded
- Investigation revealed claims were fabricated
- Emily is guilty of false reporting

### Obstruction of Justice (MCL 750.411)

**General obstruction statute:** Willfully obstructing/resisting official in performance of duty

**Application:**
- Lying to court (different from perjury if no oath)
- Concealing evidence relevant to investigation
- Threatening/intimidating witnesses
- Interfering with police investigation

## Evidence Preservation and Documentation {#evidence-preservation}

### Importance of Documentation

All criminal referrals require **strong evidentiary support**. Prosecutor won't pursue case without clear evidence.

### Evidence Categories

**1. Documentary Evidence (Highest priority)**
- Emails between defendants showing coordination
- Text messages (threatening or coordinating alienation)
- Court records showing false testimony, ex parte orders
- HealthWest evaluation report with bias evidence
- Financial records showing fraudulent practices
- Screenshots of social media posts poisoning child against father

**2. Testimony/Witness Statements**
- Your testimony about incidents
- Child's statements (if documented)
- Therapist/psychologist observations
- School counselor observations
- Other family members' testimony

**3. Records**
- Medical/psychological records showing child's emotional distress
- School records showing behavioral changes
- Therapy notes documenting alienation
- Family court transcripts
- Prior police reports

### Evidence Preservation Steps

1. **Secure digital evidence:**
   - Screenshots of text messages/emails with timestamps
   - Download/save all communications
   - Back up to external drive or cloud storage
   - Print and retain physical copies

2. **Organize chronologically:**
   - Create timeline with dates, times, incidents
   - Cross-reference documents
   - Create index of evidence

3. **Label and authenticate:**
   - Mark exhibits (Email-1, Email-2, etc.)
   - Include source information
   - Note how you obtained evidence

4. **Avoid alteration:**
   - Do NOT edit, modify, or reformat evidence
   - Preserve in original format
   - Demonstrate chain of custody

5. **Maintain confidentiality:**
   - Limit disclosure of sensitive evidence (child's identity, etc.)
   - Follow privacy laws regarding child information
