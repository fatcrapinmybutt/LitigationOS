## 2. CIVIL VS. CRIMINAL CONTEMPT ANALYSIS

### Definitions

**Civil Contempt** is coercive — its purpose is to compel compliance with a court order. The contemnor "holds the keys to the jail" and can secure release by complying. Sanctions cease when compliance is achieved.

**Criminal Contempt** is punitive — its purpose is to punish past disobedience and vindicate the court's authority. Penalties are fixed and do not depend on future compliance. There is no opportunity to "purge" the contempt.

### Comparison Table

| Aspect | Civil Contempt | Criminal Contempt |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Purpose** | Coerce future compliance | Punish past misconduct |
| **Willfulness required** | Not strictly required (inability is a defense) | Yes — must be willful and intentional |
| **Ability to "purge"** | Yes — comply to end sanctions | No — fixed sentence |
| **Jail time** | Indeterminate (until compliance) | Fixed — up to 93 days per act |
| **Fines** | Up to $100 per violation (MCL 552.644) | Up to $7,500 per act |
| **Burden of proof** | Preponderance of the evidence | Beyond a reasonable doubt |
| **Due process** | Basic (notice + opportunity to comply) | Full (notice, hearing, right to counsel, potential jury trial) |
| **Right to counsel** | If jail is possible and indigent | Yes, if jail is possible |
| **Right to jury trial** | No | Only if penalty exceeds 6 months |
| **Typical use** | Denial of parenting time, nonpayment | Repeated flagrant disobedience, courtroom disruption |

### Analysis: Which Applies to Pigors v. Watson

**Primary Recommendation: CIVIL CONTEMPT**

Civil contempt is the appropriate vehicle for this case for several reasons:

1. **The goal is compliance.** Plaintiff seeks restoration of parenting time with L.D.W. — not mere punishment of Defendant. Civil contempt is designed to coerce the custodial parent into allowing parenting time as ordered.

2. **Statutory mandate.** MCL 552.644 specifically mandates civil contempt proceedings when FOC efforts to resolve parenting time disputes are unsuccessful. The statute enumerates specific remedies (makeup time, fines, jail, license suspension) tailored to parenting time enforcement.

3. **Lower burden of proof.** Civil contempt requires only preponderance of the evidence — a significantly lower bar than the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard required for criminal contempt. Given our documented 329+ days of denied parenting time and 27+ separate violations, this standard is easily met.

4. **Practical advantages.** Civil contempt allows the court to order makeup parenting time immediately. It also permits the court to modify the existing order to prevent future violations (e.g., changing exchange locations, adding enforcement conditions).

5. **Preserves escalation pathway.** Filing civil contempt preserves the option of criminal contempt if Defendant continues to violate orders after being found in civil contempt — creating an escalation ladder.

**Secondary Consideration: CRIMINAL CONTEMPT**

Criminal contempt may also be warranted given the severity and duration of Defendant's violations (329+ days, 27+ documented violations). The pattern of behavior — including filing ex parte motions to suspend Father's parenting time, obtaining orders without proper evidentiary hearings, and systematic interference documented at 1,521 PPO weaponization incidents — may constitute the "repeated, flagrant disobedience" that justifies criminal contempt.

**Strategic Recommendation:** File civil contempt as the primary motion. In the motion, reserve the right to seek criminal contempt sanctions if Defendant's pattern of willful noncompliance continues after the show cause hearing. Reference the documented pattern to establish that this is not an isolated incident but systematic obstruction.

## 4. MCL 600.1701 — CONTEMPT POWERS ANALYSIS

### Statutory Framework

MCL 600.1701 grants courts of record (including circuit courts) broad contempt powers. The statute authorizes punishment for any person guilty of:

- **(a)** Misbehavior in the presence of the court or so near as to interrupt proceedings
- **(b)** Misbehavior of officers of the court in their official capacity
- **(c)** Disobedience or resistance to a lawful writ, process, order, rule, or command of the court
- **(d)** Interference with the exercise of powers of the court
- **(e)** Disobeying a subpoena or refusing to be sworn/testify
- **(f)** Disobeying or refusing to comply with any order for payment of alimony or support

### Application to This Case

**Subsection (c) is directly applicable**: Defendant's systematic denial of Plaintiff's court-ordered parenting time constitutes "disobedience or resistance to a lawful... order... of the court."

**Subsection (f) may also apply** if support-related issues are intertwined with the parenting time violations.

### Penalties Under MCL 600.1701 / 600.1715

| Penalty | Civil Contempt | Criminal Contempt |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Fine** | Up to $7,500 (general); up to $100 per violation under MCL 552.644 | Up to $7,500 per act |
| **Imprisonment** | Indeterminate — until compliance | Up to 93 days per act |
| **Attorney fees** | Court may order payment | Court may order payment |
| **Other** | Conditional license suspension, community corrections, supervised compliance | Fixed term — no purge |

### Key Limitation

MCL 600.1701 grants the power; MCL 552.644 provides the specific enforcement mechanism for parenting time. The motion should cite both: MCL 600.1701 for the court's inherent contempt authority, and MCL 552.644 for the specific parenting-time sanctions framework.

## 5. MCR 3.208 — FRIEND OF THE COURT'S ROLE

### FOC Authority and Duties

Under MCR 3.208 and the Friend of the Court Act (MCL 552.501 *et seq.*):

1. **Investigation.** The FOC investigates complaints regarding custody, parenting time, and support violations. When a parent files a complaint about denied parenting time, the FOC reviews records to determine whether a violation has occurred.

2. **Attempted Resolution.** The FOC first attempts to resolve disputes through alternative procedures — meetings, mediation, or informal negotiation. If these efforts are unsuccessful, the FOC is **required** to commence a civil contempt proceeding under MCL 552.644.

3. **Contempt Initiation.** The FOC may file a Motion and Order to Show Cause (Form FOC 19) to initiate contempt proceedings. The FOC can also schedule a hearing before a judge or referee.

4. **Notice Requirements.** The FOC must serve the alleged violator with notice specifying:
   - The specific allegations of violation
   - The amount owed (if financial)
   - Possible sanctions if found in contempt
   - The right to request a hearing on modifying the parenting time order

5. **Hearing Participation.** The court may proceed without the FOC's presence unless the FOC has necessary evidence or information. The FOC may testify regarding its investigation findings.

### Application to This Case

**FOC (Pamela Rusco) — Muskegon County**

The FOC at 990 Terrace St, Muskegon, MI 49442 should be involved in enforcement. However, a party (including a pro se litigant) may also file independently using Form MC 230.

**Dual-Track Strategy:**
- **Track 1:** File formal complaints with the FOC for each parenting time violation. The FOC is then obligated under MCL 552.644 to initiate civil contempt if informal resolution fails.
- **Track 2:** File the party's own Motion and Order to Show Cause (MC 230) directly with the court. This ensures enforcement proceeds even if the FOC is slow to act.

### Filing Deadlines

- Parenting time complaints to the FOC should generally be filed within **56 days** of the alleged violation (per county FOC policy in Macomb, Washtenaw, and Kalamazoo counties — check Muskegon County's specific policy).
- There is no strict statute of limitations on civil contempt itself, but courts are more receptive to timely filings.

## 6. MCR 3.606 — CONTEMPT PROCEEDINGS PROCEDURE

### Initiation (MCR 3.606(A))

**Indirect contempt** (acts outside the court's presence, such as denial of parenting time) must be initiated by:

1. An **ex parte motion** supported by an **affidavit**
2. The affidavit must state **specific factual allegations**
3. The affidavit must be **verified** by someone with personal knowledge of the facts
4. The affidavit must clearly indicate whether the proceeding is **civil or criminal** contempt

### Order to Show Cause (MCR 3.606(B))

After the motion and affidavit are filed, the court will issue an **Order to Show Cause** which:

1. Requires the respondent to appear at a specified date and time
2. States the specific conduct alleged to constitute contempt
3. Lists the possible sanctions
4. Informs the respondent of their right to counsel
5. Must be served no sooner than **7 days** before the hearing (personal service) or **9 days** (service by mail)

### Hearing (MCR 3.606(C))

At the show cause hearing:

1. The respondent must explain why they should not be held in contempt
2. If the respondent fails to appear, the court may issue a **bench warrant**
3. The judge informs the alleged contemnor of charges and possible sanctions
4. The respondent may enter a plea
5. If contested, a trial date is set
6. Basic due process is required: notice, nature of charges, and opportunity to defend
7. If jail is a possible sanction and the respondent is indigent, **counsel must be appointed** or the right to counsel must be waived
8. There is **no right to a jury trial** unless the penalty exceeds six months' incarceration

### Due Process Requirements

Michigan courts have consistently held that even in civil contempt proceedings, the following are required:

- **Notice** of the specific allegations
- **Opportunity to be heard** and present a defense
- **Right to counsel** if incarceration is possible
- **Specificity of charges** — the respondent must know exactly what conduct is alleged
- **Proper service** of all papers

Failure to provide these protections is grounds for reversal on appeal. See *In re Contempt of Rochlin*, 186 Mich App 639 (1990).

## 7. MOTION FOR SHOW CAUSE PROCEDURE

### Step-by-Step Filing Process

#### Step 1: Prepare the Motion and Supporting Affidavit

- Use SCAO Form **MC 230** (Motion, Affidavit, and/or Order to Show Cause) — this is the party-filed form
- Complete all identifying information (case number, court, parties)
- In the affidavit section, state **specific facts** for each violation:
  - Date of the violation
  - What the court order required
  - How Defendant violated the order
  - Any communications or attempts to exercise parenting time
  - Witnesses, if any

#### Step 2: Compile Supporting Evidence

- Attach copies of the violated court order(s)
- Parenting time logs showing denied dates (329+ days)
- Communications (texts, emails) showing attempts to exercise parenting time and Defendant's refusals
- Documentation of the ex parte motion filed 8/8/25 and resulting order
- FOC complaints previously filed
- Any police reports or witness statements

#### Step 3: File with the Court Clerk

- File the original motion, affidavit, and all exhibits with the **Circuit Court Clerk, Muskegon County**
- Pay the applicable filing fee (check current schedule — typically $20-$80 for motions)
- Obtain filed-stamped copies

#### Step 4: Obtain the Order to Show Cause

- Present the motion to the **judge** (or the judge's clerk) for signature on the Order to Show Cause portion
- The judge sets a hearing date (typically 14-28 days out)
- The Order to Show Cause directs Defendant to appear and explain

#### Step 5: Serve Defendant

- Serve the filed motion, affidavit, exhibits, and signed Order to Show Cause on Defendant
- Service methods: **personal service** (preferred) or **first-class mail** (add 2 days to the notice period)
- Minimum notice: **7 days** (personal service) or **9 days** (mail service) before the hearing
- File proof of service with the court

#### Step 6: Serve the Friend of the Court

- Provide a copy to the FOC (Pamela Rusco)
- The FOC may file a response or recommendation

#### Step 7: Prepare for the Hearing

- Organize evidence chronologically
- Prepare a summary of violations (dates, descriptions, evidence references)
- Prepare proposed findings of fact and proposed order
- Bring originals and copies of all exhibits for the judge, Defendant, and court file

#### Step 8: Attend the Show Cause Hearing

- Present evidence methodically
- Be prepared to address Defendant's "good cause" defenses
- Request specific remedies (see Section 8)

## 8. REMEDIES AVAILABLE

### Under MCL 552.644 — Parenting Time Contempt

If the court finds Defendant in contempt for violating a parenting time order without good cause, the following sanctions are available:

#### 8.1 Makeup Parenting Time (MCL 552.642, 552.644(1)(c))

- Court orders parenting time of the **same type and duration** as was denied
- Must be exercised within **one year** of the denial
- The wronged parent **chooses the dates** with advance notice to the other parent and the FOC
- **Application:** Request 329+ days of makeup parenting time — this alone represents a massive equitable remedy

#### 8.2 Additional Terms and Conditions (MCL 552.644(1)(a))

- Court may impose **more specific terms** to enforce parenting time
- Examples: fixed exchange locations, third-party supervision of exchanges, detailed scheduling
- **Application:** Request highly specific exchange conditions to prevent future interference

#### 8.3 Modification of Parenting Time Order (MCL 552.644(1)(b))

- After notice and hearing, the court may **modify** the existing order
- Must serve the child's best interests
- **Application:** Request modification to include enforcement mechanisms (e.g., police enforcement clause, specific pickup/dropoff provisions)

#### 8.4 Fine — Up to $100 Per Violation (MCL 552.644(1)(d))

- $100 × 27+ documented violations = **$2,700+ in fines**
- Fines are per-violation, creating significant cumulative financial exposure for Defendant
- **Application:** Request the maximum fine for each documented violation

#### 8.5 Jail (MCL 552.644(1)(e)-(f))

- Court may commit Defendant to **county jail**
- May include **work release** provisions
- Civil contempt jail is **indeterminate** — Defendant can secure release by complying
- Maximum for civil contempt: generally **93 days** (unless ongoing refusal to perform a specific act)
- **Application:** Request jail as a coercive sanction to compel compliance, with purge conditions (e.g., allowing uninterrupted parenting time for 90 days)

#### 8.6 License Suspension (MCL 552.644(1)(g))

- Court may conditionally suspend:
  - Driver's license
  - Occupational license
  - Recreational license (e.g., hunting, fishing)
- Suspension conditioned on future compliance
- **Application:** Request conditional suspension as additional enforcement leverage

#### 8.7 Community Corrections (MCL 552.644(1)(h))

- If available in the jurisdiction, the court may order participation in a **community corrections program**
- **Application:** Available in Muskegon County — request if jail is deemed excessive by the court

#### 8.8 Court Supervision with Conditions (MCL 552.644(1)(i))

- Court may place Defendant under **supervision** with conditions including:
  - Participation in a **parenting program**
  - **Counseling** (individual or co-parenting)
  - Regular reporting to the FOC
- **Application:** Request mandatory completion of a parenting education program

#### 8.9 Attorney Fees and Costs (MCL 600.1715; general equity powers)

- Court may order Defendant to pay Plaintiff's **costs** incurred in enforcement
- For represented parties: actual attorney fees
- For pro se parties: recoverable **costs** (filing fees, service fees, copying costs) but generally **not** the value of the pro se party's own time (see Section 10)

#### 8.10 Bench Warrant (MCL 552.644)

- If Defendant fails to appear at the show cause hearing, the court may issue a **bench warrant** for arrest
- Vehicle immobilization may also be ordered in severe cases

## 9. BURDEN OF PROOF — EVIDENTIARY STANDARDS

### Civil Contempt Standard

In Michigan civil contempt proceedings, the moving party must prove the violation by a **preponderance of the evidence** — meaning that it is more likely than not that the violation occurred.

**Key holdings:**

- *Porter v. Porter*, 285 Mich App 450 (2009): The party seeking enforcement bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that the order was violated.
- The MJI Checklist for civil contempt confirms: "preponderance of the evidence or clear and unequivocal evidence" depending on judicial discretion and context.

### What Must Be Proven

| Element | Standard | Evidence Needed |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| A clear, unambiguous order existed | Documentary proof | Copy of the parenting time order |
| Respondent had knowledge of the order | Actual or constructive notice | Proof of service; party's participation in proceedings |
| Respondent violated the order | Preponderance of the evidence | Parenting time logs, communications, police reports, witness statements |
| Violation was without good cause | Shift to respondent | Once violation is shown, burden shifts to Defendant to prove good cause |

### Burden-Shifting Framework

1. **Plaintiff's initial burden:** Prove (by preponderance) that (a) a clear order existed, (b) Defendant knew about it, and (c) Defendant failed to comply.

2. **Burden shifts to Defendant:** Once Plaintiff establishes the violation, Defendant must prove "good cause" for noncompliance under MCL 552.645.

3. **"Good cause" is narrowly defined:** Under the Support and Parenting Time Enforcement Act, good cause means circumstances genuinely beyond the party's control. Vague claims of "safety concerns" without supporting evidence, or use of ex parte motions as pretextual devices, do not constitute good cause.

### Criminal Contempt Standard (If Pursued)

If criminal contempt is also sought, the standard is **beyond a reasonable doubt** — significantly higher. However, given 1,065 evidence items for contempt and 4/4 elements satisfied at 100%, even this higher standard may be achievable for the most egregious violations.

### Application to Our Case

With 329+ documented days of denied parenting time, 27+ separate violations, and 1,065 evidence items in the database, the preponderance standard for civil contempt is satisfied with overwhelming evidence. The evidence portfolio includes:

- **Documentary evidence:** Court orders, FOC records, docket entries
- **Communication records:** Texts, emails showing denial of parenting time
- **Pattern evidence:** 25 contempt-related claims, 1,521 PPO weaponization incidents
- **Temporal evidence:** 329+ days spanning the duration of the case
- **Defendant's own filings:** Ex parte motion of 8/8/25 constitutes an admission of intent to deny parenting time

## 10. PRO SE CONSIDERATIONS

### General Pro Se Rights in Michigan

Pro se litigants in Michigan are held to the **same standards** as attorneys regarding procedural rules, evidence, and substantive law. However, courts are encouraged to ensure pro se parties understand the proceedings and their rights.

### Attorney Fees — Critical Limitation

**General Rule:** Pro se litigants generally **cannot** recover "attorney fees" for their own time spent on legal work. This is because attorney fees are intended to reimburse costs of hiring a separate legal professional.

- *Kay v. Ehrler*, 499 U.S. 432 (1991): Non-lawyer pro se litigants cannot be awarded attorney fees for their own time.
- This rule is followed in Michigan under both MCL 600.2591 and the general "American Rule" (each party pays their own fees unless a statute says otherwise).

**What Pro Se Litigants CAN Recover:**

| Recoverable | Not Recoverable |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Court filing fees | Value of own time |
| Service of process costs | "Attorney fees" for self-representation |
| Copying/printing costs | Research time |
| Mileage to court (if ordered) | Preparation time |
| Expert witness fees (if any) | Lost wages (generally) |
| Postage costs | |

### Strategic Workaround for Costs

Even though attorney fees cannot be recovered as a pro se party, the court's contempt sanctions include:

1. **Fines** — up to $100 per violation (27+ violations = $2,700+)
2. **Makeup parenting time** — the primary remedy, which has no cost barrier
3. **License suspension** — punitive leverage with no cost to Plaintiff
4. **Jail** — the ultimate coercive sanction

**Recommendation:** Focus the motion on remedies that don't depend on attorney-fee recovery. Emphasize makeup parenting time, fines, and behavioral compliance orders.

### Pro Se Filing Tips

1. **Use SCAO forms exactly as designed.** Do not create custom forms — judges expect standardized forms and may reject non-conforming filings.

2. **Type everything.** Handwritten motions are technically acceptable but make a poor impression.

3. **Cite statutes and rules by number.** Reference MCL 552.644, MCR 3.208, MCR 3.606 in your motion — this demonstrates competence and helps the judge.

4. **Attach the violated order.** Always include a copy of the specific order Defendant violated.

5. **Be specific about dates and facts.** Avoid generalities like "Defendant has repeatedly denied parenting time." Instead: "On [date], Plaintiff arrived at [location] at [time] as required by the [date] Order. Defendant refused to present the child, stating [specific words if known]."

6. **Bring extra copies.** Bring at least 4 copies of everything: original for the court, one for Defendant, one for the FOC, and one for your records.

7. **Request a findings of fact.** Ask the judge to make specific findings of fact on the record — these are critical if an appeal becomes necessary.

## 14. WINNING STRATEGIES

### Strategy 1: Lead with the Numbers

329+ days of denied parenting time and 27+ documented violations are devastating numbers. Lead with them. Courts respond to quantified patterns, not generalized complaints.

**Presentation:** Create a single-page violation summary chart — Date | What Order Required | What Happened | Days Denied. Let the chart speak.

### Strategy 2: Focus on the Child's Best Interests

Frame every argument around the child's interests under MCL 722.23. Parenting time exists for the child's benefit, not the parent's. Defendant's interference harms the child's relationship with Father, which MCL 722.27a(1) presumes is in the child's best interests.

### Strategy 3: Demonstrate Willfulness Through Pattern

A single missed exchange could be inadvertent. 27+ violations over 329+ days is a pattern that proves willfulness by its very existence. The ex parte motion of 8/8/25 is particularly powerful — Defendant affirmatively sought court intervention to deny parenting time, proving deliberate intent.

### Strategy 4: Exhaust FOC Remedies First (or Show You Tried)

Courts expect parties to work with the FOC before filing contempt. Document every FOC complaint filed and every response (or lack thereof). If the FOC has been unresponsive or ineffective, that strengthens the case for judicial intervention.

### Strategy 5: Request Specific, Measurable Remedies

Don't ask for vague relief ("reasonable parenting time"). Request:
- Specific makeup dates and times
- Specific fines ($100 × [number] violations = $[amount])
- Specific compliance conditions
- Specific consequences for future violations

Judges are more likely to grant specific requests than open-ended ones.

### Strategy 6: Prepare for the "Good Cause" Defense

Defendant will likely argue safety concerns, the child's wishes, or prior court authorization. Prepare rebuttals:
- "Safety concerns" without CPS findings or police reports are unsupported
- A child's wishes don't override court orders (especially for young children)
- Ex parte orders obtained without proper evidentiary hearings do not constitute "good cause"

### Strategy 7: Document Everything in Real Time

From today forward, document every attempt to exercise parenting time:
- Send text messages confirming pickup times ("I will be at [location] at [time] per the Court's order of [date]")
- If denied, document the denial immediately ("Arrived at [time]. [Defendant/child] was not present. No communication received.")
- Take timestamped photos/screenshots

### Strategy 8: Be Professional and Factual

As a pro se litigant, your conduct is your best advocacy. Be respectful, organized, and factual. Avoid emotional outbursts, personal attacks, or irrelevant grievances. Let the evidence speak.

### Strategy 9: Request Progressive Sanctions

Start with makeup parenting time and fines. Tell the court you seek compliance, not punishment — this aligns with civil contempt's purpose. But make clear that if Defendant continues to violate orders, escalation (including jail) is appropriate.

### Strategy 10: Preserve the Appellate Record

Request that the court make specific findings of fact on the record. If the court denies relief, these findings (or the absence of them) are essential for any appeal. Ask the court to state:
- Whether a clear order existed
- Whether Defendant violated it
- Whether the violation was willful
- Whether Defendant established good cause
