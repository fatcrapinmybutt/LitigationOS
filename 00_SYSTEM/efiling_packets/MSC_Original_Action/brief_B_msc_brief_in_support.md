## INTRODUCTION

This Brief is submitted in support of Petitioner's Petition for Superintending Control filed in this Court as an original action.

Petitioner Andrew J. Pigors is the father of a young child, L.D.W., born November 9, 2022. For over 700 consecutive days, Petitioner has been completely separated from his daughter — not because any court has found that contact with her father would endanger her, but because the trial court eliminated all parenting time without conducting the analysis required by Michigan law, without holding an evidentiary hearing, and without providing Petitioner the procedural protections guaranteed by the Michigan and United States Constitutions.

This is not a case where a trial court exercised discretion within the bounds of law and Petitioner merely disagrees with the result. This is a case where the trial court **failed to perform mandatory legal duties**, **exceeded its jurisdiction**, and created a **shadow record** of evidence that Petitioner was never permitted to see or challenge. The trial court directed evaluations to be submitted to the "judge's secretary, not to be filed with clerk" — thereby ensuring that the evidence upon which it relied would never appear in the official record and could never be reviewed on appeal.

These are not errors subject to correction through normal appellate review. They are **structural failures** that have corrupted the proceedings from the ground up and that only this Court's exercise of superintending control can remedy.

## I. JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

### A. This Court's Jurisdiction

This Court possesses original jurisdiction over this Petition under Const 1963, art 6, § 4, which vests this Court with "general superintending control over all courts" and "power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs." MCR 7.304(A) authorizes original proceedings in this Court, and MCR 7.306(A) specifically authorizes complaints for superintending control.

MCL 600.1701 provides complementary statutory authority for superintending control proceedings, confirming the broad scope of this Court's power to correct lower court action.

### B. Standard for Superintending Control

Superintending control is an extraordinary remedy. This Court has established a three-part test:

**First**, the party must demonstrate that the lower court has failed to perform a clear legal duty or has exceeded its jurisdiction. *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351–352 (1975); *Hinkle v Wayne Co Clerk*, 467 Mich 337, 340 (2002).

**Second**, the party must show there is no other adequate legal remedy available. *Lapeer County Clerk v Lapeer Circuit Court*, 469 Mich 146, 155 (2003).

**Third**, the party must demonstrate that the extraordinary writ is necessary to prevent material injustice. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–442 (1993); *In re Hague*, 412 Mich 532 (1982).

This standard is informed by the historical scope of mandamus and related prerogative writs, but is broader. *Chevalier v Barnard*, 290 Mich 202, 206 (1939); *Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich 478, 480 (1929). The Court exercises superintending control "to prevent a failure of justice" and to ensure that courts within its supervision comply with the law. *Teasel v Dep't of Mental Health*, 419 Mich 390, 408–409 (1984).

This brief demonstrates that all three elements are met.

## II. THE THREE-PART TEST FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL IS SATISFIED

### A. The Trial Court Failed to Perform Clear Legal Duties

#### 1. Failure to Conduct Best Interest Analysis

Michigan law is unequivocal: before any court may modify custody or parenting time, it must evaluate and make findings on all 12 statutory best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23(a)–(l). *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994) ("[T]he trial court is required to consider all the statutory best interest factors."); *DeRose v DeRose*, 469 Mich 320, 333 (2003); *Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457, 465 (1996).

This is not a discretionary act — it is a mandatory legal duty. A trial court that modifies custody without conducting this analysis has, by definition, failed to perform a clear legal duty.

The trial court in this case has never conducted the MCL 722.23 analysis. It suspended all parenting time — the functional equivalent of awarding sole physical custody to the mother — without any findings on any of the 12 factors. This is a textbook case of a trial court failing to perform a mandatory duty.

#### 2. Failure to Hold Evidentiary Hearings

MCR 3.210(C) requires the trial court to hold a hearing before entering a custody or parenting time order when a party has requested one. MCR 3.207(B) governs interim orders and limits the circumstances under which a court may act without a full hearing.

Petitioner repeatedly requested evidentiary hearings. The trial court denied these requests and entered dispositive orders without any hearing. This constitutes a failure to perform the duty imposed by the court rules.

#### 3. Failure to Protect Due Process Rights

Under both the Michigan Constitution (art 1, § 17) and the United States Constitution (Amend XIV), a parent is entitled to notice and an opportunity to be heard before the state may interfere with the fundamental right to parent. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–754 (1982); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 649 (1972); *Lassiter v Dep't of Social Services*, 452 US 18, 27 (1981).

The right to parent is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). When a fundamental right is at stake, due process demands rigorous procedural protections.

The trial court afforded Petitioner none of these protections. It:
- Suspended parenting time without notice or hearing;
- Denied motions without conducting hearings;
- Directed that evidence be submitted outside the official record;
- Suppressed Petitioner's filings.

Each of these failures independently constitutes a failure to perform a clear legal duty. Taken together, they represent a systemic denial of due process.

#### 4. Failure to Apply Parenting Time Protections

MCL 722.27a creates a statutory presumption that parenting time is in the best interest of the child. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that parenting time "shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." MCL 722.27a(9) provides specific protections against wholesale denial of parenting time. *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 49–51 (1992); *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

The trial court disregarded these mandatory protections entirely, eliminating all parenting time without the findings required by statute. *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 499, 512 (2003); *Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556, 572 (2015).

### B. No Adequate Legal Remedy Exists

The second prong of the superintending control test requires a showing that no adequate legal remedy exists. *Lapeer County Clerk*, 469 Mich at 155. This element is clearly met.

#### 1. The Pending Appeal Cannot Provide Timely Relief

Petitioner has an appeal pending before the Court of Appeals (Case No. 366810). However, the appellate process takes months or years to complete. During this time, the separation between Petitioner and L.D.W. continues — and the harm compounds irreversibly.

L.D.W. was born on November 9, 2022. She is now approximately three years old. The over 700 days of separation span nearly all of her conscious memory. Every additional day that passes without contact permanently alters the parent-child bond. No appellate reversal, no matter how complete, can restore this lost developmental time.

*Hinkle*, 467 Mich at 340, recognizes that a remedy is "adequate" only if it can effectively redress the injury. A remedy that arrives too late to prevent irreparable harm is not adequate.

#### 2. The Appellate Record Is Corrupted

The trial court's practice of routing evidence outside the official record means that the Court of Appeals will be reviewing an incomplete record. Evidence considered by the trial court in making its rulings will not appear in the record on appeal. This structural defect cannot be cured through the normal appellate process — it can only be addressed through this Court's exercise of superintending control, which allows examination of the trial court's conduct itself, not merely the adequacy of the record.

#### 3. Over 700 Days Without a Remedy

Petitioner has now gone over 700 days without meaningful access to his child. He has filed motions in the trial court. He has appealed. He has preserved every issue. And yet he remains completely separated from his daughter, with no end in sight through normal channels.

This duration — over two years — demonstrates that the available remedies are not merely slow, but fundamentally inadequate to address the ongoing constitutional injury.

### C. Extraordinary Circumstances Warrant This Court's Intervention

The third prong requires a showing that the extraordinary writ is necessary to prevent material injustice. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 441–442; *In re Hague*, 412 Mich at 540.

#### 1. Fundamental Constitutional Rights

The rights at stake — parental liberty and due process — are among the most fundamental recognized by American law. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Santosky*, 455 US at 753; *Stanley*, 405 US at 649. When fundamental rights are being violated on an ongoing basis, material injustice is self-evident.

#### 2. A Child's Welfare

L.D.W. is not merely a subject of litigation — she is a person whose welfare is at stake. Developmental science is clear that prolonged separation from a parent during early childhood causes lasting harm. This Court has a responsibility not only to the parties, but to the child whose interests the trial court was supposed to protect.

#### 3. Institutional Integrity

The Real Party in Interest's employment within the Michigan court system — specifically, the Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division — raises concerns that transcend this individual case. When a court system's own employee receives favorable treatment in a custody dispute, public confidence in the judiciary is undermined. This Court's exercise of superintending control would affirm its commitment to the impartial administration of justice.

#### 4. Access to Justice

Petitioner is proceeding pro se. The Michigan Constitution (art 1, § 13) guarantees every suitor the right to prosecute or defend their suit. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 374 (1971), recognizes that where the state holds a monopoly over the resolution of fundamental disputes, it must ensure meaningful access. *M.L.B. v S.L.J.*, 519 US 102, 116–117 (1996), extends this principle to parental rights cases.

The trial court's systematic exclusion of Petitioner from the proceedings — suppressing filings, denying hearings, channeling evidence away from the record — has denied Petitioner the meaningful access to justice guaranteed by the Constitution.

## III. THE TRIAL COURT'S CONDUCT REQUIRES DISQUALIFICATION

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides for disqualification of a judge when, "from an objective standpoint," the judge's conduct "would create in the mind of a reasonable person a perception of a lack of impartiality."

The cumulative record establishes that a reasonable person would perceive a lack of impartiality in the trial court's handling of this case:

1. **Ex parte communications** with parties or counsel without notice to Petitioner;
2. **Off-docket evidence routing** — directing that evaluations be submitted to the judge's secretary rather than filed with the clerk;
3. **Systematic denial of hearings** on Petitioner's motions while granting relief to the Real Party in Interest;
4. **Suppression of Petitioner's filings** from the official record;
5. **Failure to acknowledge the Watson employment nexus** and its implications for impartiality;
6. **700+ days of parental separation** sustained without any evidentiary hearing or best interest findings.

Under *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 326 (2006), the question is not whether the judge is actually biased, but whether a "reasonable, informed observer would conclude that the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned." The record in this case answers that question decisively.

Petitioner respectfully requests that this Court order the disqualification of Hon. Jenny L. McNeill and the transfer of Case Nos. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP to a different judge.

## IV. THE NORMAL APPELLATE PROCESS IS INADEQUATE

This section addresses directly why the pending Court of Appeals case (No. 366810) does not moot or preclude this Petition.

**First**, the COA cannot order the immediate restoration of parenting time pending appeal. Only this Court, exercising its superintending control power, can direct the trial court to act immediately.

**Second**, the COA's review is limited to the record. The trial court has deliberately corrupted that record by routing evidence outside it. The COA cannot review what it cannot see.

**Third**, even if the COA reverses, the case will be remanded to the same judge — perpetuating the same structural problems. Only this Court can order disqualification and reassignment.

**Fourth**, the timeline of normal appellate review is measured in months and years. L.D.W.'s childhood is measured in the same units. The ordinary pace of litigation is fundamentally incompatible with the urgency of a young child's developmental needs.

For all these reasons, the pending appeal does not provide an adequate remedy, and this Court's intervention is necessary.
