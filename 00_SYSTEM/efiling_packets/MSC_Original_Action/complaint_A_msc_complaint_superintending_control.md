## I. JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT

1. This Court has original jurisdiction over this Complaint for Superintending Control pursuant to Const 1963, art 6, § 4, which provides:

> "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts; power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs; and appellate jurisdiction as provided by rules of the supreme court."

2. MCR 7.304(A) authorizes the filing of an original action in this Court, including complaints for superintending control under MCR 7.306.

3. MCR 7.306(A) provides that a complaint for superintending control may be filed when a lower court has failed to perform a duty, has exceeded its jurisdiction, or has otherwise acted in a manner requiring correction.

4. MCR 7.316(A)(1) authorizes this Court to issue orders of superintending control "to correct or prevent a clear legal error by a lower court or tribunal when the error would otherwise not be subject to adequate appellate review."

5. MCL 600.1701 provides additional statutory authority for superintending control proceedings.

6. Petitioner has a related appeal pending before the Michigan Court of Appeals, Case No. 366810, which does not provide adequate or timely relief for the extraordinary circumstances presented here.

## IV. STANDARD FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL

21. Superintending control is an extraordinary remedy available when the normal appellate process is inadequate. This Court has articulated a three-part test for the exercise of superintending control:

### A. Three-Part Test

22. **First**, the party seeking relief must demonstrate that the lower court has failed to perform a clear legal duty, or has exceeded its jurisdiction. *Hinkle v Wayne Co Clerk*, 467 Mich 337, 340 (2002); *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351–352 (1975).

23. **Second**, the party must show there is no other adequate legal remedy. *Lapeer County Clerk v Lapeer Circuit Court*, 469 Mich 146, 155 (2003).

24. **Third**, the party must show that the issue presented is one of significant public interest or that the failure to act will result in material injustice. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–442 (1993).

### B. Relationship to Mandamus

25. While superintending control is related to the common-law writ of mandamus, it is broader in scope. This Court has explained that superintending control "is not limited to the traditional grounds for mandamus, but also encompasses review of clear legal errors that would otherwise not be subject to adequate appellate review." *Chevalier v Barnard*, 290 Mich 202, 206 (1939); *Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich 478, 480 (1929).

### C. Standard Applied Here

26. As demonstrated below, Petitioner satisfies all three elements:

- The trial court has failed to perform mandatory legal duties (best interest analysis, evidentiary hearings, due process protections) and has exceeded its jurisdiction (ex parte orders, off-docket evidence);
- No adequate legal remedy exists because the pending appeal cannot prevent the ongoing daily harm of parent-child separation; and
- The constitutional dimensions of this case — involving the fundamental right to parent, structural court bias, and the welfare of a minor child — present issues of significant public interest.

## V. ARGUMENT

### A. The Trial Court Failed to Perform Clear Legal Duties

27. **Mandatory Best Interest Analysis.** Under MCL 722.23, a trial court must evaluate all 12 statutory best interest factors before making any custody determination or modification. This is not discretionary — it is a mandatory legal duty. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994) (holding that the trial court must make findings on each of the statutory best interest factors).

28. The trial court in this case has never conducted this mandatory analysis. It suspended Petitioner's parenting time and effectively modified custody without any findings on the statutory factors. This constitutes a clear failure to perform a legal duty.

29. **Due Process Protections.** The Due Process Clauses of the Michigan and United States Constitutions require, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a parent's custodial rights are modified. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–754 (1982); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 649 (1972).

30. The right to parent is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Accordingly, interference with this right demands the highest procedural protections.

31. The trial court denied Petitioner these protections by:
   - Suspending parenting time without notice or hearing;
   - Denying motions without hearings in violation of MCR 2.119;
   - Suppressing Petitioner's filings from the record;
   - Channeling evidence outside the record and beyond Petitioner's ability to review and challenge.

32. **Evidentiary Hearing Requirements.** MCR 3.210(C) and MCR 3.207(B) establish requirements for hearings in custody and parenting time matters. The trial court's refusal to conduct evidentiary hearings before making dispositive custody rulings violates these rules.

33. **Parenting Time Protections.** MCL 722.27a establishes that parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child and creates a presumption that parenting time is in the child's best interest. The trial court's wholesale elimination of parenting time without the required analysis is a clear legal error. See *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 49–51 (1992).

### B. The Trial Court Exceeded Its Jurisdiction

34. A court exceeds its jurisdiction when it acts in a manner not authorized by law. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 442 (1993). The trial court exceeded its jurisdiction in multiple respects:

35. **Ex Parte Orders Without Authority.** The trial court entered orders affecting custody and parenting time on an ex parte basis without the emergency circumstances required by MCR 3.207(B). Ex parte modification of parental rights is permitted only in narrow, emergency circumstances — none of which were present here.

36. **Off-Docket Evidence Routing.** The trial court directed that evaluations "be submitted to judge's secretary, not to be filed with clerk." By creating a shadow record of evidence considered by the court but not available to the parties, the trial court exceeded the bounds of proper judicial conduct and its authority under the Michigan Court Rules.

37. **Judicial Bias and Failure to Recuse.** The trial court's conduct demonstrates bias and prejudice against Petitioner warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). The cumulative pattern — ex parte contacts, one-sided evidence consideration, denial of hearing rights, and suppression of Petitioner's filings — would cause a reasonable person to question the court's impartiality. See *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 326 (2006).

### C. No Adequate Legal Remedy Exists

38. The pending appeal (COA Case No. 366810) does not provide an adequate remedy for the injuries Petitioner suffers. The normal appellate process takes months to years to resolve. During this time:

39. **Irreparable Harm Continues Daily.** Every day that Petitioner is separated from L.D.W. causes irreparable harm to the parent-child bond. A child's formative years cannot be recaptured. The separation has now exceeded 700 days — nearly the entirety of L.D.W.'s conscious memory. No appellate reversal can restore this lost time.

40. **Constitutional Injury Is Ongoing.** The deprivation of Petitioner's fundamental parental rights is not a past injury — it is an ongoing constitutional violation that compounds daily. The appeal cannot halt this ongoing harm.

41. **Structural Defects Cannot Be Cured by Appeal.** The trial court's creation of a shadow record, suppression of filings, and ex parte communications have corrupted the very record upon which any appeal must be based. An appellate court reviewing an incomplete and tainted record cannot provide meaningful relief.

42. These circumstances establish that the normal appellate process is inadequate and that only this Court's exercise of superintending control can prevent continued irreparable harm. *Lapeer County Clerk*, 469 Mich at 155; *Hinkle*, 467 Mich at 340.

### D. Extraordinary Circumstances Warrant Immediate Intervention

43. This case presents extraordinary circumstances warranting this Court's intervention:

44. **Duration of Separation.** Over 700 consecutive days of complete separation between a father and his young child, without any finding that contact would endanger the child, is extraordinary by any measure.

45. **Fundamental Rights at Stake.** The right to parent is among the most fundamental rights protected by the Constitution. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Santosky*, 455 US at 753. The wholesale elimination of this right without due process demands the highest level of judicial scrutiny.

46. **Systemic Concerns.** The Real Party in Interest's employment within the Michigan court system raises concerns about structural bias that transcend this individual case. This Court has an institutional interest in ensuring that the courts under its superintending control operate free from even the appearance of favoritism.

47. **Child Welfare.** L.D.W. is being deprived of a meaningful relationship with her father during the most critical developmental period. Developmental science establishes that prolonged parent-child separation during early childhood causes lasting psychological harm. This Court has both the authority and the responsibility to act.

48. **Public Interest.** This case raises issues of significant public interest regarding the procedural protections owed to parents in custody proceedings, the limits of judicial discretion, and the accountability of trial courts operating under this Court's superintending authority.
