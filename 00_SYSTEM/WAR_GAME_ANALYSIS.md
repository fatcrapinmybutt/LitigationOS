### Top 5 Defense Arguments & Our Counters

#### Defense #1: Judicial Immunity (Stump v. Sparkman, 435 U.S. 349) — Judge McNeill is absolutely immune from §1983 liability for judicial acts

**🛡️ Their Argument:** Judicial Immunity (Stump v. Sparkman, 435 U.S. 349) — Judge McNeill is absolutely immune from §1983 liability for judicial acts

**⚔️ Our Counter:** Judicial immunity does not apply to (1) non-judicial acts (administrative decisions, ex parte communications outside proceedings) or (2) acts taken in complete absence of jurisdiction. Issuing 24+ ex parte orders without statutory authority exceeds jurisdiction. Pulliam v. Allen — injunctive relief available against judges. The claim targets the PATTERN, not individual rulings. Seek injunctive/declaratory relief, not damages against judge personally.

**⚠️ Honest Weakness:** Judicial immunity is extremely broad. Courts almost always find judges acting in judicial capacity. The 'absence of jurisdiction' exception is vanishingly narrow. This is the strongest defense.

#### Defense #2: Rooker-Feldman Doctrine — Federal court lacks jurisdiction to review state court judgments

**🛡️ Their Argument:** Rooker-Feldman Doctrine — Federal court lacks jurisdiction to review state court judgments

**⚔️ Our Counter:** Rooker-Feldman only bars claims that are 'inextricably intertwined' with state court judgments. We challenge the PROCESS, not the outcome. Due process violations in how orders were entered are independent claims. Skinner v. Switzer (2011) — §1983 claims challenging procedures are not barred. We do not ask federal court to reverse custody order.

**⚠️ Honest Weakness:** If the complaint appears to seek reversal of custody outcomes, Rooker-Feldman will bar it. Must carefully frame as procedural/constitutional challenges.

#### Defense #3: Younger Abstention — Federal court should abstain from interfering with ongoing state proceedings

**🛡️ Their Argument:** Younger Abstention — Federal court should abstain from interfering with ongoing state proceedings

**⚔️ Our Counter:** Younger requires (1) ongoing state proceedings, (2) important state interests, (3) adequate opportunity to raise federal claims. If state proceedings have concluded or if there is no adequate opportunity to raise constitutional claims in state court, Younger does not apply. Bad faith/harassment exception: state proceedings conducted in bad faith defeat abstention. Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n.

**⚠️ Honest Weakness:** If the custody case is still open/active, Younger is a real risk. Must argue bad faith exception or that proceedings are effectively concluded.

#### Defense #4: Heck v. Humphrey Bar — §1983 claims that would imply invalidity of state court judgments are barred

**🛡️ Their Argument:** Heck v. Humphrey Bar — §1983 claims that would imply invalidity of state court judgments are barred

**⚔️ Our Counter:** Heck applies to claims that necessarily imply the invalidity of a conviction or sentence. Extended to some civil contexts but narrowly. We do not seek to invalidate the custody order — we seek damages for constitutional violations in the PROCESS of entering orders. The due process claim can succeed without disturbing the underlying custody determination.

**⚠️ Honest Weakness:** Any relief that would effectively reverse custody orders triggers Heck. Must be extremely careful in framing relief sought.

#### Defense #5: No State Action — Private parties (Watson, Berry) are not state actors subject to §1983

**🛡️ Their Argument:** No State Action — Private parties (Watson, Berry) are not state actors subject to §1983

**⚔️ Our Counter:** Private parties who conspire with state actors are subject to §1983 under the conspiracy theory. Dennis v. Sparks (1980) — private parties who corruptly conspire with judge act under color of state law. Also: joint action test, nexus test. If Berry (attorney) coordinated ex parte orders with judge, he acted under color of law.

**⚠️ Honest Weakness:** Proving conspiracy between private parties and judge requires strong evidence of coordination. Circumstantial evidence may be insufficient at pleading stage.
