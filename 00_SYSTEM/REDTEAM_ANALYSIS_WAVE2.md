# I. WATSON EXPANDED COMPLAINT (13 Counts, $1M-$5.5M)

## 1. Weaknesses

- **Count IX (Fraud on the Court):** This is an extraordinarily difficult claim. Fraud on the court requires evidence of an intentional scheme directed at the judicial machinery itself. Most courts interpret this narrowly (e.g., *Hazel-Atlas Glass Co. v. Hartford-Empire Co.*, 322 U.S. 238 (1944)). The Watson defendants are private parties, not court officers -- the argument that their false statements constitute "fraud ON the court" (vs. fraud IN the court) will face a high bar. Opposing counsel will argue this should be fraud/misrepresentation, not fraud on the court.
- **Count VII (Malicious Prosecution):** Requires showing the underlying proceeding terminated in Plaintiff's favor. If the PPO was modified rather than dismissed, the "favorable termination" element is weak. Michigan follows *Friedman v. Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1 (1981) -- must show proceeding was initiated WITHOUT probable cause. The PPO issuance itself suggests the court found some basis.
- **Count XIII (Loss of Parental Companionship):** This is typically a child's claim, not a parent's. Under *Berger v. Weber*, 411 Mich 1 (1981), this cause of action belongs to the CHILD for loss of parental society. As a parent claiming loss of companionship with his child, Plaintiff may be asserting an action that Michigan hasn't recognized in this direction.
- **Database-derived harm counts** (e.g., "280 harm incidents" for Lori Watson) -- opposing counsel will challenge the methodology and admissibility of database-generated statistics as hearsay/self-serving.

## 2. Defense Predictions

- **12(b)(6) Motion:** Defense will argue Counts IX (Fraud on Court), XIII (Loss of Companionship), and possibly VII (Malicious Prosecution) fail to state a claim.
- **Anti-SLAPP / Litigation Privilege:** Defense may argue that statements made in connection with judicial proceedings are absolutely privileged under Michigan's litigation privilege (*Maiden v. Rozwood*, 461 Mich 109 (1999)), attacking Counts V (Defamation) and VI (Slander).
- **Motion to Dismiss / Consolidate:** Defense will move to consolidate with or dismiss in favor of the pending custody case 2024-001507-DC, arguing these are custody disputes repackaged as torts.
- **MCR 2.114 Sanctions Motion:** Defense will argue some counts are frivolous (particularly Fraud on Court against private parties) and seek sanctions.
- **Statute of Limitations Defense:** Defamation/slander = 1 year (MCL 600.5805(9)). Defense will demand specific dates for each defamatory statement and move to dismiss any outside the window.

## 3. 12(b)(6) Sufficiency Assessment

- **Survive:** Counts I (Conspiracy), II (IIED), III-IV (Tortious Interference), X (Conversion), XI (Unjust Enrichment), XII (NIED) -- these are well-pled with specific factual allegations.
- **At Risk:** Count V (Defamation Per Se), Count VI (Slander) -- need specific statements, dates, and third-party publication. "Database records document" is not the same as pleading specific defamatory statements.
- **Vulnerable:** Count VII (Malicious Prosecution), Count IX (Fraud on Court), Count XIII (Loss of Companionship) -- legal theories may not support the claims as pled.

## 4. Evidence Gaps

- Specific dates and verbatim content of defamatory statements (Counts V-VI)
- Documentation of "favorable termination" for malicious prosecution (Count VII)
- Third-party witness testimony corroborating intimidation (Counts I-III)
- Medical/psychological records documenting emotional distress (Counts II, XII)
- Financial records documenting actual economic losses (Counts X-XI)
- [VERIFY] tags throughout need resolution before filing

## 5. SOL Issues

- **CRITICAL:** Defamation (Count V) and Slander (Count VI) -- 1-year SOL under MCL 600.5805(9). If statements occurred more than 1 year before filing, these counts are time-barred.
- Assault/battery-adjacent claims in conspiracy -- 2 years
- General tort claims -- 3 years (MCL 600.5805(10))
- Unjust enrichment -- 6 years (MCL 600.5807(8))
- File IMMEDIATELY to preserve defamation/slander claims.

## 6. Procedural Risks

- Service on 5 defendants requires significant logistics
- Pro se filing without fee waiver may trigger filing fee issues
- Some defendants may file for removal to federal court (unlikely but possible if constitutional claims emphasized)
- Potential for defendants to retain single counsel, creating unified defense front

## 7. Strategic Recommendations

- **Strengthen before filing:** Add specific defamatory statements with dates. Remove or restructure Count IX (Fraud on Court) and Count XIII (Loss of Companionship) -- or be prepared to defend them vigorously.
- **Prioritize filing to preserve SOL** on defamation claims.
- **Prepare for consolidation battle** -- draft opposition to consolidation motion in advance.

# II. SHADY OAKS MEGA-COMPLAINT (22 Counts, $2.6M-$17.6M)

## 1. Weaknesses

- **Count I (State RICO):** Michigan RICO (MCL 750.159i-j) requires proof of a "pattern of racketeering activity" -- at least two predicate acts. The predicate acts must be independently criminal. Ledger fraud and coerced lease terms may not clearly qualify as predicate offenses. This is the most ambitious count and the most vulnerable to dismissal.
- **Damages Range ($2.6M-$17.6M):** The gap is enormous. A court may view the high end as speculative. Per *Hofmann v. Auto Club Ins. Assn.*, 211 Mich App 55 (1995), damages must be proved with reasonable certainty, not speculation.
- **Multiple Corporate Defendants:** Alden Global Capital and Partridge Equity Group are likely shielded by corporate veil. Piercing requires showing fraud or injustice -- the Partridge check endorsement is good but may not be enough standing alone.
- **Dissolved Entity Issue:** While using a dissolved entity's name in court IS problematic, converting it to a RICO predicate may be a stretch.
- **Evidence Status:** Nearly ALL exhibits are marked "PLACEHOLDER" or "TO ACQUIRE" in the evidence map. Filing with this evidence status is extremely risky.

## 2. Defense Predictions

- **Corporate Defense Battery:** Alden Global and Partridge will file 12(b)(2) (personal jurisdiction) and 12(b)(6) motions. They'll argue they're holding companies with no direct operations in Michigan.
- **Arbitration Clause:** If the lease contains an arbitration clause, defense will move to compel arbitration, potentially gutting many claims.
- **RICO Dismissal Motion:** Defense will file focused motion to dismiss the RICO count, citing failure to allege predicate criminal acts with specificity.
- **Anti-SLAPP for Attorney Defendants:** Jeremy Brown and Aaron Cox will invoke litigation privilege and argue their conduct is protected legal advocacy. Attorney immunity is broad.
- **Preemption Arguments:** Federal manufactured housing standards may preempt some state habitability claims.

## 3. 12(b)(6) Sufficiency Assessment

- **Moderate:** Fraud counts, breach of contract, IIED -- need more specific factual backing.
- **Vulnerable:** RICO (Count I), claims against Alden Global/Partridge (jurisdiction), claims against attorneys (litigation privilege).

## 4. Evidence Gaps

## 5. SOL Issues

# IV. 14TH CIRCUIT MOTIONS (8 Motions)

## 1. Weaknesses

- **Motion to Disqualify McNeill:** Judicial disqualification under MCR 2.003 is extremely difficult. Must show actual bias, not perceived bias. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996) sets a high bar. Personal observations during proceedings rarely qualify.
- **Motion to Set Aside Orders:** Challenging final judgment under MCR 2.612 after the appeal period requires showing fraud, mistake, newly discovered evidence, or void judgment. Very narrow window.
- **Motion for Contempt:** Must show willful violation of a specific court order. If custody orders are ambiguous, contempt is difficult to prove.
- **Filing Multiple Motions Simultaneously:** Courts may view this as a "paper blizzard" and react negatively, particularly from a pro se litigant.

## 2. Defense Predictions

- **Opposition to Disqualification:** Defense (and possibly the judge herself) will argue insufficient grounds. The chief judge may summarily deny.
- **Res Judicata / Collateral Estoppel:** Defense will argue that issues resolved in the Final Judgment cannot be relitigated through motions.
- **Frivolous Motion Sanctions:** If multiple motions are filed together, defense may seek MCR 2.114 sanctions.
- **Appeal Pending Argument:** If COA 366810 is pending, defense may argue the trial court lacks jurisdiction to modify orders under appeal.

## 3. Sufficiency Assessment

- Emergency motion for restoration of parenting time is the STRONGEST filing -- irreparable harm is clear and ongoing.
- Custody modification motion is well-grounded in changed circumstances.
- Support modification is straightforward if financial circumstances changed.
- Disqualification and set-aside are high-risk/high-reward.

## 4. Evidence Gaps

- Financial affidavit needed for support modification
- Affidavit of bias/prejudice needed for disqualification (specific instances)
- Documentation of changed circumstances since Final Judgment
- Proof of efforts to contact/notify opposing party (emergency motion requirement)

## 5. Procedural Risks

- **Jurisdiction Question:** With COA 366810 pending, does the trial court retain jurisdiction to modify orders? Generally yes for emergency matters and matters not on appeal, but defense will argue otherwise.
- Filing multiple motions may trigger scheduling delays
- Emergency motion requires genuine emergency showing -- court may deny if delay not demonstrated
- MCR 2.003(D) requires affidavit for disqualification -- must be properly sworn

## 6. Strategic Recommendations

- **File Emergency Motion for Restoration FIRST and ALONE** -- this is the most sympathetic filing and has the best chance of immediate relief.
- **Wait on disqualification** -- file only if emergency motion is denied and pattern of bias continues.
- **Stagger filings** -- 1-2 weeks apart to avoid "paper blizzard" perception.
- **Lead with custody modification + support modification** as a second pair.
- **Save set-aside motion** as a last resort or post-COA decision.

# V. COA EXPANDED BRIEF (14,070 words, No. 366810)

## 1. Weaknesses

- **Word Count:** At 14,070 words, this exceeds the MCR 7.212(B) limit of 16,000 words for an appellant's brief -- but it's close. Additional sections may push over.
- **Pro Se Standard:** While courts give pro se litigants leeway, the brief will be held to legal standards. Some arguments may be viewed as factual disputes rather than legal errors.
- **Vodvarka Argument:** The argument that the trial court failed to apply Vodvarka is strong, BUT the court may find that the circumstances (PPO, safety concerns) constituted proper cause for modification.
- **Harvey Argument:** The failure to make best-interest findings is strong on paper, but the COA may find substantial compliance or harmless error if the record supports the custody outcome.

## 2. Defense Predictions

- **Appellee's Brief:** Will argue (a) the trial court properly considered all factors, (b) substantial evidence supports the custody determination, (c) procedural errors were harmless, (d) Father's behavior justified the restrictions.
- **Standard of Review Argument:** Defense will emphasize the deferential "great weight" standard and argue the COA should not reweigh evidence.
- **Harmless Error:** Even if procedural errors occurred, defense will argue the outcome would have been the same.
- **Safety/Best Interest:** Defense will argue L.D.W.'s safety justified all measures taken.

## 3. Sufficiency Assessment

- **STRONG Issues:** Due process violations (Issue II) -- the progressive deprivation of parenting time without evidentiary hearings is the strongest argument. Multiple ex parte orders without notice is constitutionally problematic.
- **STRONG:** Harvey failure -- missing best-interest findings is clearly reversible error under settled law.
- **MODERATE:** Vodvarka threshold -- strong argument but the court may find changed circumstances existed.
- **MODERATE:** The 500+ days of deprivation creates powerful equitable argument but must be framed as legal error, not just unfairness.

## 4. Evidence Gaps

- Register of Actions must be certified
- All orders referenced must be in the appendix
- Transcripts: Are they available? If not, the settled statement procedure must be followed

## 5. SOL Issues

## 6. Procedural Risks

- Appendix must comply with MCR 7.212(D)
- Certificate of service required
- If transcript substitute used, must comply with MCR 7.210(B)(3)

## 7. Strategic Recommendations

- **HIGHEST PRIORITY FILING IN THE SYSTEM.** File before anything else.
- **Proofread for MCR 7.212 compliance** -- technical deficiencies = rejection.
- **Lead with due process** (Issue II) -- this is the most legally defensible and emotionally compelling argument.
