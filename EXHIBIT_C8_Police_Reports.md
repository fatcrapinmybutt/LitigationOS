# EXHIBIT C-8: Police Reports (9 NSPD Cases)

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-8
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC 1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises the North Shore Police Department (NSPD) records documenting nine separate police contacts involving Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors. The records span from 2023 through 2025 and encompass welfare checks, incident reports, and investigations initiated in connection with allegations made by or on behalf of Defendant Emily A. Watson and her family members. The critical fact established by this collection of police records is uniform and unambiguous: across all nine NSPD contacts, Plaintiff was never arrested, never charged, and never found to have committed any offense.

The NSPD records collectively refute the narrative of dangerousness that was used to justify the deprivation of Plaintiff's parental rights. Despite repeated allegations including claims of suicidal ideation, arsenic poisoning, physical assault, drug use, and threats, law enforcement investigation consistently found no basis for criminal action. In one notable report, Officer Ella Randall documented that Emily A. Watson herself admitted to methamphetamine use, directly contradicting her allegations of drug-related concerns regarding Plaintiff.

These records are official law enforcement documents, created by neutral third-party investigators in the ordinary course of police business, and they constitute the most reliable category of evidence available to rebut the factual predicates underlying the ex parte orders and contempt proceedings at issue in this case.

## Legal Significance

The police reports are essential to demonstrating that the deprivation of Plaintiff's constitutional rights lacked any legitimate factual basis. The ex parte orders (Exhibit C-2) and the ongoing suspension of parenting time were predicated on allegations of danger to the minor child, L.D.W. If those allegations had any substance, one would expect to find among nine separate police investigations at least one arrest, one charge, or one finding of wrongdoing. The complete absence of any adverse law enforcement action across all nine contacts is powerful evidence that the allegations were fabricated and that the judicial proceedings based on those allegations were fundamentally unjust.

Under Section 1983, a plaintiff must demonstrate that the deprivation of constitutional rights was caused by state action taken under color of law. The police reports establish that the state's own investigative apparatus repeatedly found no basis for the allegations that were simultaneously being used by the state court to strip Plaintiff of his parental rights. This creates a profound disconnect: the same government that investigated Plaintiff nine times and found nothing simultaneously deprived him of his most fundamental liberty interest based on the very allegations its own investigations had debunked.

The reports also support the inference that the allegations were made in bad faith as part of a coordinated campaign to weaponize the judicial and law enforcement systems against Plaintiff. The pattern of repeated, escalating allegations, each failing to produce any criminal finding, is consistent with the type of vexatious conduct that courts have recognized as evidence of malicious intent. This pattern directly supports the conspiracy claim under Section 1983.

## Key Evidence

- **9 NSPD police contacts:** Spanning 2023 through 2025
- **Zero arrests:** Not a single arrest resulted from any of the nine contacts
- **Zero charges:** No criminal charges were filed as a result of any investigation
- **Zero findings of wrongdoing:** Every investigation concluded without adverse action against Plaintiff
- **Allegation #1 - Suicidal ideation:** Welfare checks conducted, Plaintiff cleared each time (Oct/Nov 2023)
- **Allegation #2 - Arsenic poisoning:** Emergency room toxicology testing returned NEGATIVE results
- **Allegation #3 - Physical assault:** Police investigation found no evidence of physical violence
- **Allegation #4 - Drug use:** Officer Ella Randall documented that Emily A. Watson admitted to methamphetamine use, not Plaintiff
- **Allegation #5 - Threats:** AppClose communication records (Exhibit C-10) show normal parental communication
- **NS2505044 (Aug 7, 2025):** Albert Watson admission of premeditated plan for ex parte orders (detailed in Exhibit C-3)
- **Pattern of escalation:** Allegations became more severe over time as each prior allegation failed to produce results

## Constitutional Implications

The police reports are directly relevant to the due process analysis under the Fourteenth Amendment. When a court deprives a parent of custody based on allegations that the government's own investigative agencies have repeatedly found to be unsubstantiated, the deprivation lacks the rational basis required by both procedural and substantive due process. The disconnect between the investigative findings (no basis for any allegation) and the judicial action (complete deprivation of parental rights) demonstrates that the judicial proceedings were not driven by evidence or concern for child welfare, but by other motivations, including the structural bias documented in Exhibit C-6.

The reports also bear on the First Amendment retaliation claim. The pattern of false reporting to police, followed by the use of police contacts as a basis for court proceedings, constitutes a scheme to chill Plaintiff's exercise of constitutional rights. When a person knows that any attempt to exercise parental rights or access the courts will be met with a new round of false allegations and police contacts, the chilling effect on constitutional activity is severe and constitutionally cognizable.
