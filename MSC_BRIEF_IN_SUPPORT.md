## INTRODUCTION

This Brief is submitted in support of Petitioner's Petition for Superintending Control (MCR 7.304; Const. 1963, art. 6, Sec. 4) filed in this Court as an original action.

Petitioner Andrew J. Pigors is the father of a young child, L.D.W., born November 9, 2022. For over 248 consecutive days, Petitioner has been completely separated from his son — not because any court has found that contact with his father would endanger him, but because the trial court eliminated all parenting time without conducting the analysis required by Michigan law, without holding an evidentiary hearing, and without providing Petitioner the procedural protections guaranteed by the Michigan and United States Constitutions.

This is not a case where a trial court exercised discretion within the bounds of law and Petitioner merely disagrees with the result. This is a case where the trial court **failed to perform mandatory legal duties**, **exceeded its jurisdiction**, and created a **shadow record** of evidence that Petitioner was never permitted to see or challenge. The trial court directed evaluations to be submitted to the "judge's secretary, not to be filed with clerk" — thereby ensuring that the evidence upon which it relied would never appear in the official record and could never be reviewed on appeal.

**The scope of misconduct is extensively documented in the record.** A comprehensive review of the evidentiary record across both the custody proceeding (Case No. 2024-001507-DC) and the PPO proceeding (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) reveals a pervasive pattern of judicial misconduct spanning multiple distinct categories:

1. **General Conduct Episodes** — Systematic deviation from Michigan Judicial Benchbook standards, including directing evaluations to the "judge's secretary, not to be filed with clerk," thereby creating a shadow record immune from appellate review.

2. **Recusal and Bias Indicators** — Despite documented personal and professional relationships between the trial judge and adverse parties (detailed in Exhibit B), repeated refusal to recuse under MCR 2.003.

3. **Contempt and Sanction Abuse** — Petitioner was sentenced to a combined 59 days of incarceration across three show-cause proceedings, including incarceration for sending birthday messages to his child through the court-approved AppClose platform.

4. **Ex Parte Proceedings** — On August 8, 2025, five separate ex parte orders were entered in a single day without notice to Petitioner, completely suspending all parenting time. This occurred one day after a police report (NSPD NS2505044) documented that the ex parte filing was premeditated.

5. **Mental Health Evaluation Manipulation** — A court-ordered HealthWest evaluation found Petitioner fit (psychosis: 0, substance abuse: 0, danger: 0), yet this evaluation was excluded from the record while unsworn allegations were credited.

6. **Service and Notice Defects** — Multiple proceedings were conducted without proper notice to Petitioner under MCR 2.107, including proceedings that resulted in the suspension of all parenting time.

The severity of this pattern is overwhelmingly concentrated in the most serious categories. The documented misconduct is not limited to isolated procedural errors but reflects a sustained course of conduct that has deprived Petitioner of his fundamental parental rights for 248 consecutive days.

### C. This Pattern Cannot Be Addressed One Order at a Time

Circuit court remedies — motions to reconsider, motions for disqualification, objections on the record — operate on individual orders. They are designed for isolated errors. But when the documented record reveals a systematic pattern of judicial misconduct spanning six distinct categories across two related proceedings, individual motions cannot remedy the structural problem. As this Court recognized in *Maldonado v. Michigan*, the purpose of superintending control is to address situations where the lower court's conduct creates a systemic failure that cannot be corrected through ordinary appellate processes.

The trial court has explicitly told Petitioner: *"Do not file anymore, I will not look at it."* When the court itself forecloses the remedies that might correct individual errors, the only adequate remedy is this Court's exercise of superintending control over the entire proceeding.

## VI. EX PARTE ACTIONS DEMONSTRATE ONGOING IRREPARABLE HARM

### A. Documented Ex Parte Pattern

The evidentiary record demonstrates that ex parte proceedings constitute the single most dominant category of judicial misconduct in this case. The trial court's repeated resort to ex parte orders — entering orders that strip fundamental parental rights without notice, hearing, or opportunity to be heard — represents an ongoing and irreparable deprivation of Petitioner's constitutional rights. The August 8, 2025 "Five Orders Day," in which five separate ex parte orders were entered simultaneously without any notice to Petitioner, exemplifies the severity and urgency of this pattern. Each day that passes without this Court's intervention is another day that Petitioner's son grows up without his father — a harm that no retroactive remedy can undo.
