# EXHIBIT E-4: Hearing Transcripts Showing Judicial Misconduct

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-E-4
**Filing:** Formal Complaint to Judicial Tenure Commission
**Respondent:** Hon. Jenny L. McNeill (P58235)
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC, 14th Circuit Court

---

## Description

This exhibit documents Hon. Jenny L. McNeill's conduct during hearings in Pigors v. Watson, including the fourth continued hearing on the ex parte suspension of Father's parenting time. During these hearings, Hon. McNeill abandoned her role as neutral arbiter and instead: (1) cross-examined witnesses herself as if she were opposing counsel; (2) muted Father three times during a Zoom hearing while he was presenting evidence; (3) told Father to "shut my mouth" when he objected to an improper discussion; and (4) discussed with Emily A. Watson the requirement that Father take prescription medication as a condition for seeing his child — constituting the unauthorized practice of medicine and coercive conditioning of a fundamental parental right.

## Relevant Canon Violations

**Canon 3(B)(4) — Patient, Dignified, and Courteous:** A judge shall be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others with whom the judge deals in an official capacity. Telling a pro se litigant to "shut my mouth" during a hearing violates this canon on its face.

**Canon 3(B)(2) — Right to Be Heard:** Muting a party three times during a hearing effectively denies that party's right to be heard. A judge must afford every person with a legal interest the opportunity to be heard according to law.

**Canon 3(B)(5) — Performance Without Bias:** A judge shall perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice. Cross-examining witnesses on behalf of one party while silencing the other demonstrates outcome-determinative bias.

**Canon 3(B)(1) — Faithful to the Law:** Discussing the imposition of medication requirements with one party — outside the scope of any medical testimony or expert evidence — exceeds the court's authority and violates the judge's duty to follow the law.

## Key Evidence

### Judge Cross-Examined Witnesses (Judge as Advocate)

During the fourth continued hearing on the ex parte suspension of Father's parenting time, Hon. McNeill took over questioning of witnesses. Rather than allowing Father (pro se) and Mother (then represented by counsel) to conduct examination and cross-examination, Hon. McNeill directly questioned witnesses with leading questions designed to elicit testimony unfavorable to Father.

This conduct is documented in the contradiction map of court records:

> "Judicial canons require judge to remain neutral arbiter. At 4th continued hearing, Judge McNeill: (1) cross-examined witnesses herself, (2) discussed medication with one party, (3) told other party to 'shut my mouth,' (4) sentenced him to jail for objecting. Four separate neutrality violations in single hearing."

### Father Muted Three Times During Hearing

During the hearing conducted via Zoom, Father was muted three times while attempting to present evidence and make objections. Each instance of muting prevented Father from:

- Presenting exculpatory evidence
- Making timely objections to inadmissible evidence
- Cross-examining witnesses
- Preserving the record for appeal

The muting was not the result of technical difficulties. Father was selectively silenced while Mother and her witnesses were permitted to speak freely.

### "Shut My Mouth" — Verbatim Statement

When Father objected to the improper medication discussion between Hon. McNeill and Emily A. Watson, the judge told Father to "shut my mouth." This occurred on the record during a formal hearing. Father's objection was legally well-founded: neither the judge nor Emily A. Watson was qualified or legally authorized to mandate prescription medication as a condition of parenting time.

### Medication Coercion — Unauthorized Practice of Medicine

During the same hearing, Hon. McNeill and Emily A. Watson discussed on the record that prescription medication would be the "only way" Father could see his child. This discussion:

1. **Exceeded judicial authority.** No medical expert testified. No prescription was reviewed by a qualified professional. The court has no authority to practice medicine.
2. **Conditioned fundamental rights on medication compliance.** A parent's constitutional right to a relationship with their child cannot be conditioned on taking psychiatric medication absent a competency finding supported by expert testimony.
3. **Constituted unauthorized practice of medicine.** Neither Hon. McNeill nor Emily A. Watson holds a medical license. Discussing and determining that a specific individual must take prescription medication as a condition of exercising parental rights falls outside the scope of judicial or lay authority.

### Contempt for Objecting

Father's objection to the medication discussion resulted in Hon. McNeill finding Father in contempt and sentencing him to two weeks in jail (Show Cause #5). Father's "contempt" consisted of objecting — as he had every right to do — to an improper judicial discussion about forced medication. The contempt finding was itself retaliatory (see Exhibit E-5).

## Significance

This exhibit documents a single hearing in which Hon. McNeill committed four separate violations of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct. The judge abandoned neutrality by cross-examining witnesses; silenced a pro se litigant by muting him three times; used demeaning language ("shut my mouth") directed at that litigant; and engaged in the unauthorized practice of medicine by discussing forced medication requirements with the opposing party. When Father objected to this conduct, he was jailed. This pattern of conduct — silencing, demeaning, acting as advocate for one party, and retaliating against objections — is incompatible with judicial office.
