# EXHIBIT H: Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill Law Partnership Records

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-H
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit presents public records documenting the prior law partnership between the three judges who have presided over Plaintiff's interrelated cases across multiple courts in Muskegon County. Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, Hon. Kenneth Hoopes (Chief Judge, 14th Circuit), and Hon. Maria Ladas-Hoopes (60th District Court) were all partners at the law firm of Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill, located at 435 Whitehall Road, North Muskegon, Michigan. This prior professional relationship — undisclosed by Judge McNeill — creates an appearance of impropriety and a structural conflict of interest that pervades the entire Muskegon County court system for this litigant.

## Legal Significance

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification when a judge has a personal bias or prejudice concerning a party or the party's attorney. Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires a judge to avoid the appearance of impropriety. Canon 3(C)(1) requires disqualification when the judge's "impartiality might reasonably be questioned."

The partnership between all three presiding judges creates an interconnected judicial network:

1. **Judge McNeill** (14th Circuit) presides over the custody case (2024-001507-DC) and PPO case (2023-5907-PP). She was a partner at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill. She **failed to disclose** this prior professional relationship to the parties.

2. **Chief Judge Hoopes** (14th Circuit) is the judge who would receive a disqualification motion under MCR 2.003 if Father moved to disqualify Judge McNeill. He is Judge McNeill's **former law partner**. He is also the **husband** of Judge Ladas-Hoopes. Chief Judge Hoopes presided over Lane B (Case No. 2025-002760-CZ), which was dismissed with prejudice.

3. **Judge Ladas-Hoopes** (60th District Court) presides over the 60th District Court — the same court where Judge McNeill's husband, Cavan Berry, serves as an attorney magistrate. Judge Ladas-Hoopes is the **wife** of Chief Judge Hoopes and a **former partner** of Judge McNeill.

This three-court judicial network means Father cannot obtain a fair hearing before any judge in Muskegon County. The disqualification mechanism itself is compromised because the Chief Judge who would rule on a disqualification motion is Judge McNeill's former partner. This Court's original jurisdiction under MCR 7.306 is the only adequate remedy.

## Key Evidence

- **Law Firm:** Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill, 435 Whitehall Road, North Muskegon, Michigan.
- **Partners:**
  - Hon. Jenny L. McNeill (now 14th Circuit Judge) — attended Northern Illinois University College of Law (1996).
  - Hon. Kenneth Hoopes (now Chief Judge, 14th Circuit) — graduated University of Iowa College of Law (1992).
  - Hon. Maria Ladas-Hoopes (now 60th District Judge) — graduated University of Iowa College of Law (1992, same class as Hoopes).
- **Hoopes and Ladas-Hoopes:** Husband and wife. Both graduated from the same law school in the same year. Both became judges in Muskegon County courts.
- **McNeill's Non-Disclosure:** Judge McNeill never disclosed to the parties that her former law partners now preside as Chief Judge of the 14th Circuit and as a Judge of the 60th District Court.
- **McNeill's Prior Role:** Before becoming a judge, McNeill served as a Friend of the Court Referee — the same office (FOC) that now recommends against Father in the custody case.
- **Educational Ties:** McNeill (NIU College of Law, 1996) and her husband Cavan Berry (NIU College of Law, 1998) attended the same law school two years apart.

## Constitutional Implications

The Due Process Clause guarantees the right to a fair and impartial tribunal. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 876 (2009) (due process requires recusal when there is a serious risk of actual bias). When the presiding judge, the chief judge who would hear a recusal motion, and a district judge from a related proceeding are all former law partners — and when two of them are married — the structural bias is so pervasive that no remedy exists within the county court system.

This is precisely the circumstance in which the Michigan Supreme Court's superintending control authority under Const. 1963, art. 6, § 4, and MCR 7.306 is most needed: when the entire local judicial infrastructure is structurally conflicted and no adequate remedy exists at the trial court level.
