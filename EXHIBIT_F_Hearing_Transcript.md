# EXHIBIT F: Hearing Transcript — October 29, 2025

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-F
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit is the transcript (or relevant portions thereof) from the hearing held on October 29, 2025, before Hon. Jenny L. McNeill. This was the fourth continued hearing on the matter of the ex parte suspension of Father's parenting time. The transcript documents extraordinary judicial conduct: the presiding judge cross-examined witnesses herself, muted Father's audio three times during the remote proceeding, told Father to "shut my mouth" when he raised objections, and — together with Emily A. Watson — discussed conditioning Father's parenting time on his taking prescription medication. Father was sentenced to jail for contempt at this hearing, with his "contempt" consisting of objecting to the medication condition.

## Legal Significance

The hearing transcript demonstrates multiple grounds for superintending control:

1. **Judge as Advocate.** Hon. Jenny L. McNeill cross-examined witnesses herself — a function reserved for the parties and their representatives, not the bench. A judge who descends into the arena of advocacy abandons the neutrality required by the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 2(B), and the Due Process Clause.

2. **Suppression of Father's Participation.** Father was muted three (3) times during the hearing. Muting a litigant during a custody proceeding — where his fundamental parental rights are at stake — effectively denies his right to be heard. *Goldberg v. Kelly*, 397 U.S. 254, 267–68 (1970) (due process requires an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner).

3. **Medication as Condition for Parenting Time.** The transcript records a colloquy between Judge McNeill and Emily A. Watson in which they discussed requiring Father to take prescription medication as the "only way" he could see L.D.W. Neither the judge nor the opposing party is a licensed physician. Conditioning parental rights on medication compliance — absent a medical professional's recommendation introduced through proper evidentiary channels — constitutes the unauthorized practice of medicine and a coercive violation of bodily autonomy.

4. **Contempt for Exercising Rights.** Father was sentenced to jail for contempt at this hearing. His "contempt" was objecting to the medication condition — an objection that was both legally appropriate and constitutionally protected. Using the contempt power to silence a pro se litigant's objection to an unlawful condition is an abuse of judicial authority.

5. **Directive to "Shut My Mouth."** The court told Father to "shut my mouth" — a directive that, combined with the muting and contempt sentence, reveals a pattern of silencing the disfavored party rather than adjudicating the merits.

## Key Evidence

- **Hearing Date:** October 29, 2025, before Hon. Jenny L. McNeill.
- **Proceeding Type:** Fourth continued hearing on ex parte suspension of parenting time.
- **Judge Cross-Examined Witnesses:** The court took over questioning of witnesses, abandoning the role of neutral arbiter and acting as an advocate against Father.
- **Father Muted Three (3) Times:** Father's audio was cut during the remote proceeding, preventing him from being heard on his own custody matter.
- **Court Statement:** Judge McNeill told Father to *"shut my mouth"* when he attempted to object.
- **Medication Condition Discussed:** Judge McNeill and Emily A. Watson discussed requiring Father to take prescription medication as a prerequisite to seeing L.D.W. The record reflects this was described as the "only way" Father could have parenting time.
- **Contempt Sentence:** Father was sentenced to incarceration for objecting to the medication condition.

## Constitutional Implications

The hearing of October 29, 2025, illustrates a complete collapse of judicial neutrality and due process:

- **Due Process (U.S. Const. Amend. XIV):** Muting a litigant, silencing objections, and jailing him for raising them denies the meaningful opportunity to be heard that the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees.
- **Right to Bodily Autonomy:** Conditioning parental rights on medication compliance — without medical evidence or professional recommendation — violates the constitutional right to refuse unwanted medical treatment. *Washington v. Harper*, 494 U.S. 210, 221–22 (1990).
- **First Amendment:** Punishing Father with contempt for voicing an objection to an unlawful condition implicates the right to petition the government for redress. *BE&K Construction Co. v. NLRB*, 536 U.S. 516, 525 (2002).
- **Access to Courts:** Telling a litigant to "shut my mouth" and muting his audio transforms a hearing into a proceeding in name only, violating the right of access to courts guaranteed by Const. 1963, art. 1, § 13.
