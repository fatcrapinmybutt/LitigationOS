## I. INTRODUCTION

## III. PROPER CAUSE AND CHANGE OF CIRCUMSTANCES

11\. Before modifying a custody order, the Court must first determine whether the moving party has demonstrated either proper cause or a change of circumstances. MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508-509 (2003).

12\. "Proper cause" means one or more appropriate grounds that have or could have a significant effect on the child's life sufficient to warrant a reevaluation of the custody order. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 511.

13\. A "change of circumstances" requires conditions that have or could have a significant effect on the child's well-being since the entry of the last custody order. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 513; *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 93 (2010).

14\. Father demonstrates the following changes of circumstances and proper cause, each of which independently satisfies the threshold requirement and which collectively present an overwhelming case for modification:

### A. Ex Parte Custody Reversal Without Due Process

15\. On July 17, 2025, the prior court (Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, now subject to a pending disqualification motion, Filing #3) entered an ex parte order that reversed the established 50/50 custody arrangement and awarded Mother sole physical custody — **without providing Father notice or an opportunity to be heard.**

16\. This ex parte reversal eliminated 438 days of established shared custody in a single stroke, without any evidentiary hearing, without testimony, and without Father's participation. This constitutes a deprivation of Father's due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment and Const 1963, art 1, § 17. *See Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) (three-factor balancing test for procedural due process); *Fuentes v. Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67, 80 (1972) (prior notice and hearing required before deprivation of protected interest); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014) (due process protections must be applied before depriving a parent of the care and custody of a child); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753-54 (1982) (the fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents); MCR 3.207(A) (requirements for ex parte orders in domestic relations proceedings).

16a\. The ex parte order was predicated in part on a recording obtained by Lori Watson (Mother's relative and fellow Kent County employee, Employee ID 1190) in apparent violation of MCL 750.539c (felony eavesdropping — punishable by up to two years' imprisonment). Evidence obtained through illegal eavesdropping constitutes "fruit of the poisonous tree" and should not serve as the basis for custody modification. *See Mapp v. Ohio*, 367 U.S. 643 (1961); *Wong Sun v. United States*, 371 U.S. 471 (1963); *People v. Beavers*, 393 Mich 554 (1975).

16b\. The pattern of ex parte orders in this case is itself a change of circumstances. The record reflects a 44% ex parte order rate in proceedings involving these parties, with **100% of ex parte orders favoring Mother** — a statistical pattern that is facially inconsistent with neutral adjudication. *See Craig v. Boren*, 429 U.S. 190 (1976) (intermediate scrutiny for gender-based classifications); *United States v. Virginia*, 518 U.S. 515 (1996) (exceedingly persuasive justification required for gender-based disparate treatment).

### A-1. Zero Clear and Convincing Evidence Findings — August 8, 2025 Order

16c\. MCL 722.27a(3) expressly provides: "Parenting time shall not be restricted unless the court determines by **clear and convincing evidence** that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." (Emphasis added). This statutory mandate imposes a heightened evidentiary burden before a court may restrict — let alone completely eliminate — a parent's time with their child.

16d\. The August 8, 2025 order suspending **all** parenting time was issued with **zero written findings** of clear and convincing evidence. The court never articulated — either on the record or in a written order — what specific evidence met the clear and convincing standard, what specific danger to L.D.W.'s physical, mental, or emotional health was identified, or how such danger was proven to the requisite heightened standard. This omission is not a technicality; it is a structural due process failure. *Lieberman v. Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 82-83 (2017) (trial court must make specific findings when restricting parenting time under MCL 722.27a(3); restriction without clear and convincing evidence findings constitutes reversible error).

16e\. The United States Supreme Court has held that when the State moves to destroy weakened familial bonds, it must provide the parents with fundamentally fair procedures, including a heightened standard of proof. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753-54 (1982). The Michigan Supreme Court has likewise recognized that proceedings affecting parental rights require meaningful procedural safeguards. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014). The complete absence of clear and convincing evidence findings in the August 8, 2025 order renders that order constitutionally infirm and independently warrants vacatur.

16f\. The *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92-93 (2010) framework requires that before modifying an established custodial environment, the court must find clear and convincing evidence that modification serves the child's best interests — yet here the court applied this heightened burden *against* Father (to strip his custody) without ever making the findings the statute demands. The evidentiary void is fatal to the August 8, 2025 order.

### C-1. The "Second Evaluation" — Clinically Implausible, Procedurally Defective, and Evidentiary Failure

22a\. On **September 11, 2025** — just **seven (7) days** after the first evaluation established a clean baseline — a second HealthWest evaluation was conducted by **Kassandra M. Gansen** (LMSW) and **Melissa DeAugustine** (LBSW). This second evaluation produced a finding of "Rule-out Delusional Disorder." The seven-day interval between a clean baseline and a "rule-out" finding for a serious psychiatric condition is **clinically implausible**. Delusional Disorder does not manifest in seven days following a clean baseline assessment. The radical divergence between the two evaluations — conducted by the same agency within one week — demands scrutiny.

22b\. Critically, the second evaluator herself acknowledged the diagnostic uncertainty, admitting in the report: **"It is difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional."** This admission is fatal to any reliance on this evaluation. A "rule-out" designation is, by clinical definition, an expression of **diagnostic uncertainty** — it is not a confirmed diagnosis. It signifies that the condition has not been ruled in or out and requires further investigation. To treat a "rule-out" notation as a confirmed finding of delusional disorder is a misapplication of clinical terminology that no competent expert would endorse.

22c\. The second evaluation pathologized Father's statements about the legal proceedings — including his statements that "this whole thing is illegal" and "the last assessor didn't see anything wrong with me" — as evidence of delusional thinking. However, given the documented procedural irregularities in this case (ex parte orders without notice, rejection of the favorable first evaluation, suspension of parenting time without clear and convincing evidence findings), Father's concerns about the legality of the proceedings appear **factual, not delusional**. Characterizing legitimate legal criticism as psychiatric pathology constitutes circular reasoning: the court's irregular conduct generates the complaints, and the complaints are then used to justify further restrictions on the complaining parent. This reasoning would pathologize any litigant who objects to procedural irregularities.

22d\. Under MRE 901(a), the proponent of evidence must produce "evidence sufficient to support a finding that the matter in question is what its proponent claims." No proper authentication was provided for the second evaluation. No witness testified to the document's authenticity in open court. No chain of custody was established. No certification or business records foundation was laid under MRE 803(6). The document was never shown to Father in a hearing setting, never subjected to cross-examination, and never authenticated by its purported author through testimony.

22e\. Under MRE 702 and the *Daubert* framework, expert testimony — including written expert reports — must be based on sufficient facts or data, be the product of reliable principles and methods, and reflect a reliable application of those principles to the facts. *See Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.*, 509 U.S. 579, 589 (1993); *Gilbert v. DaimlerChrysler Corp.*, 470 Mich 749, 780 (2004). The second evaluation fails every prong of *Daubert*: (a) it contradicts the same agency's findings from seven days earlier without explaining why; (b) the evaluator's own admission that she cannot determine whether Father's concerns are factual or delusional demonstrates insufficient methodology; (c) a "rule-out" designation is not a reliable conclusion but an open question; and (d) pathologizing legitimate legal complaints is not a reliable application of psychiatric principles.

22f\. The reliance on a clinically implausible, procedurally defective, and diagnostically uncertain "rule-out" notation — to deprive a parent of all contact with his child, while simultaneously rejecting the authenticated, favorable first evaluation from seven days earlier — represents a fundamental failure of evidentiary procedure. This evidentiary void independently constitutes a change of circumstances warranting de novo review by an impartial judicial officer.

## IV. BEST INTEREST OF THE CHILD — MCL 722.23 FACTORS

29\. If the Court finds proper cause or a change of circumstances (which Father respectfully submits is self-evident), the Court must then evaluate the best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23. *Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004).

30\. Where the proposed modification would change the established custodial environment, the moving party bears the burden of proof by clear and convincing evidence. *Harvey*, 470 Mich at 194; *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92-93 (2010) (reaffirming the clear and convincing evidence standard and holding that the established custodial environment must be analyzed with specificity). Where modification would not change the established custodial environment, the burden is by a preponderance of the evidence. *Id.* The United States Supreme Court has held that the clear and convincing standard is constitutionally required when the State seeks to destroy permanently the parent-child bond. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 769 (1982).

31\. Father submits that the **established custodial environment** was the 50/50 arrangement that existed for 438 days, and that **restoring** that arrangement would restore — not change — the established custodial environment. Therefore, the preponderance standard applies.

Father participated in L.D.W.'s daily care, feeding, bathing, bedtime routines, and developmental activities. The emotional bond between Father and L.D.W. was strong and well-documented during the shared custody period.

33\. This bond has now been severed for more than 230 days — not by Father's choice, but by court order entered without hearing. Every additional day of separation inflicts further harm on the parent-child bond. **This factor favors Father and modification.**
