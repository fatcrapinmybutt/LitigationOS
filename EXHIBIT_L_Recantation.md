# EXHIBIT L: Emily A. Watson Recantation — October 13, 2023

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-L
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit documents the statement made by Defendant Emily A. Watson to the Norton Shores Police Department on October 13, 2023, in which she recanted her prior allegations of physical abuse. In her statement, Watson told police that *"nothing was physical."* Two days later, on October 15, 2023, Watson filed a Personal Protection Order (PPO) petition (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) alleging the very conduct she had recanted to police. This sequence — recantation followed by PPO filing — is the foundational evidence that Defendant weaponized the PPO process for custody leverage rather than for protection from genuine domestic violence.

## Legal Significance

Under MCL 600.2950, a PPO may be issued upon a showing that the respondent has engaged in specific acts of domestic violence or harassment. The integrity of the PPO system depends on the truthfulness of the petitioner's allegations.

1. **Recantation Followed by Filing.** Watson told police on October 13, 2023, that *"nothing was physical."* Two (2) days later — on October 15, 2023 — she filed a PPO petition alleging physical violence. This timeline is irreconcilable: either the October 13 statement to police was true (in which case the PPO petition was false) or the October 13 statement was false (in which case Watson lied to police). Either interpretation demonstrates that Watson's PPO filing was not a good-faith request for protection.

2. **PPO Used as Custody Weapon.** The PPO (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) became the foundation for subsequent restrictions on Father's parenting time. Under Michigan law, a PPO is not a custody determination — yet it was used as one. The PPO restricted Father's contact with L.D.W. and was later cited as grounds for contempt proceedings (Exhibit G), incarceration, and ultimately the complete termination of parenting time.

3. **Pattern of Escalating False Allegations.** The recantation-then-filing sequence was the first in a pattern of escalating false allegations documented in the police reports (Exhibit K): "suicidal" allegations (cleared), "arsenic poisoning" (ER toxicology negative), "physical assault" (police found no evidence), and "drug use" (Officer Randall documented Watson's own meth use). Each allegation was more extreme than the last, and each was unsupported by evidence.

4. **Impeachment Value.** Watson's October 13, 2023 recantation directly contradicts the sworn allegations in her PPO petition filed two days later. Under MRE 613, a prior inconsistent statement is admissible to impeach a witness's credibility. This recantation fatally undermines the credibility of every subsequent allegation Watson has made in these proceedings.

## Constitutional Implications

The weaponization of the PPO process — filing for a protection order two days after recanting the very allegations that underpin it — implicates both due process and the integrity of the courts. The PPO was not obtained for protection; it was obtained as a tactical device to restrict Father's access to L.D.W. and to create a pretext for contempt proceedings that would incarcerate Father and prevent his participation in custody proceedings.

When a court enters and enforces a PPO based on allegations that the petitioner herself recanted to police two days earlier, and when that PPO is then used as the foundation for 59 days of incarceration and the complete termination of parenting time, the judicial process has been corrupted. This Court's superintending control is necessary to address the trial court's failure to examine the credibility of the PPO petitioner in light of her documented recantation.
