# EXHIBIT K: NSPD Police Reports — Nine Contacts, Zero Arrests

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-K
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit compiles nine (9) Norton Shores Police Department (NSPD) contacts involving the parties over the period from 2023 through 2025. Across all nine contacts, Father was never arrested, never charged, and never found to have committed any offense. The reports consistently cleared Father of allegations made by Defendant Emily A. Watson and her family. Critically, one police report documents Officer Ella Randall's observation that Emily A. Watson appeared to be under the influence of methamphetamine — a finding that directly contradicts the narrative Watson presented to the family court.

## Legal Significance

The police reports serve as independent, third-party corroboration that Father posed no danger to L.D.W. and that Defendant's allegations were consistently unsupported by law enforcement findings:

1. **Pattern of Baseless Allegations.** Over the course of 2024 and into 2025, Emily A. Watson filed multiple PPO violation claims and police complaints against Father. Almost all were baseless and unsupported by law enforcement investigation. This pattern is consistent with the weaponization of legal processes for custody advantage.

2. **PPO Violation #5 — Report MU2422249 (November 15, 2024).** Officers confirmed Father had NOT violated the PPO during the November 15, 2024 custody exchange. Despite this exculpatory police report, the court found Father in criminal contempt at the hearing on this same incident (SC#5, Exhibit G) and sentenced him to 14 days' incarceration.

3. **Officer Randall's Observation — Defendant's Substance Use.** Officer Ella Randall documented that she *"believed this was a result of meth use"* when observing Emily A. Watson. This finding was never investigated by the court. Instead, the court credited Watson's allegations against Father while ignoring the police officer's contemporaneous concern about Watson's own substance use.

4. **Evidentiary Exclusion.** Despite being the most objective third-party evidence available — law enforcement officers who investigated the allegations in real time — these police reports were disregarded by the trial court. The court's custody findings (Exhibit C) credited Watson's allegations while ignoring the police determination that those allegations were unfounded.

## Key Evidence

- **Total NSPD Contacts:** Nine (9) police contacts involving the parties (2023–2025).
- **Total Arrests of Father:** Zero (0).
- **Total Criminal Charges Against Father:** Zero (0).
- **Report MU2422249 (November 15, 2024):**
  - Officers investigated alleged PPO violation during custody exchange.
  - Finding: Father did NOT violate the PPO.
  - No arrest. No charges.
  - Despite this finding, Father was sentenced to 14 days' incarceration for contempt (SC#5).

- **PPO Violation Claims by Watson:**
  - Multiple PPO violation claims filed throughout 2024–2025.
  - *"Almost all of which were baseless and unsupported by law enforcement."*
  - Pattern consistent with weaponization of the PPO system for custody advantage.

- **Officer Ella Randall's Observation:**
  - Officer Randall documented that she believed Emily A. Watson's behavior *"was a result of meth use."*
  - This finding was never pursued by the family court.
  - Watson's own substance use concerns were ignored while Father was subjected to medication conditions.

- **Emily A. Watson's Escalating False Allegations:**
  - "Suicidal" allegations → Welfare checks cleared Father.
  - "Arsenic poisoning" allegation → ER toxicology: NEGATIVE. No charges.
  - "Physical assault" allegations → Police: no evidence found.
  - "Drug use" allegations → Projection (Officer Randall: Watson admitted meth use).

## Constitutional Implications

The trial court's treatment of the police evidence represents a one-sided evidentiary approach that violates fundamental fairness. Nine police contacts producing zero arrests and zero charges is powerful evidence that Father poses no threat to L.D.W. The court's decision to ignore this evidence — while crediting the uncorroborated allegations of a party whose own substance use was documented by law enforcement — produces a custody determination that is not merely erroneous but constitutionally infirm.

Due process requires that custody decisions be based on reliable evidence, not on a selective reading of the record that credits one party's allegations while systematically excluding evidence that contradicts those allegations. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976). This Court's superintending control is necessary to ensure that the lower court's evidentiary approach comports with the requirements of due process.
