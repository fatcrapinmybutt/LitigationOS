# EXHIBIT C-2: August 8–9, 2025 Ex Parte Orders

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-2
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC §1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises the five ex parte orders issued by Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill on August 8–9, 2025, in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, Fourteenth Circuit Court, Muskegon County, Michigan. These orders, issued within a single 24-hour period, collectively stripped Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors of all custody rights and suspended all parenting time with his minor child, L.D.W. — transforming the existing 50/50 joint custody arrangement into sole custody for Defendant Emily A. Watson with zero parenting time for Plaintiff.

The orders were issued entirely ex parte. Plaintiff received no prior notice that any proceedings were pending, no notice that any motion had been filed, and no opportunity to appear, present evidence, or be heard before the orders were entered. Plaintiff learned of the orders only after they had been entered and his parental rights had been eliminated. The timing of these orders — one day after the NSPD police report documenting Albert Watson's premeditated plan to secure ex parte custody (see Exhibit C-3, NS2505044) — demonstrates that the deprivation was both coordinated and premeditated.

The issuance of five separate orders within a single day, all directed at eliminating one parent's custodial rights without any hearing, is unprecedented in the record of Case No. 2024-001507-DC and represents the most extreme deprivation of parental rights possible short of termination.

## Legal Significance

These ex parte orders are the central act of constitutional deprivation in this §1983 complaint. The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires, at an irreducible minimum, that a person be given notice and an opportunity to be heard before being deprived of a constitutionally protected liberty interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). The issuance of these orders without any notice or hearing constitutes a per se violation of procedural due process.

The Supreme Court has recognized that ex parte proceedings are the archetype of procedural unfairness and has prohibited them except in the most extraordinary circumstances requiring immediate action to prevent irreversible harm. *Fuentes v. Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67, 81–82 (1972). Even where emergency ex parte action is justified, due process requires a prompt post-deprivation hearing. No such hearing was provided here. Plaintiff's parenting time has remained suspended for over 255 days without any adversarial proceeding at which Plaintiff could challenge the basis for the suspension.

The five orders also establish the element of state action under §1983, as they were issued by a state judicial officer acting under color of state law. When a judge acts in the clear absence of all jurisdiction — issuing orders without notice or hearing in a manner that violates clearly established constitutional rights — the doctrine of judicial immunity does not apply. *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356–57 (1978) (immunity applies only to judicial acts, not administrative or executive acts performed without jurisdiction).

## Key Evidence

- **August 8, 2025:** Five separate ex parte orders entered in Case No. 2024-001507-DC
- **August 9, 2025:** Additional order(s) continuing the ex parte deprivation
- **Zero notice:** Plaintiff received no advance notice of any pending motion, hearing, or proceeding
- **Zero opportunity to be heard:** No hearing was conducted at any point before the orders were entered
- **Complete deprivation:** Orders eliminated 100% of Plaintiff's custody and parenting time — from 50/50 to zero
- **No emergency finding on record:** No articulated finding of immediate, irreversible harm justifying emergency ex parte action
- **Premeditation evidence:** Orders were issued one day after Albert Watson told police the family planned to obtain ex parte custody the next day (NS2505044, August 7, 2025)
- **July 29, 2025:** Last day Plaintiff had any contact with L.D.W. — separation now exceeds 248 days

## Constitutional Implications

The ex parte orders implicate multiple constitutional provisions. First, the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause prohibits the deprivation of fundamental liberty interests without adequate procedural safeguards. The right to the custody and companionship of one's child is among the most fundamental of all liberty interests. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). The complete elimination of that right through ex parte proceedings — with no notice, no hearing, and no post-deprivation remedy — violates both procedural and substantive due process.

Second, the pattern of ex parte orders, combined with the evidence of premeditation by private actors (Exhibit C-3), raises a due process concern under the state-created danger doctrine. When state actors create or substantially contribute to the danger of constitutional injury through affirmative acts, they may be held liable under §1983. Here, the issuance of ex parte orders based on a premeditated scheme, without independent judicial inquiry, constitutes the type of conscience-shocking conduct that violates substantive due process. *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833, 846 (1998).
