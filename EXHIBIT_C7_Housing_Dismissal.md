# EXHIBIT C-7: Lane B Dismissal Records

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-7
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC §1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

## Description

This exhibit comprises the court records from Case No. 2025-002760-CZ in the Fourteenth Circuit Court, Muskegon County, Michigan, in which Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors brought civil claims related to housing violations, property destruction, and unlawful lockout. The case was assigned to Hon. Kenneth Hoopes, Chief Judge of the Fourteenth Circuit Court and former law partner of Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill at the firm of Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill.

Chief Judge Hoopes dismissed Case No. 2025-002760-CZ with prejudice. The dismissal was entered without an evidentiary hearing, without allowing Plaintiff to present testimony or documentary evidence, and without addressing the substantive objections Plaintiff had raised in his filings. Plaintiff's claims regarding property lockout, destruction of personal property, water shutoff, and habitability violations were summarily eliminated from the judicial record without any adjudication on the merits.

The dismissal with prejudice is particularly significant because it permanently bars Plaintiff from refiling these claims in state court, effectively immunizing the defendants in that action from accountability for conduct that implicated both state statutory protections and fundamental property rights.

## Legal Significance

The dismissal records establish a pattern of coordinated adverse judicial action across courts controlled by former law partners. When examined alongside the custody case (McNeill) and the criminal proceedings (Ladas-Hoopes), the housing dismissal (Hoopes) completes a picture of systematic deprivation: Plaintiff lost his child, his home, and his freedom across three courts presided over by three former law partners — none of whom disclosed their prior professional relationship.

The dismissal without evidentiary hearing is independently significant as a due process violation. The Fourteenth Amendment requires that before a person is deprived of a property interest, the state must provide notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). Dismissal with prejudice is the most extreme sanction available in civil litigation — it permanently destroys the plaintiff's claims. Imposing such a sanction without permitting the plaintiff to present evidence or be heard violates the fundamental requirements of procedural due process.

The dismissal also supports the §1983 conspiracy claim. The pattern — McNeill strips custody, Hoopes dismisses housing claims, Ladas-Hoopes presides over criminal charges — demonstrates a coordinated effort to deprive Plaintiff of his constitutional rights across multiple forums. The common thread is the former law partnership, which provides both the opportunity for coordination and the motive for mutual protection among the three judicial officers.

## Constitutional Implications

The dismissal of Plaintiff's housing claims without a hearing implicates the Due Process Clause's guarantee of meaningful access to justice. The Supreme Court has held that the right of access to the courts is a fundamental right protected by the First Amendment and the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Christopher v. Harbury*, 536 U.S. 403, 415 n.12 (2002). When a judge dismisses claims with prejudice — permanently barring future litigation — without providing any hearing, the litigant has been denied meaningful access to the judicial process.

The housing dismissal, viewed in conjunction with the custody and criminal proceedings before the other former law partners, also implicates the Equal Protection Clause. Plaintiff was treated differently from similarly situated litigants — not because of the merits of his claims, but because of the structural relationships among the judges presiding over his cases. This disparate treatment, rooted in undisclosed judicial relationships rather than legal merit, constitutes a denial of equal protection under the law. The totality of the pattern — custody stripped (McNeill), housing dismissed (Hoopes), criminal charges (Ladas-Hoopes) — demonstrates that Plaintiff was not litigating in a neutral forum, but rather before a tribunal structurally predisposed to rule against him.
