### II. LEGAL STANDARD FOR JUDICIAL DISQUALIFICATION

5. **MCR 2.003(C)(1)** provides that disqualification of a judge is warranted
   when:

   > **(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or
   > attorney.
   >
   > **(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has
   > either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights
   > of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868
   > (2009), or (ii) has been a material witness concerning the matter in
   > controversy, or (iii) is likely to be a material witness concerning the
   > matter in controversy, or (iv) has personal knowledge of disputed
   > evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding.

6. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that disqualification is required where
   a judge's conduct creates an appearance of impropriety that would cause a
   reasonable person to question the judge's impartiality. *Cain v Dep't of
   Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

7. In *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, the Court of Appeals reinforced
   that a pattern of one-sided rulings, combined with procedural irregularity,
   may establish an objective basis for disqualification.

8. *In re Contempt of Henry* further establishes that judicial conduct that
   departs from established procedural rules—particularly in the context of
   ex parte proceedings—may demonstrate bias warranting disqualification.

9. Constitutional due process under both the **Fourteenth Amendment to the
   United States Constitution** and **Michigan Constitution Article 1, § 17**
   guarantees every litigant the right to an impartial tribunal. A biased
   judge is a structural defect that cannot be rendered harmless. *Tumey v
   Ohio*, 273 US 510, 523 (1927).

### III. STATEMENT OF FACTS SUPPORTING DISQUALIFICATION

#### A. Improper Ex Parte Order and Procedural Irregularities

10. Judge McNeill issued an ex parte custody order without satisfying the
    threshold requirements of **MCR 3.207(B)(1)**, which requires a showing
    that the child faces an immediate and irreparable risk of harm before
    ex parte relief may be granted.

11. As documented in Movant's prior filings, the circumstances did not
    "justify an ex parte custody order (MCR 3.207(B)(1)). By issuing the
    order despite the judge's own" acknowledgment of the insufficiency of the
    grounds, the court departed from established procedural safeguards.

12. The proper course under Michigan law was that upon receipt of
    "the ex parte order, the Friend of the Court (FOC) should have been
    engaged to attempt a quick" resolution prior to any unilateral restriction
    of parental rights. The failure to follow this established procedure
    compounded the prejudice to Movant.

13. The ex parte order was entered without adequate notice or opportunity to
    be heard, in violation of the procedural due process requirements of the
    Fourteenth Amendment and Mich Const Art 1, § 17.

#### B. Self-Reinforcing Bias and Reliance on Personal Knowledge

14. Evidence in the record demonstrates that restrictions imposed by the
    initial ex parte order were "then used as a basis to restrict custody,
    creating a self-reinforcing bias. Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)," this
    constitutes the judge relying upon her own personal knowledge of disputed
    evidentiary facts—facts generated by the court's own irregular
    procedures—rather than evidence properly admitted through adversarial
    proceedings.

15. This self-referential reasoning chain—where the court's own irregular
    order becomes the factual predicate for further restrictions—is precisely
    the type of bias that MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) is designed to prevent.

#### C. Evidence of Predetermined Disposition

16. Statements attributed to the proceedings reflect a predetermined judicial
    attitude regarding custodial matters. The assertion that "Judges HATE
    parental alienation" suggests a disposition formed prior to and independent
    of the evidence presented in this specific case, contrary to the
    requirement of individualized adjudication under Canon 3 of the Michigan
    Code of Judicial Conduct.

17. A reasonable observer, aware of these statements, would harbor legitimate
    doubts about whether the outcome of this custody dispute was determined on
    the merits of the evidence or on the basis of generalized judicial
    attitudes applied prejudicially against Movant.

#### D. Pattern of Systematic Denial of Parental Rights

18. As of the date of this filing, Movant has been separated from his minor
    child L.D.W. for in excess of **329 days**. This prolonged and systematic
    deprivation of parental contact, absent a finding of unfitness or danger
    supported by clear and convincing evidence, is itself indicative of bias.

19. The extended duration of separation—nearly the entire life of the
    relationship between father and child during critical developmental
    years—suggests that the court's orders are not calibrated to the
    best-interest factors of **MCL 722.23** but are instead a product of
    predetermined disposition against Movant.

20. These issues have been "documented in Andrew's Affidavit of Bias and
    Judicial" misconduct, previously filed with the court, which provides
    additional factual detail supporting the grounds stated herein.

#### E. Judicial Canon Violations

21. Judge McNeill's conduct violates multiple Canons of the Michigan Code
    of Judicial Conduct:

    - **Canon 1** (Integrity and Independence): A judge shall uphold the
      integrity and independence of the judiciary. Issuing procedurally
      irregular ex parte orders and creating self-reinforcing factual
      predicates undermines public confidence in the judiciary.

    - **Canon 2** (Appearance of Impropriety): A judge shall avoid
      impropriety and the appearance of impropriety. The pattern of conduct
      described herein would cause a reasonable person to question whether
      the proceedings are being conducted impartially.

    - **Canon 3** (Duties of Judicial Office — Ex Parte Contacts): A judge
      shall perform the duties of judicial office impartially and diligently.
      Canon 3(A)(4) specifically prohibits ex parte communications concerning
      a pending or impending proceeding. The procedural irregularities in the
      issuance of the ex parte order, without proper engagement of the Friend
      of the Court, raise questions about compliance with this Canon.

### IV. ARGUMENT

#### A. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a): Personal Bias or Prejudice

22. The totality of the circumstances—the procedurally deficient ex parte
    order, the self-reinforcing restriction cycle, the predetermined
    statements regarding parental alienation, and the 329+ day separation—
    collectively establish that Judge McNeill is biased or prejudiced against
    Movant within the meaning of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a).

23. Under *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996), the test is
    not whether the judge is actually biased in her own subjective assessment,
    but whether a reasonable, objective observer who is informed of all the
    circumstances would conclude that the judge's impartiality might
    reasonably be questioned.

24. Here, the evidence satisfies this standard. A reasonable observer,
    knowing that:
    - An ex parte order was issued without meeting MCR 3.207(B)(1) thresholds;
    - That order was then used as a self-reinforcing basis for further
      restrictions;
    - The judge demonstrated a predetermined disposition toward parental
      alienation claims;
    - A father has been separated from his child for 329+ days without a
      finding of unfitness;

    would reasonably question the judge's impartiality.

#### B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b): Personal Knowledge of Disputed Facts

25. By generating disputed facts through irregular procedures and then
    relying on those facts as a basis for further orders, Judge McNeill has
    acquired "personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the
    proceeding" within the meaning of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).

26. This is not a case where a judge merely observed courtroom testimony. The
    court's own procedural actions created the factual predicate upon which
    subsequent restrictions were based, making the court simultaneously
    fact-creator and fact-finder—a role incompatible with judicial
    impartiality.

#### C. Constitutional Due Process Requires Disqualification

27. The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Michigan
    Constitution Article 1, § 17 guarantee Movant the right to an impartial
    tribunal. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that in cases
    involving a "serious risk of actual bias," recusal is constitutionally
    required. *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868 (2009).

28. The parental liberty interest at stake is a fundamental right protected
    by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).
    When a fundamental right is at stake, the need for an impartial
    decision-maker is at its apex.

29. Movant has been denied meaningful access to his child for a period
    exceeding 329 days. The gravity of this deprivation, combined with the
    procedural irregularities documented above, creates a serious risk of
    actual bias that rises to the level of a constitutional violation.
