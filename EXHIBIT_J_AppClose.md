# EXHIBIT J: AppClose Communication Records

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-J
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit presents communication records from AppClose, the court-approved co-parenting platform used by the parties. These records document two critical patterns: (1) Father's active, consistent, and collaborative parenting communication, and (2) Defendant Emily A. Watson's systematic obstruction of communication, denial of parenting time, and alienating behavior. Critically, the contempt charges in SC#6 and SC#7 (Exhibit G) were based on birthday messages Father sent to L.D.W. through this same court-approved platform.

## Legal Significance

AppClose records are the most objective, contemporaneous evidence of each parent's parenting behavior. Unlike testimony, which can be shaped by self-interest, AppClose records are timestamped, uneditable, and complete. They directly address multiple MCL 722.23 best interest factors:

1. **Factor (a) — Love, Affection, Emotional Ties.** Father's AppClose records demonstrate consistent attempts to maintain a loving relationship with L.D.W. — including birthday messages, requests for video calls, and regular inquiries about the child's well-being.

2. **Factor (c) — Capacity to Provide.** Father used AppClose proactively: sharing schedules, requesting medical updates, requesting video chats, and documenting cooperative behavior.

3. **Factor (j) — Willingness to Facilitate Close Relationship.** The records directly address this factor. Father's messages show affirmative efforts to facilitate Mother's relationship with L.D.W. and to maintain a cooperative co-parenting dynamic. Mother's responses demonstrate the opposite: refusal to respond to scheduling requests, denial of video chats, failure to share medical information, failure to report L.D.W.'s educational milestones, and denial of parenting time for extended periods.

4. **Contempt Paradox.** Father was sentenced to 45 days' incarceration for birthday messages sent through AppClose — the platform the court itself designated for parental communication. This creates an impossible bind: use the court-approved platform and face contempt, or stop communicating with the child and lose under Factor (a). Either result eliminates Father from L.D.W.'s life.

## Key Evidence

- **Father's Communication Pattern (July 2023 – July 2025):**
  - Proactive parenting: scheduled exchanges, requested medical updates, requested video chats.
  - Collaborative tone: shared plans, proposed solutions, attempted mediation.
  - Consistent contact: maintained regular communication despite escalating obstruction.
  - Birthday messages to L.D.W. sent via AppClose — the court-approved platform.

- **Mother's Communication Pattern:**
  - Reactive and dismissive: failed to respond to scheduling requests.
  - Denied video chats: Father's requests for video calls with L.D.W. were refused.
  - Withheld medical information: Failed to share details about L.D.W.'s health, including doctor visits.
  - Withheld educational information: Failed to report L.D.W.'s developmental milestones.
  - Denied parenting time: During the Easter period, Father was denied parenting time for twenty-five or more consecutive days.
  - A recording captured L.D.W. calling another man "dad" — evidence of identity confusion resulting from alienation.

- **Contempt Basis (SC#6 and SC#7):**
  - Father's birthday messages to L.D.W. via AppClose were the sole basis for the contempt finding.
  - These messages were sent through the court-approved platform.
  - The messages consisted of birthday wishes — no threatening, harassing, or contemptuous content.

## Constitutional Implications

The AppClose records expose a fundamental contradiction in the trial court's approach: Father is punished for using the court-approved communication platform, while Mother's systematic refusal to communicate and denial of parenting time goes unsanctioned. This double standard — punishing the cooperative parent while rewarding the obstructive one — produces an outcome directly contrary to the child's best interests.

Under MCL 722.23(j), willingness to facilitate a close relationship with the other parent is a mandatory best interest factor. The AppClose records show Mother scored poorly on this factor, yet the trial court found Factor (j) favored Mother. This factual inversion — directly contradicted by the objective, contemporaneous record — constitutes clear error requiring this Court's intervention.
