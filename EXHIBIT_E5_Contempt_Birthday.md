# EXHIBIT E-5: Contempt Orders for Birthday Messages via Court-Approved Platform

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-E-5
**Filing:** Formal Complaint to Judicial Tenure Commission
**Respondent:** Hon. Jenny L. McNeill (P58235)
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC, 14th Circuit Court

---

## Description

This exhibit documents three Show Cause contempt orders entered by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill against Father, resulting in a total of 59 days of incarceration. Father was jailed for sending birthday messages to his minor child, L.D.W., through AppClose — a court-approved co-parenting communication platform that the court itself ordered the parties to use. The contempt findings, the severity of the sentences, and the consequences suffered by Father (loss of two homes and two jobs) demonstrate a pattern of disproportionate punishment that constitutes an abuse of the contempt power and a violation of Father's constitutional rights.

## Relevant Canon Violations

**Canon 1 — Upholding the Integrity of the Judiciary:** Jailing a father for sending birthday messages to his child through a court-approved platform undermines public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary.

**Canon 2(A) — Appearance of Impropriety:** The disproportionate nature of the sentences — 59 total days of incarceration for messages sent through a platform the court itself required — creates the appearance that the contempt power was used as a weapon rather than a tool of justice.

**Canon 3(B)(5) — Performance Without Bias:** The asymmetric application of the contempt power — used against Father for routine parental communications while Mother's documented violations of court orders went unaddressed — demonstrates outcome-determinative bias.

**Canon 3(B)(1) — Faithful to the Law:** Contempt sanctions must be proportionate to the conduct. MCL 600.1701 and MCR 3.606 authorize contempt for willful violations of court orders, but the sanctions must bear a reasonable relationship to the gravity of the offense. Sentencing a father to 59 days in jail for birthday messages is facially disproportionate.

## Key Evidence

### Show Cause #5 — 14 Days (November 15, 2024)

On November 15, 2024, Hon. McNeill found Father in contempt and sentenced him to 14 days in the Muskegon County Jail.

- **Basis:** Father objected during a hearing to the improper discussion of forced medication between Hon. McNeill and Emily A. Watson (see Exhibit E-4)
- **Context:** Father was muted three times during the hearing; police report exculpatory to Father was ignored
- **Consequence:** Father served 14 days in jail

### Show Cause #6 and #7 — 45 Days (November 26, 2025)

On November 26, 2025, Hon. McNeill found Father in contempt on two additional Show Cause orders and sentenced him to 45 days in the Muskegon County Jail.

- **Basis:** Father sent birthday messages to L.D.W. through AppClose
- **Context:** AppClose is the court-approved co-parenting platform. The court's own order (Recommendation dated March 26, 2025) directed: "Parties to Communicate via co-parenting application, 'AppClose.' Plaintiff to send invite. Defendant/Mother to accept invitation."
- **Consequence:** Father served 45 days in jail

### The Court's Own Order Required AppClose

The court ordered both parties to use AppClose for communication. The court's recommendation stated:

> "Parties to Communicate via co-parenting application, 'AppClose.' Plaintiff to send invite. Defendant/Mother to accept invitation. Holiday parenting times to be by agreement."

Father used the court-ordered platform exactly as directed. He sent birthday messages to his child. He was then jailed for 45 days for doing precisely what the court told him to do.

### Collateral Consequences

The 59 days of incarceration caused catastrophic collateral consequences:

1. **Loss of Housing:** Father lost two homes as a direct result of incarceration — he could not pay rent or maintain his residence while jailed
2. **Loss of Employment:** Father lost two jobs — employers terminated his employment due to unexplained absences caused by incarceration
3. **Loss of Parenting Time:** While jailed, Father had no contact with L.D.W., compounding the separation already imposed by the ex parte orders
4. **Financial Devastation:** Filing fees, bonds ($250 motion deposit), and lost wages created insurmountable financial barriers to further legal proceedings

### First Amendment Implications

A parent sending birthday messages to his own child through a court-approved communication platform is protected speech and protected associational activity under the First Amendment. Using the contempt power to jail a father for exercising these rights implicates:

- **First Amendment — Free Speech:** Messages to one's own child are core protected speech
- **First Amendment — Right of Association:** The parent-child relationship is a fundamental associational right
- **Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process:** A parent's right to a relationship with their child is a fundamental liberty interest (*Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000))

## Significance

This exhibit demonstrates that Hon. McNeill used the contempt power not to enforce legitimate court orders but to punish Father for maintaining any connection with his child. The fact that Father was jailed for using the very platform the court ordered him to use reveals the circular trap constructed by the court's orders: Father was ordered to communicate via AppClose, then jailed for communicating via AppClose. The 59-day cumulative sentence — resulting in the loss of two homes and two jobs — is grossly disproportionate by any measure and reveals a pattern of punitive conduct directed at Father.
