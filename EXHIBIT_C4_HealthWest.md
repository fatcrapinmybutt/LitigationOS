# EXHIBIT C-4: HealthWest Evaluation

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-4
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC §1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit is the HealthWest psychological evaluation of Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors, conducted pursuant to court order in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. The evaluation was completed by licensed mental health professionals and produced comprehensive clinical findings regarding Plaintiff's psychological fitness, parenting capacity, and risk factors. The evaluation's conclusions were unequivocal: Plaintiff was found to be a fit parent with no clinical indicators warranting restriction of parental rights.

The clinical results were as follows: Psychosis Score = 0; Substance Abuse Score = 0; Danger Score = 0; LOCUS (Level of Care Utilization System) Score = 12, corresponding to Level One — the least restrictive level of care. These results, generated through standardized clinical instruments, confirmed that Plaintiff presented no psychological, substance abuse, or safety risk factors of any kind. The evaluation affirmatively supported Plaintiff's capacity to parent the minor child, L.D.W.

Despite these unambiguous clinical findings, Defendant Hon. Jenny L. McNeill excluded this court-ordered evaluation from consideration in the custody proceedings. The deliberate exclusion of exculpatory, court-ordered evidence that directly contradicted the basis for the deprivation of Plaintiff's parental rights constitutes a fundamental violation of due process.

## Legal Significance

The HealthWest evaluation is critical to the §1983 claim for two independent reasons. First, it is direct evidence that the deprivation of Plaintiff's custody rights lacked any legitimate factual basis. The allegations underlying the ex parte orders (Exhibit C-2) rested on claims regarding Plaintiff's mental fitness and alleged danger to the child. The court-ordered clinical evaluation refuted every one of those allegations with objective, standardized clinical data. A LOCUS Score of 12 (Level One) means the evaluating professionals concluded that Plaintiff required no mental health intervention whatsoever — the clinical antithesis of the portrait painted by the ex parte proceedings.

Second, and more significantly, the deliberate exclusion of this evidence by the presiding judge demonstrates the type of conscience-shocking judicial conduct that transforms a procedural error into a substantive due process violation. A judge who orders an evaluation, receives results that exonerate the accused parent, and then excludes those results from the record while simultaneously stripping that parent of all custody rights, is not engaged in neutral adjudication. This conduct evidences actual bias — a predetermined outcome that no amount of favorable evidence could alter.

The exclusion of exculpatory evidence also violates fundamental principles of evidentiary fairness. While family courts enjoy discretion in evidentiary matters, the deliberate exclusion of court-ordered evidence favorable to a party is an abuse of discretion so egregious as to constitute a denial of due process. *Goldberg v. Kelly*, 397 U.S. 254, 271 (1970) (due process requires the opportunity to present evidence and confront adverse evidence).

## Key Evidence

- **Court-ordered evaluation:** The HealthWest evaluation was ordered by the court itself — not sought independently by Plaintiff
- **Psychosis Score: 0** — No psychotic symptoms or indicators detected
- **Substance Abuse Score: 0** — No substance abuse history, use, or risk factors detected
- **Danger Score: 0** — No dangerousness indicators detected
- **LOCUS Score: 12 (Level One)** — Least restrictive level of care; no clinical intervention needed
- **Professional conclusion:** Father deemed fit parent by licensed clinical evaluators
- **Excluded by McNeill:** Despite being court-ordered, the evaluation was excluded from consideration in custody proceedings
- **Exculpatory nature:** Evaluation directly contradicted every allegation used to justify the deprivation of Plaintiff's parental rights

## Constitutional Implications

The exclusion of the HealthWest evaluation strikes at the core of both procedural and substantive due process. Procedurally, the Due Process Clause guarantees a meaningful opportunity to be heard, which necessarily includes the right to present relevant, probative evidence in one's defense. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). When a court excludes the very evidence it ordered — evidence that exonerates the party facing deprivation of fundamental rights — the proceeding ceases to be a genuine adjudication and becomes a mechanism for predetermined outcomes.

Substantively, the exclusion of exculpatory clinical evidence, combined with the issuance of orders stripping parental rights, satisfies the "shocks the conscience" standard for substantive due process violations. *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833, 846 (1998). No rational, unbiased judicial officer would order an evaluation, receive results completely favorable to the evaluated party, and then exclude those results while ruling against that party on the very issues the evaluation addressed. This conduct is explicable only as the product of bias, and it demonstrates the need for federal intervention to protect Plaintiff's constitutional rights.
