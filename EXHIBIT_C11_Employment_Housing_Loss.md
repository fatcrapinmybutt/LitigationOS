# EXHIBIT C-11: Employment and Housing Loss Documentation

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-C-11
**Filing:** Federal Civil Rights Complaint, 42 USC 1983
**Court:** United States District Court, Western District of Michigan
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson et al.

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises documentation of the catastrophic economic consequences suffered by Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors as a direct and proximate result of the unconstitutional contempt proceedings and incarceration detailed in Exhibit C-5. Specifically, this exhibit documents the loss of two separate jobs and two separate residences, each directly caused by Plaintiff's incarceration for contempt of court.

The first period of incarceration, SC#5 (14 days beginning on or about November 15, 2024), resulted in the loss of Plaintiff's employment and housing at that time. Plaintiff could not report to work while jailed and was terminated. The loss of employment led to the inability to pay rent, resulting in the loss of his residence. The second period of incarceration, SC#6 and SC#7 (45 days beginning on or about November 26, 2025), replicated this devastating cycle: Plaintiff again lost both his job and his home because he was physically incarcerated and unable to fulfill his employment and housing obligations.

The cumulative effect of 59 days of incarceration has been economic devastation. Plaintiff lost not only the immediate income and housing, but also the stability and employment history necessary to rebuild. Each cycle of incarceration destroyed the economic progress Plaintiff had made since the prior incarceration, creating a downward spiral of economic harm directly attributable to the unconstitutional actions of the Defendants.

## Legal Significance

The employment and housing loss documentation is essential to establishing compensatory damages under Section 1983. A plaintiff who proves a constitutional violation is entitled to compensatory damages for all injuries proximately caused by that violation, including economic losses. *Memphis Community School District v. Stachura*, 477 U.S. 299, 306-07 (1986). The losses documented here, being the direct and foreseeable consequence of unconstitutional imprisonment, are fully compensable.

The documentation also supports the claim for punitive damages. The severity and predictability of the collateral consequences of incarceration demonstrate that the contempt orders were not merely procedurally deficient, but affirmatively destructive. A judge who imprisons a pro se litigant for filing motions and sending birthday messages, knowing that the imprisonment will result in loss of employment and housing, has acted with the callous indifference to constitutional rights that warrants punitive damages. *Smith v. Wade*, 461 U.S. 30, 56 (1983).

Furthermore, the economic devastation caused by the contempt orders amplifies the due process violation. The Supreme Court has recognized that the severity of the deprivation is a relevant factor in the due process calculus. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976). When the deprivation extends beyond liberty (imprisonment) to economic destruction (loss of jobs and homes), the procedural protections required by due process are correspondingly heightened. The total absence of adequate procedural protections in the contempt proceedings is thus even more constitutionally deficient when measured against the magnitude of harm inflicted.

## Key Evidence

- **Job Loss #1:** Employment terminated as a result of SC#5 incarceration (November 2024). Plaintiff could not report to work while jailed for 14 days.
- **Housing Loss #1:** Residence lost following Job Loss #1. Inability to pay rent due to loss of income from incarceration.
- **Job Loss #2:** Employment terminated as a result of SC#6/SC#7 incarceration (November-December 2025). Plaintiff could not report to work while jailed for 45 days.
- **Housing Loss #2:** Residence lost following Job Loss #2. Same cascade: incarceration caused job loss, which caused housing loss.
- **Total incarceration:** 59 days across two periods (14 + 45 days)
- **Cumulative economic harm:** Two complete cycles of employment and housing destruction
- **Recovery impairment:** Each incarceration destroyed the economic recovery Plaintiff had achieved since the prior incarceration
- **Ongoing consequences:** Loss of employment history, rental history, and economic stability continue to impair Plaintiff's ability to support himself and provide for L.D.W.

## Constitutional Implications

The employment and housing losses documented in this exhibit illustrate the cascading constitutional harms that flow from the abuse of contempt power. The Fourteenth Amendment protects not only the liberty interest at stake in the incarceration itself but also the property interests destroyed as a consequence. When a court imprisons a person without adequate procedural protections, and that imprisonment foreseeably destroys the person's employment and housing, the constitutional violation encompasses the full scope of the resulting harm.

The economic devastation also bears on the Section 1983 analysis of the parent-child relationship. A parent who has been stripped of employment and housing is severely impaired in his ability to provide for his child and to demonstrate the stability that courts consider in custody determinations. The contempt orders thus created a self-reinforcing cycle of constitutional injury: the imprisonment destroyed Plaintiff's economic stability, which was then used to justify the continued deprivation of his parental rights. This vicious cycle, initiated and perpetuated by unconstitutional state action, is precisely the type of harm that Section 1983 was enacted to remedy.
