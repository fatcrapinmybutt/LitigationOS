## II. LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A. Michigan Court Rules

**MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)–(b) — Grounds for Disqualification.** A judge is disqualified when the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney, or when the judge has personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding. Ex parte communications create precisely this condition — the judge obtains information from one party that the other party has no opportunity to confront or rebut.

**MCR 3.206(B)(5)–(6) — Requirements for Ex Parte Orders in Domestic Relations.** Where ex parte orders are entered in domestic relations cases, the court must comply with specific procedural safeguards including verified pleadings and specific findings of irreparable harm.

**MCR 3.207(B)(1) — Temporary Orders.** Temporary orders affecting custody and parenting time require notice and opportunity to be heard except in narrowly defined emergency circumstances.

**MCR 2.612 — Relief from Judgment or Order.** Orders obtained through fraud, misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party, or where the judgment is void, may be set aside.

### B. Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct

**Canon 3(A)(4) — Prohibition on Ex Parte Communications.** "A judge should accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding, or the person's lawyer, full right to be heard according to law, and, except as authorized by law, neither initiate nor consider ex parte communications concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

**Canon 2 — Appearance of Impropriety.** "A judge should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities."

### C. Constitutional Protections

**U.S. Const. Amend. XIV, § 1 — Due Process.** No State shall deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law.

**Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).** The Supreme Court established a three-factor balancing test for procedural due process: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used; and (3) the government's interest, including the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional procedural safeguards.

**In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955).** "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process. Fairness of course requires an absence of actual bias in the trial of cases. But our system of law has always endeavored to prevent even the probability of unfairness."

**Cain v. Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich. 470 (1996).** Michigan Supreme Court authority on due process requirements in proceedings affecting fundamental rights.

### D. Additional Authority

**In re Hatcher, 443 Mich. 426 (1993).** Orders entered without proper jurisdiction or due process are void and subject to collateral attack.

**Armstrong v. Manzo, 380 U.S. 545 (1965).** A judgment rendered without notice to an interested party is constitutionally defective.
