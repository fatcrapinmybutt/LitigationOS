### I. INTRODUCTION

1. This Motion requests that the Court consolidate Case No. 2024-001507-DC (custody/parenting time proceeding) and Case No. 2023-5907-PP (Personal Protection Order proceeding) for all purposes, including discovery, motions practice, evidentiary hearings, and trial.

2. These two cases involve the same parties, arise from the same nucleus of operative facts, present overlapping legal and factual issues, and are already assigned to the same presiding judge. Consolidation is warranted by judicial economy, avoidance of inconsistent results, and the fundamental reality that these cases cannot be fairly adjudicated in isolation.

3. The PPO proceeding is being weaponized as a tactical tool in the custody litigation. Consolidation is the only procedural mechanism to prevent continued abuse of the PPO process.

---

### II. LEGAL STANDARD

4. MCR 2.505(A) provides: "When actions involving a substantial and common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated; and it may make orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay."

5. The decision to consolidate is within the sound discretion of the trial court, guided by whether consolidation promotes judicial economy and avoids prejudice. The predominant question is whether the cases share common questions of law or fact. *See Decker Mfg. Corp. v Mitsubishi Heavy Industries*, 168 Mich App 22; 423 NW2d 577 (1988).

6. Michigan courts have recognized that consolidation is particularly appropriate in family division cases where multiple proceedings between the same parties involve overlapping factual determinations. MCR 3.205 (Jurisdiction in Domestic Relations) and MCR 3.221 (Parenting Time Enforcement) contemplate coordinated proceedings.

---

#### D. Overlapping Legal Issues

   - The best interests of the minor child.

14. These factual determinations are identical in both proceedings. Adjudicating them separately creates an unacceptable risk of inconsistent findings and duplicative proceedings.

### IV. THE PPO IS BEING WEAPONIZED IN CUSTODY PROCEEDINGS

15. The PPO proceeding is not being pursued in good faith for personal protection. It is being systematically deployed as a litigation weapon to gain tactical advantage in the custody case.

17. This is not an isolated incident. Throughout these proceedings, PPO filings have been timed to coincide with or immediately precede critical custody events:
   - Show-cause motions filed days before custody hearings;
   - PPO modification requests timed to custody motion deadlines;
   - Allegations in PPO filings recycled verbatim into custody pleadings.

18. The effect of this weaponization has been devastating to Father's parental rights:
   - **50/50 parenting time** → **79 overnights/year** (July 2024) → **ZERO overnights** (August 2025);
   - Father has been denied ALL parenting time for **more than 200 consecutive days**;
   - Mother has been awarded **sole physical AND sole legal custody**;
   - The PPO has been cited as justification for each reduction in Father's parenting time.

19. Emily Watson herself characterized the PPO as **"the only thing keeping [her] safe"** in Show Cause #7. Yet the Norton Shores Police Department has investigated **seven or more (7+)** allegations and found NO violations and NO criminal conduct in every instance. The PPO is not protecting anyone — it is being used as a sword, not a shield.

20. The clearest evidence of weaponization is the August 7, 2025 smoking-gun admission by Albert Watson (Defendant's father) to Norton Shores police: **"they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son."** The following morning, August 8, 2025, five ex parte orders were entered suspending ALL parenting time — exactly as pre-planned by the Watson family. This proves the PPO process was coordinated as a tactical weapon in custody litigation, not used for genuine protection.

---

### V. JUDICIAL ECONOMY REQUIRES CONSOLIDATION

20. Separate proceedings impose unnecessary costs and burdens on:
   - **The Court**: Duplicate hearings on identical facts, duplicate review of identical evidence, risk of inconsistent findings;
   - **The parties**: Duplicate filing fees, duplicate discovery, duplicate hearing preparation, duplicate service costs;
   - **The minor child**: Prolonged litigation creating continued instability; each separate hearing is a separate source of conflict and delay.

21. The evidence record for these combined proceedings encompasses extensive documentary evidence across more than **1,085 indexed documents**. Having two separate courts (even if both are Judge McNeill) independently evaluate overlapping subsets of this evidence is inefficient and creates unnecessary risk of inconsistent findings.

22. Consolidation is particularly appropriate here because the PPO findings directly impact custody determinations. Under current separate proceedings, a PPO finding could be used as a basis for custody modification without the custody court conducting its own independent evidentiary review. Consolidation ensures that all evidence is evaluated holistically.

---

### VI. NO PREJUDICE FROM CONSOLIDATION

23. No party will be prejudiced by consolidation because:
   - Both cases are already before the same judge;
   - Both cases involve the same parties;
   - Both cases involve the same core evidence;
   - Consolidation does not deprive either party of any procedural right;
   - The Court retains discretion to conduct separate hearings on discrete issues if warranted.

24. To the extent Defendant objects that PPO proceedings are "separate" from custody, this argument is defeated by her own practice of filing PPO show-cause motions timed to custody hearings and citing PPO allegations in custody pleadings. Defendant cannot simultaneously treat the cases as connected (for tactical purposes) and separate (to avoid consolidated review).
