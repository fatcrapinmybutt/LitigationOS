## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. Standard for Compelling Discovery (MCR 2.313(A))

5. MCR 2.313(A) provides:

> "If a party fails to furnish a discovery response or permit discovery as requested under this subchapter, the party seeking discovery may move for an order compelling discovery."

6. The scope of discovery under MCR 2.302(B)(1) is broad:

> "Parties may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, which is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action."

7. All categories of discovery sought are directly relevant to the 12 best interest factors under MCL 722.23, which govern custody and parenting time determinations.

### B. Defendant's Discovery Failures Are Willful

8. The pattern of non-compliance is not inadvertent. It is consistent with Defendant's broader strategy of:

   a. Filing 52+ ex parte motions to obtain orders without Father's knowledge or participation;

   b. Relying on unsubstantiated allegations while withholding the evidence that would disprove those allegations;

   c. Preventing Father from obtaining evidence that would demonstrate Mother's alienating conduct, including:
      - Communications with Watson family members coordinating interference with parenting time;
      - Albert Watson's admission that the police report was filed specifically to obtain the ex parte order;
      - Evidence that the PPO was obtained through false statements (MCL 750.423 — perjury);

   d. Creating an asymmetry of information that allows Mother to make accusations without evidentiary support while Father is denied access to contradicting evidence.

### C. Sanctions Are Warranted (MCR 2.313(B))

9. MCR 2.313(B)(2) authorizes the following sanctions for failure to comply with a discovery order:

   (a) An order that designated facts be taken as established;
   (b) An order refusing to allow the disobedient party to support or oppose designated claims;
   (c) An order striking pleadings or parts thereof;
   (d) An order dismissing the action or entering default judgment;
   (e) An order treating the failure as contempt of court.

10. Under *Dean v Tucker*, 182 Mich App 27 (1990), sanctions must be proportional to the discovery violation and the prejudice caused. Here, the prejudice is severe: Father has been denied parenting time for more than 200 consecutive days based on allegations he cannot effectively challenge because the underlying evidence has been withheld.

11. Under *Vicencio v Ramirez*, 211 Mich App 501 (1995), exclusion of evidence is an appropriate sanction when discovery violations are willful or prejudicial. Father requests that any evidence not previously disclosed be excluded from future proceedings.

12. Under *Barnard Mfg Co v Gates Performance Engineering*, 285 Mich App 362 (2009), the most severe sanctions (dismissal, default) are reserved for the most egregious violations. The systematic withholding of evidence in a case involving a child's welfare and a parent's fundamental constitutional rights warrants the most serious consideration.

### D. Prejudice to Plaintiff

13. The failure to provide discovery has caused the following specific prejudice:

   a. **Unable to Challenge PPO Basis:** Without the underlying evidence supporting the PPO allegations, Father cannot effectively move to terminate the PPO under MCR 3.707(A);

   b. **Unable to Demonstrate Alienation:** Without communications between Watson family members, Father cannot prove the coordinated campaign to alienate L.D.W. from Father (MCL 722.23(j));

   c. **Unable to Challenge Financial Claims:** Without financial records, Father cannot challenge child support calculations or demonstrate Mother's capacity to provide (MCL 722.23(c));

   d. **Unable to Prepare for Hearings:** The Court has scheduled hearings where Father must present his case, but he cannot prepare without discovery responses;

   e. **Unable to Challenge Evaluator Bias:** Without the Court's letter to the second evaluator, Father cannot demonstrate that the evaluation was tainted by prejudicial information, particularly given that the HealthWest evaluation found Psychosis Score: 0, no substance abuse, and no risk factors.

### E. Brady-Type Obligation in Family Proceedings

14. While *Brady v Maryland*, 373 US 83 (1963) applies directly to criminal proceedings, Michigan courts have recognized analogous disclosure obligations in civil proceedings where fundamental rights are at stake. *See In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (2014).

15. The deprivation of parental rights implicates fundamental liberty interests protected by the Due Process Clause of both the United States Constitution (Amend. XIV) and the Michigan Constitution (Const 1963, art 1, § 17). *See Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982).

16. When fundamental parental rights are at stake, the obligation to disclose exculpatory evidence should be no less rigorous than in criminal proceedings.
