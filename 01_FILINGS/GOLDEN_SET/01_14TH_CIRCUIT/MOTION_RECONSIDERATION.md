4. A "palpable error" is one that is "obvious, clear, unmistakable, plain, or, in other words, readily apparent from the record." *Corpac v Rubin & Rothman*, 10 F.3d 1278, 1281 (7th Cir. 1993); adopted in Michigan practice.

5. Plaintiff demonstrates **multiple palpable errors** warranting reconsideration and a different disposition, as set forth below.

# PART II: BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

## I. LEGAL STANDARD

MCR 2.119(F)(3) provides:

> "Generally, and without restricting the discretion of the court, a motion for rehearing or reconsideration which merely presents the same issues ruled on by the court, either expressly or by reasonable implication, will not be granted. The moving party must demonstrate a palpable error by which the court and the parties have been misled and show that a different disposition of the motion must result from correction of the error."

The Michigan Court of Appeals has held that "palpable" means "easily perceptible, plain, obvious, readily intelligible." *Corpac v Rubin & Rothman*, 10 F.3d 1278; *Woods v SLB Property Mgmt, LLC*, 277 Mich App 622, 630 (2008).

## II. THE PALPABLE ERRORS ARE PLAIN AND OBVIOUS

### A. Clinical Evidence Destroys the Factual Predicate

The HealthWest evaluation is not marginally favorable — it is **categorically exonerating**. Every domain the Court was concerned about scored at **zero** — the lowest possible level indicating no clinical concern:

- **Psychosis: 0** — No thought disorder
- **Substance Use: 0** — No substance abuse
- **Danger to Self: 0** — No risk
- **Danger to Others: 0** — No risk
- **Impulse Control: 0** — No concerns
- **Anger Control: 0** — No concerns
- **SMI/SED: "Neither"** — Not severely mentally ill
- **LOCUS: Level One** — Lowest severity tier
- **Urgency: "Routine"** — Not urgent

A court order depriving a parent of **all** contact with his child for **more than 200 consecutive days** based on allegations that are **categorically refuted** by clinical evidence is the definition of palpable error.

### B. Fundamental Right Requires Heightened Scrutiny

The right of a parent to the companionship, care, and custody of his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Deprivation of this fundamental right requires, at minimum:

1. **Notice and an opportunity to be heard** — *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976);
2. **Clear and convincing evidence** of unfitness — *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982);
3. **Specific findings** on the record — MCR 2.517; MCL 722.23.

None of these safeguards were employed here. The orders were entered ex parte, without findings, and based on allegations now proven clinically false.

### C. The Child Is Suffering Irreparable Harm

L.D.W. was approximately two years old when separation began and is now approximately three. This is a **critical developmental period** for attachment formation. *See* Lamb, M.E. (2010), *The Role of the Father in Child Development* (5th ed.) (documenting severe developmental consequences of parent-child separation during ages 2-4).

The HealthWest clinician documented: *"Andrew primarily identified his three year old son as support and reason for living, noting that he cries every day that he can not see him."* The harm of continued separation is ongoing and irreparable.

### D. A Different Disposition Must Result

Had the HealthWest clinical findings been before the Court on August 8, 2025, the Court could not have found that Father posed a danger to the child. The entire factual predicate for the ex parte orders has been demolished. Correction of this error requires restoration of parenting time.

## III. CONCLUSION

The August 8, 2025 orders were based on allegations now proven clinically false, entered without due process, and predicated on a pre-planned legal maneuver rather than genuine emergency. The HealthWest evaluation — conducted at this Court's own direction — found Father to be non-dangerous, non-psychotic, substance-free, and at the lowest severity level on every clinical measure. Continued enforcement of these orders constitutes ongoing deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right based on palpable error.
