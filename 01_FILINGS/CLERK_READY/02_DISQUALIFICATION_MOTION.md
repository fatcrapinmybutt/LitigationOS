# MOTION FOR DISQUALIFICATION OF JUDGE JENNY L. McNEILL PURSUANT TO MCR 2.003

&nbsp;

## I. INTRODUCTION

1. This Motion is brought pursuant to **MCR 2.003(C)(1)**, which provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney, or when the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned.

2. This Motion is supported by an Affidavit of Bias (Exhibit A), a Statistical Analysis of Judicial Conduct (Exhibit B), and a comprehensive Exhibit Index cataloguing all supporting evidence.

3. Judge McNeill has demonstrated a pervasive and documented pattern of personal bias against Plaintiff through: the entry of **24 ex parte orders** (18.26% of all orders — 3.65× the normal judicial rate); **59 days of incarceration** imposed without proper contempt safeguards; systematic denial of Plaintiff's motions while granting Defendant's; and the total severance of Plaintiff's relationship with his three-year-old son for **229+ consecutive days**.

## II. GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

### A. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

6. The ex parte orders disproportionately affected Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights, including the August 8, 2025 ex parte order that **completely suspended all parenting time** between Plaintiff and his three-year-old son — a suspension that has now persisted for more than **229+ consecutive days** without restoration.

7. Seven (7) show cause proceedings have been initiated against Plaintiff:

The Court's willingness to subject Plaintiff to repeated contempt proceedings, the majority of which were meritless, while simultaneously failing to act on Plaintiff's own motions for enforcement of parenting time, demonstrates differential treatment constituting bias.

The right to be heard is fundamental to due process and is specifically protected by Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which requires that a judge "shall accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding . . . the right to be heard according to law."

*(See Exhibit C — Hearing Transcript References)*

*(See Exhibit E — Hearing Transcript Reference)*

15. This is direct, documented evidence that Defendant's family **manufactured a police report** for the specific purpose of obtaining ex parte judicial relief. The Court subsequently entered orders based on filings that relied upon this manufactured evidence, without independent inquiry into the evidence's reliability.

*(See Exhibit F — NSPD Report NS2505044)*

## III. LEGAL STANDARD

18. Under **MCR 2.003(C)(1)**, a judge is disqualified when:

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney; or

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard as set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

19. The test is whether the judge's conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired." *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975).

20. A pattern of one-sided rulings can cumulatively establish bias. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001).

21. The appearance-of-impropriety standard does not require proof of actual bias. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App at 671.

22. The Michigan Supreme Court has emphasized that the appearance-of-impropriety standard serves a prophylactic function — protecting the integrity of the judicial system. *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

23. In contempt proceedings resulting in incarceration, Michigan law requires heightened procedural protections. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 95 (1987). The failure to provide these protections independently warrants disqualification.

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. The Pattern of One-Sided Adjudication Compels Disqualification

24. This is not a case of isolated judicial error or mere adverse rulings. This Motion does not challenge the outcome of any individual ruling. Rather, it challenges the **process** by which rulings were made — a process that systematically denied Plaintiff notice, hearing, and the opportunity to be heard while granting Defendant's requests on an ex parte basis.

25. The pattern includes:

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;a. Every show cause against Plaintiff was granted at initiation, even though nearly half were ultimately dismissed or mooted;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;b. Parenting time was ordered, then suspended via ex parte order without MCR 3.207(B) findings;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;c. Defendant's 305 documented interference incidents went unaddressed;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;d. Plaintiff was muted during proceedings, denied meaningful participation;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;e. Financial barriers ($250 deposit) were imposed on an indigent pro se litigant;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;f. AI legal research was disparaged rather than substantively evaluated;

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;g. Manufactured evidence (NSPD NS2505044) was accepted without scrutiny.

26. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001), recognizes that a pattern of adverse rulings, combined with other indicators of bias, supports an inference of personal bias or prejudice.

### B. The Ex Parte Rate Is Independently Disqualifying

27. Judge McNeill's **18.26% ex parte order rate** — 24 of 131 orders entered without notice or hearing — is approximately **3.65 times the normal rate** for Michigan family courts. Each ex parte order suspending parenting time constituted a deprivation of Plaintiff's fundamental liberty interest in the care and custody of his child without due process. *See Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

28. The frequency of ex parte orders is not merely an anomaly — it demonstrates a systematic willingness to adjudicate matters affecting Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights without affording him notice or the opportunity to be heard.

### C. The Incarceration Without Safeguards Demonstrates Bias

29. The imposition of 59 days of incarceration without the procedural protections mandated by *In re Contempt of Dougherty* — including the right to counsel under MCL 600.2145 — independently warrants disqualification. These incarcerations directly caused the loss of three jobs and three months of homelessness, compounding the harm to both Plaintiff and L.D.W.

### D. Cumulative Error Requires Disqualification

30. Even if no single category of violation independently compels disqualification, the cumulative effect requires it. Michigan recognizes the cumulative error doctrine. *See Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

31. The cumulative record includes:

|----------|---------|
| Ex parte orders entered | 24 of 131 (18.26% — 3.65× normal) |
| Show cause proceedings against Plaintiff | 7 (2 dismissed, 1 mooted) |
| Days of incarceration (contempt) | 59 |
| Consecutive days of zero parenting time | 215+ |
| Documented interference incidents (unaddressed) | 305 |
| Jobs lost to incarceration | 3 |
| Months of homelessness | 3 |
