## I. JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT

1. This Court has original and plenary jurisdiction to issue an Order of Superintending Control over any Michigan court. Const 1963, art 6, § 4 provides:

   > "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts; power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs; and appellate jurisdiction as provided by rules of the supreme court."

2. MCR 7.304(A) authorizes the filing of an original action in this Court, including complaints for superintending control under MCR 7.306.

3. MCR 7.306(A) provides that a complaint for superintending control may be filed when a lower court has failed to perform a duty, has exceeded its jurisdiction, or has otherwise acted in a manner requiring correction.

4. MCR 7.316(A)(1) authorizes this Court to issue orders of superintending control "to correct or prevent a clear legal error by a lower court or tribunal when the error would otherwise not be subject to adequate appellate review."

5. MCL 600.1701 provides additional statutory authority for superintending control proceedings.

6. The Supreme Court's superintending control power is constitutionally conferred, plenary in scope, and not dependent on the existence of any other remedy. *Judges of the 74th Judicial District v Bay County*, 385 Mich 710, 727 (1971). This power is "extraordinary" and extends to "the entire range of supervisory authority necessary to ensure the proper and efficient administration of justice." *Id.*

7. This Court's superintending control authority permits it to "correct abuses of judicial power, to compel the performance of judicial duties, and to ensure the orderly administration of justice" when no other remedy is adequate. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–42 (1993).

8. Petitioner has a related appeal pending before the Michigan Court of Appeals, Case No. 366810. That appeal does not provide adequate or timely relief for the extraordinary circumstances presented here. This Petition is filed concurrently with, and incorporates by reference, Petitioner's Application for Writ of Mandamus, Petition for Writ of Habeas Corpus, and Application to Proceed In Forma Pauperis.

## IV. STANDARD FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL

### A. Three-Part Test

42. Superintending control is an extraordinary remedy available when the normal appellate process is inadequate. This Court has articulated a three-part test:

43. **First**, the party seeking relief must demonstrate that the lower court has failed to perform a clear legal duty, or has exceeded its jurisdiction. *Hinkle v Wayne Co Clerk*, 467 Mich 337, 340 (2002); *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351–52 (1975).

44. **Second**, the party must show there is no other adequate legal remedy. *Lapeer County Clerk v Lapeer Circuit Court*, 469 Mich 146, 155 (2003).

45. **Third**, the party must show that the issue presented is one of significant public interest or that the failure to act will result in material injustice. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–42 (1993); *In re Hague*, 412 Mich 532, 540 (1982).

46. This standard is informed by the historical scope of mandamus and related prerogative writs. *Teasel v Dep't of Mental Health*, 419 Mich 390, 408–09 (1984).

---

### B. Relationship to Mandamus

47. While superintending control is related to the common-law writ of mandamus, it is broader in scope. This Court has explained that superintending control "is not limited to the traditional grounds for mandamus, but also encompasses review of clear legal errors that would otherwise not be subject to adequate appellate review." *Chevalier v Barnard*, 290 Mich 202, 206 (1939); *Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich 478, 480 (1929). The Court exercises superintending control "to prevent a failure of justice" and to ensure that courts within its supervision comply with the law. *Teasel*, 419 Mich at 408–09.

---

### C. Standard Applied Here

48. As demonstrated below, Petitioner satisfies all three elements:

   - The trial court has failed to perform mandatory legal duties (best interest analysis, evidentiary hearings, parenting time enforcement, due process protections) and has exceeded its jurisdiction (ex parte orders without emergency, off-docket evidence routing, punitive civil contempt);
   - No adequate legal remedy exists because the pending appeal cannot prevent the ongoing daily harm of parent-child separation, and the corrupted record renders appellate review structurally deficient;
   - The constitutional dimensions of this case — involving the fundamental right to parent, structural court bias, the welfare of a minor child, and the documented scale of 1,127 judicial violations — present issues of significant public interest that only this Court can address.

## V. ARGUMENT

### A. The Trial Court Failed to Perform Clear Legal Duties

**Issue:** Whether the trial court's failure to conduct a best interest analysis, hold evidentiary hearings, enforce parenting time, and protect Petitioner's due process rights constitutes a failure to perform clear legal duties.

**Rule:** Under Michigan law, a trial court must evaluate all 12 statutory best interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)–(l) before making any custody determination or modification. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994); *DeRose v DeRose*, 469 Mich 320, 333 (2003); *Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457, 465 (1996). The Due Process Clauses of the Michigan and United States Constitutions require, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a parent's custodial rights are modified. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–54 (1982); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 649 (1972); *Lassiter v Dep't of Social Services*, 452 US 18, 27 (1981). The right to parent is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

**Application:**

49. **Mandatory Best Interest Analysis.** The trial court in this case has never conducted the mandatory analysis under MCL 722.23. It suspended Petitioner's parenting time and effectively modified custody without any findings on the statutory factors. This is not a case where the court exercised discretion and reached a debatable conclusion; the court performed no analysis at all.

50. **Mandatory Parenting Time Enforcement.** MCL 552.644 provides that the court "**shall**" enforce parenting time orders. The Legislature's use of "shall" — as opposed to "may" — creates a mandatory, non-discretionary duty. Despite Watson's documented violations of parenting time, the trial court has refused to enforce parenting time, has taken no enforcement action against Watson, and has instead imposed punitive sanctions exclusively against Petitioner. This constitutes a failure to perform the mandatory duty imposed by MCL 552.644.

51. **Parenting Time Protections.** MCL 722.27a creates a statutory presumption that parenting time is in the best interest of the child. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that parenting time "shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." The trial court's wholesale elimination of parenting time without the required analysis violates these mandatory protections. *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 49–51 (1992); *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010); *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 512 (2003); *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 27–28 (2010); *Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556, 572 (2015); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014).

52. **Evidentiary Hearing Requirements.** MCR 3.210(C) and MCR 3.207(B) establish requirements for hearings in custody and parenting time matters. Petitioner repeatedly requested evidentiary hearings. The trial court denied these requests and entered dispositive orders without any hearing — in violation of the court rules and Petitioner's fundamental right to be heard.

53. **Due Process Violations.** The trial court denied Petitioner the protections guaranteed by Const 1963, art 1, § 17 and US Const amend XIV by:

   - **(a)** Suspending parenting time without notice or hearing;
   - **(b)** Denying motions without hearings in violation of MCR 2.119;
   - **(c)** Suppressing Petitioner's filings from the record;
   - **(d)** Channeling evidence outside the record and beyond Petitioner's ability to review and challenge;
   - **(e)** Imposing a $250 bond barrier on all motions, effectively denying Petitioner access to the court.

**Conclusion:** Each of these failures independently constitutes a failure to perform a clear legal duty. Taken together, they represent a systemic denial of due process that satisfies the first prong of the superintending control standard.

### B. The Trial Court Exceeded Its Jurisdiction

**Issue:** Whether the trial court's ex parte orders, off-docket evidence routing, punitive civil contempt, and failure to recuse constitute an excess of jurisdiction.

**Rule:** A court exceeds its jurisdiction when it acts in a manner not authorized by law. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 442 (1993).

**Application:**

54. **Ex Parte Orders Without Authority.** The trial court entered orders affecting custody and parenting time on an ex parte basis at a rate of 44% (24 of 55 orders). Ex parte modification of parental rights is permitted only in narrow, emergency circumstances under MCR 3.207(B). No such emergency circumstances were present or documented here. The systematic use of ex parte orders on substantive custody matters is an exercise of power unauthorized by law.

55. **Off-Docket Evidence Routing.** By directing that evaluations "be submitted to judge's secretary, not to be filed with clerk," the trial court created a shadow record of evidence. No provision of the Michigan Court Rules authorizes a court to consider evidence outside the official record while simultaneously denying a party access to that evidence. This practice exceeded the bounds of the trial court's authority. *People v Cress*, 468 Mich 678, 691 (2003).

56. **Punitive Use of Civil Contempt.** Civil contempt is coercive, not punitive. When contempt sanctions lose their coercive character and become punitive, the court has exceeded its civil contempt authority. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 96–97 (1987). Jailing Petitioner for 59 days on conditions he could not satisfy — while denying him the right to counsel recognized in *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480, 490 (1990) — transformed civil contempt into criminal punishment without criminal procedural protections.

57. **Judicial Bias and Failure to Recuse.** The cumulative pattern — ex parte contacts, one-sided evidence consideration, denial of hearing rights, suppression of Petitioner's filings, disproportionate sanctions, and zero enforcement of Watson's violations — would cause a reasonable person to question the court's impartiality under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 326 (2006).

**Conclusion:** The trial court's conduct constitutes an excess of jurisdiction at multiple levels, satisfying the first prong of the superintending control standard through an independent pathway.

### C. No Adequate Legal Remedy Exists

**Issue:** Whether the pending Court of Appeals appeal, mandamus, habeas corpus, or any other available remedy is adequate to address the totality of the lower court's dysfunction.

**Rule:** A remedy is adequate only if it can effectively redress the injury. *Hinkle*, 467 Mich at 340. The party must show there is no other adequate legal remedy. *Lapeer County Clerk*, 469 Mich at 155.

**Application:**

58. **Irreparable Harm Continues Daily.** Every day that Petitioner is separated from his son causes irreparable harm to the parent-child bond. The current separation has now reached **229 consecutive days** since July 29, 2025, with a total of 269 days of withholding across two periods. L.D.W. was born November 9, 2022 — he is approximately 3 years and 5 months old. The fact that Petitioner maintained successful 50/50 custody for 438 days under the temporary order — only to have it revoked via ex parte order — makes the current separation all the more devastating. No appellate reversal, no matter how complete, can restore this lost developmental time.

59. **Constitutional Injury Is Ongoing.** The deprivation of Petitioner's fundamental parental rights is not a past injury — it is an ongoing constitutional violation that compounds daily. Each day without contact deepens the alienation between father and son and makes eventual reunification more difficult.

60. **The Appellate Record Is Corrupted.** The trial court's practice of routing evidence outside the official record means that the Court of Appeals will be reviewing an incomplete and tainted record. An appellate court cannot review what it cannot see. This structural defect cannot be cured through the normal appellate process — it can only be addressed through this Court's superintending control, which allows examination of the trial court's conduct itself, not merely the adequacy of the record.

61. **The Pending Appeal Cannot Provide Timely or Complete Relief.** The Court of Appeals appeal (No. 366810) is pending but:

   - **(a)** Cannot order the immediate restoration of parenting time;
   - **(b)** Cannot remove a biased judge — even if the COA reverses, the case will be remanded to the same judge, perpetuating the same structural problems;
   - **(c)** Operates on a timeline measured in months and years — incompatible with a young child's developmental needs;
   - **(d)** Reviews individual orders for abuse of discretion — it cannot conduct the systemic review necessary to identify and address a pattern of misconduct spanning 1,127 judicial violations.

62. **Mandamus Addresses Specific Acts Only.** Mandamus (MCR 7.306) compels specific acts. It cannot restructure an entire case's management, remove a judge, and monitor future compliance.

63. **Habeas Corpus Addresses Custody Only.** A writ of habeas corpus would address the immediate custody question but not the underlying procedural violations, judicial bias, contempt abuse, or evidence suppression that created the current situation.

64. **The JTC Addresses Judicial Conduct Only.** A Judicial Tenure Commission complaint — which Petitioner has filed — addresses the judge's fitness for office but provides no direct relief to Petitioner or L.D.W.

**Conclusion:** No individual remedy is adequate to address the totality of the lower court's dysfunction. Superintending control is the only remedy with sufficient breadth to address every dimension of the trial court's failure.

### D. Extraordinary Circumstances Warrant Immediate Intervention

**Issue:** Whether the circumstances of this case are sufficiently extraordinary to warrant this Court's exercise of superintending control.

**Rule:** The extraordinary writ must be necessary to prevent material injustice. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–42 (1993); *In re Hague*, 412 Mich 532, 540 (1982). When fundamental constitutional rights are being violated on an ongoing basis, material injustice is self-evident.

**Application:**

65. **Duration of Separation.** After 438 days of successful 50/50 custody under the temporary order, the trial court reversed the arrangement via ex parte order, resulting in **229 consecutive days** of complete separation between a father and his young son — with a total of 269 days of withholding across two periods — without any finding that contact would endanger the child. This is extraordinary by any measure. This is not a gap in visitation — it is a complete and total severance of the parent-child relationship, accomplished through judicial inaction and affirmative misconduct. The trial court's revocation of a functioning custody arrangement that had operated successfully for over fourteen months underscores the arbitrary nature of the court's actions.

66. **Fundamental Rights at Stake.** The right to parent is among the most fundamental rights protected by the Constitution. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Santosky*, 455 US at 753; *Stanley*, 405 US at 649. The wholesale elimination of this right without due process demands the highest level of judicial scrutiny.

67. **Child Welfare Emergency.** L.D.W. is being deprived of a meaningful relationship with his father during the most critical developmental period of his life. Developmental research establishes that prolonged, involuntary parent-child separation during early childhood causes severe emotional, psychological, and developmental harm. Every day that passes without intervention deepens this harm irreversibly.

68. **Access to Justice.** The Michigan Constitution (art 1, § 13) guarantees every suitor the right to prosecute or defend their suit. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 374 (1971), recognizes that where the state holds a monopoly over the resolution of fundamental disputes, it must ensure meaningful access. *M.L.B. v S.L.J.*, 519 US 102, 116–17 (1996), extends this principle to parental rights cases. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), requires that the procedural protections afforded match the magnitude of the rights at stake.

69. The trial court's systematic exclusion of Petitioner from proceedings — suppressing filings, denying hearings, channeling evidence away from the record, imposing $250 bond barriers — has denied Petitioner the meaningful access to justice guaranteed by the Constitution.

70. **Systemic Concerns and Public Interest.** The Real Party in Interest's employment within the Michigan court system raises concerns about structural bias that transcend this individual case. This case presents issues of significant public interest:

   - **(a)** The rights of pro se litigants to fair proceedings in Michigan family courts;
   - **(b)** The enforceability of mandatory parenting time statutes;
   - **(c)** The limits of civil contempt power when used against indigent litigants;
   - **(d)** The effect of financial barriers on access to judicial remedies;
   - **(e)** The duty of courts to protect children's fundamental right to relationships with both parents;
   - **(f)** The appearance of institutional bias when a court system employee receives favorable treatment.

71. This Court's intervention will establish important precedent on the scope of superintending control in family law cases and the obligations of circuit courts to comply with mandatory statutes.

**Conclusion:** The extraordinary circumstances of this case — including the duration of separation, the severity of constitutional violations, the child welfare emergency, and the systemic concerns — compel this Court's intervention.
