## I. INTRODUCTION

1. I, Andrew Pigors, file this formal complaint against the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill, Judge of the 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County, Michigan, for persistent violations of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, the Michigan Court Rules, and the constitutional rights of a litigant.

2. This complaint is supported by a careful review of the complete court record, which reveals a pervasive pattern of judicial conduct violations across multiple canons of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, including repeated ex parte adjudication, refusal to consider one party's evidence, disparate treatment of the parties, and systematic denial of due process.

3. The violations are not isolated errors. They form a **recurring pattern** of proceedings in which Complainant was denied meaningful participation, ex parte order entry, and procedural irregularity that has resulted in a **236-day parent-child separation** (from August 8, 2025 to April 1, 2026) imposed without due process.

## II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

4. Complainant is the father of minor children who are the subject of custody proceedings (Case No. 2024-001507-DC) before Judge McNeill.

5. Since the inception of these proceedings, Judge McNeill has:

   a. Entered **24 of 55 total orders (43.6%) on an ex parte basis** — without notice to Complainant and without opportunity to be heard;

   b. On **August 8, 2025 alone**, entered **FIVE separate ex parte orders** suspending all of Complainant's parenting time;

   c. Failed to schedule the required follow-up hearing within 14 days as mandated by **MCR 3.207(B)(3)**;

   d. Maintained the suspension for **236 consecutive days** (from August 8, 2025 to April 1, 2026) and counting, without a best-interest hearing under MCL 722.23;

   e. Demonstrated numerous contradictions in the record — including finding "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" (August 23, 2024 Final Judgment) while subsequently ordering a total suspension of Father's parenting time; refusing to view Father's exculpatory photographs (August 28, 2025) while relying on Defendant's unsworn ex parte allegations; and imposing a $250 filing bond on Father only (May 16, 2025) while Defendant faced no comparable restriction — indicating unreliable and arbitrary decision-making;

   f. Demonstrated a temporal pattern of procedural irregularity with the opposing party and their appellate attorney that creates a reasonable appearance of impropriety.

6. Complainant acknowledges that he was found in violation of the personal protection order and served a period of incarceration. Complainant does not minimize these events. However, PPO violations — even those resulting in incarceration — do not constitute legal grounds to permanently sever the parent-child relationship without a best-interest hearing under MCL 722.23. The Michigan Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a parent's constitutional right to the care and custody of their children is fundamental and may not be terminated without due process of law. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014); *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The appropriate remedy for PPO violations is the criminal sanction already imposed — not the indefinite suspension of all parenting time without any hearing on the merits. This candid disclosure underscores that the judicial misconduct documented herein — the ex parte adjudication, the refusal to hold hearings, the disparate treatment — cannot be excused by Complainant's own imperfect history.

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND — PATTERN OF JUDICIAL MISCONDUCT

### A. Ex Parte Orders Without Notice (24 of 55 Orders = 43.6%)

6. Throughout these proceedings, Judge McNeill has entered **24 of 55 total orders on an ex parte basis** — meaning 43.6% of all judicial orders in this case were issued without providing notice to Complainant and without affording him an opportunity to be heard. This rate of ex parte adjudication is extraordinary and, standing alone, raises serious questions about whether Complainant has received the procedural protections guaranteed by the Michigan Constitution and the Michigan Court Rules.

7. The most egregious single instance occurred on **August 8, 2025**, when Judge McNeill entered **five separate ex parte orders on a single day**, collectively suspending all of Complainant's parenting time. Complainant received no advance notice of any of these orders, no opportunity to present evidence or argument, and no hearing of any kind before the court eliminated his contact with his children. MCR 3.207(B)(3) requires that when an ex parte order affecting custody or parenting time is entered, a hearing must be scheduled within 14 days. No such hearing was scheduled within 14 days. In fact, review of the docket chronology reveals that the average delay between ex parte orders and subsequent hearings in this case is **20.7 days** — nearly 50% longer than the court rule permits.

8. The record further reveals that orders in this case **frequently precede hearings** rather than follow them: 90 instances of orders preceding hearings compared to 71 instances of hearings preceding orders. This inversion of the normal judicial process — in which evidence is heard before rulings are made — is consistent with predetermined outcomes rather than deliberative adjudication.

### B. Refusal to Consider Father's Evidence

9. At multiple hearings, Judge McNeill refused to consider evidence proffered by Complainant. When Complainant attempted to present documentary evidence, testimony, and verified filings in support of his positions, the court declined to receive them, effectively muting one party while permitting the other to proceed unimpeded. This pattern is reflected in the court record across multiple docket entries. For example, on August 28, 2025, the Court refused to view Father's photographs from the July 29, 2025 exchange showing the child in good health and spirits — while simultaneously relying on Defendant's unsworn ex parte allegations. On September 4, 2025, the Court again declined to consider Father's photographic evidence while sustaining the ex parte suspension. These inconsistencies — finding equal parental bond yet suspending all contact; refusing exculpatory evidence while accepting ex parte submissions — are consistent with arbitrary, one-sided decision-making rather than the careful weighing of evidence required by MCL 722.23.

10. At no point has Judge McNeill conducted a proper best-interest hearing under MCL 722.23(a)–(l) to evaluate the 12 statutory factors before suspending Complainant's parenting time. The suspension has now lasted **236 consecutive days** (from August 8, 2025 to April 1, 2026) without such a hearing — the longest parent-child separation in this case's history.

### C. Self-Ruling on Own Disqualification (MCR 2.003(D) Violation)

11. Complainant filed a motion for disqualification of Judge McNeill pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), alleging actual bias and prejudice. Rather than referring the motion to the Chief Judge of the 14th Circuit Court for determination — as expressly required by MCR 2.003(D) — Judge McNeill ruled on her own disqualification motion and denied it. The court rule is unambiguous: "The challenged judge shall refer the motion to the chief judge, who shall decide the motion de novo." MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a). By ruling on her own disqualification, Judge McNeill violated a mandatory procedural safeguard designed to ensure impartial review of bias claims. The record reflects multiple instances of disqualification procedure violations, including the Court's self-ruling on challenges to her own authority on July 16, 2024 (six motions denied), February 14, 2025 (parenting time motion dismissed), and across the September–November 2025 hearings on the ex parte objection.

### D. Imposing $250 Bond on Father's Filings Only

12. Judge McNeill imposed a **$250 filing bond** on Complainant's filings — a requirement not imposed on the opposing party. This bond effectively operates as a financial barrier to court access for one party only. The United States Supreme Court has held that conditioning access to the courts on payment of fees violates due process where a party cannot afford the cost. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 380–381 (1971). No finding was made that Complainant's filings were frivolous, and no bond was required of Defendant despite Defendant's own filing history. This disparate treatment compounds the appearance of partiality.

### E. Disparate Treatment — Zero Sanctions on Mother

13. Despite documented violations by Defendant Emily Watson — including obstruction of court-ordered parenting time, refusal to facilitate make-up parenting days, and filings containing material contradictions — Judge McNeill has imposed **zero sanctions** on Defendant. By contrast, Complainant has been subjected to ex parte suspensions, a filing bond, and the effective termination of his parental relationship. This stark asymmetry in judicial treatment, when viewed in the aggregate, creates an overwhelming appearance of favoritism and is inconsistent with the even-handed administration of justice.

## IV. CANONS OF THE MICHIGAN CODE OF JUDICIAL CONDUCT VIOLATED

### A. Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

14. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's pattern of proceedings in which Complainant was denied meaningful participation, including the entry of 24 ex parte orders (43.6% of all orders), the imposition of a filing bond on only one party, and the refusal to consider Complainant's evidence, undermines public confidence in the integrity of the judiciary in violation of Canon 1.

15. **Rule:** Canon 1 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides that "[a]n independent and honorable judiciary is indispensable to justice in our society" and that "[a] judge should participate in establishing, maintaining, and enforcing, and should personally observe, high standards of conduct so that the integrity and independence of the judiciary may be preserved." The Michigan Supreme Court has emphasized that "the appearance of impropriety is as damaging to public confidence as actual impropriety." *In re Ferrara*, 458 Mich 350, 369 (1998). The standard for judicial misconduct includes conduct that undermines public confidence in the judiciary; judges must maintain impartiality and avoid even the appearance of impropriety. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426; 505 NW2d 834 (1993).

16. **Application:** A careful review of this case reveals a pervasive pattern of Canon 1 violations. The sheer volume of ex parte adjudication — nearly half of all orders entered without notice — is irreconcilable with the principle that judges must "personally observe high standards of conduct." When a litigant is separated from his children for 236 days (from August 8, 2025 to April 1, 2026) based substantially on orders entered without his knowledge, the public's confidence in the integrity of the court is necessarily and gravely undermined.

17. **Conclusion:** Judge McNeill's pattern of conduct violates Canon 1 by eroding the foundational principle that the judiciary operates with integrity and impartiality.

### B. Canon 2 — Avoiding Impropriety and the Appearance of Impropriety

18. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's ex parte communications and dealings, her appearance of favoritism toward Defendant, and the temporal pattern of procedural irregularity with Defendant's legal representatives constitute impropriety or the appearance of impropriety in violation of Canon 2.

19. **Rule:** Canon 2(A) provides that "[a] judge should respect and comply with the law and should conduct himself or herself at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary." Canon 2(B) provides that "[a] judge should not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment" and should not "convey or permit others to convey the impression that they are in a special position to influence the judge."

20. **Application:** The record reveals a recurring temporal pattern: Defendant files a motion or ex parte request; Judge McNeill rules on the same day without notice to Complainant; and Ron Berry (Defendant's appellate attorney) files supporting documents within 48 hours. This pattern, which repeats across months of litigation, is documented throughout the docket. Specific instances include: April 29, 2024 (ex parte order same day as Defendant's facilitative conference); July 1, 2024 (emergency ex parte custody order granting sole custody to Defendant); August 8, 2025 (five ex parte orders entered the same day Defendant's motion was filed). The improbability of this recurring timing pattern, absent some form of coordination or communication, creates an overwhelming appearance that Defendant and her representatives enjoy a special position of influence with the court.

21. **Conclusion:** The documented pattern of same-day ex parte rulings coordinated with Defendant's filings, combined with the disparate treatment of the parties, violates Canon 2 by creating an unmistakable appearance of impropriety and favoritism.

### C. Canon 3 — Performing the Duties of Judicial Office Impartially and Diligently

#### Canon 3(A)(3) — Ex Parte Communications

22. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill violated the prohibition on ex parte communications by entering 24 orders without notice to Complainant and by participating in a pattern of same-day rulings on Defendant's ex parte filings.

23. **Rule:** Canon 3(A)(3) provides that "[a] judge should accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding, or the person's lawyer, full right to be heard according to law." The Michigan Judges' Benchbook, §5.4, further establishes specific procedures for ex parte orders in domestic relations cases, including strict notice requirements and mandatory follow-up hearings within 14 days. MCR 3.207(B)(3).

24. **Application:** Canon 3(A)(3) is the most frequently and severely violated canon in this case. The five ex parte orders entered on August 8, 2025, standing alone, represent a concentrated violation of Canon 3(A)(3) — five separate orders stripping a parent of all contact with his children, entered without a single word of notice. The Benchbook analysis further confirms non-compliance: ex parte procedures under DV Benchbook §5.4 require specific safeguards — including verified pleadings, sworn testimony, and hearings within 14 days — none of which were followed. The August 8, 2025 ex parte motion contained no certification of notice efforts, no finding of immediate irreparable harm, and the first full hearing (October 29, 2025) occurred 82 days later, far exceeding the 14-day requirement of MCR 3.207(B)(3).

25. **Conclusion:** Judge McNeill's systematic use of ex parte orders — affecting the most fundamental rights at stake in this litigation — violates Canon 3(A)(3) and the procedural protections it is designed to ensure.

#### Canon 3(B)(2) — Maintaining Order and Decorum

26. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill failed to maintain proper judicial decorum by muting Complainant during hearings and refusing to permit him to present evidence or argument.

27. **Rule:** Canon 3(B)(2) requires a judge to "be faithful to the law and maintain professional competence in it" and to "be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others."

28. **Application:** The record reveals multiple instances of Canon 3(B)(2) violations. Complainant was effectively silenced at hearings — unable to present evidence, respond to allegations, or exercise his right to be heard. While a judge may maintain order in the courtroom, using procedural mechanisms to silence one party while permitting the other to proceed unchecked is not maintenance of decorum; it is the denial of due process.

29. **Conclusion:** Judge McNeill's treatment of Complainant at hearings violated Canon 3(B)(2) by failing to afford the courtesy and procedural fairness required of a judicial officer.

### D. Canon 4 — Outside Activities and Pattern of Procedural Irregularity

30. **Issue:** Whether the documented Watson→McNeill→Berry temporal pattern of procedural irregularity constitutes or reflects improper outside activities, relationships, or communications in violation of Canon 4.

31. **Rule:** Canon 4 addresses a judge's extra-judicial activities and requires that such activities not "cast reasonable doubt on the judge's capacity to act impartially as a judge" or "interfere with the proper performance of judicial duties." Read in conjunction with Canon 2(B), this canon prohibits a judge from permitting others to convey the impression that they hold special influence.

32. **Application:** The record establishes a three-party pattern of procedural irregularity: (1) Emily Watson files a motion or ex parte request; (2) Judge McNeill rules favorably on the same day without notifying Complainant; (3) Ron Berry files supporting documentation within 48 hours. Ron Berry's voicemail appears as evidence Item #6 in the objection table to Emily Watson's ex parte motion — direct evidence of Berry's involvement in the ex parte process. Emily Watson (Defendant's sister) acts as de facto legal counsel, preparing and filing documents while maintaining a channel of communication with both the court and Berry that excludes Complainant. This pattern of procedural irregularity, repeated across months, is highly unlikely based on the record absent some form of communication or relationship that falls outside proper judicial channels.

33. **Conclusion:** The documented temporal pattern of procedural irregularity raises serious questions about improper communications or relationships that violate Canon 4's requirements of impartiality and independence.
