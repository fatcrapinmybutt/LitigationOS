## INTRODUCTION

The violations documented herein are drawn from the court record, including filed orders, transcripts, docket entries, and contemporaneous documentation. Cross-references to the Exhibit Package Index (Attachment D) are provided throughout.

## A. EX PARTE CONTACTS AND COMMUNICATIONS

**150 Documented Violations | Applicable Standard: MCJC Canon 3(B)(8), MCR 3.703**

### Legal Standard

Canon 3(B)(8) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides: "A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

MCR 3.703 establishes specific procedural requirements for ex parte PPOs, including evidentiary standards that must be met before entry without hearing.

### Pattern Description

Judge McNeill entered orders at an ex parte rate of 44% — meaning that nearly half of all substantive orders in these cases were entered without notice to or hearing from Complainant. This rate is facially extraordinary and, standing alone, warrants investigation.

### Specific Instances

#### Category A-1: Orders Entered Without Hearing

1. **Ex Parte PPO Entries** — Judge McNeill entered Personal Protection Orders without conducting the evidentiary analysis required by MCL 600.2950 and MCL 600.2950a. These orders were entered based solely on the petition of one party, without affording Complainant the opportunity to be heard, present evidence, or cross-examine.

2. **Ex Parte Custody Modifications** — Substantive modifications to custody arrangements were entered without hearing, without motion practice by either party, and without the best-interest analysis required by MCL 722.23.

3. **Ex Parte Sanctions Orders** — Financial sanctions were imposed against Complainant without prior notice that sanctions were being considered and without opportunity to respond.

#### Category A-2: Communications Outside Presence of Opposing Party

4. **Unilateral Communications** — The record reflects instances where information from one party was received and acted upon by Judge McNeill outside the presence of the other party, resulting in orders that could only have been based on ex parte information.

#### Category A-3: Orders Based on Unvetted Information

5. **Reliance on Unsworn Representations** — Judge McNeill entered substantive orders based on unsworn representations by one party without requiring sworn testimony, evidentiary foundation, or cross-examination.

## B. BIAS AND PREJUDICE

**167 MCR 2.003 Disqualification Violations | Applicable Standard: MCJC Canon 2, Canon 3(B)(5), MCR 2.003(C)(1)**

### Legal Standard

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification of a judge where the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party. MCJC Canon 3(B)(5) prohibits a judge from manifesting bias or prejudice by words or conduct.

The test is objective: whether a reasonable person would have a well-grounded fear of receiving unfair treatment. *Cain v. Michigan Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996).

### Pattern Description

Judge McNeill's conduct across 1,127 documented violations establishes a pattern of systematic bias against Complainant. The bias manifests in four observable forms: (1) derogatory language in official orders; (2) one-sided application of legal standards; (3) predetermined outcomes; and (4) differential treatment of the parties.

### Specific Instances

#### Category B-1: Derogatory Language in Orders

1. **Use of "Crazy"** — Judge McNeill characterized Complainant's legal positions using the word "crazy" in official court orders. This language has no place in judicial writing and evidences personal animus rather than legal analysis. It is bias manifested by words in direct violation of Canon 3(B)(5).

2. **Use of "Liar"** — Judge McNeill labeled Complainant a "liar" in official court orders. A judicial determination that testimony is not credible is substantively different from a personal attack labeling a party a liar. The latter reflects prejudice, not adjudication.

#### Category B-2: One-Sided Application of Legal Standards

3. **Selective Evidentiary Standards** — Judge McNeill applied heightened evidentiary scrutiny to Complainant's submissions while accepting opposing party's representations without similar scrutiny. Evidence favorable to Complainant — including a negative drug screen — was disregarded without explanation.

4. **Asymmetric Motion Practice** — Complainant's motions were denied without substantive analysis or were met with sanctions, while opposing party's requests were granted routinely, often ex parte.

#### Category B-3: Predetermined Outcomes

5. **Checkbox Orders Without Findings** — Judge McNeill issued orders using checkbox forms without the factual findings or legal analysis required by MCR 2.517. The use of checkbox orders in complex custody matters — without any written findings — suggests outcomes were predetermined rather than adjudicated.

6. **Disregard of Exculpatory Evidence** — Complainant's negative drug screen was ignored. When a court disregards evidence favorable to one party without explanation, it evidences predetermination.

#### Category B-4: Differential Treatment

7. **Sanctions for Filing Motions** — Complainant was sanctioned $250 for the act of filing motions. Filing motions is a constitutionally protected exercise of the right of access to courts. Sanctioning a party for exercising this right, in the absence of a finding of frivolousness under MCR 1.109(E), constitutes differential treatment and retaliation.

## C. DUE PROCESS DENIAL

**175+ Documented Violations | Applicable Standard: US Const Amend XIV, MI Const 1963 Art 1 §17, MCR 2.517**

### Legal Standard

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, §17 of the Michigan Constitution require that no person be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. A parent's right to the care and custody of a child is a fundamental liberty interest. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (2014).

MCR 2.517 requires that in actions tried without a jury, the court shall find the facts specially, state separately its conclusions of law, and direct entry of the appropriate judgment.

### Pattern Description

Judge McNeill systematically deprived Complainant of fundamental due process protections in proceedings that resulted in the deprivation of his parental rights — a liberty interest of the highest constitutional order.

### Specific Instances

#### Category C-1: Custody Deprivation Without Hearing

1. **No Best-Interest Analysis** — Judge McNeill deprived Complainant of custody without making findings under MCL 722.23. The best-interest factors are mandatory in custody determinations. Failure to analyze them renders the deprivation of custody constitutionally deficient.

2. **No Evidentiary Hearing** — Custody was effectively modified without an evidentiary hearing at which Complainant could present testimony, submit evidence, and cross-examine witnesses.

#### Category C-2: Motions Denied Without Analysis

3. **Summary Denial Without Findings** — Complainant's motions were denied without the factual findings or legal analysis required by MCR 2.517. A motion that raises cognizable legal issues must be addressed on its merits, not dismissed summarily.

4. **Failure to Address Arguments** — Judge McNeill failed to address Complainant's legal arguments in orders denying motions, in violation of the requirement that courts articulate the basis for their decisions.

#### Category C-3: Right to Be Heard Denied

5. **Truncated Hearings** — Where hearings occurred, Complainant's presentation time was restricted, testimony was cut short, and evidence was excluded without proper objection or ruling.

6. **Denial of Opportunity to Respond** — Orders were entered before Complainant had the opportunity to file responsive pleadings within the time periods established by the Michigan Court Rules.

#### Category C-4: MCL 722.23 Best Interest Factor Failures

Under MCL 722.23, the court must evaluate the following factors before modifying custody. Judge McNeill failed to address any of them:

## D. RETALIATION

**Documented Pattern | Applicable Standard: US Const Amend I, MI Const 1963 Art 1 §§3, 13**

### Legal Standard

The First Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, §§3 and 13 of the Michigan Constitution protect the right of access to courts. Judicial retaliation against a party for exercising legal rights is misconduct per se. *BE&K Construction Co. v. NLRB*, 536 US 516 (2002) (discussing chilling effects on right to petition).

### Pattern Description

There is a demonstrable correlation between Complainant's exercise of legal rights — primarily the filing of motions — and adverse judicial action by Judge McNeill. This correlation is documented through timeline analysis showing that sanctions, adverse orders, and escalating restrictions followed Complainant's filings with a temporal proximity that compels the inference of retaliation.

### Specific Instances

1. **$250 Sanctions for Filing Motions** — Judge McNeill imposed monetary sanctions of $250 against Complainant for filing motions. The motions raised cognizable legal issues including constitutional due process claims. No finding of frivolousness was made under MCR 1.109(E). The sanctions were imposed for the act of filing itself — a constitutionally protected activity.

2. **Escalating Orders Following Motions** — Following Complainant's filing of motions challenging Judge McNeill's orders, the court entered increasingly restrictive orders against Complainant. The temporal pattern — motion filed, then adverse order entered — is documented in the timeline analysis (Attachment C).

3. **PPO Weaponization** — The record documents 27 instances of PPO weaponization, wherein the Personal Protection Order mechanism was deployed not for its statutory protective purpose but as a tool to restrict Complainant's parental rights and access to the court system. An additional 381 impeachment items in the category of JUDICIAL_PPO_WEAPONIZATION document this pattern.

### Correlation Analysis

| Event | Response | Temporal Gap |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Motion to disqualify filed | Sanctions imposed | Days |
| Motion for hearing filed | Ex parte order entered | Days |
| Appeal-related filing | Restrictions escalated | Days–Weeks |

This pattern is analyzed statistically in Attachment C.

## E. PROCEDURAL MISCONDUCT

**161 Documented Violations | Applicable Standard: MCR 2.107, MCR 2.517, MCR 2.612(C), MCR 3.206, MCL 600.2950/2950a**

### Legal Standard

Michigan Court Rules establish mandatory procedural requirements for the adjudication of domestic relations and personal protection order cases. These requirements exist to ensure fairness, accuracy, and constitutional adequacy of proceedings that affect fundamental rights.

### Pattern Description

Judge McNeill systematically failed to comply with mandatory procedural requirements across multiple categories of court rules.

### Specific Instances

#### Category E-1: MCR 2.517 Violations — Failure to Make Required Findings

1. **Checkbox Orders** — Judge McNeill routinely issued orders in checkbox format without the factual findings required by MCR 2.517(A)(1)–(3). In custody cases involving fundamental liberty interests, findings of fact are not optional.

2. **No Conclusions of Law** — Orders depriving Complainant of custody and imposing sanctions contained no conclusions of law, in violation of MCR 2.517.

#### Category E-2: MCR 2.107 Violations — Service and Notice Failures

3. **Inadequate Notice** — Orders were entered and enforced before Complainant received notice as required by MCR 2.107.

4. **Service Irregularities** — The record reflects irregularities in service of process that deprived Complainant of the ability to respond within required timeframes.

#### Category E-3: MCL 600.2950/2950a Violations — PPO Procedural Failures

5. **Failure to Meet Statutory Standard** — PPOs were entered without findings that the statutory standard of MCL 600.2950 was met, including the requirement to demonstrate a reasonable apprehension of violence.

6. **Failure to Consider Statutory Factors** — MCL 600.2950a establishes factors the court must consider. Judge McNeill failed to analyze these factors in PPO determinations.

7. **126 MCL 600.2950/2950a Violations** — The sheer volume of violations of the PPO statutes — 126 documented — establishes that the procedural failures were not occasional oversights but systematic disregard for statutory requirements.

#### Category E-4: MCR 2.612(C) Violations — Relief from Judgment

8. **Denial of Relief Without Analysis** — Motions for relief from judgment under MCR 2.612(C) were denied without the analysis required by the rule, which mandates consideration of specific grounds including mistake, newly discovered evidence, and fraud.

#### Category E-5: Judicial Benchbook Deviations

9. **545 Documented Deviations** — The Michigan Judicial Institute publishes benchbooks providing guidance on mandatory procedural requirements. Judge McNeill deviated from these benchbook standards 545 times, as documented in the impeachment database. These deviations are not matters of judicial discretion; they represent failures to follow established mandatory procedures.

## CONCLUSION

The misconduct documented in this Narrative is not a collection of isolated errors subject to the normal appellate process. It is a pattern of systematic judicial misconduct that has deprived a parent of fundamental constitutional rights and a child of the relationship with a fit parent. The pattern is established by 1,127 documented violations across five categories, supported by 15,171 impeachment items, and demonstrated through statistical analysis showing escalation and retaliation.

The Complainant respectfully submits that this pattern of conduct warrants formal investigation by the Michigan Judicial Tenure Commission.
