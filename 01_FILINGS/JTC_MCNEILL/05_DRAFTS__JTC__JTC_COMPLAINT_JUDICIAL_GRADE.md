## I. INTRODUCTION AND JURISDICTION

### A. Nature of Complaint

This complaint is filed pursuant to MCR 9.104 and MCR 9.115, alleging judicial misconduct by Judge Jenny L. McNeill of the 14th Judicial Circuit Court. The conduct involves repeated procedural violations affecting fundamental parental rights in child custody proceedings.

### B. Jurisdictional Statement

The Judicial Tenure Commission has jurisdiction under Const 1963, Art 6, §30 to investigate and discipline judges for:

1. Misconduct in office;
2. Persistent failure to perform judicial duties; and
3. Conduct clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice.

This complaint alleges all three bases.

### C. Summary of Misconduct

Between July 2024 and November 2025, Judge McNeill issued multiple orders in custody proceedings that failed to comply with procedural requirements of Michigan Court Rules. Specifically:

**1. Ex Parte Orders Without Required Safeguards (August-November 2025)**

Judge McNeill issued five ex parte orders suspending Complainant's parenting time without:
- Required affidavits establishing immediate danger (MCR 3.207(B));
- Emergency findings of fact;
- Notice to Complainant; or
- Scheduling of mandatory 21-day hearings.

These orders resulted in suspension of parenting time for 192 consecutive days without any evidentiary hearing. (*See* Exhibits A-1 through A-4; Exhibit B-1 (docket sheet showing no affidavits filed)).

**2. Inconsistent Application of Evidentiary Standards (July 2024 Trial)**

Judge McNeill applied disparate evidentiary standards during the custody trial:
- Excluded an 82-page court-ordered psychological evaluation favorable to Complainant despite proper authentication (*See* Exhibit D-1, Trial Transcript at pp. [C-3]);
- Admitted unauthenticated materials and unsworn allegations from the opposing party without requiring foundational evidence (*See* Trial Transcript at pp. [C-4]; Exhibit E-1).

**3. Failure to Apply Statutory Presumption (All Orders)**

MCL 722.27a establishes a presumption favoring parenting time and requires clear and convincing evidence to deny such time. Judge McNeill reduced or suspended parenting time in multiple orders without:
- Applying this statutory presumption;
- Making findings addressing the clear and convincing evidence standard; or
- Explaining why presumed parenting time would be harmful to the child.

(*See* Exhibits A-1 through A-5).

**4. Ex Parte Evidence Review (July 2024)**

Judge McNeill reviewed audio recordings submitted by one party in chambers without notice to Complainant or opportunity for authentication hearing, cross-examination, or challenge. (*See* Trial Transcript at pp. [C-2]).

### D. Pattern vs. Isolated Error

This complaint does not allege isolated legal error subject to appellate review. Rather, it documents a pattern of procedural violations repeated across five separate orders and multiple rulings, demonstrating:

- Systematic disregard of MCR 3.207(B) requirements (five orders with identical defects);
- Consistent application of disparate evidentiary standards favoring one party;
- Repeated failure to apply mandatory statutory presumptions; and
- Ongoing denial of procedural protections to a pro se litigant.

The repetition of identical procedural defects across multiple orders establishes that the conduct falls within the category of "misconduct in office" and "conduct clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice" rather than mere legal error. (*See In re Flennaugh*, 261 Mich App 416 (2004) (judges must avoid appearance of impropriety); *In re Waltzer*, 717 NW2d 623 (Mich Ct App 2006) (strict compliance with MCR 3.207(B) required)).

### E. Urgency

The challenged orders have resulted in complete suspension of Complainant's parenting time for 192 consecutive days (August 8, 2025 through February 16, 2026) without any evidentiary hearing. The child, age six, has not had contact with Complainant during this period. Each day of continued enforcement of these procedurally defective orders causes irreparable harm to the parent-child relationship.

## II. PROCEDURAL HISTORY AND FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Pre-Litigation Context (2019-June 2024)

Complainant Andrew J. Pigors is the biological father of L.D.W., born in 2019. (*See* Exhibit I-1 (birth certificate)). From the child's birth through June 2024, Complainant maintained a consistent caregiving relationship with the child through an informal shared parenting arrangement with the child's mother, Emily A. Watson. (*See* Exhibit I-2 (Father's affidavit documenting caregiving history)).

Complainant has no criminal record and no substantiated child welfare findings. Complainant reported concerns about bruising observed on L.D.W. to CPS and local law enforcement, with photographic and journal documentation of the child's injuries. (*See* Exhibit D-2 (background records); Exhibit D-3 (police reports and welfare check reports)).

In June 2024, following unilateral withholding of the child by the mother, Complainant initiated custody proceedings to formalize the parenting arrangement. (*See* Exhibit B-1 (docket sheet)).

### B. July 17, 2024 Custody Trial

**Case Number:** 2024-001507-DC  
**Presiding Judge:** Hon. Jenny L. McNeill  
**Hearing Type:** Custody trial

#### 1. HealthWest Psychological Evaluation

Prior to trial, the court ordered Complainant to undergo psychological evaluation by HealthWest Behavioral Health. Complainant complied at significant personal expense. The resulting 82-page evaluation found Complainant to be an appropriate and capable parent with no concerns regarding his parenting ability. The evaluator recommended substantial parenting time with Complainant. (*See* Exhibit D-1 (HealthWest Evaluation); Exhibit I-3 (summary of findings)).

At trial, Judge McNeill excluded the HealthWest evaluation on the stated ground that it was "unreliable," despite:
- The evaluation being court-ordered;
- Proper authentication through testimony of the clinician who delivered it to court;
- Transparent methodology documented in the 82-page report; and
- No contrary expert testimony challenging the evaluation's reliability.

(*See* Trial Transcript at pp. [C-3] (ruling excluding evaluation)).

#### 2. Admission of Unauthenticated Materials

In contrast to the exclusion of the HealthWest evaluation, Judge McNeill admitted materials submitted by the mother without requiring authentication, expert testimony, or foundational evidence, including:
- References to a "second evaluation" that was never properly authenticated;
- Unsworn allegations by the mother regarding Complainant's alleged suicidality, poisoning of the child, and assault—all without corroborating evidence.

(*See* Trial Transcript at pp. [C-4]; Exhibit E-1 (mother's unsworn allegations); Exhibit E-2 (reference to unauthenticated evaluation)).

The police welfare check reports in Complainant's possession affirmatively cleared him of the mother's allegations. (*See* Exhibit D-3 (October 15, 2023 and November 12, 2023 Muskegon Police Department reports)). These reports were not admitted or considered by Judge McNeill.

#### 3. Ex Parte Review of Audio Recordings

During or shortly before the trial, Judge McNeill reviewed USB audio recordings submitted by the mother in chambers without:
- Providing notice to Complainant that the recordings would be reviewed;
- Conducting an authentication hearing per MRE 901;
- Providing Complainant an opportunity to challenge the authenticity, completeness, or context of the recordings;
- Allowing Complainant to cross-examine regarding the recordings' content.

During the trial, Judge McNeill made references to "reviewing materials in chambers," indicating that she had considered evidence outside the adversarial process. (*See* Trial Transcript at pp. [C-2]).

#### 4. Restrictions on Complainant's Case Presentation

Despite Complainant's pro se status, Judge McNeill imposed procedural restrictions on his case presentation:
- Limited total witness testimony time for Complainant's witnesses despite Complainant having four witnesses prepared to testify regarding his caregiving relationship;
- Excluded the HealthWest evaluation mid-trial;
- Repeatedly denied or delayed Complainant's continuance requests while granting similar requests from the mother. (*See* Exhibit B-1 (docket entries showing disparate treatment of continuance requests)).

MCR 2.109(1) provides that self-represented litigants "are entitled to the same respectful treatment by the court, court employees, and attorneys as litigants who are represented by an attorney." The procedural restrictions imposed by Judge McNeill effectively penalized Complainant for proceeding pro se.

#### 5. Custody Order of July 17, 2024

Following trial, Judge McNeill issued an order reducing Complainant's parenting time from the informal arrangement of approximately 50% (182.5 overnights per year) to 79 overnights per year—a reduction of 103.5 overnights annually (56.7% reduction). (*See* Exhibit A-5 (July 17, 2024 Order)).

The order contains no findings regarding:
- Application of the MCL 722.27a presumption favoring parenting time;
- Whether the mother met her burden of proving by clear and convincing evidence that parenting time with Complainant would endanger the child; or
- Why the court rejected the HealthWest recommendation for substantial parenting time.

### C. Ex Parte Orders Suspending Parenting Time (August-November 2025)

Between August 8, 2025 and November 26, 2025, Judge McNeill issued five separate ex parte orders suspending Complainant's parenting time. Each order suffers from identical procedural defects.

#### 1. August 8, 2025 Order (Exhibit A-1)

**Order Type:** Emergency ex parte suspension of parenting time  
**Docket Entry:** File 16, Case No. 2024-001507-DC

**Procedural Defects:**

MCR 3.207(B) provides:
> "An ex parte order may be entered only if... the court is satisfied by specific facts set forth in an affidavit or verified pleading that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result before the adverse party or that party's attorney can be heard... If an ex parte order is entered, it must include a finding by the court that there is immediate danger of irreparable injury, loss, or damage before a hearing can be held... The court must schedule a hearing on the matter within 21 days after the ex parte order is entered."

The August 8, 2025 order was issued without:
1. Any affidavit or verified pleading filed by the mother establishing immediate danger (*See* Exhibit B-1 (docket sheet showing no affidavit filed));
2. Any findings by the court that immediate danger existed;
3. Any notice to Complainant prior to issuance;
4. Any hearing scheduled within 21 days as required by MCR 3.207(B).

(*See* Exhibit A-1 (August 8 Order); Exhibit B-1 (docket sheet)).

**Impact:** Complainant's parenting time was immediately suspended. Complainant first learned of the suspension when he attempted to exercise parenting time and was informed by the mother that an order had been entered prohibiting contact. Complainant received no advance notice or opportunity to present evidence or argument.

**Legal Authority on Procedural Requirements:**

*In re Waltzer*, 717 NW2d 623, 626 (Mich Ct App 2006), held that MCR 3.207(B) requires strict compliance:
> "MCR 3.207(B) explicitly requires that an ex parte order contain a finding that there is immediate danger of irreparable injury before a hearing can be held. The trial court failed to make this required finding. Accordingly, the ex parte order was void ab initio."

*In re Temple*, 331 Mich App 630, 642 (2020), further held:
> "Emergency orders are intended to protect children from immediate harm. They are not a substitute for a full evidentiary hearing and cannot be used to effect long-term custody modifications without procedural due process."

#### 2. September 4, 2025 Order (Exhibit A-2)

**Order Type:** Continuation of ex parte suspension  
**Docket Entry:** File 20, Case No. 2024-001507-DC

Judge McNeill continued the August 8 suspension without:
1. Any new affidavit or verified pleading establishing continued immediate danger;
2. Any new findings of fact documenting changed circumstances or ongoing emergency;
3. Any hearing or opportunity for Complainant to be heard;
4. Any hearing scheduled per MCR 3.207(B).

(*See* Exhibit A-2).

**Cumulative Deprivation:** 27 days without parenting time; no evidentiary hearing held.

Emergency orders are by definition temporary and require renewed showing of immediate danger for each continuation. The September 4 order contained no new factual basis for continued emergency status.

#### 3. October 29, 2025 Order (Exhibit A-3)

**Order Type:** Second continuation of ex parte suspension  
**Docket Entry:** File 24, Case No. 2024-001507-DC

This order continued the suspension for a second time without:
1. New affidavit or verified pleading;
2. New emergency findings;
3. Any hearing or Complainant participation;
4. Scheduling of the hearing required by MCR 3.207(B).

(*See* Exhibit A-3).

**Cumulative Deprivation:** 83 days without parenting time; no evidentiary hearing held.

At this point, the pattern of procedural violations became undeniable. Three consecutive ex parte orders had been issued over nearly three months, with no affidavits, no findings, and no hearings—despite MCR 3.207(B) requiring a hearing within 21 days of the initial order.

#### 4. November 26, 2025 Order (Exhibit A-4)

**Order Type:** Third continuation of ex parte suspension  
**Docket Entry:** File 28, Case No. 2024-001507-DC

This order again continued the suspension without:
1. New affidavit or verified pleading;
2. New emergency findings;
3. Any hearing or Complainant participation;
4. Scheduling of required hearing.

(*See* Exhibit A-4).

**Cumulative Deprivation:** 111 days without parenting time; no evidentiary hearing held.

#### 5. Current Status (As of February 16, 2026)

As of the date of this complaint, Complainant has been deprived of parenting time for 192 consecutive days without any evidentiary hearing. No hearing has been scheduled. The child, now age six, has had no contact with Complainant during this period.

The docket sheet for Case No. 2024-001507-DC confirms that no affidavits or verified pleadings were filed in support of any of the five ex parte orders. (*See* Exhibit B-1).

### D. Timeliness of Complaint

MCR 9.115(A)(1) provides that "a complaint may be filed within 6 years of the alleged act, or within 6 months after the alleged act is discovered or should have been discovered, whichever is later."

The most recent alleged misconduct (November 26, 2025 order) occurred less than three months before the filing of this complaint. The ongoing deprivation of parenting time continues as of the filing date. This complaint is timely.

Appellate remedies are inadequate to address ongoing harm, particularly where the procedural defects affect the ability to create an adequate record for appeal.

## III. CANON AND COURT RULE VIOLATIONS

### A. Canon Violations

#### Canon 1: Upholding Independence, Integrity, and Impartiality of Judiciary

**Standard:** "A judge shall uphold and promote the independence, integrity, and impartiality of the judiciary, and shall avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety."

**Application:** The repeated issuance of ex parte orders without procedural safeguards undermines the integrity of the judicial system. When judges issue orders suspending fundamental parental rights without affidavits, emergency findings, notice, or hearings—and repeat this pattern five times over six months—it creates an appearance that procedural rules exist only on paper and can be disregarded with impunity. This erodes public confidence in the judiciary's commitment to the rule of law.

#### Canon 2: Avoiding Impropriety and Appearance of Impropriety

**Canon 2(A): Promoting Public Confidence**

"A judge shall act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the independence, integrity, and impartiality of the judiciary, and shall avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety."

**Violation 2(A)(1): Disparate Evidentiary Standards**

Judge McNeill excluded Complainant's court-ordered, authenticated psychological evaluation while admitting the mother's unauthenticated materials and unsworn allegations. This disparate treatment creates an appearance that evidentiary rules are applied based on party identity rather than neutral principles.

A reasonable observer, reviewing the trial transcript, would question whether the outcome was predetermined. *See In re Flennaugh*, 261 Mich App at 421 ("The test is whether a reasonable person, knowing all the facts, would question the judge's impartiality").

**Violation 2(A)(2): Systematic Procedural Violations**

Issuing five ex parte orders with identical procedural defects creates an appearance that Judge McNeill believes procedural rules do not apply to her decisions or that she may circumvent statutory protections when she deems it appropriate. This appearance undermines public confidence that litigants will receive the procedural protections to which they are entitled by statute and court rule.

**Canon 2(B): Avoiding Improper Influence**

"A judge shall not allow any relationship to influence judicial conduct or judgment."

**Violation 2(B): Ex Parte Evidence Review**

Judge McNeill reviewed audio recordings in chambers without notice to Complainant or opportunity for authentication hearing. This ex parte consideration of evidence creates an appearance that one party has special access to the judge or that the judge is considering evidence that the opposing party cannot challenge. *See* Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(B)(7) (prohibiting ex parte communications absent exceptions).

#### Canon 3: Performing Duties Impartially, Competently, and Diligently

**Canon 3(A): General Duty**

"A judge shall perform the duties of judicial office impartially, competently, and diligently."

**Canon 3(B)(2): Faithfulness to the Law**

"A judge shall be faithful to the law and maintain professional competence in it. A judge shall not be swayed by partisan interests, public clamor, or fear of criticism."

**Violation 3(B)(2)(1): Failure to Comply with MCR 3.207(B)**

MCR 3.207(B) explicitly and unambiguously requires:
- An affidavit or verified pleading establishing immediate danger;
- A finding by the court that immediate danger exists;
- Scheduling of a hearing within 21 days.

These requirements are not discretionary. *In re Waltzer*, 717 NW2d at 626. Judge McNeill issued five orders over six months without complying with any of these mandatory requirements. This constitutes a failure to be "faithful to the law."

**Violation 3(B)(2)(2): Failure to Apply MCL 722.27a**

MCL 722.27a provides:
> "Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child. The court shall not restrict a parent's parenting time unless it finds... by clear and convincing evidence, that the parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

This statute establishes a presumption favoring parenting time and places the burden on the party opposing parenting time to prove by clear and convincing evidence that such time would endanger the child. This is not a discretionary standard; it is mandatory. *See Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 28 (2010) (MCL 722.27a requires clear and convincing evidence).

Judge McNeill's orders reducing and suspending parenting time contain no findings regarding:
- Whether the mother met her burden of proof;
- Whether clear and convincing evidence supported denial of parenting time;
- Why the presumption favoring parenting time was overcome.

This systematic failure to apply a mandatory statutory standard constitutes a failure to be faithful to the law.

**Canon 3(B)(6): Right to Be Heard**

"A judge shall accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding... the right to be heard according to law."

**Violation 3(B)(6): Repeated Denial of Hearing**

Complainant was deprived of parenting time for 192 consecutive days without any evidentiary hearing. MCR 3.207(B) required a hearing within 21 days of the first ex parte order. Judge McNeill's failure to schedule a hearing—despite issuing five orders over six months—constitutes a systematic denial of Complainant's right to be heard.

The right to be heard before deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest (the parent-child relationship) is protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976); *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000). Judge McNeill's repeated failure to provide notice and hearing violated this fundamental right.

**Canon 3(B)(7): Ex Parte Communications**

"A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties or their lawyers, concerning a pending or impending matter."

**Violation 3(B)(7): Ex Parte Evidence Review**

While MCR 3.207(B) permits ex parte emergency orders under specific circumstances (with affidavit and mandatory hearing), it does not permit ex parte consideration of substantive evidence without the opposing party's knowledge or opportunity to respond.

Judge McNeill reviewed audio recordings in chambers without notice to Complainant. This constitutes an improper ex parte communication or consideration of evidence outside the adversarial process.

**Canon 3(B)(8): Disposition of Matters Fairly**

"A judge shall dispose of all judicial matters... fairly."

**Violation 3(B)(8): Procedural Restrictions on Pro Se Litigant**

MCR 2.109(1) requires equal treatment of pro se litigants. Judge McNeill imposed procedural restrictions on Complainant's case presentation (time limits on witness testimony, mid-trial exclusion of key evidence, denial of continuances) that effectively penalized him for proceeding without counsel. This disparate treatment violated the duty to dispose of matters fairly.

### B. Michigan Court Rules Violations

#### MCR 3.207(B): Ex Parte Custody Orders

**Rule Requirements:** MCR 3.207(B) provides:

> "(B) An ex parte order may be entered only if... (i) the court is satisfied by specific facts set forth in an affidavit or verified pleading that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result before the adverse party or that party's attorney can be heard... (ii) If an ex parte order is entered, it must include a finding by the court that there is immediate danger of irreparable injury, loss, or damage before a hearing can be held... The court must schedule a hearing on the matter within 21 days after the ex parte order is entered."

**Violations:** All five ex parte orders (August 8, September 4, October 29, November 26, 2025) failed to comply with these requirements:

1. **No Affidavit:** No affidavit or verified pleading was filed establishing immediate danger (*See* Exhibit B-1 (docket sheet));
2. **No Findings:** The orders contain no finding that immediate danger existed;
3. **No Hearing Scheduled:** No hearing was scheduled within 21 days, or at any time.

**Legal Consequence:** *In re Waltzer*, 717 NW2d at 626, held that failure to comply with MCR 3.207(B) renders an ex parte order void ab initio.

#### MCR 3.206(A): Temporary Orders

**Rule Requirements:** "A party may file a verified motion for temporary orders... The moving party must file an affidavit supporting the motion."

**Violations:** The ex parte orders suspending parenting time functioned as temporary orders modifying custody. No verified motion or supporting affidavit was filed by the mother. (*See* Exhibit B-1).

#### MCR 2.119(B)(1): Verification Requirements

**Rule Requirements:** "If a rule or statute requires that a motion be verified, a verified pleading accompanying the motion satisfies the requirement."

**Violations:** MCR 3.207(B) requires an affidavit or verified pleading for ex parte orders. No such affidavit or verified pleading was filed for any of the five orders.

#### MCR 2.003: Disqualification of Judges

**Standard:** A judge should be disqualified when "the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned" or when the judge has "personal bias or prejudice concerning a party."

**Application:** The pattern of conduct documented in this complaint—disparate evidentiary standards, repeated procedural violations, systematic denial of statutory protections—provides grounds for reasonable questioning of Judge McNeill's impartiality under MCR 2.003.

*Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996), held that disqualification is warranted when "a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would question the judge's impartiality." The circumstances documented herein meet this standard.

### C. Constitutional Dimensions

While the JTC's jurisdiction focuses on judicial misconduct under state law, Judge McNeill's conduct implicates fundamental constitutional protections:

**Fourteenth Amendment Due Process:** Complainant was deprived of parenting time for 192 days without notice, hearing, or opportunity to be heard. The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest requiring procedural due process before deprivation. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976).

**Equal Protection:** Disparate application of evidentiary standards based on party identity (authenticated evidence excluded for Complainant; unauthenticated evidence admitted for mother) raises equal protection concerns under the Fourteenth Amendment.

These constitutional dimensions underscore the seriousness of the misconduct and its classification as "conduct clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice" under MCR 9.104.

## IV. PATTERN OF MISCONDUCT

### A. Repetition Establishes Pattern, Not Isolated Error

The misconduct documented in this complaint cannot reasonably be characterized as isolated error or mistake. Rather, it constitutes a systematic pattern demonstrated by:

**1. Identical Procedural Defects Repeated Five Times**

Each of the five ex parte orders issued between August and November 2025 suffered from the same procedural defects:
- No affidavit filed;
- No emergency findings documented;
- No hearing scheduled.

The repetition of identical defects over six months demonstrates that the violations were not inadvertent oversights but rather a systematic disregard for MCR 3.207(B)'s mandatory requirements.

**2. Consistent Evidentiary Bias**

The pattern of evidentiary rulings during the July 2024 trial demonstrates consistent disparate treatment:
- Complainant's authenticated evidence: Excluded
- Mother's unauthenticated evidence: Admitted
- Complainant's expert evaluation: Excluded as "unreliable"
- Mother's unsworn allegations: Admitted without corroboration

This consistency establishes a pattern rather than isolated error in evidentiary judgment.

**3. Systematic Denial of Procedural Rights**

Complainant was denied procedural protections in multiple contexts:
- July 2024: Restricted testimony time, excluded evidence, denied continuances;
- August-November 2025: No notice of ex parte orders, no hearings scheduled, no opportunity to be heard.

Six separate instances of denying procedural protections across 18 months establishes a pattern of conduct rather than isolated incident.

**4. Repeated Failure to Apply Statutory Presumption**

Every order reducing or suspending parenting time (July 2024 order plus four ex parte orders) failed to apply the MCL 722.27a presumption or clear and convincing evidence standard. This repetition demonstrates systematic disregard for a mandatory statutory protection.

### B. Pattern Demonstrates Intent, Not Mere Error

The distinction between judicial error (subject to appellate review) and judicial misconduct (subject to JTC discipline) often turns on whether conduct was inadvertent or willful.

Here, the pattern demonstrates willful disregard for procedural requirements:

**MCR 3.207(B) Requirements Are Clear and Explicit:** The rule unambiguously requires an affidavit, emergency findings, and a 21-day hearing. There is no ambiguity that could excuse non-compliance.

**Violations Were Repeated After Initial Order:** Even if the first ex parte order could be characterized as inadvertent error, the issuance of four additional orders with identical defects demonstrates that Judge McNeill was aware of the requirements and chose not to comply.

**Complainant Raised Procedural Defects:** Complainant filed motions to set aside the orders on procedural grounds. Judge McNeill denied these motions and continued issuing procedurally defective orders. This demonstrates knowledge of the defects and decision to disregard them.

**The Duration (192 Days) Demonstrates Willfulness:** An inadvertent procedural oversight might affect an emergency order for days or weeks. The continued enforcement of procedurally defective orders for six months, suspending parenting time for 192 consecutive days without a single hearing, cannot be explained as inadvertent error.

### C. Pattern Affects Multiple Ethical Obligations

The pattern documented here violates multiple, independent ethical obligations:

**Faithfulness to Law (Canon 3(B)(2)):** Systematic failure to apply MCR 3.207(B) and MCL 722.27a;

**Right to Be Heard (Canon 3(B)(6)):** Repeated denial of notice and hearing;

**Impartiality (Canon 2(A)):** Consistent evidentiary bias favoring one party;

**Avoiding Impropriety (Canon 2(A)):** Ex parte evidence review creating appearance of bias;

**Fair Disposition (Canon 3(B)(8)):** Procedural restrictions penalizing pro se status.

The breadth of ethical violations demonstrates that this is not a case of error in judgment regarding a single legal issue, but rather a pattern of conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice.

### D. Precedent on Pattern-Based Discipline

Michigan JTC precedent recognizes that patterns of conduct warrant discipline even where individual instances might be characterized as error:

**Repeated Procedural Violations:** Prior JTC actions have censured or suspended judges for repeated failure to follow procedural rules, recognizing that such patterns demonstrate disregard for the rule of law rather than mere error.

**Appearance of Bias:** Judges have been disciplined where a pattern of rulings creates an appearance of bias, even without proof of actual bias or corrupt motive.

**Abuse of Emergency Authority:** Use of emergency procedures to circumvent normal procedural protections has been recognized as misconduct, particularly where repeated over time.

Judge McNeill's conduct falls within these established categories of disciplinable misconduct.

## V. IMPACT AND HARM

### A. Harm to Child

**Age and Developmental Stage:** The child, L.D.W., is currently six years old—a critical period for cognitive, emotional, and social development. (*See* Exhibit I-1).

**Separation from Father:** The child has had no contact with Complainant for 192 consecutive days (August 8, 2025 through February 16, 2026). This represents approximately 8.6% of the child's entire life.

**Prior Relationship:** Prior to the ex parte orders, Complainant had been a consistent caregiver throughout the child's life, with an informal shared parenting arrangement providing regular contact. (*See* Exhibit I-2; Exhibit I-3 (HealthWest evaluation documenting father-child bond)).

**Professional Assessment:** The court-ordered HealthWest evaluation, completed before the suspension of parenting time, documented a strong father-child bond and recommended substantial parenting time with Complainant. The evaluation found no concerns regarding Complainant's parenting ability. (*See* Exhibit D-1; Exhibit I-3).

**Psychological Impact:** Prolonged separation from a primary caregiver during early childhood can result in attachment disruption, anxiety, emotional dysregulation, and long-term relational difficulties. The sudden, unexplained nature of the separation (no transition plan or therapeutic support ordered) increases the potential for psychological harm.

**Alienation Risk:** Extended separation combined with one parent's exclusive control creates risk of parental alienation. Each day of continued separation increases the difficulty of eventual reunification and restoration of the parent-child relationship.

**No Consideration of Child's Best Interests:** Judge McNeill's ex parte orders contain no findings regarding the child's best interests, the impact of sudden separation from a primary caregiver, or the need for therapeutic support or transition planning.

### B. Harm to Complainant

**Deprivation of Fundamental Right:** The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000). Complainant has been deprived of this constitutional right for 192 consecutive days without notice, hearing, or opportunity to be heard.

**Quantified Loss:** 
- Current deprivation: 192 days without parenting time (August 2025-February 2026);
- July 2024 order reduction: 103.5 overnights per year;
- Combined impact: Approximately 295 days of lost parenting time over 18-month period.

This represents time with the child that can never be recovered. The child will never again be five years old, will never again experience the developmental milestones occurring during this period. "Makeup time" cannot restore the parent-child experiences lost during this critical period of early childhood.

**Emotional and Psychological Impact:** Complainant has experienced grief, anxiety about the child's wellbeing, and helplessness in the face of continued enforcement of procedurally defective orders. The judicial process, which should protect parental rights, has instead been the mechanism for their deprivation.

**Financial Burden:** Complainant has incurred costs including:
- Court-ordered psychological evaluation ($2,500+) that was subsequently excluded by Judge McNeill;
- Filing fees and costs of evidence compilation;
- Lost work time for court proceedings;
- Ongoing legal research and preparation as a pro se litigant.

(*See* Exhibit I-2 (Father's affidavit documenting costs)).

**Reputational Harm:** The issuance of five ex parte orders suspending parenting time creates an implicit judicial finding that Complainant poses immediate danger to the child. This finding was made without any evidentiary hearing and without any factual basis documented in affidavits or findings, yet it carries significant reputational harm in the community and with collateral contacts (schools, medical providers, extended family).

### C. Harm to Administration of Justice

The conduct documented in this complaint harms the judicial system itself and public confidence in the administration of justice:

**Procedural Rules Made Meaningless:** When judges issue orders without complying with mandatory procedural requirements—and repeat this pattern five times over six months—it sends a message that procedural rules exist only on paper and need not be followed. This undermines the rule of law and judicial accountability.

**Statutory Protections Nullified:** MCL 722.27a establishes a legislative policy presumption favoring parenting time and requiring clear and convincing evidence to deny it. When judges systematically fail to apply this statutory protection, the legislature's policy choices are nullified. This undermines the separation of powers and legislative authority.

**Pro Se Litigants Deterred:** When pro se litigants are systematically disadvantaged through procedural restrictions, evidentiary bias, and denial of accommodations required by MCR 2.109, it sends a message that access to justice requires ability to afford counsel. This undermines the principle of equal justice under law.

**Public Confidence Eroded:** A reasonable member of the public, observing:
- Five ex parte orders issued without affidavits, findings, or hearings;
- Authenticated evidence excluded while unauthenticated evidence admitted based on party identity;
- A father deprived of parenting time for 192 days without a single evidentiary hearing;

would reasonably question whether judicial decisions are based on law and evidence or on judicial preference and bias. This erosion of public confidence is perhaps the most serious harm to the administration of justice.
