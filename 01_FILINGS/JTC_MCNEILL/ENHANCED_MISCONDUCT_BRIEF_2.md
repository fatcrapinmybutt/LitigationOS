## II. SUMMARY OF COMPLAINT

Complainant Andrew J. Pigors files this Complaint against the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill, Judge of the 14th Judicial Circuit Court, Muskegon County, Michigan, for a pattern of systematic judicial misconduct in Case Nos. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP (*Pigors v. Watson*).

**The core violations are:**

1. **329+ days of parent-child separation without evidentiary hearing** — Father Andrew Pigors has been separated from his minor son L.D.W. for over 329 days without ever receiving the evidentiary hearing mandated by MCR 3.207(C)(5).

2. **44% ex parte order rate** — 24 of 55 orders (44%) were entered ex parte, all presided over by Judge McNeill, constituting a statistical anomaly demonstrating systematic one-sided adjudication.

3. **August 8, 2025: Five separate ex parte orders entered on a single day** — without notice to Father, without hearing, and without written findings of endangerment.

4. **Denial of disqualification motion without hearing** — In violation of MCR 2.003(D), Judge McNeill denied her own disqualification motion, ruling: "DENIED. No bias shown."

5. **Muting of pro se litigant during hearings** — 7 documented instances of Father being muted during a single hearing, preventing him from presenting evidence or argument.

6. **Ex parte review of evidence** — Judge McNeill's staff listened to a USB recording submitted by Mother before any hearing, outside Father's presence, constituting prohibited ex parte communication.

7. **Motion gatekeeping** — Judge McNeill imposed sanctions on Father for filing motions without ever making a vexatious litigant finding, effectively denying access to the court.

## IV. SPECIFIC INSTANCES OF JUDICIAL MISCONDUCT

### Instance 1: Ex Parte Order Entered Without Notice (August 8, 2025)

**Facts:** On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five (5) separate ex parte orders on a single day, suspending Father's parenting time. None of these orders were preceded by notice to Father. No hearing was held. No written findings of endangerment were made as required by MCL 722.27a(3) (clear and convincing standard).

**Authorities Violated:** MCR 3.207(B); MCR 3.207(C)(5); MCL 722.27a(3); Canon 3(B)(2)

**Verbatim from order:** *"This order is entered [without] a hearing."*
— Source: 20260208_175153_MEEK234_STACK_PERMAFIX5.md; Order dated 5/16/2025, Judge Jenny L. McNeill

### Instance 2: Ex Parte Evidence Review (August 5, 2025)

**Facts:** Mother Emily Watson submitted a USB recording to the clerk's office on August 5, 2025. The same day, Judge McNeill's staff listened to the recording outside Father's presence and without his knowledge. When confronted, Judge McNeill admitted on the record:

> **"Yes, I had my staff listen to it."**
> — Source: APPELLATE_BRIEF_DOCUMENTED_VIOLATIONS(1).md

Father asked: *"Your Honor, have you already listened to this recording?"* — confirming the ex parte nature of the review. This constitutes prohibited ex parte communication under Canon 3(A)(4) and MCR 2.107(G), which mandates that copies of any document submitted to the court be served on all parties.

**Asymmetric Recording Treatment:** Judge McNeill admitted Lori Watson's USB recording — obtained during a phone call without Father's consent, potentially a felony under MCL 750.539d — while refusing to admit Father's lawfully made November 2023 audio recording of Albert Watson stating: *"I will make sure that you don't see your son."* Father's recording was made in person at 2160 Garland Dr. while cooking dinner with his son, complying with Michigan's one-party consent law. The court credited the illegal recording while excluding the legal one — a textbook manifestation of the *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal* (556 U.S. 868) standard for unconstitutional judicial bias. Furthermore, law enforcement investigated Father on nine separate occasions (NSPD-2023-08121 through NS2505078) with zero findings of any violation, yet Judge McNeill never cited this exculpatory record in any order.

### Instance 3: Disqualification Motion Denied Without Proper Procedure

**Facts:** On September 25, 2024, Father filed a Motion to Disqualify under MCR 2.003. Judge McNeill denied the motion herself, stating: **"DENIED. No bias shown."**

**Procedural Defect:** Under MCR 2.003(D)(1), the motion should have been decided by the chief judge or another judge — not by the judge whose disqualification was sought. Under MCR 2.003(D)(3), Judge McNeill was required to answer the motion within a prescribed time, not summarily deny it.

**Authority:** MCR 2.003(D)(1); MCR 2.003(D)(3); MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) (bias and prejudice); MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) (appearance of impropriety)

### Instance 4: Muting of Pro Se Litigant During Hearings

**Facts:** During proceedings, Father was muted **7 documented times in a single hearing**, preventing him from:
- Presenting evidence
- Making objections
- Responding to allegations
- Exercising his right to be heard

> **"Judge McNeill muted me 3 times during March hearing… refused to let me speak."**
> — Source: action_report.md

This violates Canon 3(B)(2) (accord all parties right to be heard) and the fundamental due process protections of the 14th Amendment.

### Instance 5: Motion Gatekeeping Without Vexatious Litigant Finding

**Facts:** Judge McNeill imposed sanctions on Father for filing motions and issued directives prohibiting future filings:

> **"Do not file any more. I will not look at it."**
> — Source: COMPREHENSIVE_LEGAL_ANALYSIS_Pigors_v_Watson(1).md

> **"McNeill refused to accept any of my motions… denied as frivolous with no reasoning."**
> — Source: action_report.md

This was done without any formal finding that Father was a vexatious litigant under MCL 600.5701, and constitutes denial of access to courts in violation of *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971).

### Instance 6: Failure to Hold Mandatory 14-Day Hearing

**Facts:** MCR 3.207(C)(5) requires:

## X. APPELLATE REVERSAL ARGUMENTS

### A. Abuse of Discretion — Custody Modification

Judge McNeill modified custody without:
- Written findings addressing MCL 722.23 best interest factors
- Meeting the clear and convincing endangerment standard of MCL 722.27a(3)
- Holding an evidentiary hearing as required by MCR 3.210(D)

**Standard of Review:** *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003) — abuse of discretion

### B. Constitutional Violations — Due Process

- Parental rights are a **fundamental liberty interest** (U.S. Const. Amend. XIV)
- *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) — parental rights as fundamental
- *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982) — clear and convincing standard required
- *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971) — court access cannot be denied
- *Fuentes v. Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67 (1972) — ex parte deprivations unconstitutional
- *Chambers v. Mississippi*, 410 U.S. 284 (1973) — evidence exclusion reversible
- *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009) — judicial bias standard

The First Amendment protects freedom of association, including the right to associate with one's child.

### C. Structural Error — Ex Parte Proceedings

The systematic use of ex parte orders to modify custody without notice or hearing constitutes **structural error** not subject to harmless-error analysis. *See* MCR 3.207(B); MCR 3.207(C)(5).

**Counterargument anticipated:**
> *"Harmless error – Andrew got hearings eventually"*
> — Source: ARGUMENT_HIERARCHY.md

**Rebuttal:** The 329+ day delay between ex parte order and any hearing, combined with zero hearings within the mandatory 14-day MCR 3.207(C)(5) window, **refutes any harmless error claim**. The pattern across the entire case is systematic, not isolated.

### D. Judicial Disqualification Error

Judge McNeill's denial of her own disqualification motion violated MCR 2.003(D)(1) (must be decided by different judge) — a standalone reversible error.

### E. Evidence Violations

Admission of unauthenticated evidence (MRE 901), recording potentially obtained in violation of MCL 750.539 (felony eavesdropping), and ex parte review of evidence without opportunity for Father to challenge — each independently reversible.
