### III. CATEGORY ONE: EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS AND ORDERS — CANON 3(A)(4)

8. Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides that a judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications or receive information made outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding, except as authorized by law.

9. **August 8, 2025 — Five Ex Parte Orders in a Single Day.** On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five (5) separate orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, all ex parte, all without prior notice to me, and all favoring Opposing Party Watson. I received no advance notice of any filing, motion, or request that precipitated these orders. I was afforded no opportunity to respond before entry. The issuance of five ex parte orders on a single day demonstrates a pattern of systematic exclusion of one party from the judicial process.

10. **August 5, 2025 — Admitted Ex Parte Review of Evidence.** On or about August 5, 2025, Judge McNeill stated on the record, in words to the effect of: "Yes, I had my staff listen to it," referring to a USB drive containing audio evidence. This admission establishes that Judge McNeill received and reviewed substantive evidence outside the presence of the parties, without notice, and without affording me the opportunity to be present, object, or offer rebuttal. This constitutes an ex parte communication regarding pending matters in direct violation of Canon 3(A)(4).

11. **November 29, 2024 — Secretary Communications on PPO and Jail Status.** On November 29, 2024, Judge McNeill's secretary transmitted emails regarding the status of a Personal Protection Order and my jail status. These communications occurred outside the presence of the parties, were not filed on the record, and concerned substantive matters in the pending case. A judge's staff acting at the judge's direction to gather or communicate case information outside party presence constitutes imputed ex parte conduct in violation of Canon 3(A)(4).

12. **Routing of Second HealthWest Evaluation.** The second HealthWest psychological evaluation pertaining to me was routed to Judge McNeill's secretary rather than through the Clerk of Court. The proper procedure requires that evaluations be filed with the Clerk's office and made available to all parties. Routing a substantive evaluation to the judge's personal staff circumvents the record, prevents contemporaneous access by the opposing party, and constitutes an ex parte receipt of case-dispositive evidence.

13. **September 8, 2025 — Instruction Not to File Documents at Clerk's Office.** On September 8, 2025, Judge McNeill instructed me not to enter documents at the Clerk's office. This directive effectively channeled my filings away from the public record and the normal filing process, creating conditions under which the judge could control, review, or exclude filings outside the adversarial process. Directing a party away from the Clerk's office undermines the integrity of the record and facilitates ex parte control of proceedings.

14. **Statistical Pattern.** The forty-four percent (44%) ex parte rate — twenty-four (24) of fifty-five (55) orders — with one hundred percent (100%) of those ex parte orders favoring a single party, is not the product of isolated judicial error. It is a structural pattern of exclusion that violates both the letter and the spirit of Canon 3(A)(4) and the Due Process Clauses of the Michigan and United States Constitutions.

14A. **Expanded Ex Parte Analysis.** A comprehensive analysis of 1,435 court documents revealed fifty-two (52) separate ex parte orders entered across these proceedings — substantially exceeding the twenty-four (24) identified in the primary docket analysis. This expanded figure demonstrates that ex parte procedure was the structural norm throughout these cases, not a rare emergency measure.

14B. **Same-Day Evidence-to-Order Pipeline.** On August 5, 2025, a USB recording was submitted to the court. Judge McNeill issued an ex parte suspension order the same day. I was not served until August 8, 2025 (a Friday). The first objection hearing was August 11, 2025 (a Monday), giving me zero business days to prepare. MCR 2.119 and MCR 2.305 require a minimum of five business days' notice. The fourth documented instance of inadequate service by Opposing Party Watson was ignored by the Court.

14C. **Brady v Maryland Violation — Ghost Evaluation.** Judge McNeill ordered me to undergo a HealthWest psychological evaluation. I complied and was cleared. Judge McNeill then obtained a second evaluation without my knowledge, authorization, or consent. I was never provided a copy, never informed of its existence, and never afforded an opportunity to challenge it. Court orders referenced this second evaluation as a basis for decisions. Judge McNeill specifically instructed me to email the first evaluation to her secretary rather than filing through the Clerk of Court, preventing public record creation and proper filing. This constitutes a violation of *Brady v Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963).

14D. **Criminally Obtained Recording.** The USB recording that triggered the August 2025 suspension was made by Lori Watson (Emily's mother) without my knowledge or consent, in violation of MCL 750.539, Michigan's two-party consent wiretapping statute — a felony. Emily's father testified to the phone call but not to the recording itself, leaving a fatal authentication gap under MRE 901. No foundation was established regarding who made the recording, when, how, whether it was edited, or whether it was properly preserved. See *People v Bednarz*, 360 Mich 552 (1960); *People v Burge*, 722 NW2d 470 (2006).

### IV. CATEGORY TWO: DENIAL OF THE RIGHT TO BE HEARD — CANON 3(B)(2)

15. Canon 3(B)(2) provides that a judge shall afford to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding the right to be heard according to law. MCR 3.207(C)(5) requires that a hearing be held within fourteen (14) days of the filing of certain motions in domestic relations cases.

16. **Muting During Hearings — Seven Incidents in a Single Hearing.** During at least one hearing, Judge McNeill muted my audio feed seven (7) separate times while I was attempting to present argument, testimony, or objections. Muting a party during a hearing is the functional equivalent of removing that party from the courtroom. Doing so seven times in a single proceeding demonstrates a deliberate intent to prevent me from being heard, in direct violation of Canon 3(B)(2).

17. **"Do Not File Any More. I Will Not Look at It."** Judge McNeill stated, on the record, words to the effect of: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." This directive constitutes a blanket refusal to consider filings by a party, an abdication of the judicial duty to review pleadings, and a direct denial of the right to be heard. No court rule authorizes a judge to refuse to consider filings by a party absent a formal vexatious-litigant finding under MCR 2.625.

18. **Zero Hearings Within the Fourteen-Day MCR 3.207(C)(5) Window.** Throughout the entirety of these proceedings, not a single hearing was held within the fourteen (14) day window mandated by MCR 3.207(C)(5). The consistent failure to schedule timely hearings on properly filed motions constitutes a systematic deprivation of the right to be heard and a pattern of non-compliance with the Michigan Court Rules.

19. **May 16, 2025 — Order Entered "Without a Hearing."** On May 16, 2025, Judge McNeill entered an order that states on its face that it was entered "without a hearing." The entry of substantive orders affecting custody, parenting time, or the rights of the parties without affording any hearing whatsoever violates Canon 3(B)(2), MCR 3.207, and constitutional due process.

20. **Motion Gatekeeping Without Vexatious Litigant Finding.** Judge McNeill imposed restrictions on my ability to file motions without making a formal finding of vexatious litigation under MCR 2.625 and without following the procedural requirements of that rule. MCR 2.625 requires specific findings before a court may restrict a party's filing rights. Judge McNeill's informal gatekeeping lacks legal authority and denies me the right to seek relief from the court.

### V. CATEGORY THREE: BIAS AND PREJUDGMENT — CANON 2 / RULE 2.11

The systematic exclusion of an entire party's evidence is inconsistent with the duty of impartial consideration and constitutes prejudgment of the merits.

### VI. CATEGORY FOUR: ABUSE OF CONTEMPT POWER

28. The contempt power is among the most coercive powers available to a court and must be exercised with restraint, in accordance with due process, and only upon clear and convincing evidence.

29. **February 28, 2025 — Contradictory Order: Fourteen Days Jail and Dismissed.** On February 28, 2025, Judge McNeill entered an order that simultaneously imposed fourteen (14) days of incarceration for contempt and dismissed the underlying matter. Using contempt power to incarcerate a father for exercising protected parental communication constitutes an abuse of judicial authority and a disproportionate sanction that shocks the conscience.

31. **$250 Bond as Barrier to Filing.** Judge McNeill imposed a $250 bond requirement as a condition of filing motions. This financial barrier, imposed without statutory authority and without a vexatious-litigant finding under MCR 2.625, functions as a de facto denial of access to the courts for a pro se litigant of limited means.

32. **Weaponization of Show Cause Proceedings.** Show cause proceedings have been used repeatedly as an instrument to threaten, intimidate, and punish me for exercising my rights as a litigant and as a parent, rather than for any legitimate remedial purpose. The pattern of show cause issuance directed exclusively at one party, while ignoring the documented violations of the other, confirms the retaliatory character of these proceedings.

### VII. CATEGORY FIVE: FAILURE TO FOLLOW GOVERNING LAW

33. A judge is required to be faithful to the law and to maintain professional competence in it. Canon 3(A)(1).

34. **Zero Written best interests findings Under MCL 722.23.** MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve (12) specific factors that a court must evaluate and make written findings on when determining custody and parenting time. Throughout the entire pendency of Case No. 2024-001507-DC, Judge McNeill has never issued a single written best-interests finding under MCL 722.23. Every custody and parenting-time determination has been made without the analysis mandated by statute, rendering each such order legally deficient.

35. **Overturned 50/50 Custody Without Vodvarka Finding.** Judge McNeill overturned an existing fifty-fifty (50/50) custody arrangement without making the change-in-circumstances finding required by *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003). Under *Vodvarka*, a court may not modify an established custodial environment absent proper cause or a change of circumstances, demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence. No such finding was made.

36. **Disregard of HealthWest Evaluation Clearing Father.** A HealthWest psychological evaluation was conducted and cleared me of the concerns raised by Opposing Party Watson. Judge McNeill disregarded this evaluation, dismissing it as a "ghost" evaluation, without any evidentiary basis for rejecting the professional findings. The disregard of a court-ordered or professionally conducted evaluation, without stated reasons and without contrary evidence, constitutes a failure to follow the law governing the consideration of evidence in custody proceedings.

37. **Failure to Apply MCL 722.23 Parenting Time Standards.** MCL 722.23 sets forth the standards governing parenting time, including the presumption that parenting time is in the best interests of the child. Judge McNeill has failed to apply these standards, has restricted and eliminated my parenting time without the required analysis, and has done so without written findings supporting the restriction.

38. **No Clear-and-Convincing Evidence Standard Applied.** Where the law requires clear and convincing evidence — including for contempt findings and for restrictions on parenting time — Judge McNeill has failed to articulate or apply this evidentiary standard. Orders restricting my parental rights and imposing incarceration have been entered without any statement that the requisite burden of proof has been met.
