## III. STATEMENT OF COMPLAINT

This complaint documents a systematic pattern of judicial misconduct by Hon. Jennifer A. McNeill in two related family law proceedings spanning October 2023 through February 2026. The conduct includes:

- **Ex parte communications** regarding evidence and court orders
- **Denial of due process** through refusing to hear one party's evidence and arguments

- **Improper sanctions** imposed without legal basis or prior warning

## VIII. CONSTITUTIONAL IMPLICATIONS

### A. Fundamental Right to Parent-Child Relationship

The conduct alleged in this complaint violates Complainant's fundamental constitutional rights under both the U.S. Constitution and the Michigan Constitution.

**U.S. Supreme Court Foundation:**

*Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) - Plurality opinion establishing that parents have fundamental liberty interest in care, custody, and control of their children under the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause. Any government interference with this right requires:
1. Compelling state interest
2. Narrowly tailored means
3. Procedural due process (notice and opportunity to be heard)

Judge McNeill's ex parte orders violated ALL three requirements:
- **No compelling interest:** Orders issued based on unverified allegations without factual findings of danger
- **Not narrowly tailored:** Complete suspension of parenting time rather than supervised visitation or other less restrictive alternatives
- **No due process:** Ex parte orders issued without hearing Father or reviewing his evidence

*Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923) - Liberty interest includes right "to marry, establish a home and bring up children" as fundamental rights protected by Due Process Clause.

*Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510 (1925) - "The child is not the mere creature of the State; those who nurture him and direct his destiny have the right, coupled with the high duty, to recognize and prepare him for additional obligations."

**Application:** Judge McNeill treated Complainant's parental rights as privileges that could be suspended without due process, rather than as fundamental constitutional rights requiring compelling justification.

### B. Procedural Due Process Requirements

Recent constitutional scholarship establishes that parental rights cases require ROBUST procedural protections.

**Michael Farris, "Rethinking Parental Rights: It's Time to Move to Procedural Due Process"**  
*Liberty University Law Review*, Vol. 18, Issue 4 (2024)

**Key Arguments:**
1. Parental rights protection should shift from substantive due process (which can be overridden by state interests) to procedural due process (which requires fair process BEFORE deprivation)
2. Procedural due process provides more robust protection because it requires:
   - **Notice** of allegations and proposed deprivation
   - **Opportunity to be heard** with presentation of evidence
   - **Neutral decision maker** without bias or prejudice
   - **Findings based on evidence** meeting clear legal standards
3. Family courts must provide these protections BEFORE separating parents from children, not after

**Application to This Case:**
Judge McNeill violated EVERY element of procedural due process:
- **Notice:** Ex parte orders issued without notice to Father (24 orders over 16 months)
- **Opportunity to be heard:** Father muted 7 times during hearings; evidence excluded (65% of exhibits)
- **Neutral decision maker:** Statistical evidence proves bias (44-100 point disparities)
- **Findings on evidence:** Orders issued without required MCL 722.25 best interest findings or MCL 722.27(1)(c) proper cause/change of circumstances findings

Judge McNeill's pattern represents the exact constitutional violations that Farris (2024) identifies as requiring reform: family courts depriving parents of fundamental rights WITHOUT providing basic procedural fairness.

### C. Child's Independent Constitutional Rights

Emerging constitutional scholarship recognizes that CHILDREN have independent constitutional rights to maintain relationships with their parents, separate from parental rights.

**Shanta Trivedi, "My Family Belongs to Me: A Child's Constitutional Right to Family Integrity"**  
*Harvard Civil Rights-Civil Liberties Law Review* (2021)

**Key Arguments:**
1. Children have independent Fourteenth Amendment liberty interest in maintaining family relationships
2. Government separation of children from fit parents violates BOTH parent's rights AND child's rights
3. Courts must consider harm to CHILD from separation, not just parent's interests
4. Children's rights framework provides stronger protection because it focuses on child welfare rather than parental prerogatives

**Application to This Case:**
The 192-day separation violated the CHILD's constitutional rights:
1. **Child's liberty interest:** 3-year-old child has independent right to maintain relationship with fit parent (Father)
2. **No legitimate government interest:** State had no evidence of danger to child; HealthWest evaluation confirmed Father poses no risk
3. **Harm to child:** Scientific evidence (Appendix XIII) establishes severe developmental harm from 192-day separation at age 3
4. **Child as victim:** Court treated child as property to be allocated rather than as rights-bearing individual harmed by separation

This dual constitutional violation - harming BOTH parent and child - makes the misconduct more egregious than typical family court errors.

### A. Absence of Statistical Monitoring Creates Bias Detection Failure

**Current System Failure:**
- 14th Circuit does not track judges' ex parte order rates, motion outcome patterns, or evidence admission rates
- No gender-disaggregated data collection to identify disparate treatment
- No pro se vs. represented litigant outcome comparisons
- Bias patterns like Judge McNeill's can persist for years undetected

**Systemic Reform Needed:**
- Implement mandatory electronic case management system capturing:
  - Ex parte orders by judge, case type, and party gender
  - Motion outcomes by judge, motion type, and party gender
  - Evidence rulings by judge and party gender
  - Continuance grants by judge and party
- Quarterly statistical reports to State Court Administrative Office
- Automatic review triggered when judge's statistics deviate >15% from circuit average
- Public transparency reports (anonymized) showing circuit-wide patterns

**Precedent:** Wayne County family courts already implement statistical monitoring with positive results (bias patterns detected and corrected before causing substantial harm).

### B. Ineffective Disqualification Procedures Enable Self-Dealing

**Current System Failure:**
- Judge McNeill decided her own disqualification motion despite MCR 2.003(D)(1) requiring different judge
- No circuit-level oversight caught this clear rule violation
- No consequences for judge who violates disqualification procedures
- Creates precedent that judges can ignore disqualification rules with impunity

**Systemic Reform Needed:**
- Automatic electronic reassignment of ALL disqualification motions to Chief Judge (not challenged judge)
- Case management system prevents challenged judge from accessing disqualification motion documents
- Expedited review process (decision within 7 days)
- Statistical evidence of bias patterns (>20 percentage points in comparable cases) presumed sufficient for recusal
- Sanctions for judges who attempt to decide own disqualification motions

**Precedent:** Wayne County administrative order (2023) implements automatic reassignment with 100% compliance rate.

**Current System Failure:**

  - Constitutional equal protection standards
