## COUNT I: CANON 3(B)(5) — EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS AND ORDERS

### THE BACKBONE VIOLATION: 24 OF 55 ORDERS (43.6%) ENTERED EX PARTE

10. Canon 3(B)(5) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides: "A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

11. MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that ex parte orders in domestic relations cases be supported by "[s]pecific facts in a verified pleading or affidavit showing that irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice."

12. Of the 55 orders entered in the underlying cases, Respondent entered 24 (43.6%) ex parte — all without proper notice to Complainant and without the verified factual showing required by MCR 3.207(B)(1). The litigation database documents 150 separate EX_PARTE_VIOLATION entries in the judicial violations table and 96 MCR 3.207(B) benchbook deviations — all attributed to Respondent.

13. The pattern of ex parte orders is chronological and persistent:
   - **April 11, 2024**: PPO Amended ex parte. Notice to appear issued.
   - **April 29, 2024**: Ex Parte Order for Custody, Parenting Time and Child Support entered.
   - **July 1, 2024**: Emergency ex parte order granting sole custody to Watson without full hearing.
   - **August 6-8, 2025**: Mass ex parte orders suspending ALL parenting time (see Count IX).

14. **HealthWest Evaluation — Direct Ex Parte Communication**: Respondent ordered Complainant to undergo a psychological evaluation at HealthWest. The judge's secretary then emailed Complainant, instructing that the completed evaluation be sent to chambers only — not filed on the record. Respondent received and relied upon the evaluation off the record and ex parte, contrary to Canon 3(B)(5)'s prohibition on considering ex parte communications concerning pending proceedings.

15. The ex parte rate of 43.6% is not an occasional procedural shortcut — it is a systemic practice that denies Complainant notice and opportunity to be heard on matters directly affecting his fundamental parental rights.

## COUNT VI: DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS

### NO HEARINGS, NO FINDINGS, NO EVIDENCE REVIEW

31. The Due Process Clauses of the United States Constitution (Amend. XIV) and the Michigan Constitution (Art. 1, § 17) guarantee that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. The United States Supreme Court has recognized parental rights as "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

32. Respondent systematically denied Complainant due process through the following:

   (a) **No best-interest hearing**: Parenting time suspended for 567+ days without a hearing on the twelve best-interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)-(l).

   (b) **Refusing exculpatory evidence**: On multiple occasions, Respondent refused to review or admit evidence offered by Complainant, including photographs from the July 29 exchange showing the child comfortable and uninjured, and an exonerating police report dated November 15, 2024.

   (c) **$250 filing bond**: Respondent imposed a $250 bond requirement for each new motion — a financial barrier to court access that violates *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971), which prohibits conditioning access to the courts on ability to pay when fundamental interests are at stake.

   (d) **Contempt → jail → job loss → housing loss cascade**: Respondent's contempt findings led to Complainant's incarceration, which caused job loss and housing instability. These consequences were then cited against Complainant in custody proceedings, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of harm.

33. The benchbook documents 87 violations under MCR 3.210(C) (post-judgment hearings without notice) and 35 violations under MCR 3.207 (temporary order deficiencies), all attributed to Respondent.
