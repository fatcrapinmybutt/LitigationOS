## III. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. Elements of Legal Malpractice in Michigan

Michigan law requires a plaintiff asserting legal malpractice to prove four elements:

> (1) the existence of an attorney-client relationship;
> (2) negligence in the representation of the plaintiff;
> (3) that the negligence was a proximate cause of an injury; and
> (4) the fact and extent of the injury.

*Simko v. Blake*, 448 Mich 648, 655 (1995); *Charles Reinhart Co. v. Winiemko*, 444 Mich 579, 585–86 (1994).

### B. The Standard of Care

An attorney is required to exercise the degree of care, skill, and proficiency commonly exercised by ordinary, reasonably competent members of the legal profession. *Simko*, 448 Mich at 656. The standard is measured by the "reasonable attorney" benchmark — what a reasonably competent attorney would have done under the same or similar circumstances. *Id.*

### C. The Case-Within-a-Case Doctrine

Michigan follows the "case-within-a-case" doctrine, which requires the malpractice plaintiff to demonstrate that "but for" the attorney's negligence, the outcome of the underlying matter would have been different. *Charles Reinhart Co. v. Winiemko*, 444 Mich 579, 586 (1994). The plaintiff must prove that, had the attorney acted competently, the plaintiff would have obtained a more favorable result. *Id.*

This doctrine effectively requires the malpractice plaintiff to re-litigate the underlying case — proving both that the attorney was negligent *and* that the underlying case would have been won (or a better result achieved) absent the negligence. *Seery v. Yale-New Haven Hospital*, cited in *Simko*, 448 Mich at 659.

### D. Expert Affidavit Requirement

MCL 600.2912d requires that a malpractice complaint be accompanied by an affidavit of merit. The affidavit must be signed by a qualified professional who has reviewed the case and concluded that the defendant's conduct fell below the applicable standard of care. The affidavit must be filed with the complaint or within 91 days of filing. Failure to comply with this requirement may result in dismissal. *Scarsella v. Pollak*, 461 Mich 547, 549 (2000).

### E. Statute of Limitations

The statute of limitations for legal malpractice is two years, running from the date the claim accrues — typically the date the plaintiff discovers or should have discovered the negligent act or omission. MCL 600.5805(8); MCL 600.5838a(1). The discovery rule tolls the statute until the plaintiff knows or should know the factual basis for the claim. *Gebhardt v. O'Rourke*, 444 Mich 535, 544 (1994).
