## I. PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

This Memorandum of Law is submitted in support of Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors's Complaint against nine Defendants arising from a multi-year scheme to defraud, harass, retaliate against, and ultimately destroy the home of a manufactured housing tenant in Muskegon County, Michigan. The Complaint asserts four federal counts (Fair Housing Act, RICO, RICO Conspiracy, and § 1983 Due Process) and eight supplemental state claims, all arising from the same core facts.

This brief demonstrates that each claim is well-pled, that jurisdiction and venue are proper, and that the damages alleged — including treble damages under RICO, the Michigan Conversion statute, and the Michigan Consumer Protection Act — are legally supported.

## II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

The essential facts, set forth in detail in the Complaint, are summarized here for the Court's convenience.

Plaintiff is the titleholder of a manufactured home at Lot 17, 1977 Whitehall Road, Laketon Township, Muskegon County, Michigan. The park is controlled through a network of corporate shells ultimately owned by Alden Global Capital, LLC, a New York hedge fund with a $275 million manufactured housing portfolio.

Over a period of years, Defendants: (1) falsified Plaintiff's billing records, omitting $6,085.99 in payments and producing three irreconcilable ledger versions; (2) coerced a lease modification increasing rent by 113%; (3) exposed Plaintiff to raw sewage (EGLE VN-017235); (4) blocked three sales of Plaintiff's home; (5) retaliated against Plaintiff for exercising legal rights; (6) filed lawsuits through a dissolved entity (fraud on court); (7) diverted Plaintiff's $1,300.26 payment to an unrelated entity (Partridge Securities); and (8) executed an unlawful lockout — drilling locks and posting a "FREE" sign — that destroyed Plaintiff's home and personal property.

The result: Plaintiff was rendered homeless, lost all personal property, and lost parenting time with his minor child.

## III. ARGUMENT

### A. The Fair Housing Act Claims Are Well-Pled (Count I)

#### 1. Legal Standard — CREAC

**Conclusion:** Plaintiff states a valid claim under the Fair Housing Act, 42 U.S.C. §§ 3601–3619.

**Rule:** The FHA makes it unlawful to "discriminate against any person in the terms, conditions, or privileges of sale or rental of a dwelling, or in the provision of services or facilities in connection therewith." 42 U.S.C. § 3604(b). Discrimination includes retaliation against a tenant for exercising rights protected by the FHA. *Walker v. City of Lakewood*, 272 F.3d 1114, 1128 (9th Cir. 2001). The FHA protects against retaliatory conduct directed at tenants who assert housing rights. *Ohana v. 180 Prospect Place Realty Corp.*, 996 F. Supp. 238, 240 (E.D.N.Y. 1998).

**Explanation:** Retaliation claims under the FHA require: (1) the plaintiff engaged in protected activity; (2) the defendant was aware of the protected activity; (3) the defendant took an adverse housing action; and (4) a causal connection exists between the protected activity and the adverse action. The temporal proximity between protected activity and adverse action may establish causation. *Bloch v. Frischholz*, 587 F.3d 771, 783 (7th Cir. 2009).

**Application:** Plaintiff engaged in protected activity by reporting sewage violations to EGLE, challenging fraudulent billing, and asserting his right to sell his home under MCL 125.2319. Defendants were aware of each instance — they received the EGLE notice, responded to billing disputes, and blocked the sales. Defendants took adverse actions including refusing rent, filing retaliatory eviction, and destroying Plaintiff's home. The temporal sequence — complaints followed by escalating retaliation culminating in lockout — establishes causation.

Moreover, Defendants subjected Plaintiff to **disparate treatment** — singling him out for adverse billing practices, maintenance neglect, and eviction while similarly situated tenants were not subjected to the same treatment.

**Conclusion:** The Fair Housing Act claims are well-pled and survive any motion to dismiss.

### B. The RICO Claims Satisfy All Statutory Elements (Counts II & III)

#### 1. Legal Standard — CREAC

**Conclusion:** Plaintiff's RICO claims under 18 U.S.C. §§ 1962(c) and (d) are well-pled.

**Rule:** A civil RICO claim under § 1962(c) requires: "(1) conduct (2) of an enterprise (3) through a pattern (4) of racketeering activity." *Sedima, S.P.R.L. v. Imrex Co.*, 473 U.S. 479, 496 (1985). A "pattern" requires at least two predicate acts within ten years, and "continuity plus relationship." *H.J. Inc. v. Northwestern Bell Tel. Co.*, 492 U.S. 229, 239 (1989).

**Explanation:**

**Enterprise.** An "association-in-fact enterprise" requires only "a purpose, relationships among those associated with the enterprise, and longevity sufficient to permit the associates to pursue the enterprise's purpose." *Boyle v. United States*, 556 U.S. 938, 946 (2009). The enterprise need not have a formal structure.

**Pattern.** Predicate acts are "related" if they "have the same or similar purposes, results, participants, victims, or methods of commission." *H.J. Inc.*, 492 U.S. at 240. Continuity may be shown by a "closed period of repeated conduct" or "past conduct that by its nature projects into the future." *Id.* at 241.

**Predicate Acts.** Mail fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1341) requires: (1) a scheme to defraud; (2) use of the mails in furtherance thereof. Wire fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1343) is identical but substitutes wire transmissions. Extortion under the Hobbs Act (18 U.S.C. § 1951) includes obtaining property through "wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear."

**Application:**

*Enterprise:* The association-in-fact enterprise consists of Alden, HOA, Shady Oaks, Partridge, VRM, and Cricklewood — corporate entities linked by common ownership and purpose. The individual Defendants (Davis, VanDam, Browley) conducted the enterprise's affairs at the park level. The enterprise's purpose was to extract maximum rent from manufactured housing tenants through fraud and coercion while using corporate shells to evade accountability.

*Pattern:* The predicate acts span years, share common participants (all Defendants), a common victim (Plaintiff), common methods (billing fraud, lease coercion, property destruction), and a common purpose (revenue extraction). The North Morris Estates criminal conviction demonstrates that the pattern extends beyond Shady Oaks — Alden's enterprise operates the same playbook across its $275 million portfolio.

*Predicate Acts:*
- **Mail fraud**: At least three fraudulent ledger mailings, each a separate predicate act. Billing statements omitting $6,085.99 in payments. Eviction notices based on fabricated arrears.
- **Wire fraud**: Electronic billing manipulation through centralized systems. Electronic diversion of $1,300.26 payment from Shady Oaks to Partridge Securities.
- **Extortion**: Coerced lease modification obtained through threats of eviction and homelessness — a 113% rent increase extracted under duress.

*Injury:* Plaintiff suffered concrete injury to property (home destroyed, payments stolen) directly caused by the racketeering activity.

**Conclusion:** The RICO claims are well-pled and entitle Plaintiff to treble damages under 18 U.S.C. § 1964(c).

#### 2. RICO Conspiracy (Count III)

Under § 1962(d), it is sufficient to allege that each Defendant agreed to the objective of the conspiracy. *Salinas v. United States*, 522 U.S. 52, 65 (1997). A conspirator need not commit a predicate act — agreement to participate is sufficient. Each Defendant's overt acts, detailed in the Complaint, establish their knowing participation.

### C. The Section 1983 Due Process Claim Is Viable (Count IV)

**Conclusion:** Plaintiff states a viable § 1983 claim for deprivation of property without due process.

**Rule:** Section 1983 provides a remedy when a person acting "under color of" state law deprives another of rights secured by the Constitution. 42 U.S.C. § 1983. Private parties may act under color of state law when they are "willful participant[s] in joint activity with the State or its agents." *Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 U.S. 922, 941 (1982).

**Explanation:** The Supreme Court has recognized that private parties who misuse state judicial processes to deprive others of property may be liable under § 1983. In *Lugar*, the Court held that a private party who obtains a prejudgment attachment through state procedures acts under color of state law. The key inquiry is whether the private party invoked "the aid of state officials to take advantage of state-created attachment procedures." *Id.* at 942.

**Application:** Defendants filed eviction proceedings in Michigan state courts: (1) in the name of a dissolved entity that had no legal capacity to sue; (2) based on fabricated billing records (three irreconcilable ledgers); and (3) supported by a redacted ledger that concealed exculpatory entries. Defendants used the state courts as instruments to advance their fraudulent scheme — the courts processed the eviction, and Defendants ultimately executed a self-help lockout to seize Plaintiff's property.

The state action element is satisfied because Defendants invoked state judicial machinery to deprive Plaintiff of his property interest in his titled home, leasehold, and personal property — all based on fraud.

**Conclusion:** The § 1983 claim is well-pled.

### D. Supplemental Jurisdiction Over State Claims Is Proper (Counts V–XII)

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1367(a), supplemental jurisdiction extends to "all other claims that are so related to claims in the action within [the Court's] original jurisdiction that they form part of the same case or controversy under Article III." *United Mine Workers v. Gibbs*, 383 U.S. 715, 725 (1966) (state and federal claims derive from a "common nucleus of operative fact").

All eight supplemental state claims — conversion, fraud, MCPA, retaliatory eviction, MMHCA violations, constructive eviction, veil piercing, and civil conspiracy — arise from the identical facts underlying the federal claims. They involve the same parties, the same property, the same time period, and the same fraudulent scheme. Supplemental jurisdiction is proper.

### E. Corporate Veil Piercing Is Warranted (Count XI)

**Conclusion:** The corporate veil of the shell entities should be pierced to hold Alden and HOA directly liable.

**Rule:** Michigan courts pierce the corporate veil where: (1) the entity is a "mere instrumentality" of another; (2) the corporate form was used to commit fraud or wrong; and (3) the plaintiff suffered unjust loss. *Foodland Distribs. v. Al-Naimi*, 220 Mich. App. 453, 456 (1996). Factors indicating instrumentality include: common ownership, commingled funds, undercapitalization, failure to observe corporate formalities, and use of the entity to promote fraud. *Id.*

**Application:** The evidence of instrumentality is overwhelming:

1. **Common ownership and control**: All entity Defendants are ultimately owned or controlled by Alden through HOA.
2. **Commingled funds**: Plaintiff's $1,300.26 check, payable to Shady Oaks, was cashed by Partridge Securities — a different entity.
3. **Interchangeable identity**: The lease was under "Shady Oaks," then replaced with one from "Cricklewood." Different entity names were used for different purposes.
4. **Undercapitalization**: Shady Oaks was dissolved yet continued operating — a dissolved entity has zero capitalization.
5. **Disregard of formalities**: Filing lawsuits under a dissolved entity's name is the ultimate disregard of corporate form.
6. **Fraud and wrong**: The billing fraud, coerced lease, dissolved-entity court fraud, and property destruction are the wrongs for which the corporate form was used as a shield.
7. **Unjust loss**: Plaintiff lost his home, property, and housing stability.

Additionally, Shady Oaks's post-dissolution operations are **ultra vires** under MCL 450.4802, which limits a dissolved LLC to winding-up activities. Operating a park, collecting rent, executing leases, and filing lawsuits are not winding-up activities. All such acts are void.

**Conclusion:** Veil piercing is warranted, making Alden and HOA directly liable for all damages.

### F. Damages Support Treble Recovery

Multiple statutes authorize treble damages:

1. **RICO** (18 U.S.C. § 1964(c)): "shall recover threefold the damages he sustains." Treble RICO damages are **mandatory**, not discretionary. *Agency Holding Corp. v. Malley-Duff & Assocs.*, 483 U.S. 143, 151 (1987).

2. **Michigan Conversion** (MCL 600.2919a): "3 times the amount of actual damages sustained." Applies to property wrongfully converted — here, the home and personal property destroyed during the lockout.

3. **Michigan Consumer Protection Act** (MCL 445.911): Court "may" award treble damages where the violation is willful and knowing. Given the deliberate, multi-year nature of the fraud, treble damages are warranted.

The treble damage provisions apply to different categories of underlying damages and are not duplicative. RICO treble applies to damages from racketeering activity (billing fraud, wire fraud, extortion). Conversion treble applies to the value of property destroyed. MCPA treble applies to consumer protection violations. The total recovery, including punitive damages for willful misconduct, supports the $2.45 million to $17.24 million range alleged.

**Punitive damages** are further warranted against Alden under the pattern evidence — including the North Morris Estates criminal conviction — which demonstrates that Alden's misconduct is not an isolated incident but a deliberate business model applied across a $275 million portfolio. Punitive damages are necessary for deterrence. *See BMW of N. Am., Inc. v. Gore*, 517 U.S. 559 (1996) (considering pattern of misconduct in punitive damages analysis).
