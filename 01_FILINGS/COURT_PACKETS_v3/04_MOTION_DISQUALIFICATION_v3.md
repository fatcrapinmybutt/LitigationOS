## IV. LEGAL STANDARD FOR JUDICIAL DISQUALIFICATION

### A. MCR 2.003(C)(1): Personal Bias or Prejudice

46. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge is "personally biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." The rule specifically identifies the following grounds for disqualification, among others:

   > **(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
   >
   > **(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

47. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) requires proof of **actual bias or prejudice** — that is, a judicial state of mind that prevents the judge from fairly and impartially deciding the case. The standard does not require proof that the judge has acted with subjective malice; rather, it requires "specific facts demonstrating a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible." *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

48. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides an alternative, objective standard drawn from *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009): disqualification is warranted where, based on "objective and reasonable perceptions," there is "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party." This standard is met where the probability of actual bias is "too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Caperton*, 556 US at 877.

### B. Michigan Supreme Court and Court of Appeals Precedent

49. **Crampton v. Dep't of State, 395 Mich 347 (1975):** The Michigan Supreme Court held that due process requires a fair and impartial tribunal. Where a judge's conduct demonstrates bias or prejudgment, the constitutional guarantee of due process mandates disqualification regardless of whether the judge believes he or she can be fair. The right to an impartial tribunal is fundamental and non-waivable, and the appearance of impartiality is as constitutionally significant as actual impartiality.

50. **Cain v. Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich 470 (1996):** The Supreme Court established the controlling standard for judicial disqualification based on bias. The Court held:

   > "A judge is disqualified when, objectively speaking, the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decision-maker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable."

   The *Cain* Court further instructed that disqualification must be evaluated by examining the **totality of the circumstances**, not isolated incidents. Where a pattern of conduct — including hostile demeanor, one-sided rulings, procedural irregularities, and failure to follow mandatory legal standards — cumulatively demonstrates bias, disqualification is required. The Court emphasized that the standard protects not only against actual prejudice but against the "probability" of prejudice as assessed by an objective observer.

51. **In re MKK, 286 Mich App 546 (2009):** The Court of Appeals held that a trial court's failure to make required findings under the Child Custody Act, combined with conduct suggesting prejudgment, may warrant disqualification. In *In re MKK*, the Court noted that where a trial judge has demonstrated "a pattern of making findings that are not supported by the evidence and failing to apply the correct legal standard," the cumulative effect may demonstrate the kind of bias that requires disqualification.

   The *In re MKK* holding is directly applicable here. Judge McNeill has repeatedly failed to make the required BIF findings under MCL 722.23, has issued custody and parenting time orders without proper evidentiary foundation, and has demonstrated a pattern of conduct reflecting prejudgment of Plaintiff/Father's positions. This is precisely the scenario contemplated by the Court of Appeals in *In re MKK*.

52. **Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp, 248 Mich App 573 (2001):** The Court of Appeals recognized that a **pattern of rulings** — even if each individual ruling might be defensible in isolation — may cumulatively demonstrate bias sufficient to warrant disqualification. The Court stated:

   > "While individual rulings, standing alone, may not be sufficient to demonstrate bias, a consistent pattern of rulings that favor one party over another, particularly where those rulings are accompanied by hostile conduct, procedural irregularities, and failure to apply the correct legal standard, may give rise to a reasonable inference of bias."

   This principle is critical here. No single order by Judge McNeill may, in isolation, constitute proof of bias. But the **totality** — the unbroken pattern of adverse rulings against Plaintiff/Father, the hostile demeanor, the ex parte indicators, the failure to apply the BIF, the progressive denial of parenting time, and the systematic favoritism toward Watson/Rusco — demonstrates bias as clearly as any single egregious act could.

53. **Gates v. Gates, 256 Mich App 420 (2003):** The Court of Appeals addressed disqualification in the context of a custody dispute and confirmed that a trial judge's failure to conduct a proper analysis of the Best Interest Factors, combined with conduct suggesting that the judge had prejudged the custody determination, may constitute grounds for disqualification. The Court emphasized that the BIF analysis is not a discretionary exercise but a mandatory statutory requirement, and that failure to conduct it properly may reflect "more than mere legal error" — it may reflect "a predisposition against the party whose interests are disserved by the failure."

### C. MCR 2.003(D): Procedural Requirements

54. MCR 2.003(D) sets forth the procedural requirements for a motion to disqualify:

   a. **MCR 2.003(D)(1):** A motion to disqualify must be filed in writing and must set forth "with particularity the grounds for disqualification" and the "facts and reasons supporting the grounds."

   b. **MCR 2.003(D)(1)(a):** The motion must be verified by oath or affirmation, or by a declaration pursuant to MCR 1.109(D)(3).

   c. **MCR 2.003(D)(3):** The motion is to be decided by the challenged judge. If the judge does not disqualify himself or herself, the moving party may file a motion with the chief judge of the court (or, if the challenged judge is the chief judge, with the State Court Administrator) for a hearing and determination by another judge.

   d. **MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a):** If the challenged judge denies the motion, the moving party may request a de novo review by another judge within 21 days.

55. This Motion complies with MCR 2.003(D)(1) by setting forth, with the requisite particularity, the grounds for disqualification and the specific facts and reasons supporting those grounds. The Verification attached hereto satisfies the requirements of MCR 2.003(D)(1)(a).

### D. Constitutional Dimensions

56. The right to a fair and impartial tribunal is guaranteed by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963. *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347 (1975); *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976).

57. Where parental rights are at stake, the constitutional protections are heightened. The Supreme Court of the United States has recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). A custody proceeding conducted by a biased tribunal is not merely procedurally defective — it is a **constitutional violation** of the most fundamental order.

58. The Fourteenth Amendment's procedural due process guarantee requires, at minimum: (1) adequate notice; (2) an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner; and (3) a neutral and detached decision-maker. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976).
