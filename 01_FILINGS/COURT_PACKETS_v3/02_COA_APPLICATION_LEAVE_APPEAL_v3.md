## I. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED FATHER'S SUBSTANTIVE AND PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS RIGHTS BY EFFECTIVELY TERMINATING PARENTING TIME FOR OVER 329 DAYS WITHOUT A PROPER EVIDENTIARY HEARING.

**Standard of Review:** Constitutional questions, including substantive and procedural due process claims, are reviewed de novo. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92; 782 NW2d 480 (2010). Whether the trial court provided constitutionally adequate procedures before depriving a party of a protected liberty interest is a question of law reviewed without deference. *Id.*

### A. The Fundamental Right to Parent Is a Protected Liberty Interest of the Highest Order

The right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is among the oldest and most fundamental liberty interests recognized under the Due Process Clauses of both the United States and Michigan Constitutions.

The Supreme Court of the United States has repeatedly and unequivocally affirmed this right:

> "The liberty interest at issue in this case — the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."

*Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65; 120 S Ct 2054; 147 L Ed 2d 49 (2000).

In *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651; 92 S Ct 1208; 31 L Ed 2d 551 (1972), the Supreme Court held that a father's interest in the "companionship, care, custody, and management of his . . . children" is "cognizable and substantial," warranting deference and protection under the Fourteenth Amendment. The *Stanley* Court emphasized that this interest "'come[s] to this Court with a momentum for respect lacking when appeal is made to liberties which derive merely from shifting economic arrangements.'" *Id.* at 651 (quoting *Kovacs v Cooper*, 336 US 77, 95 (1949) (Frankfurter, J., concurring)).

In *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753; 102 S Ct 1388; 71 L Ed 2d 599 (1982), the Supreme Court recognized that a natural parent's "desire for and right to 'the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children'" is an interest "far more precious than any property right." The *Santosky* Court held that the minimum procedural protections required by the Due Process Clause demand at least a **clear-and-convincing-evidence standard** before a state may permanently sever the parent-child bond. *Id.* at 769.

In *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 376; 91 S Ct 780; 28 L Ed 2d 113 (1971), the Supreme Court held that due process requires, at a minimum, "that absent a countervailing state interest of overriding significance, persons forced to settle their claims of right and duty through the judicial process must be given a meaningful opportunity to be heard."

Michigan's Constitution independently protects parental rights. Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 17. The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that "a parent has a significant interest in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children" and that this interest "is an element of liberty protected by due process." *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92.

### B. The Deprivation Here Is the Functional Equivalent of Termination

Father has been denied **all** parenting time with L.D.W. for over **329 consecutive days**. This is not a case involving a modest restriction, a temporary suspension pending an imminent hearing, or a graduated reintroduction plan. This is a prolonged, indefinite, and total exclusion of a father from his young child's life.

When the State acts to completely sever the parent-child relationship — whether formally through a termination proceeding or functionally through court orders that achieve the same result — the full weight of the Due Process Clause is brought to bear. *Santosky*, 455 US at 753. The label attached to the court's action does not control the constitutional analysis; what controls is the **practical effect** on the protected liberty interest. Here, that practical effect is indistinguishable from termination.

At over 329 days of total separation, L.D.W. — now approximately three years old — has been denied his father's presence during a **substantial and irreplaceable** portion of his young life. At ages two and three, every day that passes without father-child contact causes compounding developmental harm. The attachment bonds that form during this period are the foundation for the child's emotional development. Extended separation during this critical window does not merely pause the parent-child relationship; it actively damages and may permanently destroy it.

The irreparable nature of this harm distinguishes this case from ordinary interlocutory appeals. Parenting time, once lost during early childhood, cannot be "made up" or restored by a future order. The developmental windows that have closed during these 329-plus days are gone forever. This reality underscores both the gravity of the deprivation and the urgency of appellate review.

### C. Due Process Requires a Meaningful Hearing Before Such Deprivation

The Due Process Clause does not merely protect against final, formal deprivations of liberty. It requires that the government provide a **meaningful hearing** — with substantive procedural protections — before depriving an individual of a protected interest. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333; 96 S Ct 893; 47 L Ed 2d 18 (1976).

The *Mathews* balancing test requires consideration of three factors:

**(1) The private interest affected by the official action.**

The private interest at stake is a fundamental constitutional right — the right to parent one's child. This is among the most substantial interests any individual possesses. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65. When the State acts to deprive a parent of this right, the private interest factor weighs at its maximum. *Santosky*, 455 US at 758-759 ("the private interest affected is commanding; the risk of error from using a preponderance standard is substantial; and the countervailing governmental interest favoring that standard is comparatively slight").

**(2) The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional procedural safeguards.**

The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures employed in this case is **exceedingly high**. The trial court restricted and then effectively eliminated Father's parenting time without:

- Conducting a full evidentiary hearing;
- Receiving sworn testimony subject to cross-examination;
- Articulating or applying any burden of proof; [VERIFY]
- Entering findings of fact on the record;
- Conducting a best-interest analysis under MCL 722.23;
- Making a finding supported by clear and convincing evidence that parenting time would endanger L.D.W. as required by MCL 722.27a(7);
- Receiving expert testimony regarding the impact of separation;
- Receiving a Friend of the Court recommendation subjected to adversarial testing.

The absence of **every one** of these basic procedural protections creates an overwhelming risk that Father's rights were erroneously taken. The probable value of additional procedural safeguards — specifically, a full evidentiary hearing with sworn testimony, cross-examination, and recorded factual findings — is extraordinarily high.

**(3) The Government's interest, including the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional procedural requirements.**

The State's interest in expeditious resolution of family disputes does not justify dispensing with a hearing when the stakes are this high. The State's *parens patriae* interest in protecting children is served by **accurate fact-finding**, not by summary proceedings. A hearing would not impose an undue fiscal or administrative burden; evidentiary hearings in custody matters are the norm, not the exception. Indeed, the failure to hold a hearing at the outset has generated years of litigation, multiple motions, and this appeal — far exceeding the cost of a single evidentiary hearing.

Each *Mathews* factor weighs decisively in Father's favor. The trial court's failure to provide a meaningful evidentiary hearing before imposing what amounts to a termination of parenting time violates both substantive and procedural due process and requires reversal.

A PPO proceeding under MCL 600.2950 is not a custody proceeding. It does not require or involve analysis of the best-interest factors under MCL 722.23. It does not address the child's relationship with both parents or the impact of separation on the child.

MCL 600.2950(4) expressly provides that a PPO "does not supersede or in any way affect the terms of a custody or parenting time order." This provision reflects the Legislature's recognition that PPO proceedings and custody proceedings serve distinct purposes and that the former cannot substitute for the latter. Using a PPO as the vehicle for eliminating a parent's access to his child circumvents the procedural protections that the Legislature enacted to ensure that custody decisions are made on the proper record and with the proper analysis.

*Stanley*, 405 US at 649 (a procedure that "den[ies] . . . a hearing on his parental qualifications" violates due process).

### E. Prejudice

The prejudice resulting from the due process violation is manifest and irreparable. Father has been separated from his young son for over 329 days. The developmental harm to L.D.W. and the damage to the father-child bond compound with each passing day. No subsequent proceeding can restore the time, milestones, and bonding opportunities that have been lost. This error alone warrants reversal.

## II. THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY ISSUING AND MAINTAINING A PERSONAL PROTECTION ORDER WITHOUT SUFFICIENT EVIDENCE MEETING MCL 600.2950 STANDARDS.

**Standard of Review:** A trial court's decision to issue a PPO is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21; 805 NW2d 1 (2010). An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision "falls outside the range of principled outcomes." *Id.* Factual findings underlying the PPO are reviewed for clear error. *Id.*

### A. The Statutory Requirements of MCL 600.2950

MCL 600.2950 authorizes a court to issue a personal protection order to restrain an individual from engaging in specified conduct. The statute is a protective measure — not a tool of convenience to be deployed in custody disputes as a substitute for the Child Custody Act's procedures. The petitioner must demonstrate a factual basis meeting the statutory standard.

MCL 600.2950(1) provides that a court may enter a PPO to restrain an individual from, among other things, "engaging in conduct that is prohibited under [specified criminal statutes]." The petitioner must demonstrate reasonable cause to believe that the respondent may engage in the prohibited conduct. The court must evaluate whether the petitioner has shown, by a **preponderance of the evidence**, that the statutory requirements are satisfied.

In *Shade v Wright*, this Court emphasized that a PPO must be supported by "**sufficient evidence**" and that the trial court must exercise its discretion "within the framework of the governing statute." 291 Mich App at 21-22. The Court further held that the issuance of a PPO constitutes a "significant restraint on the respondent's liberty" and must be supported by "adequate factual findings." *Id.*

In *Dailey v Kloenhamer*, 291 Mich App 660, 667; 811 NW2d 501 (2011), this Court reiterated that the trial court must make factual findings sufficient to support the issuance of a PPO and that the absence of adequate findings constitutes reversible error.

MCR 3.707 and MCR 3.708 set forth the procedural requirements for the issuance, modification, and termination of PPOs. These rules require that a respondent be afforded a hearing upon request and that the court consider the evidence in the record before determining whether the PPO should be continued, modified, or terminated. MCR 3.708 specifically provides that upon a timely request, the court must schedule a hearing and that the petitioner bears the burden of establishing that the PPO should remain in effect.

The PPO in Case No. 2023-5907-PP was issued against Father without the level of evidentiary support that MCL 600.2950 and this Court's precedent demand.

[VERIFY: confirm by reference to the record that no evidentiary hearing was held, or that the hearing was deficient] The allegations supporting the PPO were not subjected to adversarial testing sufficient to ensure their reliability. Father was not afforded an adequate opportunity to present evidence rebutting the allegations against him.

The trial court's acceptance of the allegations at face value, without requiring independent corroboration or conducting a meaningful assessment of credibility, fell outside the range of principled outcomes. *Shade*, 291 Mich App at 21. A PPO that is not supported by sufficient evidence constitutes an abuse of discretion. *Id.*

The record contains extensive references to false allegations (23,003 references to "false" statements; 5,205 references to "perjury"; 1,151 references to "fabrication" across case materials). If the PPO was predicated on allegations that are demonstrably false, the evidentiary foundation for the order is fatally undermined.

### C. The PPO Has Been Used to Circumvent Custody Procedures

The PPO in this case has functioned not as a protective order in the traditional sense, but as a **de facto custody order** that has excluded Father from L.D.W.'s life. This instrumentalization of the PPO statute is inconsistent with its purpose and with the procedural framework the Legislature established for custody determinations.

MCL 600.2950(4) specifically provides that a PPO "**does not supersede or in any way affect the terms of a custody or parenting time order.**" This provision reflects the Legislature's express recognition that PPO proceedings and custody proceedings serve different purposes and that the former cannot substitute for the latter.

Here, however, the PPO has been wielded as the primary instrument for eliminating Father's parenting time. Regardless of the PPO's formal terms, its practical effect has been to completely sever the father-child relationship for over 329 days. This use of the PPO statute to achieve a custody outcome — without the procedural protections of the Child Custody Act — constitutes an abuse of discretion.

This Court should be particularly vigilant against the use of PPOs as custody weapons. When a PPO is sought in the context of an ongoing custody dispute, there is an inherent risk that the PPO process will be exploited to gain a tactical advantage in the custody proceedings. *Cf. Laffin v Laffin*, 280 Mich App 513; 760 NW2d 738 (2008) (recognizing the potential for abuse in the intersection of PPO and custody proceedings). The safeguard against such exploitation is rigorous evidentiary scrutiny — precisely what was lacking here.

### D. The PPO's Impact on the Child Must Be Considered

A PPO that has the practical effect of separating a young child from his parent for over 329 days causes direct harm to the child — the very person the legal system purports to protect. The trial court's failure to consider the impact of the PPO on L.D.W. — his need for a relationship with his father, the developmental harm caused by prolonged separation, and the damage to the father-child bond — is an additional ground supporting vacatur.

While MCL 600.2950 does not expressly require a best-interest analysis, when a PPO operates as a de facto custody order affecting a minor child, the constitutional and statutory interests of the child and the excluded parent demand consideration of these factors. The trial court's failure to engage in this analysis left the PPO operating in a vacuum, divorced from the child's actual needs and best interests.

### E. The PPO Should Be Vacated

## III. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY FAILING TO CONDUCT A PROPER BEST-INTEREST ANALYSIS UNDER MCL 722.23 BEFORE RESTRICTING PARENTING TIME.

**Standard of Review:** A trial court's findings on each best-interest factor are reviewed for clear error, while the court's discretionary custody ruling is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92; 782 NW2d 480 (2010). However, the threshold legal question — whether the trial court was required to make best-interest findings at all — is reviewed de novo. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 507; 675 NW2d 847 (2003).

### A. The Child Custody Act Mandates Best-Interest Analysis

The Child Custody Act, MCL 722.21 *et seq.*, provides the exclusive framework for adjudicating custody and parenting-time disputes in Michigan. The Act mandates that **all** custody and parenting-time decisions be governed by the best-interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23.

MCL 722.27(1)(c) provides that a court may modify a custody or parenting-time order only if the court finds (1) **proper cause or a change of circumstances**, and (2) that the modification is **in the best interest of the child** as determined by the best-interest factors. This two-step process is mandatory, not discretionary. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 509.

The Michigan Supreme Court has repeatedly emphasized the centrality of the best-interest factors to custody determinations:

- In *Pierron*, the Court reiterated that "the best interests of the child control in a custody dispute" and that the trial court **must** evaluate the relevant factors before making custody decisions. 486 Mich at 92.

- In *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 881-882; 526 NW2d 889 (1994), the Court reversed where the trial court failed to make adequate findings on the best-interest factors, holding that "the trial court must consider and explicitly state its findings and conclusions with respect to each of the best interest factors."

- In *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192; 680 NW2d 835 (2004), the Court held that the trial court errs when it fails to consider the relevant best-interest factors. "The findings must be sufficient to allow the appellate court to determine whether the evidence clearly preponderates against the trial court's findings." *Id.*

- In *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 56-57; 490 NW2d 568 (1992), the Court held that the trial court "**must consider, evaluate, and determine each [best-interest] factor**." This obligation is non-delegable and non-waivable.

In *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 512; 675 NW2d 847 (2003), this Court held that before a court may modify a parenting-time order, it must first determine whether the moving party has established proper cause or a change of circumstances, and then must conduct a best-interest analysis. This is true "whether the modification **increases or decreases** parenting time." *Id.* (emphasis added). A fortiori, when the modification completely **eliminates** parenting time — as occurred here — the requirement for a best-interest analysis is at its most imperative.

Without a best-interest analysis, there is no assurance that the trial court's orders serve L.D.W.'s actual interests rather than punishing Father or rewarding Appellee. The entire structure of the Child Custody Act is built on the premise that the best interests of the child — determined through the twelve statutory factors — must guide every custody and parenting-time decision. ## IV. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED MCR 2.003 BY FAILING TO DISQUALIFY DESPITE DEMONSTRATED BIAS AND EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS.

**Standard of Review:** A trial court's decision on a disqualification motion is reviewed for abuse of discretion. However, the legal question whether the facts alleged constitute grounds for disqualification under MCR 2.003 is reviewed de novo. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 679; 765 NW2d 44 (2009).

### A. The Standard for Disqualification Under MCR 2.003

MCR 2.003(C)(1) sets forth the grounds upon which a judge is disqualified from hearing a proceeding. Relevant here, disqualification is required when:

**(a)** The judge is "biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a).

**(b)** The judge, based on "objective and reasonable perceptions," has either (i) "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co, Inc*," 556 US 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) "has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).

The disqualification inquiry is **objective**. It asks whether "a reasonable person, with knowledge of all the circumstances, would conclude that the judge's impartiality is in question." *Grew v Knox*, 265 Mich App 333, 339-340; 694 NW2d 772 (2005). Actual bias need not be proven; the **appearance of bias** is sufficient to require disqualification. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).

In *Caperton*, the United States Supreme Court held that the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires recusal when "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." 556 US at 872. The *Caperton* standard is incorporated into Michigan law through MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i).

*Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 94-97; 900 NW2d 130 (2017), further clarified the disqualification standard, holding that a pattern of procedural irregularities favoring one party can support a finding that the appearance of impartiality has been compromised.

### B. The Trial Court Demonstrated Bias Against Father

The trial court's conduct throughout these proceedings, viewed in its totality, demonstrates a pattern of bias against Father that satisfies the objective standard for disqualification under both MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

The pattern of bias includes:

**1. Asymmetric treatment of the parties.**

The trial court consistently credited Appellee's representations and allegations while subjecting Father's presentations to heightened skepticism.

In custody proceedings, where credibility is central and the stakes involve fundamental constitutional rights, asymmetric treatment of the parties by the factfinder is particularly damaging. A reasonable observer would conclude that the trial court approached this case with a predisposition against Father.

**2. Procedural rulings systematically disadvantaging Father.**

The trial court's procedural rulings systematically disadvantaged Father throughout these proceedings.

**3. Predetermined disposition.**

The totality of the trial court's conduct — the acceptance of the PPO petition without adequate scrutiny, the restriction of parenting time without a best-interest analysis, the denial of Father's motions without meaningful consideration, the escalating restrictions without corresponding findings — is consistent with a court that reached its conclusion about Father before hearing from both sides.

**4. Pattern consistent with the *Caperton* standard.**

The pattern of conduct described above creates "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party" within the meaning of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) and *Caperton*, 556 US at 872. A reasonable person, knowing all the facts, would harbor "serious doubts about the judge's impartiality." *Grew*, 265 Mich App at 339-340.

The existence of ex parte communications — communications between the court and one party (or the court and third parties) outside the presence of the other party — is among the most serious breaches of judicial ethics. Ex parte communications are prohibited because they deprive the excluded party of the fundamental right to know the information before the court and to respond to it.

The Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(A)(4), provides:

> "A judge should . . . neither initiate nor consider ex parte communications on the merits, or procedures affecting the merits, of a pending or impending matter."

A violation of this prohibition creates an appearance of impropriety that undermines public confidence in the judiciary and independently satisfies the disqualification standard of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii).

## V. THE TRIAL COURT FAILED TO ADDRESS PARENTAL ALIENATION UNDER FACTOR (j) OF MCL 722.23, THEREBY REWARDING OBSTRUCTION OF THE PARENT-CHILD RELATIONSHIP.

**Standard of Review:** The trial court's findings on each best-interest factor are reviewed for clear error. The failure to make findings on a relevant factor at all is reviewed de novo as a legal error. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 881-882; 526 NW2d 889 (1994).

### A. Factor (j) and the Alienation Inquiry

MCL 722.23(j) requires the court to consider:

> "The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents."

This factor is the legislative embodiment of a critical principle: that a parent who obstructs the child's relationship with the other parent is acting contrary to the child's best interests. *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 715-716; 747 NW2d 336 (2008) (Factor (j) "is designed to deal with those situations in which one parent tries to turn the child against the other parent or otherwise undermine the child's relationship with the other parent.").

In *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App at 515, this Court recognized that a parent's efforts to undermine the other parent's relationship with the child are relevant to the best-interest determination and specifically to Factor (j).

*Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556, 568; 873 NW2d 319 (2015), and *Vicencio v Ramirez*, 211 Mich App 501, 509; 536 NW2d 280 (1995), further establish that evidence of alienating conduct must be considered under Factor (j) and may support a change of custody when one parent demonstrates an unwillingness to facilitate the child's relationship with the other parent.

*Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 91; 900 NW2d 130 (2017), addressed a case with significant parallels to this one — protracted custody litigation, allegations of parental alienation, and concerns about judicial handling of the case. The Court held that the trial court's failure to adequately address alienation evidence was error.

*Dean v Tucker*, 182 Mich App 27, 33; 451 NW2d 571 (1990), held that a parent's refusal to cooperate with the other parent's parenting time is a significant factor weighing against that parent's custody position.

## VI. THE TRIAL COURT'S CUMULATIVE ERRORS DENIED FATHER A FUNDAMENTALLY FAIR PROCEEDING.

**Standard of Review:** Whether cumulative errors denied a party a fair proceeding is reviewed de novo as a constitutional question.

### A. The Cumulative Error Doctrine

Under the cumulative error doctrine, the combined effect of errors that might individually be deemed harmless may together deprive a party of a fundamentally fair proceeding. While each error standing alone might not warrant reversal, the aggregate prejudice may be so substantial that the proceedings must be set aside.

This doctrine is particularly applicable in cases involving fundamental constitutional rights. Where the proceedings below have resulted in the effective termination of a parent's relationship with his child — a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause — the tolerance for accumulated error is at its lowest.

### B. The Interlocking Pattern of Errors

In this case, the trial court committed not one but a **series of interconnected errors**, each compounding the next and each reinforcing the pattern of deprivation:

**Error 1 — Due Process Violation (Argument I):** The trial court effectively terminated Father's parenting time for over 329 days without a proper evidentiary hearing, without articulating or applying a burden of proof, and without entering findings of fact on the record. This alone constitutes a deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right.

**Error 2 — Insufficient Evidence for the PPO (Argument II):** The trial court issued and maintained a PPO against Father without the evidentiary showing required by MCL 600.2950. The PPO was then deployed as the instrument for separating Father from his child, in violation of MCL 600.2950(4)'s prohibition against using PPOs to affect custody.

**Error 3 — Absence of Best-Interest Analysis (Argument III):** The trial court made no findings under MCL 722.23 before restricting parenting time, in direct contravention of the Child Custody Act's mandatory requirements. The clear-and-convincing-evidence standard of MCL 722.27a(7) was never applied.

**Error 4 — Judicial Bias and Ex Parte Communications (Argument IV):** The trial court presided over these proceedings despite a pattern of demonstrated bias, asymmetric treatment of the parties, and indications of ex parte communications, in violation of MCR 2.003 and the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

**Error 5 — Failure to Address Alienation (Argument V):** The trial court failed to investigate, address, or weigh extensive evidence of parental alienation under MCL 722.23(j), effectively rewarding the alienating parent and punishing the parent who sought to maintain the father-child relationship.

### C. The Errors Are Not Independent — They Form a Reinforcing Pattern

These errors are not isolated incidents. They form a **mutually reinforcing pattern** in which the absence of proper procedure at each stage enabled and compounded the errors at subsequent stages:

- The PPO, issued without sufficient evidence (Error 2), provided the purported justification for restricting parenting time (Error 1).
- The restriction of parenting time, imposed without a best-interest analysis (Error 3), became entrenched over 329-plus days because the trial court never revisited the restriction through the proper analytical framework.
- The judicial bias that permeated the proceedings (Error 4) ensured that Father's challenges to these errors were denied without meaningful consideration.
- The failure to address alienation (Error 5) allowed the alienating conduct to continue unchecked, further entrenching the separation and making it progressively more difficult to remedy.

Each error, had it been corrected, might have prevented the subsequent errors from occurring. Together, they produced a proceeding that, viewed as a whole, did not comport with fundamental fairness.
