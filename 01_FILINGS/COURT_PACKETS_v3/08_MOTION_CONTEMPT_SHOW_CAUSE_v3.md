## IV. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. MCR 3.606 — Contempt in Domestic Relations Cases

42. **MCR 3.606** governs contempt proceedings in domestic relations cases. The rule provides:

> **(A)** For purposes of this rule, "contempt" consists of any act or omission that is a failure to obey a court order entered in a domestic relations case.

> **(B)** A person may be found guilty of contempt if:
> (1) there is a court order;
> (2) the person knew of the order;
> (3) the person violated the order; and
> (4) the violation was willful.

MCR 3.606(A)-(B) [VERIFY — exact text of current rule].

43. Under MCR 3.606, the moving party bears the initial burden of establishing by a **preponderance of the evidence** that a valid court order existed, the respondent had knowledge of the order, and the respondent failed to comply with the order. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 93; 413 NW2d 392 (1987) [VERIFY — correct citation and holding].

44. Once the moving party establishes these elements, the burden shifts to the respondent to demonstrate that noncompliance was not willful — i.e., that the respondent was unable to comply despite making reasonable, good-faith efforts to do so. *See In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 668; 765 NW2d 44 (2009) [VERIFY — correct citation].

### B. MCL 600.1701 — Contempt Power

45. **MCL 600.1701** sets forth the statutory authority of Michigan courts to punish contempt. The statute provides, in relevant part:

> The supreme court, circuit court, and all other courts of record, have power to punish by fine or imprisonment, or both, persons guilty of any neglect of, or resistance to, any process, rule, or order of the court, or any misbehavior in the presence of the court, or so near thereto as to directly tend to interrupt its proceedings, or any other contempt of the authority of the court.

MCL 600.1701(a)-(g) [VERIFY — exact text of relevant subsections].

46. The purpose of civil contempt is not punitive but **coercive** — it is designed to compel compliance with a court order and to vindicate the authority of the court. *See In re Contempt of Robertson*, 209 Mich App 433, 436; 531 NW2d 763 (1995) [VERIFY].

47. In a civil contempt proceeding, the contemnor holds the "keys to the jail" — she can purge herself of contempt by complying with the court's order. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 93 [VERIFY].

### C. MCL 722.27a(7) — Parenting Time Enforcement

48. **MCL 722.27a(7)** specifically addresses the enforcement of parenting time orders and provides a range of remedies:

> If a child's parent, without good cause, fails to comply with a parenting time order, the court shall order the parent to make up the parenting time. In addition, the court may:
>
> (a) Modify the parenting time order;
>
> (b) Order the parent to pay reasonable costs and attorney fees incurred by the prevailing party;
>
> (c) Find the parent in contempt under section 1701 of the revised judicature act;
>
> (d) Order the parent to perform community service;
>
> (e) Take any other action that the court considers necessary or appropriate;
>
> (f) Modify the custody order.

MCL 722.27a(7)(a)-(f) [VERIFY — exact text of current statute].

49. Notably, MCL 722.27a(7) creates a **presumption** that parenting time violations shall result in makeup parenting time at a minimum. The statute uses the mandatory "shall" in requiring makeup time, while the additional sanctions listed in subsections (a) through (f) are discretionary. This underscores the Legislature's intent that parenting time orders be strictly enforced.

### D. Elements of Contempt — Clear Order, Knowledge, Willful Violation

50. To establish contempt of court in a domestic relations case, the moving party must prove each of the following elements:

#### Element 1: A Clear and Unambiguous Court Order Existed

51. There must be a court order that is clear, specific, and unambiguous in its terms. The order must be sufficiently definite that the person to whom it is directed can ascertain what conduct is required or prohibited. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 92 [VERIFY].

52. Here, this Court's parenting time orders specify the dates, times, and conditions of Father's parenting time with L.D.W. [VERIFY — describe the specificity of the orders]. The orders are clear and leave no room for reasonable disagreement about what is required of Mother.

#### Element 2: The Respondent Had Knowledge of the Order

53. The respondent must have had actual knowledge of the court order. Knowledge may be established by proof of personal service, presence in court when the order was entered, or other circumstances demonstrating actual awareness. *See Dunn v. Detroit Inter-Insurance Exchange*, [VERIFY — citation]; *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App at 668 [VERIFY].

54. Mother had actual knowledge of all parenting time orders at issue, as established by the facts set forth in Section III.B above.

#### Element 3: The Respondent Violated the Order

55. The moving party must demonstrate that the respondent failed to comply with the specific terms of the court order. This is an objective determination — either the respondent complied or she did not.

56. The specific instances of noncompliance are documented in detail in Section III.C through III.F above. The evidence demonstrates **at least seventeen (17) specific instances** of denied, interfered-with, or obstructed parenting time [VERIFY — actual count], in addition to the ongoing pattern of alienation and interference.

#### Element 4: The Violation Was Willful

57. The violation must be willful — that is, the respondent must have had the ability to comply but chose not to do so. *See In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App at 668 [VERIFY]. Willfulness may be inferred from the totality of the circumstances, including the pattern and frequency of violations, the absence of legitimate justification, and the respondent's failure to take reasonable steps to ensure compliance.
