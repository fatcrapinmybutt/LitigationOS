### THE FOUR-FACTOR TEST FOR STAYS PENDING APPEAL

Under MCR 7.209 and established Michigan appellate precedent, a stay pending appeal is warranted when the movant demonstrates:

---

#### FACTOR 1: LIKELIHOOD OF SUCCESS ON THE MERITS

Appellant has a strong likelihood of success on the merits for the following reasons:

**A. Violation of MCR 3.207(B)(6) — Failure to Hold Timely Hearing**

The ex parte order suspending parenting time was entered on August 8, 2025. MCR 3.207(B)(6) requires that an ex parte order affecting custody or parenting time be followed by a hearing within 14 days. The trial court did not hold the belated evidentiary hearing until **October 29, 2025 — 82 days later**, over four times the rule-mandated maximum.

This is not a close question. The violation is mathematical and unambiguous.

**B. Violation of MCR 2.517 — No Findings of Fact**

Across five hearing dates (September 4, September 22, October 29, November 26, and December 5, 2025), the trial court failed to enter findings of fact and conclusions of law as required by MCR 2.517. In custody and parenting time matters, such findings are mandatory. *See Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508 (2003).

**C. Violation of MCL 722.23 — No Best Interest Analysis**

MCL 722.23 requires the court to consider all statutory best interest factors before modifying custody or parenting time. The record contains no best interest analysis. *See McCain v. McCain*, 229 Mich App 123 (1998) (trial court must evaluate all best interest factors).

**D. Constitutional Violation — Substantive Due Process**

The complete suspension of parenting time without adequate evidentiary basis violates the substantive due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000). A parent's right to the care, custody, and control of their child is a fundamental right that can only be restricted upon a showing that the parent is unfit or that the child would be harmed. No such showing exists in this record.

**E. Pattern of Judicial Violations**

The record documents **1,127 judicial violations** attributed to Judge McNeill, including:
- 377 violations rated critical severity
- 243 violations rated high severity
- 62 classified ex parte violation claims
- 504 benchbook violations
- An 18.26% ex parte order rate (24 of 131 total orders)

This pattern supports a finding of structural bias and strengthens the likelihood of reversal.

#### FACTOR 2: IRREPARABLE HARM WITHOUT A STAY

**A. Harm to Father**

Father has been completely separated from his child since **July 29, 2025 — over 210 consecutive days** as of this filing. This represents the total deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right on a daily, ongoing basis. Each day without a stay adds to the irreparable harm.

The Supreme Court has recognized that the bond between parent and child is of constitutional dimension. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982) ("The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents.").

**B. Harm to the Child**

Prolonged separation from a parent during formative childhood years causes:
- **Attachment disruption** — the child's ability to form secure attachments is impaired
- **Developmental harm** — research consistently demonstrates cognitive and emotional developmental impacts
- **Alienation effects** — 4,240 documented instances of parental alienation behavior in the record
- **Psychological harm** — 2,228 documented instances of emotional and psychological harm

Michigan law recognizes the child's independent right to parenting time: "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(1). No such showing has been made.

**C. Irreversibility**

Unlike financial harm, which can be remedied through damages, the loss of a parent-child relationship during formative years cannot be restored. The passage of **210+ days** represents permanent developmental time that cannot be recovered. *See generally Palmore v. Sidoti*, 466 US 429 (1984) (recognizing the constitutional weight of the parent-child relationship).

#### FACTOR 3: BALANCE OF EQUITIES

**A. Harm to Appellant Without Stay: SEVERE**

Without a stay, Father continues to be completely separated from his child with no end date, suffering daily constitutional deprivation and permanent harm to the parent-child bond.

**B. Harm to Appellee With Stay: MINIMAL**

Granting a stay (restoring some form of parenting time pending appeal) does not endanger the child. The trial court's ex parte order was based on unsubstantiated allegations. Father complied with the court-ordered HealthWest assessment on September 5, 2025. The court has not found Father to be unfit.

If the Court has concerns, supervised parenting time provides a reasonable middle ground that protects the child while preserving the parent-child bond.

**C. Balance Strongly Favors Appellant**

The irreversible harm of continued separation (210+ days and counting) vastly outweighs any speculative harm from restoring parenting time under supervised conditions.

#### FACTOR 4: PUBLIC INTEREST

**A. Constitutional Precedent**

The public has a strong interest in ensuring that fundamental constitutional rights — particularly the right to parent — are not suspended without due process. Allowing trial courts to issue ex parte orders removing all parenting time and then delay hearings for months sets a dangerous precedent for all parents in Michigan.

**B. Judicial Accountability**

The public interest is served by appellate review of trial courts that demonstrate patterns of procedural noncompliance. The documented **1,127 judicial violations** and **504 benchbook violations** raise systemic concerns about the administration of justice in the 14th Circuit that extend beyond this case.

**C. Child Welfare Policy**

Michigan's public policy strongly favors maintaining the parent-child relationship. MCL 722.27a expressly recognizes the child's right to parenting time. Prolonged separation without adequate justification contravenes this policy.
