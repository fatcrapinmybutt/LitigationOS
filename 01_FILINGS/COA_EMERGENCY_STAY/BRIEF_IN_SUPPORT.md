## LEGAL ANALYSIS

### I. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED FATHER'S SUBSTANTIVE DUE PROCESS RIGHTS

#### Issue

Whether the trial court's ex parte suspension of all parenting time, without an evidentiary hearing or findings, violates Father's substantive due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment.

#### Rule

The Fourteenth Amendment provides that no state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1.

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that parents have a fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and control of their children:

> "The liberty interest at issue in this case — the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

> "The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents." *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982).

> "[T]he interest of a parent in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children comes to this Court with a momentum for respect..." *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972).

When a fundamental right is at stake, substantive due process requires that any government restriction be narrowly tailored and supported by adequate evidentiary basis. *See Lassiter v. Dep't of Social Services*, 452 US 18 (1981).

#### Application

Here, the trial court completely extinguished Father's parenting time — the most extreme restriction possible short of termination — via an ex parte order. The order:

1. Was issued **without notice** to Father;
2. Was based on **unsubstantiated allegations** of drug use;
3. Was entered **without an evidentiary hearing**;
4. Was continued for **over 210 days** without findings of fact;
5. Was sustained on November 26, 2025 **without a best interest analysis**; and
6. Imposed a **total deprivation** — not a modification or restriction, but a complete suspension — of parenting time.

Michigan law is clear: "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(1). The trial court made no such showing.

Moreover, Father **complied** with every court directive, including completing the HealthWest assessment on September 5, 2025. Despite compliance, the court continued the suspension.

#### Conclusion

The trial court's complete suspension of parenting time without adequate evidentiary basis, notice, hearing, or findings violates Father's substantive due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment. The deprivation of this fundamental right for 210+ days without justification is per se unreasonable.

### II. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED MCR 3.207(B)(6) TIMING REQUIREMENTS

#### Issue

Whether the trial court's 82-day delay in holding a hearing on the ex parte order violates MCR 3.207(B)(6).

#### Rule

MCR 3.207(B)(6) provides that when an ex parte order is entered affecting custody or parenting time, "the court shall schedule a hearing... as soon as possible." Michigan appellate practice interprets this to require a hearing within **14 days** — and in no event later than **21 days** — of the ex parte order. *See* discussion in appellate treatises and docket events referencing "the 21-day hearing expectation reflected in discussions of MCR 3.207(B)(6) in Michigan appellate practice."

This requirement serves a critical constitutional function: ensuring that the deprivation of a fundamental right is not extended beyond the minimum time necessary to address the emergency that justified the ex parte order.

#### Application

The ex parte order was entered on **August 8, 2025**. Under MCR 3.207(B)(6):

- A hearing should have occurred by **August 22, 2025** (14 days).
- At the latest, a hearing should have occurred by **August 29, 2025** (21 days).

Instead:

| Date | Days Since Order | What Happened |
|------|-----------------|---------------|
| Aug 8, 2025 | 0 | Ex parte order entered |
| Sep 4, 2025 | 27 | First appearance — adjourned, no hearing |
| Sep 22, 2025 | 45 | Adjourned again |
| Oct 29, 2025 | **82** | First evidentiary hearing |
| Nov 26, 2025 | 110 | Objection denied |

The **82-day delay** is over **four times** the maximum permitted period. During this entire period, Father's fundamental right to parent was suspended without any evidentiary test or judicial finding.

The docket record itself confirms awareness of the violation:

> "[T]he ex parte suspension and far beyond the 21-day hearing expectation reflected in discussions of MCR 3.207(B)(6) in Michigan appellate practice."

#### Conclusion

The trial court violated MCR 3.207(B)(6) by failing to hold a hearing within the mandatory timeframe. This violation is not harmless — it extended the deprivation of Father's fundamental constitutional right by months.

### III. THE TRIAL COURT FAILED TO ENTER REQUIRED FINDINGS OF FACT

#### Issue

Whether the trial court's failure to enter findings of fact and conclusions of law constitutes reversible error under MCR 2.517.

#### Rule

MCR 2.517(A)(1) requires that "[i]n actions tried without a jury, the court shall find the facts specially, state separately its conclusions of law, and direct entry of the appropriate judgment." In custody and parenting time cases, this requirement is particularly critical because appellate review depends on the trial court's articulation of its reasoning.

*Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508 (2003), establishes that the trial court must make specific factual findings in custody matters. Without such findings, "meaningful appellate review is impossible."

#### Application

Across **five** hearing dates — September 4, September 22, October 29, November 26, and December 5, 2025 — the trial court:

- Entered **no written findings of fact** under MCR 2.517;
- Made **no findings regarding the statutory best interest factors** under MCL 722.23;
- Provided **no articulation** of the evidentiary basis for continuing the suspension;
- Issued **no written explanation** for denying Father's objection on November 26, 2025.

The November 26 order simply denied Father's objection and continued the suspension. The docket entry states: "Court denied Father's objection to ex parte order. Parenting time remains suspended." — with no supporting analysis.

#### Conclusion

The trial court's failure to enter findings of fact as required by MCR 2.517 constitutes reversible error. This failure makes meaningful appellate review impossible and independently warrants reversal.

### IV. THE TRIAL COURT FAILED TO APPLY BEST INTEREST FACTORS

#### Issue

Whether the trial court abused its discretion by failing to consider the MCL 722.23 best interest factors before continuing the parenting time suspension.

#### Rule

MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve factors that a court "shall consider" in custody and parenting time determinations, including:

- (a) The love, affection, and emotional ties between the parties and the child;
- (b) The capacity of the parties to give the child love, affection, and guidance;
- (c) The capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care;
- (d) The length of time in a stable, satisfactory environment;
- (f) The moral fitness of the parties;
- (j) The willingness of each party to facilitate a close relationship between the child and the other parent.

*Fletcher v. Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879 (1994), holds that the trial court "must consider" all statutory factors. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 508-09, requires specific findings on each factor.

#### Application

The record contains **no analysis** of any best interest factor. The trial court:

1. Did not evaluate Factor (a) — the emotional ties between Father and child, which were being severed by the 210+ day separation;
2. Did not evaluate Factor (d) — the disruption to the child's environment caused by the complete removal of one parent;
3. Did not evaluate Factor (j) — Mother's willingness (or unwillingness) to facilitate Father's relationship with the child, despite 4,240 documented instances of PPO weaponization;
4. Did not make **any** factual findings that parenting time would "endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health" as required by MCL 722.27a(1).

The claims database contains **15 parenting time evidence claims** and **19 mental health assessment claims**, none of which were addressed in the court's orders.

#### Conclusion

The trial court's failure to apply the MCL 722.23 best interest factors constitutes an abuse of discretion requiring reversal.

On remand, a Court of Appeals may order reassignment to a different judge "where it would be inappropriate for the same judge to conduct further proceedings." This power is well-established in Michigan appellate practice.

**3. Pattern vs. Isolated Error**

This is not a case of one or two erroneous rulings. The **1,127 documented violations across multiple categories** establish a pattern of systematic noncompliance with procedural rules and constitutional requirements that goes to the fundamental fairness of the proceedings.

**4. Impact on This Case**

### VI. CONSTITUTIONAL VIOLATIONS — DUE PROCESS AND EQUAL PROTECTION

#### Issue

Whether the cumulative procedural deprivations violate the Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment and Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 17.

#### Rule — Procedural Due Process

Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), procedural due process requires consideration of: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through existing procedures; and (3) the government's interest.

The Michigan Constitution provides parallel protection: "No person shall... be deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law." Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 17.

#### Application — Procedural Due Process

Applying the *Mathews* factors:

1. **Private interest:** The parental right is a fundamental liberty interest of the highest order. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65.

2. **Risk of erroneous deprivation:** The procedures employed here — ex parte order without notice, 82-day delay to hearing, no findings of fact, no best interest analysis — create an extreme risk of erroneous deprivation. The 653 classified claims in the record (including 62 ex parte violation claims and 150 judicial misconduct claims) confirm this risk materialized.

3. **Government interest:** The government's interest in protecting children, while compelling, does not justify the complete absence of procedural safeguards. "The State's interest in finding the best home for [a] child... does not authorize the State to separate [the child] from that home without the due process of law." *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 379 (1971).

#### Rule — Equal Protection

The Equal Protection Clause requires that similarly situated persons be treated alike. When a classification affects a fundamental right, strict scrutiny applies.

#### Application — Equal Protection

Father, as a pro se litigant, was denied procedural protections routinely afforded to other parties:
- Ex parte orders entered without the heightened scrutiny applicable when fundamental rights are at stake;
- Hearings delayed beyond MCR-mandated timelines;
- Findings of fact not entered despite multiple hearing dates.

The record of 990 judicial bias indicators and 934 attorney misconduct instances suggests a systemic disparity in treatment.

#### Conclusion

The cumulative procedural deprivations — no notice, no timely hearing, no findings, no best interest analysis, 210+ days of separation — violate both the Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses. These violations are not isolated errors but constitute a structural denial of constitutional rights.
