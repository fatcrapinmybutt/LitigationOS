### IV. ARGUMENT FOR WHY LEAVE SHOULD BE GRANTED

#### A. The Trial Court's Orders Involve a Significant Constitutional Question

The suspension of all parenting time — a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment — without evidentiary hearing, required findings, or adequate procedural safeguards presents a significant constitutional question warranting this Court's review. *See Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000) ("The liberty interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court.").

Michigan law provides that a child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record that parenting time would endanger the child's health. MCL 722.27a(1). The trial court failed to make any such showing on the record.

#### B. The Trial Court Committed Clear Legal Error

The trial court violated mandatory procedural requirements:

1. **MCR 3.207(B)(6)** requires a hearing on an ex parte custody order within 14 days (or 21 days by local practice). Here, the first hearing addressing Father's objection occurred **82 days** after the ex parte order — over four times the maximum period.

2. **MCR 2.517** requires findings of fact and conclusions of law in custody decisions. The court entered no findings of fact supporting the continued suspension across five separate hearing dates.

3. **MCL 722.23** requires evaluation of all best interest factors before modifying custody or parenting time. No best interest analysis appears in the record.

#### C. Immediate Review Is Necessary to Prevent Irreparable Harm

Each day of continued separation causes irreversible damage to the parent-child bond. Child development research establishes that prolonged separation during formative years causes lasting developmental, emotional, and psychological harm. *See* MCL 722.27a(1) (recognizing child's right to parenting time); *In re Dettmer*, 260 Mich App 356 (2004).

The 210+ day separation — with no end date set — constitutes ongoing irreparable harm that cannot be remedied after appellate review of a final order.

#### D. The Case Involves Issues of Significant Public Interest

The systematic pattern of judicial violations documented in this case — including ex parte orders comprising 18.26% of all court orders (24 of 131 total orders) — raises issues of structural due process that extend beyond this individual case to the administration of justice in the 14th Circuit.
