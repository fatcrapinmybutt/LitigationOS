## III. ARGUMENT

### A. The Domestic Relations Exception Does Not Bar This Action

This Court may anticipate that Defendants will invoke the domestic relations exception to federal jurisdiction. That argument fails.

The Sixth Circuit has squarely held that "the domestic relations exception to federal jurisdiction does not apply to § 1983 actions." *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279, 291 (6th Cir. 1998). The Court explained: "We reject defendants' arguments that the underlying nature of the case constitutes a domestic relations dispute barring the exercise of jurisdiction by a federal district court." *Id.*

Accord *Pittman v. Cuyahoga Cnty. Dep't of Children & Family Servs.*, 640 F.3d 716, 724 (6th Cir. 2011) (holding that federal courts have jurisdiction over § 1983 claims arising from state custody proceedings).

Plaintiff does not seek a custody determination, divorce decree, or alimony award. Plaintiff seeks money damages and equitable relief for violations of his federal constitutional rights — precisely the type of claim that federal courts are designed to adjudicate.

The Supreme Court has emphasized that the domestic relations exception is "a narrow exception" and does not deprive "the federal courts of the power to hear cases arising from the Constitution." *Ankenbrandt v. Richards*, 504 U.S. 689, 700-01 (1992).

### B. Count I: The Conspiracy Claim Is Well-Pleaded Under *Dennis v. Sparks*

In *Dennis v. Sparks*, the Supreme Court held that "private persons, jointly engaged with state officials in [a challenged] action, are acting 'under color' of law for purposes of § 1983 actions." 449 U.S. 24, 27-28 (1980). The Court specifically addressed the scenario presented here — a conspiracy between a judge and private litigants to corrupt judicial proceedings:

> "[A] private party involved in such a conspiracy [with a judge] may be held liable under § 1983 . . . . The immunity of the judge does not extend to those who merely combated with him to deny petitioners' rights."

*Id.* at 28-29.

The *Dennis* framework is directly applicable. Plaintiff alleges:

1. **State actor involvement**: Judge McNeill, acting under color of state law as a sitting judge, concealed a disqualifying conflict and issued biased rulings.

2. **Agreement**: Upon information and belief, Watson and Berry entered into an understanding with Judge McNeill (through the Cavan Berry connection) to exploit the concealed judicial conflict.

3. **Constitutional deprivation**: Plaintiff was deprived of due process and parental rights.

Under *Tower v. Glover*, 467 U.S. 914, 920 (1984), conspiring with a state actor to deprive a person of constitutional rights places private persons "under color of state law." Under the joint action test of *Adickes v. S.H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144, 152 (1970), willful participation in joint activity with the State or its agents is sufficient state action.

The conspiracy need not be expressed in formal terms. Circumstantial evidence is sufficient. *Hooks v. Hooks*, 771 F.2d 935, 943-44 (6th Cir. 1985). Here, the circumstantial evidence is compelling: a judge conceals a familial connection to a party through the party's romantic partner, and the rulings during the concealment period systematically favor that party.

### C. Count II: Structural Bias Violated Plaintiff's Due Process Rights

The Due Process Clause requires "a fair trial in a fair tribunal" and "an absence of actual bias." *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 876 (2009) (quoting *In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955)).

The standard is objective: "whether, 'under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness,' the interest 'poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden if the guarantee of due process is to be adequately implemented.'" *Williams v. Pennsylvania*, 579 U.S. 1, 8 (2016) (quoting *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 883-84).

The Court need not find actual bias. The question is whether the circumstances create an unconstitutional "probability of bias." *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 881.

Here, the structural bias is overwhelming:

- Judge McNeill's husband is, upon information and belief, a relative of Defendant Watson's romantic partner.
- Judge McNeill concealed this connection for over 290 days.
- During this period, rulings consistently favored Watson.
- Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), this relationship mandated disqualification.

This case is analogous to the foundational structural bias cases:

- In *Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510 (1927), the Supreme Court held that a mayor-judge with a financial interest in convictions violated due process. The Court did not require proof of actual bias.

- In *Williams*, 579 U.S. at 9, the Court held that a chief justice who had personally authorized the defendant's prosecution was disqualified under an "objective standard." The risk of bias — not its reality — was determinative.

- In *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 884, the Court held that a justice's failure to recuse despite extraordinary financial support from a litigant violated due process.

The familial connection in this case is at least as disqualifying as the financial connections in *Tumey* and *Caperton*. A judge whose husband is related to the romantic partner of a litigant has a disqualifying interest that "poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment" that due process forbids her participation.

The extrajudicial source doctrine of *Liteky v. United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 554-55 (1994), reinforces this analysis. Bias arising from a source external to the judicial proceeding — such as a familial relationship — is precisely the type of bias that requires disqualification. Unlike "opinions formed by the judge on the basis of facts introduced or events occurring in the course of the current proceedings," *id.* at 555, the bias here stems from a pre-existing familial relationship that existed before and independent of the litigation.

### D. Count III: First Amendment Retaliation Is Established

The Sixth Circuit's three-part test for First Amendment retaliation under § 1983 requires:

> (1) the plaintiff engaged in protected conduct; (2) an adverse action was taken against the plaintiff that would deter a person of ordinary firmness from continuing to engage in that conduct; and (3) there is a causal connection between elements one and two.

*Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378, 394 (6th Cir. 1999) (en banc).

**Protected Conduct.** Plaintiff's filing of a JTC complaint, state court motions, and petition to the Michigan Supreme Court are quintessential exercises of the First Amendment right to petition the government for redress of grievances. *Borough of Duryea v. Guarnieri*, 564 U.S. 379, 388 (2011).

**Adverse Action.** Continued separation from one's child and adverse custody rulings are actions sufficiently severe to deter a person of ordinary firmness. The deprivation of a parent's fundamental liberty interest in the companionship of a child is among the most severe adverse actions conceivable. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000) (recognizing parents' fundamental liberty interest).

**Causal Connection.** Temporal proximity between protected activity and adverse action can establish causation. *Mickey v. Zeidler Tool & Die Co.*, 516 F.3d 516, 525 (6th Cir. 2008). The close temporal relationship between Plaintiff's October 29, 2025 JTC complaint and subsequent adverse actions supports an inference of retaliatory motive.

### E. Count IV: Monell Liability Attaches to the County of Muskegon

Under *Monell v. Department of Social Services*, 436 U.S. 658, 694 (1978), a municipality is liable under § 1983 when "execution of a government's policy or custom . . . inflicts the injury."

Municipal liability may be established through:

1. **An official policy**: a formally adopted regulation or decision. *Id.* at 694.
2. **A widespread custom or practice**: "persistent and widespread . . . practices" that are "so permanent and well settled as to constitute a custom or usage with the force of law." *City of St. Louis v. Praprotnik*, 485 U.S. 112, 127 (1988) (plurality).
3. **Deliberate indifference**: a failure to train, supervise, or screen that amounts to "deliberate indifference to the rights of persons with whom the [employees] come into contact." *Board of County Commissioners v. Brown*, 520 U.S. 397, 404 (1997) (quoting *City of Canton v. Harris*, 489 U.S. 378, 388 (1989)).

The County of Muskegon's liability is established on **all three bases**.

**First — de facto policy.** The placement of three judges from one law firm into two county courts is not a coincidence — it is a pipeline. The firm's founder (Paul M. Ladas) has a daughter on the 60th District bench. A named partner (Hoopes) is Chief Judge of the 14th Circuit. Another named partner (McNeill) handles the family division. This concentration of former colleagues and family members from one firm constitutes a de facto policy of permitting a single professional network to dominate the Muskegon County judiciary.

**Second — widespread custom.** The consistent failure of all three judges to disclose their interconnections when presiding over cases involving the same litigant demonstrates a practice "so permanent and well settled as to constitute a custom or usage with the force of law." *Praprotnik*, 485 U.S. at 127.

**Third — deliberate indifference.** The County failed to implement conflict screening policies despite the obvious risk that three judges from one firm would face conflicts in cases involving parties connected to their shared network. That risk materialized: all three presided over Plaintiff's cases, and all three ruled adversely. The County's failure was the "moving force" behind the deprivation. *Brown*, 520 U.S. at 404. The need for screening was so obvious it constitutes deliberate indifference. *City of Canton*, 489 U.S. at 390 n.10.

### F. Counts V-VI: Supplemental State Law Claims

Plaintiff's state law claims for intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED) and tortious interference with the parental relationship arise from the same nucleus of operative facts as the federal claims and are properly before this Court under 28 U.S.C. § 1367(a).

**IIED.** Michigan recognizes IIED where conduct is "so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community." *Roberts v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 422 Mich. 594, 602 (1985). Exploiting a concealed judicial conflict to separate a parent from his child for over 290 days satisfies this standard. Under *Bauserman v. Unemployment Insurance Agency*, 509 Mich. 94 (2022), constitutional tort damages are available under Michigan law.

**Tortious Interference.** Michigan recognizes tortious interference with a parental relationship. *Hester v. Dwivedi*, 217 Mich. App. 452, 461 (1996). Watson's systematic obstruction of Plaintiff's relationship with L.D.W., facilitated by biased judicial proceedings, constitutes actionable interference.

### G. Count VII: Three-Court Coordinated Deprivation

Count VII presents the most damning claim in this Complaint: three judges from one law firm, each presiding over a separate case involving one litigant, each ruling adversely, each failing to disclose their interconnections.

**The Pattern.** Judge McNeill (custody) took Plaintiff's child. Chief Judge Hoopes (civil) dismissed Plaintiff's housing lawsuit. Judge Ladas-Hoopes (district) evicted Plaintiff from his home. The combined result: childless, homeless, and financially destroyed. This pattern is not coincidence — it is either coordination or the inevitable product of unchecked institutional bias arising from a shared professional network.

**Circumstantial Evidence Standard.** A conspiracy need not be proven by direct evidence of an express agreement. *Adickes v. S.H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144, 152 (1970). Circumstantial evidence — including parallel conduct, shared motives, and an otherwise inexplicable pattern of uniform adverse outcomes — is sufficient. *Hooks v. Hooks*, 771 F.2d 935, 943-44 (6th Cir. 1985). Here, the circumstantial evidence is compelling:

1. Common professional origin (same law firm);
2. Familial bonds (marriage, parentage);
3. Same litigant across all three courts;
4. Uniform adverse outcomes in all three cases;
5. Universal failure to disclose interconnections;
6. Cumulative destruction requiring adverse rulings in all three courts.

**State Action.** The use of state-created judicial processes to deprive Plaintiff of constitutionally protected interests satisfies the state action requirement. *Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 U.S. 922, 937 (1982). Each judge acted under color of state law.

**Constitutional Deprivations.** The three-court pattern deprived Plaintiff of: (1) due process under the Fourteenth Amendment — the right to adjudication before impartial tribunals; (2) substantive due process — the fundamental liberty interest in the care and custody of one's child, *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); and (3) equal protection — systematic adverse treatment of a pro se litigant by interconnected judges.

### H. Judicial Immunity Does Not Shield the Defendant Judges

Defendants will undoubtedly raise judicial immunity. The defense fails for two independent reasons, each of which applies to **all three** Defendant judges — McNeill, Hoopes, and Ladas-Hoopes.

**First**, judicial immunity applies only to "judicial acts." *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 227 (1988). The duty to disclose a disqualifying conflict of interest is not a judicial act — it is a **ministerial and administrative duty** that is a prerequisite to the exercise of judicial power. *Id.* at 229 ("A judge is not immune for actions taken in the performance of administrative, legislative, or executive functions that judges may on occasion be assigned to perform.").

The distinction between judicial and administrative acts depends on "the nature of the act itself, i.e., whether it is a function normally performed by a judge, and [on] the expectations of the parties, i.e., whether they dealt with the judge in his judicial capacity." *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 362 (1978). Conflict disclosure is not a function "normally performed by a judge" in the resolution of disputes — it is a procedural compliance obligation akin to filing financial disclosures or completing recusal checklists. Each of the three judges had the same ministerial obligation to disclose, and each failed.

**Second**, even if conflict disclosure were deemed a judicial act, judicial immunity is overcome when a judge acts "in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 12 (1991). When a judge is mandatorily disqualified under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), the judge has no jurisdiction to preside over the case. Each of the three judges was disqualified by their undisclosed interconnections with the other judges and, in McNeill's case, with the opposing party. Continuing to exercise judicial power despite mandatory disqualification is an act taken in the complete absence of all jurisdiction.

The Supreme Court has drawn this bright line clearly:

> "If a probate judge, with jurisdiction over only wills and estates, should try a criminal case, he would be acting in the clear absence of jurisdiction and would not be immune from liability for his action."

*Bradley v. Fisher*, 80 U.S. (13 Wall.) 335, 352 (1871). Similarly, judges who are mandatorily disqualified but continue to preside have no more jurisdiction than the probate judge trying a criminal case.
