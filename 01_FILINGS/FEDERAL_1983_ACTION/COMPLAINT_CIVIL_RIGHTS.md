53. The conspiracy "itself is the actionable deprivation." *Id.* at 27.

### COUNT II: DEPRIVATION OF DUE PROCESS (FOURTEENTH AMENDMENT) — 42 U.S.C. § 1983
**(Against Defendant McNeill)**

56. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs as though fully set forth herein.

57. The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees every litigant a fair trial before an impartial tribunal. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 876 (2009).

58. "The right to an impartial adjudicator extends to all types of cases, including custody proceedings." *Id.*; see also *Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510, 523 (1927).

59. Judge McNeill's failure to disclose her spousal relationship to Cavan Berry, and the familial connection between Cavan Berry, Ronald Berry, and Watson, created a structural bias that violated Plaintiff's right to due process.

60. The test for disqualification is objective: "whether, under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness, the interest poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden if the guarantee of due process is to be adequately implemented." *Williams v. Pennsylvania*, 579 U.S. 1, 8 (2016) (quoting *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 883-84).

61. Under this objective standard, the undisclosed familial connection between Judge McNeill and Defendant Watson through Ronald Berry and Cavan Berry posed an intolerable risk of bias.

62. **Judicial immunity does not bar this claim** because:

&emsp;a. The duty to disclose a disqualifying conflict of interest is a **ministerial and administrative act**, not a judicial act. *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 229 (1988) (judicial immunity does not extend to "administrative, legislative, or executive functions that judges may on occasion be assigned to perform").

&emsp;b. A judge who presides over a case from which she is mandatorily disqualified acts in the **complete absence of all jurisdiction**. *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 12 (1991) (judicial immunity is overcome when a judge acts "in the clear absence of all jurisdiction"); *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356-57 (1978).

&emsp;c. Under *Liteky v. United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 555 (1994), the extrajudicial source doctrine recognizes that bias arising from a source outside the judicial proceeding — such as an undisclosed familial relationship — is precisely the type of bias that warrants disqualification.

63. As a direct and proximate result of Judge McNeill's deprivation of Plaintiff's due process rights, Plaintiff suffered the damages set forth herein.

Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378, 394 (6th Cir. 1999) (en banc).
