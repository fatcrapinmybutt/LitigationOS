You are hereby notified of your legal obligation to preserve all documents, electronically stored information (ESI), and tangible things that are relevant or may be relevant to the above-captioned action. This obligation arises under Michigan Court Rules 2.302 and 2.310, as well as the common law duty to preserve evidence once litigation is reasonably anticipated. See *Brenner v. **This is not a request — it is a legal obligation.** Failure to preserve evidence may result in sanctions, including adverse inference instructions, exclusion of evidence, contempt findings, or entry of default. See MCR 2.313(B)(2); *Bloemendaal v. Town & Country Sports Ctr., Inc.*, 255 Mich App 207, 213 (2002).

## CONSEQUENCES OF NON-COMPLIANCE

Spoliation of evidence — the intentional or negligent destruction, alteration, or concealment of evidence — carries serious consequences in Michigan courts:

- **Adverse inference instruction:** The court may instruct the jury (or make its own finding) that the destroyed evidence would have been unfavorable to the spoliating party. *Bloemendaal*, 255 Mich App at 213.
- **Exclusion of evidence:** The court may exclude evidence or testimony related to the destroyed materials.
- **Monetary sanctions:** The court may award costs and fees incurred as a result of spoliation.
- **Default or dismissal:** In extreme cases, the court may enter default judgment or dismiss claims.
- **Contempt of court:** Willful destruction of evidence may constitute contempt.
