## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. Corporate Veil Piercing & RICO Enterprise (Counts VIII, XII, XV)

#### 1. Conclusion

The corporate veil should be pierced to hold all Shady Oaks entities jointly and severally liable, and the coordinated pattern of racketeering activity warrants RICO treble damages under MCL 750.159i.

#### 2. Rule

Michigan courts pierce the corporate veil when the totality of circumstances shows that the corporate entity is a mere instrumentality of the controlling entity, used to perpetrate fraud or injustice. *Foodland Distributors v. Al-Naimi*, 220 Mich App 453, 457–458 (1996). The court examines whether: (1) the entities are alter egos; (2) the corporate form was used to commit fraud or wrong; and (3) the plaintiff suffered unjust loss. *Servo Dynamics, Inc. v. Spot Pack, Inc.*, 401 Mich 383 (1977).

Michigan RICO, MCL 750.159i, prohibits conducting or participating in the affairs of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity. A "pattern" requires at least two predicate acts within ten years. MCL 750.159f. Treble damages are available under MCL 750.159j.

#### 3. Explanation

Veil piercing is appropriate where entities share management, commingle operations, fail to observe corporate formalities, and are undercapitalized relative to obligations. *Foodland*, 220 Mich App at 458. The analysis is fact-intensive and considers whether the corporate structure perpetuates injustice. *Herman v. Mobile Homes Corp.*, 317 Mich 233, 243 (1947).

RICO enterprise liability attaches where entities are "associated in fact" for a common purpose and conduct affairs through predicate acts including mail fraud (18 USC § 1341), wire fraud (18 USC § 1343), larceny by conversion (MCL 750.362), and obtaining money under false pretenses (MCL 750.218).

#### 4. Application

The record overwhelmingly supports both veil piercing and RICO liability:

**Alter Ego / Instrumentality:** Seven entities registered in three states operate a single mobile home park at 1977 Whitehall Road. They share the same mailing address (77 Engle St., Englewood, NJ), the same personnel (Bryon Fields, Kim Davis, Casey VanDam, Nicole Browley), and the same LARA licensee contact. Plaintiff's lease was coerced with Cricklewood MHP LLC, but rent was collected by Shady Oaks Park MHP, and management operated under the Homes of America brand. On February 4, 2025, HOA MI Retail Homes LLC — a related entity — filed a declaratory complaint against its own subsidiaries (Case No. 2025-0000000509-CZ), a judicial admission of common control. *See Foodland*, 220 Mich App at 457–458.

**Fraud/Wrong:** The corporate structure was used to: prosecute eviction through a dissolved entity (MCL 450.4802); evade service of process (CT Corporation returned summons as "Undeliverable"); declare $0 in personal property taxes while collecting $144,000+ in annual rents; and deny Plaintiff's ownership of titled property.

**Unjust Loss:** Plaintiff lost his home, all personal property, and custody of his son. He was rendered homeless.

**RICO Pattern:** At least four predicate acts within a single year: (i) fabrication of three irreconcilable ledger versions presented to court (MCL 750.248 — filing false documents); (ii) conversion of $6,085.99 in government housing assistance and personal payments (MCL 750.362 — larceny by conversion); (iii) electronic billing fraud via Zego platform with fabricated meter readings (18 USC § 1343 — wire fraud); (iv) billing $7,633.13 post-destruction through U.S. mail (18 USC § 1341 — mail fraud). The North Morris Estates criminal conviction — at the same Englewood, NJ address — establishes the enterprise's pattern across multiple properties.

#### 5. Conclusion

The Court should pierce the corporate veil and hold all Defendants jointly and severally liable. The RICO enterprise claim entitles Plaintiff to treble damages in excess of $300,000 under MCL 750.159j.

### B. Habitability & Consumer Protection Violations (Counts III, XI, XVI, XVII, XX)

#### 1. Conclusion

Defendants breached the implied warranty of habitability, violated the Michigan Consumer Protection Act and Truth in Renting Act, and constructively evicted Plaintiff through a pattern of neglect, fraud, and coercion.

#### 2. Rule

MCL 554.139 establishes an implied warranty of habitability in every residential lease, requiring that premises are fit for their intended use and that the landlord complies with applicable health and safety laws. This warranty is non-waivable.

The Michigan Consumer Protection Act (MCPA), MCL 445.903, prohibits unfair, unconscionable, or deceptive methods, acts, or practices in the conduct of trade or commerce. Willful violations warrant treble damages. MCL 445.911.

The Truth in Renting Act, MCL 554.631 et seq., prohibits void and unenforceable lease provisions (MCL 554.633) and provides $250 per violation plus actual damages (MCL 554.636).

MCL 125.2325 requires manufactured housing community operators to maintain common areas and utility systems in safe, sanitary condition. MCL 125.2331 prohibits retaliation against tenants who exercise rights under the Mobile Home Commission Act.

#### 3. Explanation

The implied warranty of habitability requires a landlord to maintain premises in a condition fit for human habitation. Raw sewage discharge, contaminated water, and failing infrastructure constitute per se breaches. *See Trentadue v. Gorton*, 479 Mich 378 (2007) (landlord liability for dangerous conditions). When a landlord's conduct renders premises uninhabitable, the tenant may claim constructive eviction and seek damages including rent abatement and relocation costs. *Automobile Club of Michigan v. City of Detroit*, 182 Mich App 446 (1990).

The MCPA applies to landlord-tenant transactions. *Dix v. American Bankers Life Assurance Co.*, 429 Mich 410 (1987) (MCPA broadly construed). Fabricating billing records, imposing unauthorized charges, and refusing government housing assistance while suing for nonpayment constitute deceptive practices under MCL 445.903(1)(n), (s), and (cc).

#### 4. Application

**Habitability:** EGLE's own inspectors confirmed raw sewage discharging onto the ground surface near occupied homes, including Lot 17. Violation Notice VN-017235 (April 15, 2025) documented violations of Part 31 NREPA. Defendants ignored EGLE contact for 17 days and provided only cosmetic repair. This is not a close case — the state environmental agency confirmed the breach. (Exhibits I, P.)

**Consumer Protection:** Defendants' conduct reads like a textbook MCPA violation:
- Fabricated three irreconcilable ledger versions to inflate Plaintiff's balance — MCL 445.903(1)(s) (misrepresentation of a material fact);
- Rejected $1,962.45 in MDHHS housing assistance, then sued for nonpayment — creating an impossible catch-22 for vulnerable tenants;
- Charged Plaintiff a $35 NSF fee from another tenant's account (Jamie Semelbauer);
- Self-installed water meters and billed identical water/sewer gallons (a physical impossibility) — MCL 445.903(1)(n) (deceptive trade practices);
- Billed $7,633.13 *after* destroying Plaintiff's property and removing him from the lot.

Defendants' Affirmative Defense #14 — "Defendant is not required to accept third party rental assistance" — is a judicial admission that they deliberately refuse government housing assistance, then evict for nonpayment. This practice violates both the MCPA and public policy.

**Truth in Renting:** The coerced Cricklewood lease contained provisions violating MCL 554.633, including: late fees exceeding contractual limits, a payment waterfall clause that credits payments to attorney fees before rent (guaranteeing perpetual delinquency), and terms executed under duress by a dissolved entity. At least five violations at $250 each yield $1,250 in statutory damages plus actual damages.

**Constructive Eviction:** The combination of raw sewage exposure, fraudulent billing, serial eviction filings through a dissolved entity, water shutoff while a toddler was present (May 20, 2025), and the July 17 forced removal constitutes constructive eviction as a matter of law.

#### 5. Conclusion

The Court should find Defendants liable for breach of habitability, MCPA violations (with treble damages for willful conduct), Truth in Renting violations, and constructive eviction.

### C. Fraud, Conversion & Billing Manipulation (Counts I, II, III, IX, XIII, XVIII, XIX, XXII)

#### 1. Conclusion

Defendants committed conversion, common-law fraud, ledger fraud, and slander of title. Each claim is independently supported by Defendants' own records, which provide the most damning evidence against them.

#### 2. Rule

Conversion is "any distinct act of domain wrongfully exerted over another's personal property in denial of or inconsistent with the rights therein." *Foremost Ins. Co. v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 439 Mich 378, 391 (1992). Treble damages are mandatory: "not less than 3 times the value of the property at the time of conversion." MCL 600.2919a.

Fraud requires: (1) a material misrepresentation; (2) its falsity; (3) the speaker's knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard; (4) intent to induce reliance; (5) justifiable reliance; and (6) resulting damages. *Allison v. AEW Capital Mgmt., LLP*, 481 Mich 419, 425 (2008) (fraud elements under Michigan law).

MCL 600.2918 (anti-lockout statute) provides the greater of three times actual damages or $200 per occurrence when a person's possessory interest is interfered with by force, threats, or unlawful means.

#### 3. Explanation

Michigan's conversion treble-damage statute is mandatory — it provides that damages "shall not be less than" three times the property value. MCL 600.2919a. The statute applies to all forms of personal property, including mobile homes held by titled owners and converted payments. *See Trail Clinic, P.C. v. Blount, Inc.*, 114 Mich App 679 (1982).

For fraud, the standard is clear and convincing evidence. *Allison*, 481 Mich at 425. Where a defendant's own records contain the misrepresentation — such as a fabricated ledger presented to a court as truthful evidence — the clear-and-convincing standard is met by the documents themselves.

Slander of title requires a false and malicious publication that disparages a plaintiff's right in property, causing special damages. *B&B Investment Group v. Gitler*, 229 Mich App 1, 8 (1998).

#### 4. Application

**Conversion of the Mobile Home:** Plaintiff holds valid Michigan title to the 1970 Marlette (VIN 1646732). Defendants physically entered the home by drilling locks, removed all contents, posted a "FREE" sign, and told prospective buyers that Plaintiff "does not own a home." On July 17, 2025, Defendant Brandel personally took Plaintiff's stove, washer, and dryer. These are textbook acts of conversion under *Foremost*. Fair market value of the home: $15,000–$45,000. Trebled under MCL 600.2919a: $45,000–$135,000.

**Conversion of Payments:** The $6,085.99 in omitted/rejected payments was received by Defendants (or their agents) and never credited or refunded — converting Plaintiff's rent payments and government assistance to their own benefit. Trebled: $18,257.97.

**Fraud (Three Ledger Versions):** The elements of *Allison* are satisfied:
- *Material misrepresentation*: Defendants presented Version B (showing $2,180.18 due) and Version C to the Court, concealing Version A (showing a −$61.64 credit).
- *Falsity*: The 39-minute provenance chain (Version A printed 11:01 AM → emailed 11:32 AM → ZEGO screenshot 11:40 AM on August 22, 2024) proves Version A is the authentic record. Version B, printed three days before the second eviction filing, was fabricated by retroactive ledger manipulation.
- *Knowledge*: Defendants controlled the RentManager cloud platform. The page expansion from 1 to 2 pages and disappearance of the $1,300.26 check prove deliberate alteration.
- *Intent*: Version B was printed specifically to support the $3,805.44 counter-complaint.
- *Reliance*: The 60th District Court relied on the fabricated ledger to enter three judgments totaling $9,551.13.
- *Damages*: Plaintiff lost his home, personal property, and custody of his child.

**Slander of Title:** Casey VanDam's February 18, 2026 statements — "Andrew Pigors does not own a home at Shady Oaks MHC" and "he abandoned the home it is no longer his home" — are false publications made with malice (knowledge of Plaintiff's valid title), directly causing three blocked sales in violation of MCL 125.2319.

**Anti-Lockout (MCL 600.2918):** Three occurrences warrant treble damages: (1) the July 3, 2025 break-in attempt; (2) the July 17, 2025 forced removal; (3) the May 20, 2025 water shutoff while Plaintiff's toddler was in the home.

#### 5. Conclusion

Plaintiff is entitled to conversion treble damages, fraud damages proven at trial, slander of title damages, and anti-lockout statutory damages. The evidence is Defendants' own records.

### D. Constitutional Property Rights (Counts IV, V, X, XXI)

#### 1. Conclusion

Defendants violated Plaintiff's constitutional property rights, prosecuted void proceedings through a dissolved entity, and engaged in retaliatory eviction — all resulting in severe emotional distress and the destruction of the parent-child relationship.

#### 2. Rule

The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). Property rights are protected under the Fourteenth Amendment and Art. 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution. A dissolved LLC may only wind up its affairs and has no capacity to initiate new proceedings. MCL 450.4802; MCL 450.1832.

MCL 600.5720 provides that a landlord shall not retaliate against a tenant for exercising lawful rights, including reporting code violations. An eviction commenced within 90 days of a tenant's complaint to a governmental agency creates a rebuttable presumption of retaliation. MCL 600.5720(1).

Intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED) requires extreme and outrageous conduct exceeding all possible bounds of decency. *Dix v. American Bankers Life Assurance Co.*, 429 Mich 410, 420–421 (1987); *Roberts v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 422 Mich 594, 602–603 (1985).

#### 3. Explanation

Void judgments — those entered by or on behalf of entities lacking legal capacity — are nullities that confer no rights. *Travelers Ins. Co. v. Union Carbide Corp.*, 399 Mich 211 (1977). When eviction proceedings are prosecuted by a dissolved LLC, the resulting judgments and all actions taken in enforcement (including forced removal) are void ab initio. MCL 450.4802.

The retaliatory eviction presumption under MCL 600.5720 is particularly strong where the eviction follows complaints to environmental agencies. The 90-day window is measured from the protected activity (filing the complaint) to the commencement of eviction proceedings.

For IIED, the *Dix* standard requires conduct "so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community." 429 Mich at 420–421.

#### 4. Application

**Void Proceedings:** Homes of America LLC was dissolved. Attorney Cox entered appearance on behalf of a dissolved entity on July 10, 2025. All three civil dispositions ($9,551.13 total) were obtained in the name of entities without legal capacity. The July 17, 2025 forced removal was executed in enforcement of a void judgment. Every action taken is void ab initio. MCL 450.4802; *Travelers*, 399 Mich at 211.

**Retaliatory Eviction:** Plaintiff filed formal sewage complaints on March 7 and March 17, 2025. EGLE inspected on March 20, 2025 and issued Violation Notice VN-017235 on April 15, 2025. The eviction judgment was entered June 9, 2025, and the forced removal executed July 17, 2025 — both within 90 days of Plaintiff's protected complaints. The rebuttable presumption of retaliation under MCL 600.5720 is established. The retaliatory timeline is unmistakable: each time Plaintiff asserted rights, Defendants escalated — coerced lease after sewage complaints, fabricated ledger after payment challenges, property destruction after EGLE complaint.

**IIED:** This case exceeds the *Dix* standard by a wide margin. Defendants: (a) drilled locks and destroyed a home while a three-year-old child's belongings were inside; (b) posted a "FREE" sign inviting the public to scavenge Plaintiff's possessions; (c) rejected government housing assistance then evicted for nonpayment; (d) manufactured three irreconcilable ledger versions to frame Plaintiff as a chronic non-payer; (e) rendered Plaintiff homeless during active custody proceedings, directly contributing to the loss of parenting time with L.D.W.; and (f) billed $7,633.13 *after* destroying the property. The child — three years old, present during the destruction — provides an additional basis for emotional distress damages. No reasonable jury could find this conduct anything less than extreme and outrageous.

**Impact on Custody:** Plaintiff has been separated from his son since approximately August 8, 2025. "Unstable housing" — caused directly by Defendants' wrongful eviction — was used against Plaintiff in custody proceedings (*Pigors v. Watson*, Case No. 2024-001507-DC). The destruction of the parent-child bond — a fundamental liberty interest — is independently compensable. *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65; *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982).

#### 5. Conclusion

All three eviction dispositions are void ab initio. The retaliatory eviction presumption is established. The IIED claim is supported by conduct that exceeds the *Dix* standard in every respect.

Auto. Ins. Co. v. Campbell*, 538 U.S. 408, 419 (2003).

Statutory treble damages are provided by: MCL 600.2919a (conversion — mandatory); MCL 600.2918 (anti-lockout — 3× actual or $200/occurrence); MCL 445.911 (MCPA — treble for willful violations); MCL 750.159j (RICO — treble).

All five *State Farm* reprehensibility factors are at maximum:

|--------|------------|

| Indifference to health/safety | **MAXIMUM** — raw sewage near children, forced lockout with toddler present, homelessness |
| Financial vulnerability of target | **MAXIMUM** — pro se plaintiff rendered homeless, sleeping in car |
| Repeated/isolated conduct | **MAXIMUM** — 3 ledger versions, 3 blocked sales, 3 civil dispositions, 121% rent increase, serial evictions |
| Intentional malice vs. accident | **MAXIMUM** — ledger fabricated 3 days before filing; "FREE" sign on property; MDHHS rejection; post-destruction billing |

The national pattern of Alden Global Capital's predatory conduct — including the North Morris Estates criminal conviction in Michigan — further supports a punitive multiplier. Alden controls a portfolio exceeding $275 million with 10,000+ lots across 138 parks in 17 states. A meaningful punitive award is necessary to deter future predation.

**Statutory treble damages apply cumulatively to different categories of harm:**
