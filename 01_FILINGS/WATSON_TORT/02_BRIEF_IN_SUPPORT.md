## I. THIS ACTION IS PROPERLY BROUGHT AS A SEPARATE CIVIL CASE

### A. *Adair v. State* Authorizes Independent Tort Actions

The Michigan Supreme Court in *Adair v. State*, 470 Mich 105; 680 NW2d 386 (2004), established that tort claims arising from conduct related to domestic relations may be brought as independent civil actions. The *Adair* Court recognized the parent-child relationship as a fundamental liberty interest and held that interference with this relationship gives rise to actionable tort claims that are independent of the underlying domestic relations case.

### B. The Claims Are Distinct From the Custody Proceedings

The claims in this Complaint sound in tort, fraud, and equity — not domestic relations. The custody case (No. 2024-001507-DC) concerns custody and parenting time determinations. This action seeks damages for the tortious conduct of the Watson Defendants in deliberately undermining and destroying Plaintiff's parental relationship. These are different legal theories, require different elements of proof, and seek different remedies (damages rather than custody orders).

### C. Five Defendants — Only One Is Party to the Custody Case

Four of the five Defendants (Lane Watson, Lori Watson, Albert Watson, and Cody Watson) are not parties to the custody case. Their tortious conduct cannot be addressed in the domestic relations proceeding. The only court in which Plaintiff can seek redress against these non-party tortfeasors is a separate civil action.

### D. Res Judicata and Collateral Estoppel Do Not Bar This Action

Res judicata requires identity of parties, identity of causes of action, and a final judgment on the merits. *Adair v. State*, 470 Mich at 121. None of these requirements is met here:

- **Different parties**: Four of five Defendants were not parties to the custody case.
- **Different causes of action**: Tort claims for damages were not and could not have been adjudicated in the custody case.
- **No final judgment on the merits**: The custody case remains pending; no final judgment has been entered on any tort claim.

## II. LEGAL VIABILITY OF EACH COUNT

### A. Count I — Civil Conspiracy

**Elements**: (1) a concerted action; (2) between two or more persons; (3) to accomplish an unlawful objective or a lawful objective through unlawful means. *Advocacy Org. for Patients & Providers v. Auto Club Ins. Ass'n*, 257 Mich App 365, 384; 670 NW2d 569 (2003).

**Application**: All five Watson Defendants engaged in a coordinated campaign with a single objective — the elimination of Plaintiff from L.D.W.'s life. The division of labor (Emily denying parenting time; Lane and Albert intimidating at exchanges; Lori filing false court documents; Cody harassing) demonstrates concerted action. The 305 documented interference incidents constitute overt acts. The objective — depriving a parent of a fundamental constitutional right — is unlawful.

**Key Authority**:
- *Admiral Ins. Co. v. Columbia Cas. Ins. Co.*, 194 Mich App 300, 313; 486 NW2d 351 (1992)
- *Temborius v. Slatkin*, 157 Mich App 587, 599; 403 NW2d 821 (1986)

### B. Count II — Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

**Elements**: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct; (2) intent or recklessness; (3) causation; (4) severe emotional distress. *Roberts v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 422 Mich 594, 602-03; 374 NW2d 905 (1985).

**Application**: Emily Watson's conduct satisfies the high threshold for IIED. Deliberately denying a parent all contact with his child for more than 200 consecutive days, recharacterizing Plaintiff's protective CPS report as evidence of harassment, coaching the child to fear his father (305 documented interference incidents by the Watson family), and engineering 59 days of wrongful jailing constitutes conduct "so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency." *Id.* The severity of Plaintiff's distress — depression, anxiety, job loss, health deterioration — is well-documented.

**Key Authority**:
- *Graham v. Ford*, 237 Mich App 670; 604 NW2d 713 (1999)
- *Doe v. Mills*, 212 Mich App 73; 536 NW2d 824 (1995)
- Restatement (Second) of Torts § 46 (1965)

### C. Count III — Tortious Interference With Parental Relationship

**Elements**: (1) existence of a valid relationship; (2) defendant's knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional interference; (4) causation; (5) damages.

**Application**: Plaintiff's parental relationship with L.D.W. is both a biological fact and a court-recognized legal right. Each Watson Defendant knew of this relationship. Each intentionally interfered through specific, documented acts. The interference caused the complete destruction of the relationship for more than 200 consecutive days and more than 300 total days across all episodes.

**Key Authority**:
- *Adair v. State*, 470 Mich 105; 680 NW2d 386 (2004) — recognizing fundamental parental right
- *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23; 490 NW2d 568 (1992) — recognizing tort claims in parent-child context
- *Plumley v. Klein*, 388 Mich 1; 199 NW2d 169 (1972)

### D. Count IV — Tortious Interference With Custody Order

**Elements**: (1) existence of a valid legal right (the custody order); (2) knowledge of the right; (3) intentional interference with the right; (4) wrongful means; (5) damages. *Dalley v. Dykema Gossett PLLC*, 287 Mich App 296; 788 NW2d 679 (2010).

**Application**: The custody order is a court decree establishing Plaintiff's enforceable right to parenting time. Emily Watson willfully violated this order on more than 200 consecutive days. Her means were wrongful: recharacterizing Plaintiff's protective CPS report as harassment, perjury, obtaining ex parte orders through fraud, and coordinated family intimidation. This is not negligent noncompliance — it is intentional, systematic destruction of a legal right.

**Key Authority**:
- *Feldman v. Green*, 138 Mich App 360; 360 NW2d 881 (1984)
- *Cedroni Assocs., Inc. v. Tomblinson, Harburn Assocs.*, 492 Mich 40; 821 NW2d 1 (2012) — elements of tortious interference

### E. Count V — Defamation Per Se

**Elements**: (1) a false and defamatory statement concerning the plaintiff; (2) an unprivileged communication to a third party; (3) fault amounting at least to negligence; (4) damages (presumed in per se cases). *Burden v. Elias Bros. Big Boy Restaurants*, 240 Mich App 723, 727; 613 NW2d 378 (2000).

**Application**: Emily Watson's statements to the court, schools, medical providers, and the community that Plaintiff is "abusive," "dangerous," and "unfit" are provably false — no child welfare authority has substantiated any finding of abuse or neglect against Plaintiff. To the contrary, Plaintiff himself reported concerns about bruising on L.D.W. to CPS and police, with photographic and journal documentation. Statements imputing criminal conduct (child abuse) are defamatory per se under Michigan law, and damages are presumed without proof of special damages. The statements were published to numerous third parties. Emily acted with actual malice (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard).

**Privilege Defense Anticipated**: Emily may assert judicial or quasi-judicial privilege for statements made in court or to CPS. However, this privilege does not apply when statements are: (a) made outside of proceedings (to neighbors, employers, community); (b) made with knowledge of falsity; or (c) made for improper purposes. *Couch v. Schultz*, 193 Mich App 292; 483 NW2d 684 (1992). The total absence of substantiated findings, combined with Plaintiff's own documented evidence of genuine parental concern, defeats any claim of good-faith privilege.

**Key Authority**:
- *Mitan v. Campbell*, 474 Mich 21; 706 NW2d 420 (2005) — defamation framework
- *Smith v. Anonymous Joint Enterprise*, 487 Mich 102; 793 NW2d 533 (2010)

### F. Count VI — Slander

**Elements**: (1) a defamatory statement; (2) oral publication to a third party; (3) falsity; (4) damages (special damages required for slander unless per se categories apply).

**Application**: Emily Watson and Lori Watson made specific oral false statements — false claims of domestic violence, substance abuse, and child endangerment — to neighbors, community members, and Plaintiff's employers. These statements contributed to Plaintiff's loss of three jobs (special damages) and reputational harm.

**Key Authority**:
- *Heritage Optical Ctr., Inc. v. Levine*, 137 Mich App 793; 359 NW2d 210 (1984)
- *Ireland v. Edwards*, 230 Mich App 607; 584 NW2d 632 (1998)

### G. Count VII — Malicious Prosecution

**Elements**: (1) prior proceedings terminated in plaintiff's favor; (2) absence of probable cause; (3) malice; (4) special injury. *Walsh v. Taylor*, 263 Mich App 618, 633; 689 NW2d 506 (2004).

**Application**: Watson initiated or procured multiple proceedings against Plaintiff — PPO petitions, contempt motions, and ex parte applications — all based on false allegations. No proceeding has resulted in substantiated findings of abuse or neglect. Plaintiff's own reports of concern (CPS call, police reports with photographic evidence) were weaponized against him. The pattern of baseless filings demonstrates absence of probable cause and malice. Special injury: 59 days wrongful incarceration, three job losses, severe emotional distress.

**Key Authority**:
- *Friedman v. Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1; 312 NW2d 585 (1981)
- *Matthews v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Mich.*, 456 Mich 365; 572 NW2d 603 (1998)

### H. Count VIII — Abuse of Process

**Elements**: (1) an ulterior purpose; (2) an act in the use of process improper in the regular prosecution of the proceeding. *Friedman v. Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1, 30-31; 312 NW2d 585 (1981).

**Application**: Emily Watson's ulterior purpose — complete elimination of father from child's life — is documented by the 305 documented interference incidents and more than 300 total days of denied contact. Improper acts: false statements under oath, fabricated evidence to obtain 24 ex parte orders, weaponization of contempt proceedings to jail Plaintiff rather than resolve disputes. Unlike malicious prosecution, abuse of process does not require favorable termination.

**Key Authority**:
- *Spear v. Pendill*, 164 Mich 620; 130 NW 343 (1911)
- *Vallance v. Brewbaker*, 411 Mich 568; 309 NW2d 168 (1981)

### I. Count IX — Fraud on the Court

**Elements**: (1) a material false representation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent that the representation be relied upon; (4) justifiable reliance by the court; (5) resulting damage. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c).

**Application**: Emily Watson presented fabricated abuse allegations, misrepresented income, concealed medical records, and made false statements in ex parte applications. The court relied on these representations in issuing 24 ex parte orders and the inflated child support obligation. The fraud was not incidental but foundational — the adverse orders would not have issued absent the false representations.

**Key Authority**:
- MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c) — relief from judgment for fraud
- *Kukla v. Village of Antioch*, 647 F Supp 799 (ND Ill 1986) (discussing fraud on court standard)
- *Hazel Park Racing Ass'n v. Racing Comm'r*, 336 Mich 508; 58 NW2d 97 (1953)

### J. Count X — Conversion

**Elements**: (1) plaintiff had an ownership or possessory interest; (2) defendant exercised dominion over the property; (3) the dominion was wrongful and inconsistent with plaintiff's rights. *Foremost Ins. Co. v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 439 Mich 378, 391; 486 NW2d 600 (1992).

**Application**: Plaintiff owned personal property (photographs, personal effects, legal documents) that Emily Watson retained and refused to return despite demand. Her retention is wrongful and inconsistent with Plaintiff's ownership rights.

**Key Authority**:
- *Check Reporting Servs., Inc. v. Mich. Nat'l Bank*, 191 Mich App 614; 478 NW2d 893 (1991)

### K. Count XI — Unjust Enrichment

**Elements**: (1) receipt of a benefit by defendant from plaintiff; (2) an inequity resulting to plaintiff because of the retention of the benefit. *Karaus v. Bank of New York Mellon*, 300 Mich App 9, 22; 831 NW2d 897 (2012).

**Application**: Emily Watson has received $50,000+ in child support payments while denying all parenting time. The support obligation was based on misrepresented income figures. Receiving full financial support while simultaneously destroying the relationship that justifies the support structure constitutes unjust enrichment.

**Key Authority**:
- *Morris Pumps v. Centerline Piping, Inc.*, 273 Mich App 187; 729 NW2d 898 (2006)
- *Dumas v. Auto Club Ins. Ass'n*, 437 Mich 521; 473 NW2d 652 (1991)

### L. Count XII — Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

**Elements**: (1) negligent conduct; (2) genuine and serious mental distress; (3) reasonable foreseeability; (4) plaintiff within zone of danger. *Daley v. LaCroix*, 384 Mich 4, 12-13; 179 NW2d 390 (1970).

**Application**: Pleaded in the alternative to Count II. Even if the Watson Defendants' conduct is found not to be "extreme and outrageous" (required for IIED), it was at minimum negligent. It is reasonably foreseeable that blocking a father from his child for more than 200 consecutive days, filing false reports, and engaging in intimidation would cause severe emotional distress. Plaintiff is the direct target, squarely within the zone of danger.

**Key Authority**:
- *Wargelin v. Sisters of Mercy Health Corp.*, 149 Mich App 75; 385 NW2d 732 (1986)
- *Taylor v. Kurapati*, 236 Mich App 315; 600 NW2d 670 (1999)

### M. Count XIII — Loss of Parental Companionship

**Constitutional Foundation**: The U.S. Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized the parent-child relationship as a fundamental liberty interest: *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982); *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102 (1996).

**Michigan Recognition**: Michigan law recognizes loss of society and companionship as a category of compensable damages. *Breckon v. Franklin Fuel Co.*, 383 Mich 251; 174 NW2d 836 (1970); *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23; 490 NW2d 568 (1992).

**Application**: Emily Watson's intentional conduct has deprived Plaintiff of more than 300 total days of companionship with L.D.W., including more than 200 consecutive days during the current episode. This is an independently compensable injury — the loss of daily guidance, counsel, comfort, and society of one's child cannot be reduced to any other tort category. The deprivation is ongoing and intensifies with each passing day.

## III. STANDING AND CAPACITY

### A. Standing

Plaintiff has standing to bring this action because he has suffered an injury in fact (deprivation of parental rights, emotional distress, financial harm), caused by the Watson Defendants' conduct, and redressable by a favorable court decision (damages). Plaintiff's injuries are particularized and concrete — they are specific to him, not generalized grievances.

### B. Capacity

Plaintiff brings this action as an individual adult with the legal capacity to sue. MCR 2.201(B). Plaintiff proceeds pro se, as is his constitutional right. *Const 1963, art 1, § 13*; *Eldred v. Ziny*, 246 Mich 478; 224 NW 642 (1929).

### C. Pro Se Status

While pro se litigants are not exempt from procedural rules, courts are required to construe their filings liberally and hold them to less stringent standards than those imposed on attorneys. *Estelle v. Gamble*, 429 U.S. 97, 106 (1976); *Haines v. Kerner*, 404 U.S. 519, 520 (1972).

## IV. RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED DEFENSES

### A. Parental Immunity

Defendants may argue that parental immunity bars some or all of Plaintiff's claims. However:

1. **Michigan has abrogated parental immunity for intentional torts.** *Plumley v. Klein*, 388 Mich 1, 8; 199 NW2d 169 (1972).
2. **Parental immunity applies between parent and child, not between parents.** The Watson family members are not Plaintiff's parents. Emily Watson, as the other parent, does not enjoy parental immunity against Plaintiff's claims.
3. **Parental immunity does not apply to conspiracy claims** where the co-conspirators include non-parent third parties.

### B. Res Judicata and Collateral Estoppel

As discussed in Section I.D above, res judicata does not bar this action because: (a) four of five defendants are new parties; (b) tort claims were not adjudicated in the custody case; and (c) no final judgment exists on the merits of any tort claim.

### C. Judicial Privilege / Litigation Privilege

Emily Watson may claim privilege for statements made in judicial proceedings. This defense fails because:

1. **Privilege does not extend to knowingly false statements.** *Couch v. Schultz*, 193 Mich App 292; 483 NW2d 684 (1992).
2. **Privilege does not extend to statements made outside proceedings** — statements to neighbors, employers, and community members are not privileged.
3. **Privilege does not protect abuse of process** — the misuse of judicial proceedings themselves is the tort.
4. **Emily's recharacterization of Plaintiff's protective CPS report as harassment** demonstrates bad faith and overcomes any presumption of good faith.

### D. Statute of Limitations

Michigan's general tort statute of limitations is three (3) years. MCL 600.5805(2). The Watson Defendants' tortious conduct is ongoing and continuing. Under the continuing wrong doctrine, the statute of limitations does not begin to run until the last injurious act. *Horvath v. Delida*, 213 Mich App 620; 540 NW2d 760 (1995). The interference, alienation, and defamation continue to this day; accordingly, no claim is time-barred.

### E. Failure to State a Claim

The Verified Complaint alleges specific facts supporting each element of each cause of action. Plaintiff has identified specific actors, specific conduct, specific dates and durations, specific harm, and specific legal theories. The Complaint satisfies the fact-pleading requirements of MCR 2.111.

### F. First Amendment Defense to Defamation

Defendants cannot invoke the First Amendment to protect knowingly false statements of fact. *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan*, 376 U.S. 254 (1964), protects opinion and good-faith reporting — not deliberate falsehoods. Statements that Plaintiff is "abusive" and "dangerous," when contradicted by the complete absence of any substantiated child welfare finding and by Plaintiff's own documented evidence of genuine parental concern, are provably false statements of fact, not protected opinion.
