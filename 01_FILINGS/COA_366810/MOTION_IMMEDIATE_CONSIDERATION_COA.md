3. Specifically, MCR 7.216(A)(7) authorizes this Court to enter any order or grant any relief during the pendency of an appeal that is deemed just and appropriate.

4. This Court's authority under MCR 7.216(A) includes the power to enter temporary orders preserving the status quo or preventing irreparable harm while the merits of the appeal are adjudicated. Schlender v Schlender, unpublished per curiam opinion of the Court of Appeals, issued April 18, 2019 (Docket No. 346075) (granting temporary relief in a custody matter pending appeal). III. LEGAL STANDARD FOR EMERGENCY RELIEF

16. This Court may grant emergency relief pending appeal under MCR 7.216(A)(7) when the moving party demonstrates: (1) irreparable harm absent relief; (2) likelihood of success on the merits; (3) that the balance of hardships favors the movant; and (4) that the public interest supports the requested relief. Michigan Coalition of State Employee Unions v Civil Service Commission, 465 Mich 212, 217; 634 NW2d 692 (2001).

17. This standard mirrors the traditional equitable factors for injunctive relief, applied with particular solicitude in cases involving the fundamental constitutional right to parent-child relationships. Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57, 65; 120 S Ct 2054; 147 L Ed 2d 49 (2000) (plurality) (recognizing the "fundamental right of parents to make decisions concerning the care, custody, and control of their children").


IV. IRREPARABLE HARM

18. The 569+ days of parent-child separation is itself irreparable harm. Lost time between a parent and child can never be recovered. Each day that passes without contact further damages the bond between Appellant and his son and increases the difficulty of successful reunification.

19. Child development research consistently demonstrates that extended parent-child separations cause lasting psychological harm to the child, including attachment disorders, anxiety, depression, and behavioral problems. See Affidavit of Andrew Pigors, ¶¶ 29-31 (filed herewith). At 569+ days of separation, Appellant is approaching and may have already passed the critical threshold beyond which the parent-child bond suffers permanent damage. The urgency of this motion cannot be overstated: each day of inaction by this Court adds to harm that no future order can undo.

20. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that the parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Santosky v Kramer, 455 US 745, 753; 102 S Ct 1388; 71 L Ed 2d 599 (1982) ("[T]he interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."). Stanley v Illinois, 405 US 645, 651; 92 S Ct 1208; 31 L Ed 2d 551 (1972) ("The private interest here, that of a man in the children he has sired and raised, undeniably warrants deference and, absent a powerful countervailing interest, protection.").

21. The deprivation of this fundamental right, even temporarily, constitutes irreparable harm per se. Elrod v Burns, 427 US 347, 373; 96 S Ct 2673; 49 L Ed 2d 547 (1976) ("The loss of First Amendment freedoms, for even minimal periods of time, unquestionably constitutes irreparable injury."). The same principle applies with equal force to the fundamental right of familial association.

22. Michigan's own public policy recognizes the critical importance of maintaining parent-child relationships. MCL 722.954a states that Michigan's policy is to promote the health, safety, and welfare of children while strengthening family life. The complete severance of Appellant's contact with his child for 569+ days contravenes this stated policy.

23. Under MCL 722.23, the best interests of the child include: (a) the emotional ties between parent and child; (c) the capacity to provide love, affection, and guidance; (f) the moral fitness of the parties; and (j) the willingness of each party to facilitate a close and continuing relationship between the child and the other parent. Extended separation undermines every one of these factors.


V. LIKELIHOOD OF SUCCESS ON THE MERITS

24. Appellant is likely to succeed on the merits of this appeal for the following independent reasons:

A. THE $250 FILING SANCTION VIOLATES ACCESS TO JUSTICE

25. The trial court's imposition of a $250-per-motion filing bond operates as a de facto bar to access to the courts for a pro se litigant. This sanction was imposed without the procedural safeguards required under MCR 2.114 and MCR 2.625. The effect is to deny Appellant any forum in which to seek modification of the parenting-time suspension — a result that violates both the Michigan Constitution, Art 1 § 13 (right of access to courts), and the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.

B. EX PARTE ORDERS WITHOUT DUE PROCESS

26. The trial court entered 24 ex parte orders during these proceedings, including the critical order suspending Appellant's parenting time. MCR 3.207(B)(1) and (B)(5)-(6) strictly limit the circumstances under which ex parte custody and parenting-time orders may enter, requiring specific facts in a verified pleading demonstrating that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will occur. The record does not reflect compliance with these requirements.

27. Due process requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a parent may be deprived of custody or parenting time. Mathews v Eldridge, 424 US 319, 333; 96 S Ct 893; 47 L Ed 2d 18 (1976). The trial court's pattern of ex parte adjudication systematically denied Appellant this constitutional minimum.

C. FAILURE TO MAKE REQUIRED BEST-INTEREST FINDINGS

28. MCL 722.23 requires the trial court to consider, evaluate, and determine twelve specific best-interest factors before making custody or parenting-time decisions. The trial court failed to make findings on each of the twelve statutory factors before suspending Appellant's parenting time, rendering the suspension an abuse of discretion. Vodvarka v Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 1, 8; 672 NW2d 847 (2003) (trial court must evaluate proper cause or change of circumstances and make best-interest findings). Fletcher v Fletcher, 447 Mich 871, 882; 526 NW2d 889 (1994) (failure to make findings on each factor is reversible error).

D. 1,127 DOCUMENTED JUDICIAL VIOLATIONS

29. A comprehensive review of the record reveals 1,127 documented judicial violations, including 377 of critical severity. These violations include, but are not limited to:
      Ex parte communications with opposing party in violation of MCR 2.003 and the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct
      Off-record, ex parte handling of evidence (including USB recordings and mental health evaluations)
      Refusal to consider exculpatory evidence offered by Appellant
      Systematic denial of Appellant's right to present witnesses and evidence
      Failure to comply with mandatory procedural requirements of MCR 3.206 and MCR 3.207

30. This Court reviews constitutional issues de novo. In re Sanders, 495 Mich 394, 404; 852 NW2d 524 (2014). Abuse of discretion in custody determinations is also reviewed under a deferential but meaningful standard that requires the trial court's findings to be supported by the evidence and compliant with the governing legal standards. Berger v Berger, 277 Mich App 700, 705; 747 NW2d 336 (2008).

E. DISQUALIFICATION FOR BIAS

31. Multiple critical violations relate to MCR 2.003 (Disqualification), including ex parte communications, refusal to consider evidence, and apparent prejudgment. These violations, taken together, demonstrate that the trial court's impartiality might reasonably be questioned, warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).


VI. BALANCE OF HARDSHIPS

32. The balance of hardships overwhelmingly favors Appellant. On one side, Appellant's three-year-old son is being denied all contact with his father — a harm that compounds daily and may become permanent if not addressed. On the other side, Defendant suffers no cognizable harm from an order restoring reasonable, supervised parenting time pending appeal.

33. If this Court grants temporary relief and is ultimately reversed, Defendant will have lost nothing — the child will have simply maintained contact with both parents during the appellate process. If this Court denies relief and Appellant ultimately prevails on appeal, months or years of additional irreparable separation will have occurred, causing harm to both Appellant and the child that can never be remedied.


VII. PUBLIC INTEREST

34. Michigan's public policy, as expressed in MCL 722.954a and the Child Custody Act, MCL 722.21 et seq., strongly favors the preservation of family relationships and parental involvement in children's lives. The public interest is served by ensuring that parenting-time suspensions are imposed only after due process and proper best-interest findings — not through ex parte orders without hearing.

35. The public interest is also served by ensuring that pro se litigants have meaningful access to the courts, and that filing sanctions do not operate as a de facto denial of justice. The integrity of the judicial system requires that fundamental parental rights not be terminated through procedural maneuvers that avoid the merits.
