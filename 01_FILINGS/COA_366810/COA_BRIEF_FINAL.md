## IV. STANDARD OF REVIEW

Constitutional issues, including due process claims, are reviewed de novo. *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 91 (2009). Custody and parenting time decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 507 (2003). An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision falls outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes. *Berger v. Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 705 (2008). Findings of fact are reviewed for clear error. MCR 2.613(C). A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made. *Fletcher v. Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 878 (1994).

## V. ARGUMENT

### A. The Trial Court Violated Appellant's Procedural Due Process Rights

The Fourteenth Amendment prohibits states from depriving any person of liberty without due process of law. U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1. A parent's right to the care, custody, and control of his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

Under the *Mathews v. Eldridge* three-factor balancing test, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976), the court must weigh: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation and value of additional safeguards; and (3) the government's interest.

Here, all three factors weigh overwhelmingly in Appellant's favor:

**First**, the private interest — the parent-child relationship — is "far more precious than any property right." *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 758-59 (1982). Father has been completely severed from his 217-day-old relationship with Lincoln.

**Second**, the risk of erroneous deprivation was extreme and realized. The ex parte order was based on: (a) an illegal recording (MCL 750.539c felony); (b) a premeditated scheme evidenced by Albert Watson's admission; (c) no opportunity for Father to respond. Additional safeguards — such as a hearing — would have revealed all of this.

**Third**, the state's interest in protecting children was not served. Multiple police investigations found zero concerns. The drug screen was negative. The independent evaluation found zero clinical issues — until the judge interfered.

### B. The Trial Court Violated Appellant's Substantive Due Process Rights

Substantive due process protects fundamental rights from government interference regardless of the procedures used. Where a fundamental right is at stake, the government must demonstrate a compelling interest and narrow tailoring. *Washington v. Glucksberg*, 521 U.S. 702 (1997).

The complete termination of Father's parenting time is the most extreme remedy available — the functional equivalent of termination of parental rights without the procedural protections required by *Santosky*. No compelling state interest supports this termination where:

- Drug screen: **NEGATIVE**
- HealthWest evaluation #1: **ALL ZEROS**
- Police investigations (9): **ZERO findings**
- AppClose messages: **526 cooperative communications** from Father
- Documented incidents of harm: **ZERO**

The trial court imposed maximum deprivation with zero evidence of risk. This is the textbook definition of a substantive due process violation.

### C. The Ex Parte Orders Were Based on Illegally Obtained Evidence

The recording by Lori Watson was made in violation of MCL 750.539c, which provides:

> Any person who is present or who is not present during a private conversation and who willfully uses any device to eavesdrop upon the conversation without the consent of all parties thereto... is guilty of a felony punishable by imprisonment for not more than 2 years...

Evidence obtained in violation of constitutional or statutory protections must be suppressed. *Mapp v. Ohio*, 367 U.S. 643 (1961). The fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine extends to all evidence derived from the illegality. *Wong Sun v. United States*, 371 U.S. 471 (1963). Michigan follows this principle. *People v. Beavers*, 393 Mich 554 (1975).

Because the ex parte order of August 8, 2025 relied upon this feloniously obtained recording, the order — and all subsequent orders flowing from it — must be vacated.

### D. The Trial Court Exhibited Systematic Judicial Bias Requiring Reversal

Under MCR 2.003(C)(1), a judge is disqualified when the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party. The test is whether a reasonable person would perceive bias. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 881 (2009).

The statistical record here speaks for itself:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Ex parte orders | 52 |
| Ex parte rate | 44% |
| Orders favoring Mother | 100% |
| Orders favoring Father | 0% |
| Judicial canon violations documented | 1127 |
| Father's days incarcerated | 59+ |
| Father's jobs lost | 3 |
| Police investigations finding wrongdoing | 0 of 9 |

No reasonable person could examine this record and conclude the proceedings were fair. The bias is not merely apparent — it is mathematical. A 100% ruling rate in one party's favor across 52 orders is a statistical impossibility absent systematic bias.

Additionally, the trial court's ex parte communication with the HealthWest evaluator — resulting in a changed evaluation — constitutes independent grounds for disqualification. *Ward v. Village of Monroeville*, 409 U.S. 57 (1972).

### E. The Trial Court Failed to Apply MCL 722.23 Best Interest Factors

Under MCL 722.23, the court must consider twelve factors in determining custody and parenting time. *Brown v. Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 600 (2004). The trial court must make findings on each applicable factor. *Grew v. Knox*, 265 Mich App 333 (2005).

The record below contains no analysis of the best interest factors prior to the complete termination of Father's parenting time. This alone constitutes reversible error. Where the trial court terminates all parenting time — the most extreme disposition available — without best interest findings, the order cannot stand.

Factor (j) — the willingness of each parent to facilitate a close relationship between the child and the other parent — is particularly relevant. The AppClose record demonstrates 526 cooperative messages from Father and a systematic pattern of gatekeeping by Mother (28 documented manipulation instances). This factor weighs decisively for Father.

### F. The Trial Court's Orders Constitute an Abuse of Discretion

The trial court's decisions fall outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes. *Berger v. Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 705 (2008). Where exculpatory evidence overwhelmingly demonstrates no risk to the child, yet the court imposes total severance of the parent-child relationship, the discretion has been abused.

The cumulative weight of the errors below compels reversal:

1. Reliance on illegally obtained evidence (MCL 750.539c felony)
2. Disregard of exculpatory evidence (negative drug screen, zero evaluations, zero police findings)
3. Ex parte judicial communication with evaluator
4. Statistical impossibility of unbiased decision-making (100% adverse to Father)
5. Failure to apply MCL 722.23 factors
6. Disproportionate punishment (59+ days jail, 217+ days no contact)
7. Documented premeditation by adversary (Albert Watson admission)
