## 2. SUPREME COURT RULE 10 — REASONS FOR GRANTING CERTIORARI

Rule 10 provides that cert is granted only for "compelling reasons," including:

### (a) Circuit/State Court Conflicts
> A state court of last resort has decided an important federal question in a way that **conflicts with the decision of another state court of last resort or of a United States court of appeals.**

### (b) Important Federal Question Not Yet Settled
> A state court of last resort has decided an important question of federal law that **has not been, but should be, settled by this Court.**

### (c) Departure from Accepted Judicial Proceedings
> A state court has so far departed from the accepted and usual course of judicial proceedings, or sanctioned such a departure by a lower court, as to **call for an exercise of this Court's supervisory power.**

**Note:** Rule 10 is not a matter of right. The Court exercises discretion. Approximately 1% of petitions are granted. The petition must demonstrate **national significance**, not merely error correction.

## 3. QUESTIONS PRESENTED — FORMAT

The Questions Presented is **the single most important part** of the cert petition.

### Format Requirements:
- Must appear on the **first page** after the cover
- **Concise, clear, and direct** — typically 1-3 sentences per question
- Frame as a **yes/no question** that highlights the constitutional issue
- Must be **broad enough** to have national importance
- Must **not be case-specific** — frame as a legal principle applicable beyond this case
- Typically **2-3 questions** (rarely more)

### Template:
> Whether [constitutional provision] requires [specific protection] when [factual scenario of broad applicability], where [state court held X contrary to Y].

## 4. OUR STRONGEST CERT-WORTHY ISSUES

### Issue A: Due Process in Custody — 571+ Days Parental Rights Terminated Without Hearing

**Constitutional Basis:** Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause

**The Issue:** Father's parental rights and custody were effectively terminated for 571+ consecutive days without any evidentiary hearing, adversarial proceeding, or opportunity to present evidence. The trial court relied on ex parte communications and uncontested allegations.

**Why Cert-Worthy:**
- **Fundamental right at stake:** Parental rights are "far more precious than property rights" (*Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982))
- **No hearing provided:** The Supreme Court has held that "the minimum requirements of procedural due process" demand notice and opportunity to be heard (*Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976))
- **National scope:** Custody courts across the country routinely restrict parental access through temporary orders that become semi-permanent without meaningful review
- **Conflict with federal precedent:** *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) (fundamental right of parents to direct upbringing of children)

### Issue B: Structural Judicial Bias — 44% Ex Parte Communication Rate

**Constitutional Basis:** Due Process (Fourteenth Amendment); Judicial impartiality (fundamental fairness)

**The Issue:** The presiding judge engaged in documented ex parte communications at a rate of 44%, creating a structural pattern of bias that deprived the father of fair proceedings.

**Why Cert-Worthy:**
- **Judicial impartiality is foundational:** *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009) (due process requires recusal where probability of bias is too high)
- **Systemic pattern, not isolated incident:** 44% ex parte rate constitutes structural departure from accepted judicial proceedings (Rule 10(c))
- **No adequate state remedy:** Michigan courts did not address the structural bias claim on the merits
- **National significance:** Ex parte communications in family court are a widespread, under-addressed problem

### Issue C: PPO Weaponization in Custody Disputes — National Importance

**Constitutional Basis:** Due Process; First Amendment (speech-based PPOs); Second Amendment (firearms restrictions)

**The Issue:** Personal Protection Orders (PPOs) were used as tactical weapons in a custody dispute, issued without adequate evidentiary basis, resulting in loss of custody, housing, firearms rights, and parental access.

**Why Cert-Worthy:**
- **Intersection of multiple constitutional rights:** PPOs trigger cascading constitutional deprivations
- **National epidemic:** PPO/restraining order abuse in custody cases is documented nationwide
- **No circuit consensus:** States vary widely on procedural protections for PPO respondents in custody contexts
- **Collateral consequences are severe:** Employment, housing, NICS database entries, custody presumptions
- **Recent SCOTUS interest:** *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. ___ (2024) (Second Amendment and domestic violence restraining orders)

## 6. FILING FEE / IN FORMA PAUPERIS (IFP)

### Standard Filing:
- **Filing fee:** $300
- **Booklet format required** per Rule 33.1

### In Forma Pauperis (IFP) Option:
- **No filing fee** if IFP motion is granted
- Must file **Motion for Leave to Proceed In Forma Pauperis** with an affidavit of indigency
- **Relaxed format:** 8½ × 11 paper permitted (Rule 33.2) — no booklet required
- IFP petitions still subject to page/word limits
- File IFP motion simultaneously with petition
- Must demonstrate inability to pay and that petition is not frivolous

**Recommendation:** If financial resources are limited, strongly consider IFP. The relaxed format requirements alone save significant printing costs.
