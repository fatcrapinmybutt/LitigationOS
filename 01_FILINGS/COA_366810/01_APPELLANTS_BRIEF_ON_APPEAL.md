# IV. ARGUMENT

## A. ISSUE I: THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY SUSPENDING PARENTING TIME BASED ON AN EX PARTE MOTION WITHOUT PROPER NOTICE UNDER MCR 3.207

### Standard of Review

A trial court's decision to enter an ex parte order is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975). Compliance with court rules is reviewed de novo. *Haliw v Sterling Heights*, 471 Mich 700, 704 (2005). A trial court abuses its discretion when its decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388 (2006).

### Argument

MCR 3.207(C)(2) provides strict requirements for ex parte orders in domestic relations proceedings:

> A party may obtain an ex parte order in a domestic relations action . . . only on a showing, by verified statement of facts, that (a) immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice, or (b) the notice itself will precipitate adverse action before the order can be issued.

The August 8, 2025 ex parte order suspending all of Father's parenting time failed to satisfy either requirement. The ex parte motion did not contain a verified statement of facts demonstrating that delaying for the few days necessary to provide notice would result in immediate and irreparable injury to the child. (LCR, Ex Parte Motion, 08/08/2025.) No emergency circumstances were articulated.

Father's parenting time had been exercised under the existing order without incident. There was no allegation that L.D.W. was in imminent danger during Father's parenting time. The only basis for the ex parte motion was Mother's unverified allegations regarding Father's phone calls — allegations that did not rise to the level of immediate and irreparable injury required for ex parte relief. (LCR, Ex Parte Motion, 08/08/2025.)

The entry of five separate ex parte orders on August 8, 2025 — without any notice to Father — demonstrates a pattern that goes beyond isolated judicial error. It reflects a systematic circumvention of the notice requirements that are foundational to due process.

The ex parte order also violated MCR 3.207(C)(3), which requires that "[a]n ex parte order must state the reasons for granting the order without notice and must set a hearing date to review the order." The August 8, 2025 ex parte order did not comply with this requirement.

The trial court's entry of this ex parte order was a clear abuse of discretion and must be reversed.

## B. ISSUE II: THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED DUE PROCESS BY ISSUING SUBSTANTIVE CUSTODY ORDERS WITHOUT AN EVIDENTIARY HEARING (MCR 3.210)

### Standard of Review

Whether a party has been afforded due process is a question of law reviewed de novo. *Bonner v Brighton*, 495 Mich 209, 221 (2014). The adequacy of procedures in custody proceedings is reviewed de novo. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

### Argument

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and Mich Const 1963, art 1, § 17 guarantee that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. The Supreme Court of the United States has repeatedly recognized that a parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his or her child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972).

The Michigan Supreme Court has likewise held that "a parent has a significant interest in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children" which is protected by both the federal and state constitutions. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

The *Mathews v Eldridge* three-factor balancing test, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), governs the process that is due:

1. **The private interest affected**: Father's fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his son. This is among the most significant liberty interests recognized in our constitutional order.

2. **The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used**: The trial court entered the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment and the August 8, 2025 ex parte order without a full evidentiary hearing, without sworn testimony subject to cross-examination, without particularized findings of fact, and without articulating the applicable burden of proof. The risk of erroneous deprivation under these procedures is exceptionally high.

3. **The government's interest**: While the state has a legitimate interest in protecting children, that interest is served — not undermined — by ensuring that custody determinations are made through fair and accurate proceedings. Summary proceedings that bypass fundamental safeguards do not serve the state's interest; they defeat it.

MCR 3.210(D) requires that custody disputes be resolved through evidentiary hearing. The trial court's failure to conduct such a hearing before entering the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment — which granted sole custody to Mother and dismissed Father's six pending motions without adjudication — violated this requirement.

The August 8, 2025 ex parte order, which suspended all of Father's parenting time, was entered without any hearing at all. Father was not present, was not represented, and had no opportunity to contest the allegations against him. The combined effect of these procedural deficiencies has been the de facto termination of Father's parental relationship with L.D.W. for a period now exceeding 500 consecutive days — accomplished without the procedural protections that the constitution demands before such a deprivation can occur.

As the Michigan Court of Appeals recognized in *Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich App 142, 150 (2001), "a parent has a due process right to a hearing before being deprived of custody of his or her child." The trial court's failure to provide such a hearing requires reversal.

## C. ISSUE III: THE TRIAL COURT'S BEST-INTEREST FINDINGS WERE AGAINST THE GREAT WEIGHT OF THE EVIDENCE

### Standard of Review

This Court reviews a trial court's findings on each of the best-interest factors under MCL 722.23 under the great weight of the evidence standard. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879 (1994). A finding is against the great weight of the evidence when "the evidence clearly preponderates in the opposite direction." *Id.* Discretionary rulings are reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 507-08 (2003).

### Argument

MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve factors that the trial court "shall consider" in determining the best interests of the child in custody disputes:

> (a) The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.
> (b) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.
> (c) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care . . .
> (d) The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment . . .
> (e) The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home . . .
> (f) The moral fitness of the parties involved.
> (g) The mental and physical health of the parties involved.
> (h) The home, school, and community record of the child.
> (i) The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.
> (j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent . . .
> (k) Domestic violence . . .
> (l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant . . .

The trial court was required to make specific findings on each factor. *MCR 2.517(A)(1)-(2)*; *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 881-83. The record does not reflect that the trial court entered particularized findings on all twelve factors before entering the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment granting sole custody to Mother. (LCR, Final Judgment, 08/23/2024.)

**Factor (a) — Love, affection, and emotional ties.** The record contains evidence that Father maintained a loving bond with L.D.W. during the periods when parenting time was exercised. Father consistently sought to maintain contact with L.D.W. and expressed commitment to their relationship. The trial court's implicit finding that this factor favored Mother was against the great weight of the evidence.

**Factor (c) — Capacity to provide.** Father demonstrated his ability and willingness to provide for L.D.W.'s physical needs. The record does not contain evidence that Father was unable to provide food, clothing, medical care, or housing for the child.

**Factor (j) — Willingness to facilitate relationship.** This is the most critical factor in this case. The record is replete with evidence that Mother systematically obstructed Father's parenting time:

- March 26 – May 2, 2024: Mother withheld L.D.W. for 37 consecutive days. (LCR, Father's Motion, 05/13/2024.)
- October 20 – November 12, 2024: Mother denied Father court-ordered parenting time. (LCR, Father's Filings.)
- June 1, 2024 and September 12, 2024: Mother refused video chats during Father's off-weeks. (LCR, Father's Filings.)
- September 3, 2024: Mother planned vacation during Father's scheduled parenting time. (LCR, Father's Filings.)

Despite this extensive record of interference, the trial court failed to address parental alienation under Factor (j) and instead rewarded Mother's obstruction by granting her sole custody. This finding is against the great weight of the evidence. *See Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556, 565 (2015) (recognizing parental alienation as relevant to Factor (j)).

**MCL 722.27a(7) — Clear and Convincing Evidence.** MCL 722.27a(7) provides: "Parenting time shall be granted in specific terms . . . unless the court determines on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the mental, physical, or emotional health of the child." The trial court made no such finding on the record. The absence of this required finding renders the denial of Father's parenting time reversible error.

## D. ISSUE IV: THE TRIAL COURT ERRED IN WEAPONIZING THE PPO AS A BASIS FOR CUSTODY MODIFICATION

### Standard of Review

Statutory interpretation is reviewed de novo. *Klooster v City of Charlevoix*, 488 Mich 289, 295-96 (2011). Whether a trial court's custody determination complied with statutory requirements is reviewed de novo. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92.

### Argument

MCL 600.2950(4) expressly provides:

> "A personal protection order shall not be made a condition of release of a minor child to a parent and **shall not in any manner affect the provisions of a custody or parenting time order** issued by the circuit court under the Child Custody Act of 1970 . . . ."

(Emphasis added.)

Despite this clear statutory prohibition, the trial court permitted the PPO entered in Case No. 2023-5907-PP to function as the primary mechanism for restricting Father's custody and parenting time. The PPO was treated not as an independent protective measure but as a de facto custody order — entered without the procedural protections of the Child Custody Act, without a best-interest analysis under MCL 722.23, and without the evidentiary threshold required by MCL 722.27a(7).

The chronology demonstrates the weaponization:

1. **December 4, 2023**: PPO issued against Father. (LCR-PPO, Order, 12/04/2023.)
2. **April 11, 2024**: PPO amended ex parte — the same day custody complaint activity occurred. (LCR-PPO, Ex Parte Order, 04/11/2024; LCR, ROA, 04/11/2024.)
3. **April 29, 2024**: Ex parte order in custody case entered, citing the PPO proceeding. (LCR, Ex Parte Order, 04/29/2024.)
4. **August 23, 2024**: Final Judgment grants sole custody to Mother, with the PPO as a background factor restricting Father's rights. (LCR, Final Judgment, 08/23/2024.)
5. **August 8, 2025**: Further ex parte order suspends all parenting time. (LCR, Ex Parte Order, 08/08/2025.)

Michigan courts have recognized that PPOs cannot be used as a substitute for custody proceedings. *See Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31 (2010) (discussing the proper scope of PPOs); *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 598-99 (2004) (same). The trial court's use of the PPO to accomplish what MCL 600.2950(4) expressly forbids requires reversal.

Additionally, the PPO itself was entered without sufficient evidence meeting the standards of MCL 600.2950(1), which requires a showing that the respondent has engaged in conduct that includes "assault," "stalking," or specific threatening behavior. The allegations supporting the PPO consisted primarily of unverified claims that were not subjected to rigorous evidentiary scrutiny. *See Laffin v Laffin*, 280 Mich App 513, 517 (2008) (discussing the evidentiary requirements for PPOs).

## E. ISSUE V: THE TRIAL COURT DEMONSTRATED ACTUAL BIAS REQUIRING DISQUALIFICATION UNDER MCR 2.003(C)

### Standard of Review

A trial court's decision on a motion for disqualification is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 679 (2009). Whether a judge's conduct rises to the level requiring disqualification is reviewed de novo. *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co, Inc*, 556 US 868, 884 (2009).

### Argument

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification of a judge where:

> (a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
> (b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in Caperton v A T Massey Coal Co, Inc, or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard . . .

The record establishes actual bias through multiple categories of evidence:

### 1. Asymmetric Ex Parte Pattern

Of 55 orders entered in the custody and PPO proceedings, 24 (44%) were entered ex parte — all 24 by Judge McNeill, all restricting Father's rights. (LCR, ROA — complete analysis.) Not a single ex parte order was entered in Father's favor. This asymmetry is not the product of neutral adjudication; it is the hallmark of a predetermined outcome.

On August 8, 2025 alone, five separate ex parte orders were entered without notice to Father. (LCR, ROA, 08/08/2025.) The entry of five orders on a single day, all adverse to Father, without affording him any notice or opportunity to respond, is incompatible with the appearance — let alone the reality — of impartial justice.

### 2. Systematic Denial of Father's Motions

Father's motions were systematically denied. The August 23, 2024 Final Judgment dismissed Father's six pending motions without adjudication on the merits. (LCR, Final Judgment, 08/23/2024 ["Plaintiff's six (6) motions . . . Dismissed"].) Father filed motions for contempt, for modification of parenting time, and for other relief, all of which were denied. Meanwhile, Mother's motions — including the August 8, 2025 ex parte motion — were routinely granted.

### 3. Failure to Enforce Orders Against Mother

Despite documented violations of court-ordered parenting time by Mother — including withholding L.D.W. for 37 consecutive days (March 26 – May 2, 2024) — the trial court took no enforcement action against Mother. (LCR, ROA; Father's Motions.) This selective enforcement demonstrates the kind of asymmetric treatment that constitutes actual bias under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a).

### 4. Predetermined Outcome

The totality of the trial court's conduct is consistent with a court that reached its conclusion about Father before hearing from both sides:

- Orders entered before evidence was fully considered;
- Findings of fact unsupported by the record;
- Pattern consistent across 18+ months of proceedings.

The Supreme Court of the United States recognized in *Caperton*, 556 US at 884, that due process requires recusal where "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." The 44% ex parte rate, the systematic denial of Father's motions, and the selective enforcement of orders satisfy this standard.

*See also Grew v Knox*, 265 Mich App 333, 341 (2005) (discussing factors relevant to judicial bias); *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 84 (2017) (same).

## F. ISSUE VI: THE 44% EX PARTE CONTACT RATE CONSTITUTED STRUCTURAL DUE PROCESS VIOLATION

### Standard of Review

Structural constitutional errors are reviewed de novo. *People v Carines*, 460 Mich 750, 763 (1999). Whether a pattern of ex parte proceedings constitutes a structural due process violation is a question of law reviewed de novo.

### Argument

The Due Process Clause requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a person is deprived of a protected liberty interest. *Mullane v Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co*, 339 US 306, 314 (1950). This is not a mere procedural nicety — it is a structural requirement of the constitutional order.

The trial court's pattern of entering 44% of all orders ex parte transforms what should be isolated exceptions into a systemic practice. Ex parte orders are authorized in narrow circumstances — where "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice." MCR 3.207(C)(2). They are extraordinary measures, not standard operating procedure.

When nearly half of all orders in a case are entered ex parte, the proceedings can no longer be characterized as adversarial. They become, in substance, inquisitorial proceedings in which one party presents its version of events to the court and the court acts without ever hearing from the other side. This is antithetical to the Anglo-American tradition of fair adjudication.

The structural nature of this violation is underscored by the August 8, 2025 event: five separate ex parte orders entered on a single day, suspending all parenting time, adding restrictions, and modifying custody arrangements — all without Father's knowledge or participation. This is not the isolated exercise of emergency authority; it is the systematic exclusion of a litigant from proceedings that determine his most fundamental rights.

The cumulative effect of this structural violation cannot be remedied by reviewing individual orders in isolation. Each ex parte order compounded the harm of those that preceded it, creating a cascade of adverse rulings that Father could not challenge in real time because he did not know they existed. The remedy for a structural due process violation of this magnitude is reversal and reassignment to a different judge. *See In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App at 680-81 (discussing circumstances warranting reassignment).
