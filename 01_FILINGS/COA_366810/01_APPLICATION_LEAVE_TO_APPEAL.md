# IV. ARGUMENT

## A. This Case Presents Issues of Exceptional Public Importance

### 1. Structural Due Process Failure

The pattern documented in this case is not an isolated judicial error — it is a structural failure in due process protections. When 44% of all orders in a custody proceeding are entered ex parte, the proceeding ceases to be adversarial and becomes inquisitorial. This Court's guidance is essential to establish constitutional minimum standards for ex parte practice in family courts statewide.

The United States Supreme Court has recognized that "[t]he fundamental requirement of due process is the opportunity to be heard 'at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner.'" *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976) (quoting *Armstrong v Manzo*, 380 US 545, 552 (1965)). When nearly half of all orders are entered without notice or opportunity to be heard, the constitutional guarantee is rendered meaningless.

### 2. Fundamental Liberty Interest at Stake

The parent-child relationship is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The Michigan Supreme Court has likewise recognized the "significant interest" a parent has in "the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children." *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

The complete severance of Father's parenting time for 571+ days — accomplished without a single evidentiary hearing with sworn testimony — implicates this fundamental liberty interest in its most extreme form. This is not a case of reduced parenting time or modified schedules; it is a total elimination of the parent-child relationship through procedural mechanisms that bypass constitutional safeguards.

### 3. Systemic Pattern Requiring This Court's Intervention

The judicial conduct at issue reveals a pattern that the Court of Appeals cannot adequately address:

- **Statistical anomaly**: A 44% ex parte rate is, on information and belief, far outside the norm for Michigan custody proceedings.
- **Concentrated abuse**: Five ex parte orders on a single day demonstrates a deliberate strategy, not judicial economy.
- **Asymmetric treatment**: Systematic granting of Mother's motions while denying Father's motions indicates actual bias.
- **No self-correction**: The trial court has had multiple opportunities to correct course and has declined each time.

This pattern requires this Court's exercise of its constitutional authority to ensure that Michigan's family courts comply with constitutional minimum standards.

### 4. The Court of Appeals Cannot Provide Adequate Relief

Even if the Court of Appeals reverses the specific orders at issue, the systemic problems will persist:

- The trial court will retain jurisdiction over the ongoing custody matter.
- The same judge will continue to preside absent disqualification.
- The pattern of ex parte orders can resume.
- No precedent will be established to prevent similar conduct in other courts.

Only this Court can establish binding precedent that: (a) defines constitutional minimum standards for ex parte practice in family courts, (b) establishes when a pattern of asymmetric treatment constitutes actual bias, and (c) provides effective remedies for structural due process violations.

## B. Constitutional Analysis

### 1. Fourteenth Amendment — Substantive Due Process

The substantive due process claim rests on the fundamental liberty interest in parental rights. Under strict scrutiny — applicable because a fundamental right is at issue — the state must demonstrate that its action is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling government interest. *Washington v Glucksberg*, 521 US 702, 721 (1997).

No evidence in the record demonstrates that Father posed any threat to L.D.W. requiring complete severance of parenting time. The trial court's action was not narrowly tailored — supervised parenting time, therapeutic reunification, or other less restrictive alternatives were available but not seriously considered.

### 2. Fourteenth Amendment — Procedural Due Process

Under *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976):

- **Private interest**: Fundamental liberty interest in parental companionship — the weightiest interest recognized in constitutional law.
- **Risk of erroneous deprivation**: Exceptionally high — no evidentiary hearing, no sworn testimony, no cross-examination, no particularized findings.
- **Government interest**: Legitimate interest in child welfare is served, not undermined, by fair proceedings.

All three factors overwhelmingly favor Father.

### 3. Equal Protection

Father, a pro se father, received systematically different treatment than Mother, who was represented by counsel. This differential treatment implicates both gender-based and status-based equal protection concerns under rational basis review at minimum. The pattern of asymmetric treatment cannot be explained by legitimate judicial discretion.
