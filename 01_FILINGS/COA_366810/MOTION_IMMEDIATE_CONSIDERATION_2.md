**NOW COMES** Plaintiff-Appellant Andrew J. Pigors, appearing *pro se*, and respectfully moves this Honorable Court pursuant to MCR 7.211(C)(6) for an Order granting immediate consideration of the pending Application for Leave to Appeal, and in support thereof states as follows:

---

### I. INTRODUCTION

1. This is an emergency motion requesting that the Michigan Court of Appeals expedite its review of Plaintiff-Appellant's Application for Leave to Appeal from orders of the 14th Circuit Court, Family Division, Muskegon County (Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, presiding).

2. The orders below have resulted in the **complete termination** of Father's parenting time with his three-year-old son, Lincoln James Pigors (DOB: November 9, 2022). Father has been denied ALL contact with his child for **329+ consecutive days** and counting.

3. Each day that passes without appellate review causes **irreparable developmental harm** to a child in the critical attachment period of early childhood. The normal appellate timeline — measured in months or years — would cause further irreversible damage to the parent-child bond that this Court has the power to prevent.

4. MCR 7.211(C)(6) authorizes this Court to grant immediate consideration when "the delay caused by the normal briefing schedule would cause a party to suffer substantial harm."

---

### II. JURISDICTION

5. This Court has jurisdiction pursuant to MCR 7.203(B) (appeals from circuit court orders in domestic relations actions) and Const 1963, Art 6, § 10 (appellate jurisdiction of the Court of Appeals).

6. The Application for Leave to Appeal is currently pending before this Court, COA Case No. 366810, arising from the 14th Circuit Court, LC No. 2024-001507-DC.

---

### III. FACTS ESTABLISHING EMERGENCY

#### A. Complete Severance of Parent-Child Relationship

7. Father's parenting time with his son has been systematically eliminated:
   - **Pre-July 2024**: 50/50 equal parenting time (~182.5 overnights/year);
   - **July 2024**: Reduced to 79 overnights/year;
   - **August 2025**: Reduced to ZERO overnights;
   - **August 2025 – Present**: Complete denial — **329+ consecutive days** without any contact.

8. The lower court awarded Mother **sole physical custody AND sole legal custody**, and granted "Defendant's Objection to equal parenting time."

9. Father has not seen, spoken to, held, or had any form of contact with his three-year-old son in over 329 days. Lincoln has been deprived of his father during the most critical years of his development.

#### B. The Child Is Three Years Old — Critical Attachment Period

10. Lincoln James Pigors was born on November 9, 2022. He is now approximately three years and three months old. He has spent more than **one-quarter of his entire life** without any contact with his father.

11. Developmental psychology research consistently establishes that the period from birth to age five is the most critical period for attachment formation. Secure attachment to both parents during this period is essential for:
   - Emotional regulation development;
   - Cognitive development;
   - Social competence;
   - Long-term mental health outcomes;
   - The child's capacity to form healthy relationships throughout life.

12. Prolonged separation from an attachment figure during this period causes measurable, documented developmental harm that becomes increasingly difficult to reverse with the passage of time. Each additional day of separation compounds the harm.

13. At three years old, Lincoln is at the stage where he is forming his understanding of permanence, trust, and family relationships. A 329+ day absence of his father during this period may cause him to conclude that his father has abandoned him — a belief that, once formed, can be extraordinarily difficult to correct even with later reunification.

#### C. Irreparable Harm That Cannot Be Remedied by Normal Appellate Timeline

14. The normal appellate timeline for an Application for Leave to Appeal can extend six months to a year or more. If the pending Application is processed on the normal schedule:
   - Lincoln will be approaching his **fourth birthday** before any appellate review occurs;
   - The period of father-child separation will exceed **500+ days**;
   - The child will have spent approximately **one-third of his life** without his father;
   - The developmental harm will have progressed from severe to potentially permanent.

15. Unlike most appellate matters, where monetary or property interests can be restored after reversal, **lost time with a developing child cannot be restored**. There is no appellate remedy for missed milestones, lost attachment, and developmental trauma. Every day of delay is a day that can never be recovered.

16. This Court's intervention is the only mechanism available to prevent further irreparable harm. The lower court has shown no inclination to reconsider its orders, having imposed a $250 sanction and stated "Do not file any more, I will not look at it" when Father attempted to seek relief below.

### IV. THE APPLICATION RAISES SUBSTANTIAL QUESTIONS

17. The Application for Leave to Appeal raises substantial questions warranting this Court's review, including:

18. **Due Process — Ex Parte Suspension of Parental Rights**: The lower court suspended Father's parenting time through ex parte proceedings without the notice, hearing, or clear and convincing evidence required by the 14th Amendment and MCL 722.27a(3). Analysis of the record reveals **52 separate ex parte orders** — a 44% ex parte rate.

19. **Evidentiary Error — Admission of Illegally Obtained Recording**: The lower court admitted a USB recording as the sole basis for suspending Father's parenting time without authentication testimony (MRE 901), despite the recording being made in violation of Michigan's two-party consent wiretapping statute, MCL 750.539 (a felony). See *People v Bednarz*, 360 Mich 552; 104 NW2d 405 (1960); *People v Burge*, 722 NW2d 470 (2006).

20. **Brady Violation — Secret Mental Health Evaluation**: The lower court obtained a second mental health evaluation of Father without his knowledge, authorization, or consent, and used it in decision-making without disclosure. See *Brady v Maryland*, 373 US 83 (1963).

21. **Inadequate Service — Zero Business Days**: Father was served on Friday, August 8, 2025, for a Monday, August 11, 2025 hearing — providing zero business days to prepare, in violation of MCR 2.119/2.305 (minimum 5 business days). This was the fourth documented instance of inadequate service.

22. **No Findings of Fact**: The lower court failed to issue written findings of fact or conclusions of law as required by MCR 3.210(D). See *Woodworth v Woodworth*, 337 Mich 187; 59 NW2d 169 (1953).

23. **Judicial Bias**: The presiding judge pre-reviewed evidence before Father was served ("Yes, I had my staff listen to it"), used stigmatizing language from the bench, imposed an unauthorized $250 filing sanction, and barred Father from filing further motions. See MCR 2.003; *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009).

24. **Abuse of Discretion**: The lower court's complete elimination of Father's parenting time falls "outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes." See *Mallett v Mallett*, 161 Mich App 589; 411 NW2d 105 (1987); *Tremblay v Tremblay*, 185 Mich App 244; 461 NW2d 260 (1990).

---

### V. LEGAL STANDARD FOR IMMEDIATE CONSIDERATION

25. MCR 7.211(C)(6) provides that a party may file a motion for immediate consideration when "the delay caused by the normal briefing schedule would cause a party to suffer substantial harm."

26. The standard is met here because:
   - **Substantial harm is occurring daily**: Each day without appellate review is another day of parent-child separation during the critical attachment period;
   - **The harm is irreparable**: Lost developmental time cannot be restored by later appellate relief;
   - **The harm is to a child**: Three-year-old Lincoln is the most vulnerable party in this litigation and has no voice of his own;
   - **Normal timelines are inadequate**: Months or years of additional separation would make any eventual appellate relief meaningless;
   - **The lower court has foreclosed alternative relief**: The $250 sanction and "do not file" directive prevent Father from seeking relief below.

27. Michigan appellate courts have recognized that cases involving the welfare of minor children and the fundamental right to parent merit expedited consideration. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000) (parental rights are among the oldest fundamental liberty interests recognized by the Supreme Court).
