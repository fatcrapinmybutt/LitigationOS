## ARGUMENT

### I. THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY MODIFYING THE ESTABLISHED CUSTODIAL ENVIRONMENT WITHOUT CLEAR AND CONVINCING EVIDENCE

#### A. Standard of Review

Custody decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 499, 508; 675 NW2d 847 (2003). An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Id.* Findings of fact underlying a custody determination are reviewed for clear error, and questions of law are reviewed de novo. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 91; 782 NW2d 480 (2010).

#### B. Issue

Whether the trial court abused its discretion by granting sole legal and sole physical custody to Mother in the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment without first determining whether an established custodial environment existed and without requiring clear and convincing evidence to support the modification.

#### C. Rule

Under MCL 722.27(1)(c), a trial court may not modify an established custodial environment ("ECE") unless "there is presented clear and convincing evidence that it is in the best interest of the child." An ECE is established when "over an appreciable time the child naturally looks to the custodian in that environment for guidance, discipline, the necessities of life, and parental comfort." MCL 722.27(1)(c). The custodial environment of a child is established "if the child is content, comfortable, and secure in a given home." *Foskett v Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1, 8; 634 NW2d 363 (2001).

The ECE determination is a "threshold issue" that must be resolved before the court applies the best interest factors. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92. If the proposed custody modification would change an ECE, the burden of proof is elevated from preponderance of the evidence to clear and convincing evidence. MCL 722.27(1)(c). Without this threshold determination, all subsequent custody findings are legally infirm.

A court must evaluate whether an ECE exists in both parents' homes, as an ECE may exist with both parents simultaneously. *Foskett*, 247 Mich App at 8-9. When a child has an ECE with both parents and the court awards sole custody to one parent, that constitutes a modification of the ECE with the other parent, triggering the clear and convincing evidence standard. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 509-510.

#### D. Application

Here, Lincoln had an established custodial environment with Father from birth (2022) through the commencement of litigation. Father was actively involved in Lincoln's day-to-day care, provided guidance, discipline, and parental comfort, and maintained a stable home environment where Lincoln was content and secure. (R. at Complaint; R. at Docket Events, passim.)

The August 23, 2024 Final Judgment granted sole legal and sole physical custody to Mother, eliminating Father's custodial role entirely except for limited parenting time. (R. at Final Judgment, 8/23/2024.) This constituted a modification of the established custodial environment with Father, triggering the clear and convincing evidence standard under MCL 722.27(1)(c).

The trial court's order does not contain a threshold determination of whether an ECE existed with either or both parents. The order does not state the applicable burden of proof. The order does not make a finding that clear and convincing evidence supported the modification. This is precisely the type of error identified in *Pierron*, where the Michigan Supreme Court held that "the trial court erred by failing to determine the appropriate evidentiary burden before undertaking the best interest analysis." 486 Mich at 93.

Moreover, the record reflects that the Defendant "submitted an order under the 7 day rule, using parts of the rulings the Court made regarding the best interest factors." (R. at Order, 8/23/2024.) This suggests that even the written order entered was not the court's own product, but was drafted by the opposing party and adopted without independent judicial consideration of the ECE threshold.

The trial court's failure to conduct the required *Pierron* analysis and apply the correct evidentiary standard constitutes an abuse of discretion that requires reversal. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 510; *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23; 490 NW2d 568 (1992).

#### E. Conclusion

The trial court abused its discretion by granting sole custody to Mother without first determining whether an established custodial environment existed and without applying the heightened clear and convincing evidence standard required by MCL 722.27(1)(c). The custody order must be vacated and the matter remanded for proceedings applying the correct legal framework.

### II. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED APPELLANT'S PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS RIGHTS BY ENTERING EX PARTE ORDERS WITHOUT NOTICE OR HEARING

#### A. Standard of Review

Constitutional questions, including due process claims, are reviewed de novo. *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 191; 680 NW2d 835 (2004).

#### B. Issue

Whether the trial court's entry of the August 8, 2025 ex parte order suspending all parenting time, without prior notice to Father or an opportunity to be heard, violated the Due Process Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment and Mich Const 1963, Art 1, Sec 13.

#### C. Rule

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that no State shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." US Const Amend XIV, Sec 1. The Michigan Constitution similarly provides: "No person shall be . . . deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law." Mich Const 1963, Art 1, Sec 17.

A parent's right to the care, custody, and management of his or her children is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65; 120 S Ct 2054; 147 L Ed 2d 49 (2000). Because parental rights are fundamental, government interference with those rights must satisfy heightened procedural protections.

MCR 3.207(B) governs ex parte orders in domestic relations cases and imposes specific requirements: an ex parte order may be entered only upon a showing of "specific facts shown by a verified statement or affidavit" demonstrating that "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice" or that "notice itself will precipitate adverse action before an order can be issued." MCR 3.207(B)(1)-(5). The rule further requires that any ex parte order must schedule a hearing within 14 days and that the affected party be served immediately. MCR 3.207(B)(4).

#### D. Application

On August 8, 2025, the trial court entered an ex parte order suspending all of Father's parenting time based solely on Mother's unverified allegations. (R. at Ex Parte Order, 8/8/2025.) The order completely eliminated Father's contact with his three-year-old son. This action was taken without any of the procedural safeguards required by MCR 3.207(B):

**1. No notice.** Father was not notified of Mother's ex parte motion before the order was entered. Father arrived for his regularly scheduled parenting exchange only to discover that his time had been suspended. (R. at Docket Events, 8/8/2025.) MCR 3.207(B) requires that if notice is dispensed with, the court must make specific findings as to why notice itself would "precipitate adverse action." No such finding appears in the record.

**2. No verified factual basis.** The ex parte motion did not contain the "specific facts shown by a verified statement or affidavit" required by MCR 3.207(B)(1). The motion "did not set out specific, immediate, irreparable harm as required for ex parte relief." (R. at Ex Parte Motion, 8/8/2025.) The court relied on unsworn allegations to deprive a parent of all contact with his child.

**3. No immediate and irreparable injury finding.** The court did not make the required finding that "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice." MCR 3.207(B)(1). The ex parte motion "fails to allege that delaying for notice to Father would cause immediate and irreparable injury." (R. at Ex Parte Motion Analysis.)

**4. Indefinite suspension without timely hearing.** While MCR 3.207(B)(4) requires a hearing within 14 days of entry of an ex parte order, the record reflects that the suspension persisted for months before substantive hearings occurred. The evidentiary hearing was not held until September 4, 2025, nearly a month after the ex parte order. Even after hearings on September 4, October 29, and November 26, 2025, the suspension was never lifted. (R. at ROA, pp. 5-6.)

**5. Pattern of ex parte handling.** This was not an isolated incident. The record reflects that the April 11, 2024 PPO was amended ex parte, the April 29, 2024 custody order was entered ex parte, and the July 1, 2024 emergency order granting sole custody was entered ex parte. (R. at ROA, pp. 2-4.) Evidence, including a USB recording and mental health evaluation, was handled "off the record and ex parte, contrary to the prohibition on ex parte communications about pending matters." (R. at Contradiction Map.)

The combination of (a) ex parte submissions, (b) refusal to review Father's exculpatory photographs, (c) willingness to accept Mother's unsworn allegations, and (d) repeated denial of Father's right to be heard, constitutes a systematic deprivation of procedural due process. The United States Supreme Court has long held that "the right to be heard before being condemned to suffer grievous loss of any kind, even though it may not involve the stigma and hardships of a criminal conviction, is a principle basic to our society." *Goldberg v Kelly*, 397 US 254, 263 (1970).

#### E. Conclusion

The trial court violated Appellant's due process rights by entering the August 8, 2025 ex parte order without notice, without a verified factual basis, without a finding of immediate and irreparable harm, and without a timely hearing. The ex parte order and all subsequent orders continuing the suspension must be vacated. This Court should order immediate restoration of parenting time pending proper proceedings on remand.

### III. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY FAILING TO MAKE REQUIRED FINDINGS OF FACT ON THE MCL 722.23 BEST INTEREST FACTORS

#### A. Standard of Review

Findings of fact in custody cases are reviewed for clear error. MCR 2.517(A)(1). The absence of required findings constitutes clear error per se. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92; 782 NW2d 480 (2010). Whether the trial court applied the correct legal standard is reviewed de novo. *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 191; 680 NW2d 835 (2004).

#### B. Issue

Whether the trial court committed reversible error by entering the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment and subsequent orders without making specific written findings on each of the twelve statutory best interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)-(l).

#### C. Rule

MCL 722.23 requires the court to "consider, evaluate, and determine" the following twelve factors in all custody proceedings: (a) the love, affection, and emotional ties between the parties and the child; (b) capacity to give love, affection, and guidance; (c) capacity to provide food, clothing, and medical care; (d) length of time in a stable, satisfactory environment; (e) permanence as a family unit; (f) moral fitness; (g) mental and physical health; (h) home, school, and community record; (i) reasonable preference of the child; (j) willingness to facilitate a close and continuing parent-child relationship with the other parent; (k) domestic violence; and (l) any other relevant factor.

MCR 2.517(A)(1) mandates: "In actions tried on the facts without a jury or with an advisory jury, the court shall find the facts specially, state separately its conclusions of law, and direct entry of the appropriate judgment." In custody cases, this requirement is particularly stringent. The trial court must make "specific findings on each of the statutory best interest factors." *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that a "custody order that lacks specific factual findings on the statutory best interest factors is necessarily based on clear error." *Id.* at 93.

*Kubicki v Sharpe*, 306 Mich App 525, 540; 858 NW2d 57 (2014), reaffirmed that "findings of fact must be made on each of the best interest factors" and that "[f]ailure to do so constitutes clear error." *Sparks v Sparks*, 440 Mich 141, 159; 485 NW2d 893 (1992), established that each factor must be addressed on the record.

#### D. Application

The August 23, 2024 Final Judgment does not contain specific written findings on each of the twelve MCL 722.23 factors. The record reflects that the court "rendered an oral opinion" and that the written order was submitted by Defendant's counsel "under the 7 day rule, using parts of the rulings the Court made regarding the best interest factors." (R. at Order, 8/23/2024, p. 2.)

This admission demonstrates that the court's oral findings were selectively incorporated into a written order drafted by one party, rather than the court issuing its own comprehensive written findings. When Father objected, the court found Father "failed to follow the Court rules, in stating with specificity what parts of the order he objected to." (R. at Order, 8/23/2024.) This procedural ruling does not cure the underlying deficiency of the court's failure to make independent findings.

The following specific deficiencies are evident in the record:

**Factor (a) - Love, affection, emotional ties:** The record contains extensive evidence of Father's bond with Lincoln, including Lincoln clinging to Father during exchanges (R. at 10/20/2024 events) and referring to Father with terms of endearment. The court's order does not contain a specific finding on this factor addressing Father's relationship with the child.

**Factor (c) - Capacity to provide:** The record reflects Father maintained employment and stable housing. The court made no specific finding regarding Father's capacity to provide for Lincoln's basic needs.

**Factor (d) - Stable environment:** The record shows Father maintained a home at 1977 Whitehall Rd, Lot 17, Muskegon throughout the litigation. No specific finding addresses the stability of Father's environment.

**Factor (j) - Willingness to facilitate relationship:** MCL 722.23(j) requires the court to evaluate each parent's willingness to facilitate a close and continuing relationship with the other parent. The record overwhelmingly supports that Mother undermined Father's relationship with Lincoln through extended withholding periods (37 days from March-May 2024; 23 days in October-November 2024; 329+ days from July 2025), denial of information about medical appointments, and refusal of video communication during off-weeks. (R. at Docket Events, passim.) No finding addresses this critical factor as applied to Mother's conduct.

**Factor (l) - Other factors:** The court failed to consider the impact of the PPO litigation on the custody determination, the role of third parties (Albert Watson, Cody Watson) in disrupting Father's parenting time, or the cumulative effect of repeated ex parte orders on the parent-child relationship.

The post-judgment orders of May 16, 2025, May 20, 2025, and August 8, 2025 similarly lack required best interest findings. The May 20, 2025 order denying Father's custody motion stated only that "Nothing in Plaintiff's Motion indicates a change of circumstances as required by law." (R. at Order, 5/20/2025.) Even if the *Vodvarka* threshold of changed circumstances were not met, the court's failure to address the ongoing 329+ day separation as a changed circumstance is itself clearly erroneous.

#### E. Conclusion

The trial court committed clear error by entering custody orders without making specific findings on each of the twelve MCL 722.23(a)-(l) best interest factors. Under *Pierron*, this alone requires vacatur and remand for proper findings. The matter must be remanded to a different judge for a de novo custody hearing with complete best interest factor analysis.

### IV. THE $250 FILING SANCTION UNCONSTITUTIONALLY DENIED ACCESS TO THE COURTS

#### A. Standard of Review

Constitutional challenges to court orders are reviewed de novo.

#### B. Issue

Whether the trial court's May 16, 2025 order imposing a $250-per-motion filing bond unconstitutionally restricted Appellant's right of access to the courts.

#### C. Rule

The right of access to the courts is protected by both the federal and state constitutions. The First Amendment to the United States Constitution, as applied to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment, protects the right to petition the government for redress of grievances, which includes the right to file civil actions. US Const Amend I, XIV. The Michigan Constitution expressly provides: "A suitor in any court of this state has the right to prosecute or defend his suit, either in his own proper person or by an attorney." Mich Const 1963, Art 1, Sec 13. Additionally, no person may be "deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." Mich Const 1963, Art 1, Sec 17.

While courts possess inherent authority to manage their dockets and impose reasonable restrictions on vexatious litigants, any restriction on court access must satisfy constitutional scrutiny. A filing restriction must be: (1) based on a finding that the party's prior filings were frivolous, harassing, or duplicative; (2) narrowly tailored to prevent the specific abuse identified; and (3) accompanied by a mechanism to permit meritorious filings. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 101; 413 NW2d 392 (1987).

#### D. Application

On May 16, 2025, the trial court ordered: "the Plaintiff must pay a bond of $250 (for each motion) before he may file another motion in this matter. The Court will then review the motion." (R. at Order, 5/16/2025.)

This filing bond is unconstitutional for several reasons:

**1. No finding of frivolousness.** The court imposed the $250 bond without making any finding that Father's prior motions were frivolous, harassing, duplicative, or filed in bad faith. The court did not identify a single specific filing that warranted sanctions. Without such a finding, the restriction lacks the factual predicate required to justify limiting court access.

**2. Disproportionate burden on a pro se litigant.** Father is a self-represented litigant in a custody dispute involving the welfare of his minor child. A $250-per-motion bond imposes a substantial financial barrier on a party whose child support obligations and litigation expenses have already strained his resources. The bond effectively prices a pro se parent out of the courtroom, particularly when multiple motions may be necessary to address ongoing custody and parenting time concerns.

**3. Not narrowly tailored.** The bond applies to every motion without distinction. It makes no exception for emergency motions concerning child safety, motions addressing due process violations, or motions required by court rule. A parent who discovers his child is in imminent danger would be required to first pay $250 before the court would even consider the motion. This blanket restriction fails the narrow tailoring requirement.

**4. No mechanism for meritorious filings.** The order provides that "The Court will then review the motion" after the bond is paid, but provides no mechanism for a party who cannot afford the bond to demonstrate that a proposed filing has merit. There is no procedure for seeking fee waiver or for the court to review a filing on its merits before requiring payment.

**5. Context of fundamental parental rights.** This filing bond was imposed in the context of custody litigation where Father's parenting time was being progressively reduced and eventually eliminated entirely. The bond was imposed on the very same date (May 16, 2025) that additional orders restricted Father's parenting rights. (R. at Order, 5/16/2025.) The practical effect was to prevent Father from seeking judicial relief during the critical period when his parenting time was being suspended. By August 8, 2025, Father's parenting time was eliminated entirely by ex parte order, and the filing bond ensured Father could not challenge this without first paying a $250 toll.

#### E. Conclusion

The $250 filing bond imposed on May 16, 2025 is unconstitutional as applied. It was imposed without a finding of frivolousness, is not narrowly tailored, provides no mechanism for meritorious filings, and effectively denies a pro se parent access to the courts during a period when his fundamental parental rights are being eliminated. The order must be vacated.

### V. THE TRIAL COURT DEMONSTRATED ACTUAL BIAS REQUIRING DISQUALIFICATION UNDER MCR 2.003(C)(1)

#### A. Standard of Review

A trial court's denial of a motion for disqualification is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 325; 729 NW2d 533 (2006). However, the standard for assessing judicial bias incorporates an objective component: MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification where, "based on objective and reasonable perceptions," there is "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party," as articulated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009).

#### B. Issue

Whether the cumulative record of the trial court's conduct demonstrates actual bias or a serious risk of actual bias requiring disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

#### C. Rule

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that disqualification is warranted for reasons "that include, but are not limited to" the following:

>(a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
>
>(b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

The objective standard adopted by the Michigan Supreme Court in amending MCR 2.003 requires that the court assess whether "a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would harbor a legitimate doubt about the judge's impartiality." *Shulick*, 273 Mich App at 327. Bias may be demonstrated through a pattern of conduct that, viewed cumulatively, reveals favoritism or prejudice. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879-880; 526 NW2d 889 (1994).

#### D. Application

The record reflects a persistent pattern of disparate treatment that, viewed cumulatively, demonstrates actual bias or at minimum a serious risk of actual bias:

**1. Muting and silencing Appellant.** At multiple hearings throughout the litigation, Father was muted, cut off mid-objection, or told the judge would not review further filings. (R. at Tr., passim; R. at Judicial Violations.) The court's own statements acknowledge warning Father "repeatedly" about "this behavior," but the pattern of silencing a pro se litigant, while allowing opposing counsel to present argument, reflects disparate treatment rather than neutral courtroom management.

**2. Acceptance of ex parte submissions and communications.** The record documents that evidence was received "off the record and ex parte, contrary to the prohibition on ex parte communications about pending matters." (R. at Contradiction Map.) A USB recording and mental health evaluation were handled outside Father's presence. Canon 3 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct bars ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters except under narrow circumstances not present here.

**3. Refusal to consider exculpatory evidence.** When Father attempted to present photographs from the July 29, 2025 parenting exchange that would have contradicted Mother's allegations, the court refused to consider them. (R. at Contradiction Map.) A court that refuses to consider evidence favorable to one party while accepting unsworn allegations from the other party cannot be said to be operating impartially.

**4. Reliance on unsworn allegations to deprive parental rights.** The August 8, 2025 ex parte order suspending all parenting time "was based solely on allegations and unsworn evidence." (R. at Docket Events, 8/8/2025.) The court's willingness to strip a parent of all contact with his child based on unverified claims, while refusing to consider the parent's contradicting evidence, reflects a predisposition against Father.

**5. Contempt finding and disproportionate punishment.** The record reflects that when Mother spoke, Father "was not able to speak without his interruption" and "the Court found him in contempt and sentenced him to jail." (R. at Evidence Quotes.) While interrupting court proceedings is inappropriate, the escalation to a jail sentence for courtroom disruptions by a pro se litigant, in the context of a custody dispute where the litigant's child was being taken from him, reflects a punitive orientation rather than neutral judicial management.

**6. Adoption of opposing party's proposed order.** The court's Final Judgment was prepared by Defendant's counsel "under the 7 day rule, using parts of the rulings the Court made regarding the best interest factors." (R. at Order, 8/23/2024.) When Father objected, the court dismissed the objection on procedural grounds. The court's wholesale adoption of one party's proposed order, while rejecting the other party's attempts to be heard, demonstrates structural bias in the adjudicatory process.

**7. $250 filing bond to prevent challenge.** The imposition of a $250-per-motion bond on the same date as other restrictive orders created a structural barrier preventing Father from challenging the court's actions. This is consistent with a pattern of insulating the court's rulings from scrutiny.

Taken individually, some of these actions might fall within the range of judicial discretion. Taken cumulatively, they reveal a consistent pattern: Mother's filings are accepted without scrutiny, her ex parte motions are granted without verification, and her proposed orders are adopted verbatim; Father's motions are summarily denied, his evidence is refused, his voice is literally muted, and financial barriers are erected to prevent further challenge. A reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would harbor more than a "legitimate doubt" about the court's impartiality. *Shulick*, 273 Mich App at 327.

#### E. Conclusion

The cumulative record demonstrates actual bias or a serious risk of actual bias requiring disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1). This Court should order that all proceedings on remand be conducted before a different judge.

### VI. THE COMPLETE DENIAL OF PARENTING TIME FOR 329+ DAYS WITHOUT A FINDING OF ENDANGERMENT VIOLATED MCL 722.27a

#### A. Standard of Review

Parenting time decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21; 805 NW2d 1 (2010). A trial court abuses its discretion when its decision is "so palpably and grossly violative of fact and logic that it evidences a perversity of will, a defiance of judgment, or the exercise of passion or bias." *Id.* at 26.

#### B. Issue

Whether the trial court abused its discretion by suspending all parenting time between Father and Lincoln for 329+ consecutive days without making the statutorily required finding that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health.

#### C. Rule

MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time: "It is presumed to be in the best interests of a child for the child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents." MCL 722.27a(1) further provides: "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." (Emphasis added.)

The complete denial of parenting time is governed by MCL 722.27a(3), which permits a court to deny parenting time only if the court determines that "there is clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health to have parenting time with a parent." This elevated standard reflects the Legislature's recognition that the complete severance of a parent-child relationship is among the most drastic actions a court can take, second only to termination of parental rights.

*Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 22-23 (2010), held that a trial court abused its discretion by restricting parenting time without making the required findings. The Court of Appeals emphasized that "the strongest relationship possible with both parents" is the statutory goal, and that restrictions must be supported by specific findings on the record.

*Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich App 142, 150-151 (2001), held that a court may not suspend parenting time without a specific finding that such time would endanger the child. The Court reversed the trial court's parenting time restriction where "the trial court made NO such finding."

MCL 722.27a(7) provides that when safety concerns exist, the court may impose conditions on parenting time, including supervision, rather than eliminating parenting time entirely. This intermediate option demonstrates the Legislature's intent that total suspension be reserved for the most extreme circumstances.

#### D. Application

Father's last parenting time with Lincoln was July 29, 2025. (R. at Docket Events, 7/29/2025.) As of the filing of this brief, Father and Lincoln have been separated for over 329 consecutive days. During this entire period:

**1. No endangerment finding.** The trial court has never made a finding, on the record, by clear and convincing evidence, that parenting time with Father would endanger Lincoln's physical, mental, or emotional health. MCL 722.27a(3). The August 8, 2025 ex parte order suspended parenting time without any such finding. The subsequent evidentiary hearings on September 4, October 29, and November 26, 2025, did not produce a written finding that parenting time with Father would endanger the child. (R. at ROA, pp. 5-6.)

**2. No clear and convincing evidence standard applied.** The record does not reflect that the court applied the "clear and convincing evidence" standard required by MCL 722.27a(1) and (3). The court's reliance on Mother's unsworn allegations and the HealthWest assessments, without conducting the required evidentiary analysis, fails to satisfy this heightened burden.

**3. No consideration of alternatives to total suspension.** MCL 722.27a(7) permits the court to impose conditions on parenting time, including supervised visitation, therapeutic visitation, restrictions on location or duration, or other reasonable conditions. The trial court imposed no such conditions. Instead, the court opted for the most extreme remedy available, a complete elimination of all contact between Father and child, without considering or addressing less restrictive alternatives. The court never ordered supervised visitation as an intermediate step. The court never ordered therapeutic reunification. The court never imposed conditions that would permit some contact while addressing any legitimate safety concerns.

**4. Duration and harm of separation.** A 329+ day separation between a father and his young child (born 2022, approximately 3-4 years old at the time of separation) causes profound developmental and psychological harm. At this critical developmental stage, the child's attachment bonds, sense of security, and emotional development are directly impacted by the complete absence of a parent. The longer the separation persists, the more difficult reunification becomes, and the more the separation itself becomes a self-fulfilling justification for continued restriction. Every day that passes deepens the alienation between Father and child.

The record reflects that before the suspension, Lincoln had a bonded relationship with Father, including "clinging to Father" during the October 20, 2024 exchange when Albert Watson forcibly removed the child. (R. at Docket Events, 10/20/2024.) Lincoln would refer to Father with terms of endearment. The child's own behavior demonstrated a desire for continued contact with Father.

**5. Withholding pattern predating the court order.** The complete suspension since August 2025 is the culmination of a pattern of increasingly extended periods of separation: 37 consecutive days from March 26 through May 2, 2024; 23 consecutive days from October 20 through November 12, 2024; and now 329+ consecutive days beginning July 29, 2025. (R. at Docket Events, passim.) This escalating pattern, largely driven by Mother's conduct and the court's acquiescence, violates the statutory presumption that parenting time is in the child's best interest. MCL 722.27a(1).

**6. Mother's conduct under MCL 722.23(j).** The trial court's facilitation of the 329+ day separation, without addressing Mother's role in obstructing Father's parenting time, effectively rewards the very conduct that MCL 722.23(j) is designed to penalize. Factor (j) requires the court to consider each parent's "willingness and ability . . . to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." Mother's pattern of withholding, denial of communication, and filing of ex parte motions to suspend parenting time demonstrates a sustained unwillingness to facilitate the Father-child relationship.

#### E. Conclusion

The trial court abused its discretion by imposing a complete denial of all parenting time for 329+ consecutive days without making the required finding, by clear and convincing evidence, that parenting time would endanger the child. The suspension violates MCL 722.27a(1) and (3), contravenes the statutory presumption favoring parenting time, and ignores available alternatives under MCL 722.27a(7). This Court should order immediate restoration of parenting time, at minimum supervised visitation, pending a proper hearing on remand.
