# IV. ARGUMENT

## ARGUMENT I

## THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED FATHER'S CONSTITUTIONAL DUE PROCESS RIGHTS BY TERMINATING ALL PARENTING TIME FOR 329+ DAYS WITHOUT AN EVIDENTIARY HEARING

### A. Standard of Review

Constitutional questions, including due process claims, are reviewed **de novo**. *In re Sanders*, 259 Mich App 499, 507 (2003). This Court owes no deference to the trial court's conclusions on questions of constitutional law. The application and interpretation of court rules and statutes are likewise reviewed de novo. *Pierron v Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222, 227 (2009).

### B. The Fundamental Right to Parent

The right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is among the oldest and most fundamental liberty interests recognized by the United States Supreme Court. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *Meyer v Nebraska*, 262 US 390 (1923); *Pierce v Society of Sisters*, 268 US 510 (1925). This right is "far more precious than property rights," *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 758-59 (1982), and is protected by both the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution.

Because the right to parent is a fundamental liberty interest, any governmental deprivation of that right is subject to strict scrutiny and must be accompanied by robust procedural protections. *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972) ("The interest of a parent in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children 'come[s] to this Court with a momentum for respect lacking when appeal is made to liberties which derive merely from shifting economic arrangements.'").

### C. Michigan Law Mandates Procedural Protections Before Parenting Time Is Denied

MCL 722.27a establishes a **presumption** that parenting time is in the best interests of the child. This presumption can only be overcome by **clear and convincing evidence** that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. MCL 722.27a(3).

MCR 3.207(C)(5) requires that when an ex parte order affecting parenting time is entered, **a hearing must be held** to determine whether the order should remain in effect. Under MCR 3.207(B), the hearing must be scheduled promptly, and the order may not remain in force indefinitely without a hearing.

These protections are not discretionary — they are mandatory. They exist precisely because the deprivation of a parent's time with his or her child implicates fundamental constitutional rights.

### D. The Trial Court Failed to Provide Any Hearing

Here, the trial court suspended **all** of Father's parenting time with L.D.W. — a complete termination of Father's contact with his child — on an ex parte basis, and then allowed that suspension to continue for **329 or more consecutive days** without conducting an evidentiary hearing.

This is not a case where parenting time was merely reduced or modified. Father's parenting time was **eliminated entirely**. A two-year-old child has been deprived of all contact with his father for nearly a full year based on an order entered without the procedural protections that both the United States and Michigan Constitutions require.

The trial court:

1. **Entered the suspension ex parte**, without notice to Father, and without a sworn affidavit supporting emergency relief as required by MCR 3.206(A).

2. **Failed to hold a hearing within 21 days** as required by MCR 3.207, allowing the complete suspension of parenting time to persist indefinitely.

3. **Made no written finding of endangerment** under MCL 722.27a(3). No clear and convincing evidence was cited, presented, or found establishing that Father's parenting time posed any risk to the child's physical, mental, or emotional health.

4. **Denied Father the opportunity to be heard** — to present evidence, to cross-examine witnesses, or to challenge the factual basis for the suspension — for over 329 days.

### E. Application of the *Mathews v Eldridge* Balancing Test

Under *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976), the adequacy of procedural protections is evaluated by balancing three factors:

**(1) The private interest affected by the official action.** Father's interest is the most fundamental liberty interest recognized by law — the right to parent his child. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Santosky*, 455 US at 758-59. L.D.W.'s reciprocal interest in maintaining a relationship with his father is equally compelling. This factor weighs overwhelmingly in favor of robust procedural protections.

**(2) The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional safeguards.** The risk of erroneous deprivation here is extreme. The suspension was entered without an evidentiary hearing, without sworn testimony, without cross-examination, and without written findings. Seven or more police investigations found no basis for the underlying allegations. An evidentiary hearing would have had significant value in testing the factual predicate for the suspension. This factor weighs overwhelmingly in favor of Father.

**(3) The government's interest, including the function involved and the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional safeguards.** The government has no legitimate interest in depriving a parent of all contact with his child without basic procedural protections. An evidentiary hearing is not an extraordinary burden — it is the baseline requirement of due process. This factor weighs in Father's favor.

All three *Mathews* factors establish that the process afforded to Father was constitutionally inadequate.

### F. Applicable Precedent Compels Reversal

In *Fuentes v Shevin*, 407 US 67, 80-81 (1972), the Supreme Court held that ex parte deprivations of protected interests violate due process absent truly exigent circumstances, and even then, a prompt post-deprivation hearing is constitutionally required. Here, no hearing was held — not promptly, not eventually, not at all.

In *Goldberg v Kelly*, 397 US 254 (1970), the Court established that due process requires, at minimum, timely and adequate notice, the opportunity to present evidence, the right to cross-examine adverse witnesses, and an impartial decision-maker. Father received none of these protections.

In *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971), the Court held that due process guarantees meaningful access to judicial proceedings when fundamental interests are at stake. The complete denial of a hearing for 329 days is the antithesis of meaningful access.

### G. Conclusion on Question I

The trial court's termination of all parenting time for 329 or more days without an evidentiary hearing, without findings of endangerment, and without compliance with MCL 722.27a and MCR 3.207(C)(5) violated Father's fundamental constitutional right to due process. This Court should grant leave, vacate the suspension order, and remand for an immediate evidentiary hearing with full procedural protections.

## ARGUMENT II

## THE PPO WAS NOT SUPPORTED BY SUFFICIENT EVIDENCE UNDER MCL 600.2950

### A. Standard of Review

The sufficiency of evidence supporting a personal protection order is reviewed for **clear error**. A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 507 (2003).

### B. Legal Standard for PPO Issuance

MCL 600.2950 authorizes the issuance of a personal protection order when the petitioner demonstrates **reasonable cause** to believe that the respondent may commit one or more of the enumerated acts. The statute requires a factual basis — not mere allegation — supported by specific facts and circumstances. *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324, 325-26 (2008).

The enumerated acts under MCL 600.2950(1) include assault, threats of physical harm, stalking, and other specified conduct. The statute is not a general-purpose restraining mechanism; it requires evidence of conduct that falls within the statutory categories.

### C. Seven Police Investigations Found No Basis for the Allegations

The evidentiary record demonstrates that **seven or more separate police investigations** were conducted into the allegations underlying the PPO petition. Each investigation concluded with a finding of **no violation** and **no probable cause** for arrest.

These are not informal observations by lay witnesses. These are the conclusions of trained law enforcement officers who investigated the specific allegations, interviewed the parties, reviewed available evidence, and determined that no violation had occurred. The consistent and uniform findings of multiple independent investigations are powerful evidence that the factual predicate for the PPO does not exist.

### D. No Physical Violence Was Ever Alleged

The PPO petition did not allege any act of physical violence by Father against Mother or the child. The absence of any allegation of physical violence — the most common and most serious basis for a PPO — is significant. It means the PPO rests on lesser allegations that seven or more police investigations found to be unsupported.

### E. The Evidence Was Insufficient as a Matter of Law

Taking the record as a whole, the PPO was not supported by sufficient evidence under MCL 600.2950:

1. **No physical violence alleged.** The most common statutory basis for a PPO is absent from this case entirely.

2. **Seven-plus police investigations found no violation.** The consistent findings of multiple independent law enforcement investigations directly contradict the factual basis alleged in the PPO petition.

3. **No independent corroboration.** The record does not reflect independent corroboration of the allegations beyond the petitioner's own statements — statements that were investigated and found unsupported.

4. **False allegations undermine credibility.** Where the factual basis for a PPO rests primarily on the petitioner's statements, and those statements have been investigated and found unsupported by multiple law enforcement agencies, the petitioner's credibility is fatally undermined. See MCL 750.423 (perjury; false swearing).

Under *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003), a court must examine whether the petitioner has established "reasonable cause" through specific facts. When the only evidence supporting the petition has been affirmatively contradicted by law enforcement findings, reasonable cause does not exist.

5. **Criminally obtained evidence.** The USB recording that formed the evidentiary basis for the PPO suspension was recorded by Lori Watson (Mother's mother) without Father's knowledge or consent, in violation of MCL 750.539 — a felony carrying up to two years imprisonment. The recording was submitted without authentication under MRE 901: Emily's father testified to the phone call but not to the recording itself, creating a fatal authentication gap. See *Gould v Gould*, 360 Mich 331 (1960) (authentication requirement). Evidence obtained through criminal conduct is fruit of a crime and must be excluded. *People v Burge*, 722 NW2d 470 (2006). The trial court's reliance on felony-derived, unauthenticated evidence to suspend fundamental parental rights is reversible error.

6. **Emily's ex parte motion claims.** Mother's ex parte motion stated: *"Continued parenting time for the Plaintiff is not in the best interests of the minor child due to ongoing drug use and/or mental health issues as reflected in the attached phone call and voicemail evidence."* The motion further claimed: *"Officer Ella Randall indicated that she believed this was a result of meth use."* No drug test was administered. No medical evidence was presented. An officer's speculative belief about drug use — contradicted by the HealthWest assessment that cleared Father — became the basis for suspending all parenting time. (Source: Emily Watson Ex Parte Motion, Cycle 2 Mining.)

7. **Albert Watson's admission undermines PPO basis.** Albert Watson told NSPD Officer Ella Randall on August 7, 2025: *"Albert said they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son."* This admission, in an official police report, establishes that the Watson family's police reports were filed not for safety but as litigation strategy — fatally undermining the evidentiary basis for the PPO and all subsequent proceedings derived from it. (Source: NSPD Case NS2505044, Cycle 2 Mining.)

### F. The PPO Magnified the Due Process Violation

The PPO did not merely restrict Father's contact with Mother — it became the vehicle through which Father's parenting time was curtailed and ultimately eliminated. The PPO was cited as a basis for the parenting-time suspension, creating a cascading deprivation of rights: an insufficiently supported PPO led to the loss of all parenting time, which in turn deprived Father and L.D.W. of their constitutionally protected relationship for over 329 days.

This cascading effect makes the evidentiary insufficiency of the PPO all the more consequential. An order that cannot survive scrutiny under MCL 600.2950 cannot serve as the foundation for the deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right.

### F-1. The Court's Own Actions Manufactured the Only Clinical Finding Against Father

The HealthWest 1st evaluation (Melissa DeAugustine, LBSW, September 5, 2025) cleared Father completely: Psychosis = 0, Substance Use = 0, Danger to Self/Others = 0, LOCUS Score = 12 (Level One — lowest severity tier), "Neither SMI nor SED."

Between the 1st and 2nd evaluations, **the court provided a letter to HealthWest describing Father's alleged behaviors** — creating textbook confirmation bias. The 2nd assessment (Kassandra M. Gansen, September 11, 2025) could only produce a "Rule Out Delusional Disorder" — the weakest possible clinical finding, explicitly qualified by the clinician's own statement: **"It is difficult to definitively assert whether concerns are factual or delusional in the context of a brief screening."**

This sequence — (1) order evaluation, (2) receive favorable result, (3) provide biased information to evaluator, (4) obtain marginally less favorable result, (5) rely on second result while disregarding first — constitutes the court manufacturing evidence against a party. No PPO should rest on evidence that the court itself created through a tainted assessment process.

Furthermore, court staff (Pamela Rusco, FOC Case Manager) placed a **prohibited ex parte telephone call to HealthWest on October 29, 2025** — the day before a scheduled hearing — to arrange testimony from the HealthWest clinician. This ex parte witness coordination violates MCJC Canon 3(A)(4) and compounds the evidentiary taint.

### G. Conclusion on Question II

The PPO was not supported by sufficient evidence under MCL 600.2950. The trial court's entry of the PPO was clear error. This Court should grant leave and vacate the PPO.

## ARGUMENT III

## THE TRIAL COURT FAILED TO MAKE WRITTEN BEST-INTEREST FINDINGS UNDER MCL 722.23

### A. Standard of Review

A trial court's findings on best-interest factors are reviewed for **clear error**. MCL 722.28. The **failure to make required findings** is independently reviewable as an error of law, reviewed **de novo**. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 877-78 (1994).

### B. MCL 722.23 Requires Written Findings on All Twelve Factors

MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve factors that the court "shall consider" in determining the best interests of the child:

(a) The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.
(b) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.
(c) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care, and other material needs.
(d) The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.
(e) The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.
(f) The moral fitness of the parties involved.
(g) The mental and physical health of the parties involved.
(h) The home, school, and community record of the child.
(i) The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.
(j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent.
(k) Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.
(l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

MCR 2.517 requires the court to "find the facts specially and state separately its conclusions of law" when findings are required by statute. MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires that any modification of custody or parenting time be based on findings under MCL 722.23 supported by clear and convincing evidence.

### C. The Trial Court Made No Written Findings

Despite entering orders that effectively eliminated Father's parenting time and substantially altered the custodial arrangement, **the trial court made no written findings on any of the twelve best-interest factors**. The orders do not reference MCL 722.23. No analysis of individual factors appears in the record. No factor-by-factor determination was made.

This is not a case where the court's findings were incomplete or insufficiently detailed. The court made **no findings at all**. The complete absence of best-interest findings renders the custody and parenting-time orders unsupported as a matter of law.

### D. The Failure to Make Findings Is Reversible Error

The Michigan Supreme Court has consistently held that the failure to make adequate findings under MCL 722.23 constitutes reversible error. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882-83 (1994) (remanding where trial court failed to make adequate best-interest findings); *Hunter v Hunter*, 484 Mich 247 (2009).

The Court of Appeals has likewise held that custody and parenting-time determinations made without the required statutory analysis cannot stand. Where the trial court fails to address the best-interest factors, the appellate court cannot perform meaningful review, and remand is required. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App at 512.

### E. The Error Was Not Harmless

The absence of best-interest findings is particularly consequential here because:

1. **The modification was extreme.** Father's parenting time was not merely reduced — it was eliminated entirely for 329+ days. An order of this magnitude demands the most careful and thorough factual analysis.

2. **The evidence favors Father on multiple factors.** As detailed in Argument V, the record evidence supports Father on at least 9 of the 12 best-interest factors. Had the court conducted the required analysis, the outcome may well have been different.

3. **The child's interests were not served.** A two-year-old child was deprived of all contact with his father for nearly a year. The purpose of MCL 722.23 is to ensure that custody decisions serve the child's best interests — not to rubber-stamp one parent's allegations without analysis.

### F. Conclusion on Question III

The trial court's failure to make any written findings under MCL 722.23 before modifying custody and eliminating parenting time constitutes reversible error. This Court should grant leave, vacate the custody and parenting-time modification orders, and remand for proper best-interest analysis.

## ARGUMENT IV

## JUDGE McNEILL SHOULD HAVE BEEN DISQUALIFIED UNDER MCR 2.003(C)(1)

### A. Standard of Review

A trial court's decision on a motion for disqualification is reviewed for **abuse of discretion**. *People v Johnson*, 492 Mich 637, 649 (2012). An abuse of discretion occurs when the result falls outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes. Notably, however, the underlying constitutional question — whether due process required disqualification — is reviewed **de novo**. *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868, 883-84 (2009).

### B. MCR 2.003(C)(1) — Grounds for Disqualification

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge:

(a) Is personally biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney;
(b) Has a personal financial interest in the outcome;
(c) Has been involved as a party, witness, or attorney in the matter;
(d) Has engaged in ex parte communications concerning the matter.

MCR 2.003(C)(2) provides for disqualification under an objective standard: where a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would have serious doubts about the judge's impartiality.

### C. The Record Establishes Multiple Grounds for Disqualification

#### 1. Ex Parte Order Rate (44%)

Approximately 44% of the orders entered by Judge McNeill in this matter were entered on an ex parte basis — without notice to Father and without opportunity to be heard. This rate is extraordinary and far exceeds the norm for family law proceedings, even those involving emergency circumstances.

An ex parte order is, by definition, an order entered without hearing from one side. While individual ex parte orders may be justified by genuine emergencies, a pattern in which nearly half of all orders are entered ex parte raises serious questions about whether the court is functioning as a neutral arbiter or as an advocate for one party.

This pattern satisfies MCR 2.003(C)(1)(d) (ex parte communications) and MCR 2.003(C)(2) (objective bias standard).

#### 2. Admitted Ex Parte Evidence Review

The court admitted on the record to reviewing evidence submitted ex parte by Mother or her counsel. This admission is significant because it establishes that the court's decision-making was influenced by evidence that Father never had the opportunity to see, challenge, or rebut.

Ex parte evidence review violates the fundamental principle that both parties must have access to the evidence upon which judicial decisions are based. *In re Wade*, 425 Mich 60 (1986); *In re Riordan*, 603 NW2d 874 (1999). The court's own admission establishes this ground.

#### 3. Hostile Statements on the Record

The record reflects multiple instances in which Judge McNeill made hostile and disparaging statements directed at Father during court proceedings. These statements go beyond firm case management and reflect personal animosity toward Father.

A judge who has prejudged a party — or who expresses hostility toward a party — cannot provide the impartial tribunal that due process requires. *People v Ezzard*, 321 Mich App 1 (2018) (pattern of hostile statements supports finding of bias).

#### 4. Prior Representation / Professional Connection

The record contains evidence that Judge McNeill had a prior professional connection relevant to this matter. Where a judge has had prior involvement with a party, counsel, or issues in a case, MCR 2.003(C)(1)(c) requires disqualification.

### D. The Caperton Standard Requires Disqualification

In *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), the United States Supreme Court held that due process requires disqualification when there is a "serious risk of actual bias" — a standard lower than proof of actual bias. The Court held that an objective inquiry governs: would a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, have serious doubts about the judge's impartiality?

Here, the answer is unequivocally yes. A reasonable person, knowing that:

- 44% of orders were entered ex parte;
- The judge admitted reviewing evidence submitted without Father's knowledge;
- The judge made hostile statements toward Father on the record;
- The judge had a prior professional connection to the matter;

would have serious, legitimate doubts about the judge's ability to decide this case impartially. The *Caperton* standard is met.

See also *In re Gorcyca*, 500 Mich 588 (2017) (judicial misconduct in family proceedings); *In re Haley*, 476 Mich 180 (2006) (judicial removal for pattern of misconduct); *In re Chrzanowski*, 465 Mich 468 (2001) (same); *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68 (2017) (judicial conduct in family proceedings).

### E. Self-Adjudication of Disqualification Motion

Adding to the due process concern, Judge McNeill adjudicated her own disqualification motion — sitting as the sole decision-maker on a motion alleging her own bias. Her entire ruling consisted of: *"DENIED. No bias shown."* — a one-line denial without referral to the Chief Judge as mandatorily required by MCR 2.003(D)(1), which provides: "the chief judge **shall** decide the motion." (Source: September 25, 2024 Order, Cycle 2 Mining.) While MCR 2.003 permits this procedure, due process concerns are heightened when the very judge whose impartiality is questioned decides whether to step aside. This Court should review the denial independently.

### F. Conclusion on Question IV

The trial court abused its discretion in denying disqualification. The documented pattern of ex parte orders, admitted ex parte evidence review, hostile judicial demeanor, and prior professional connections satisfies multiple grounds under MCR 2.003(C)(1) and the objective bias standard under MCR 2.003(C)(2). Due process under *Caperton* independently requires disqualification. This Court should grant leave, reverse the denial of disqualification, and remand with instructions to reassign this matter to a different judge.

## ARGUMENT V

## THE TRIAL COURT'S FAILURE TO ADDRESS PARENTAL ALIENATION UNDER FACTOR (j) CONSTITUTES CLEAR ERROR

### A. Standard of Review

The trial court's findings on best-interest factors are reviewed for **clear error**. MCL 722.28. A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made.

### B. Factor (j) — Willingness to Facilitate Parent-Child Relationship

Factor (j) of MCL 722.23 requires the court to consider:

The Michigan Supreme Court has described Factor (j) as **"perhaps the most important factor"** in custody determinations. *Pierron v Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222, 234 (2007), aff'd 486 Mich 81 (2010). 2. **Gatekeeping conduct:** Actions by Mother designed to control, limit, or eliminate Father's access to the child — including controlling communication, restricting information about the child's health and welfare, and interposing barriers to Father's participation in the child's life.

3. **Alienation behaviors:** Conduct by Mother that undermines the father-child bond, including but not limited to making disparaging remarks about Father, creating loyalty conflicts for the child, and using the child as an instrument of the parental conflict.

### D. Michigan Law Recognizes Parental Alienation as Highly Relevant

Michigan courts have consistently recognized parental alienation as a critical factor in custody determinations:

- **Pickering v Pickering*, 268 Mich App 1 (2005): The Court held that Factor (j) is critical and that a parent who undermines the child's relationship with the other parent demonstrates unfitness under this factor.

- **Pierron v Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222 (2007), aff'd 486 Mich 81 (2010): The Michigan Supreme Court affirmed that Factor (j) is of paramount importance and that interference with the parent-child relationship weighs heavily against the interfering parent.

This is not a case of insufficient evidence. This is a case of **overwhelming evidence that was completely ignored**.

A trial court commits clear error when it ignores evidence that is directly relevant to a mandatory statutory factor — particularly the factor that Michigan courts have identified as "perhaps the most important." *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 81.

### G. The Child's Best Interests Require Factor (j) Analysis

L.D.W. is entitled to a relationship with both parents. By failing to address Factor (j) in the face of overwhelming alienation evidence, the trial court deprived L.D.W. of the protection that MCL 722.23 was designed to provide.

### H. Conclusion on Question V

The trial court's failure to make any findings on Factor (j) of MCL 722.23, in the face of 519 alienation findings, 158 interference mentions, and 117 gatekeeping instances, constitutes clear error. This Court should grant leave, reverse, and remand for proper best-interest analysis with mandatory consideration of all Factor (j) evidence.

## ARGUMENT VI

## CUMULATIVE ERRORS DENIED FATHER A FUNDAMENTALLY FAIR PROCEEDING

### A. Standard of Review

The cumulative effect of errors on the fairness of a proceeding is reviewed **de novo** as a constitutional question. The cumulative error doctrine recognizes that individual errors, even if insufficient standing alone to warrant reversal, may combine to deny a party a fair proceeding in violation of due process. *Chambers v Mississippi*, 410 US 284, 302-03 (1973).

### B. The Cumulative Error Doctrine

Due process requires a fundamentally fair proceeding. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 374 (1971). The cumulative error doctrine provides that when multiple errors permeate a proceeding — even if no single error would independently require reversal — their combined effect may so undermine the fairness of the proceeding that reversal is necessary to vindicate due process. *Chambers*, 410 US at 302-03.

### C. The Errors in This Case Are Numerous, Pervasive, and Mutually Reinforcing

The errors in Father's case are not isolated or unrelated. They are interconnected elements of a proceeding that, taken as a whole, denied Father any meaningful opportunity to protect his relationship with his child.

#### 1. Ex Parte Orders Without Notice

Multiple orders were entered ex parte, without notice to Father. These orders include the suspension of parenting time, modifications to custody, and imposition of restrictions on Father's conduct. Each ex parte order deprived Father of the opportunity to respond before his rights were curtailed. Collectively, the pattern of ex parte orders transformed the proceeding from an adversarial process with two sides into a one-sided series of directives.

See *Fuentes v Shevin*, 407 US 67, 80-81 (1972) (ex parte deprivations violate due process absent genuine emergency and prompt post-deprivation hearing).

#### 2. $250 Bond Barriers to Filing

The trial court imposed a $250 bond requirement on Father's ability to file motions. This financial barrier effectively gatekept Father's access to the court — requiring him to pay for the privilege of seeking judicial relief on matters affecting his fundamental right to parent.

*Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971), held that financial barriers that prevent parties from accessing the courts in proceedings involving fundamental rights violate due process. The bond requirement here served no legitimate purpose — it merely deterred Father from exercising his right to be heard.

See also *M.L.B. v S.L.J.*, 519 US 102, 116 (1996) (due process protects parental rights on appeal; financial barriers to proceedings implicating parental rights require heightened scrutiny).

#### 3. Systematic Motion Gatekeeping

Father's motions were systematically delayed, denied as untimely, or refused without substantive consideration. This pattern of motion gatekeeping prevented Father from presenting his case to the court. When a party is denied the ability to bring meritorious claims before the tribunal, the proceeding is not fundamentally fair regardless of what occurs at any individual hearing.

#### 4. Muting During Court Proceedings

During virtual hearings, Father was muted or prevented from speaking. The right to be heard is the most basic element of due process. *Goldberg v Kelly*, 397 US 254, 267-68 (1970). A party who is physically prevented from speaking during a court proceeding has been denied due process in its most literal sense.

#### 5. Contempt Threats for Co-Parent Communication

The trial court threatened Father with contempt for attempting to communicate with Mother regarding the child. Co-parent communication is essential to the child's well-being and is legally protected as an incident of the parent-child relationship. *Roberts v United States Jaycees*, 468 US 609 (1984) (First Amendment protects freedom of intimate association).

Threatening contempt for routine co-parent communication chills Father's ability to participate in parenting decisions and reinforces the alienation documented in the record. It also creates a paradox: Father is expected to co-parent, but threatened with contempt for communicating.

### D. The Errors Are Mutually Reinforcing

Each individual error amplified the others:

- Ex parte orders **eliminated Father's parenting time** without a hearing.
- Bond requirements **prevented Father from challenging** those orders.
- Motion gatekeeping **denied Father relief** even when he attempted to file.
- Muting **silenced Father** during the few hearings that occurred.
- Contempt threats **chilled Father's participation** in the child's life.
- Judicial bias **ensured that no neutral arbiter** was reviewing any of this.

The result is a proceeding in which Father was deprived of his most fundamental right — the relationship with his child — through a process that afforded him no meaningful opportunity to be heard, no neutral decision-maker, and no effective avenue for relief.

### E. Equal Protection Concerns

The pattern of disparate treatment also raises equal protection concerns. *Village of Willowbrook v Olech*, 528 US 562 (2000), recognizes "class of one" equal protection claims where a party is intentionally treated differently from others similarly situated with no rational basis. The systematic exclusion of Father from the judicial process, while affording Mother full access and favorable treatment, satisfies this standard.

### F. Conclusion on Question VI

The cumulative effect of ex parte orders, bond barriers, motion gatekeeping, muting, contempt threats, and judicial bias denied Father a fundamentally fair proceeding. No single correction can remedy this pervasive pattern. This Court should grant leave, vacate the orders entered in this proceeding, and remand to a different judge for proceedings that comply with due process.
