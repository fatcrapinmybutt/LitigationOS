## STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. Background

Appellant Andrew Pigors ("Father") and Appellee Emily Watson ("Mother") are the parents of minor children who are the subject of custody proceedings in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County. The case has been presided over by the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill since inception.

### B. The Ex Parte Orders

On August 8, 2025, the trial court entered five separate ex parte orders, including an order suspending all of Father's parenting time with the minor children. Father received no notice of the ex parte proceedings. Father was given no opportunity to be heard before the orders were entered.

The ex parte motion was filed by Emily Watson, Mother's sister, who acts as Mother's de facto legal representative throughout these proceedings. Ron Berry, an appellate attorney, is connected to the case through voicemail evidence (Item #6 in the line-by-line objection table to the ex parte motion).

### C. The Ongoing Separation

As of the date of this brief, Father has been separated from his children for **250 consecutive days** (from August 8, 2025 to April 15, 2026). No hearing has been conducted. No best interest factor analysis has been performed. No finding of serious endangerment under MCL 722.27a(3) appears in the record.

### D. The Pattern

A review of the complete docket reveals:
- **24 of 55 orders (43.6%)** entered on an ex parte basis
- **Multiple critical judicial violations** documented
- **Numerous contradictions** identified in the record — including the court's finding of "equal parental bond" (August 23, 2024) followed by a total suspension of Father's parenting time
- **Numerous documented judicial inconsistencies** — for example, the court refused to view Father's exculpatory photographs (August 28, 2025) while relying on Defendant's unsworn ex parte allegations, and imposed a $250 filing bond on Father only (May 16, 2025) while Defendant faced no comparable restriction
- A temporal pattern of procedural irregularity: Watson files → McNeill rules same day → Berry files within 48 hours

### E. Prior History

Prior to the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders, Father was actively exercising parenting time. No protective order existed against Father. No court had found Father posed any danger to the children. The children had an established custodial relationship with Father.

### F. Candid Acknowledgment Regarding PPO History

Appellant acknowledges that he was found in violation of the personal protection order and served a period of incarceration. Appellant does not minimize these events. However, PPO violations — even those resulting in incarceration — do not constitute legal grounds to permanently sever the parent-child relationship without a best-interest hearing under MCL 722.23. The Michigan Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a parent's constitutional right to the care and custody of their children is fundamental and may not be terminated without due process of law. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014); *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The appropriate remedy for PPO violations is the criminal sanction already imposed — not the indefinite suspension of all parenting time without any hearing on the merits.

## STANDARD OF REVIEW

### Plain Error Review

Because these issues were not preserved at the trial court level through contemporaneous objection, Appellant invokes the plain error standard of review under *People v Carines*, 460 Mich 750, 763 (1999). Under *Carines*, Appellant must show:

1. **Error occurred** — an error was committed;
2. **The error was plain** — clear or obvious;
3. **The plain error affected substantial rights** — it affected the outcome of the proceedings; and
4. **The error seriously affects the fairness, integrity, or public reputation of judicial proceedings** — reversal is necessary to avoid a miscarriage of justice.

Appellant submits that all four *Carines* elements are satisfied for each issue presented. The errors are not subtle; they are fundamental deprivations of constitutional rights and statutory protections.

### Additional Standards

Custody decisions are reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 1, 7 (2003). Findings of fact are reviewed for clear error. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 91 (2010). Constitutional questions are reviewed de novo. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014).

## ARGUMENT

### I. THE TRIAL COURT COMMITTED PLAIN ERROR BY SUSPENDING PARENTING TIME WITHOUT MCR 3.207(B) COMPLIANCE

**A. Error Occurred**

MCR 3.207(B) permits ex parte orders in domestic relations matters only where "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice" and requires: (1) the movant to "certify to the court in writing the efforts, if any, which have been made to give notice"; and (2) "a hearing on the motion must be held within 14 days after the order is entered." MCR 3.207(B)(3). Due process protections apply even in interim custody determinations; a court must consider the best-interest factors before altering a custody arrangement, even on a temporary basis. *Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556; 873 NW2d 319 (2015).

The record contains no certification of efforts to give notice. No finding of immediate and irreparable injury appears. No hearing was held within 14 days. The suspension has now lasted **250 days** (from August 8, 2025 to April 15, 2026) — exceeding the maximum permitted pre-hearing duration by a factor of 17.

**B. The Error Was Plain**

MCR 3.207(B) is unambiguous. Its requirements are mandatory ("must"), not discretionary. A trial court's failure to comply with express procedural requirements is a plain error. See *Carines*, 460 Mich at 763.

**C. The Error Affected Substantial Rights**

Father lost all contact with his children for 250 days (from August 8, 2025 to April 15, 2026). This is not a technical violation — it is the complete deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right. Had the court complied with MCR 3.207(B), Father would have had a hearing within 14 days, with the opportunity to present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, and contest the suspension.

**D. The Error Affects the Integrity of Proceedings**

A trial court that suspends a parent's constitutional rights without following its own procedural rules — and then maintains that suspension for 250 days (from August 8, 2025 to April 15, 2026) — undermines the fairness and public reputation of the judiciary. This Court should not allow such a departure from fundamental procedural protections to stand.

---

### II. THE TRIAL COURT COMMITTED PLAIN ERROR BY FAILING TO APPLY BEST INTEREST FACTORS

**A. Error Occurred**

MCL 722.23 requires the trial court to "consider, evaluate, and determine each of the following factors" before making a custody determination: the 12 best interest factors (a) through (l). *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 1, 7 (2003). The Michigan Supreme Court has held that a trial court must make independent findings on each best-interest factor and cannot merely adopt a FOC recommendation without independent analysis. *DeRose v DeRose*, 469 Mich 320; 666 NW2d 636 (2003). The suspension of ALL parenting time is functionally a custody modification — it transferred Father's custodial time entirely to Mother. Yet the record contains no BIF analysis whatsoever.

**B. The Error Was Plain**

The requirement to apply MCL 722.23 factors is a foundational principle of Michigan custody law. Failure to make any BIF findings before modifying custody is a clear, obvious error that any reasonable jurist would recognize. See *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81 (2010).

**C. The Error Affected Substantial Rights**

Had the trial court applied the BIF factors, the analysis would have favored Father on the majority of factors — particularly Factor (j), willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship, where Mother's conduct in obtaining and maintaining the ex parte suspension directly undermines her position. Moreover, the established custodial environment analysis — a critical threshold in custody disputes — was entirely omitted. *Foskett v Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1; 634 NW2d 363 (2001) (ECE can exist in both parents' homes simultaneously; modification requires clear and convincing evidence). Father had an established custodial environment with the children prior to the ex parte suspension, and eliminating that ECE without the requisite evidentiary standard is reversible error.

Appellant's review of all 12 factors with record evidence demonstrates:
- **Factors strongly favoring Father:** (a), (b), (c), (d), (e), (j), (l)
- **Factors neutral:** (g), (h), (i)
- **Factors requiring hearing:** (f), (k)

Moreover, the trial court's own best-interest-factor analysis found that "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and that "[t]his Factor is equal between the parties" under MCL 722.23(a). *See* Record, Final Judgment dated August 23, 2024, Best Interest Factor Analysis. The subsequent total suspension of Father's parenting time is irreconcilable with the court's own finding of equal parental bond — constituting an abuse of discretion that is plain on the face of the record.

**D. The Error Affects the Integrity of Proceedings**

Custody modification without BIF analysis is not merely procedural error — it is the court substituting its own unexamined judgment for the detailed statutory framework the Legislature enacted to protect children and parents. Public confidence in the family court system depends on adherence to this framework.

---

### III. THE TRIAL COURT COMMITTED PLAIN ERROR BY DENYING DUE PROCESS

**A. Error Occurred**

"The fundamental requisite of due process of law is the opportunity to be heard." *Grannis v Ordean*, 234 US 385, 394 (1914). The right to the care and custody of one's children is a "fundamental liberty interest" subject to the highest constitutional protection. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014).

Father received no notice before the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders. Father received no opportunity to be heard. Father's fundamental liberty interest was extinguished without any process.

**B. The Error Was Plain**

The constitutional requirement of notice and hearing before deprivation of fundamental rights is among the most clearly established principles in American law. See *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982) ("The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents.").

**C. The Error Affected Substantial Rights**

Father has been completely separated from his children for 250 days (from August 8, 2025 to April 15, 2026). Each day represents an irreversible loss of the parent-child relationship — developmental milestones missed, bonds weakened, and the alienation effect compounded.

**D. The Error Affects the Integrity of Proceedings**

When a court deprives a parent of fundamental rights without any process, it does not merely harm the individual parent — it undermines public trust in the judicial system's commitment to constitutional principles. This Court should not countenance such a departure from due process.

---

### IV. THE TRIAL COURT'S PATTERN DEMONSTRATES BIAS REQUIRING DISQUALIFICATION

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification where the judge "is personally biased or prejudiced for or against a party." *Cain v Michigan Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 496 (1996) (bias may be shown through pattern of conduct).

The pattern here is overwhelming:

1. **43.6% ex parte order rate** — nearly half of all orders entered without Father's participation
2. **Five ex parte orders on a single day** (August 8, 2025) — an extraordinary concentration
3. **Numerous documented judicial inconsistencies** — including the court's finding of equal parental bond contradicted by total suspension, refusal to view exculpatory evidence while relying on unsworn allegations, and one-sided imposition of filing bond — indicating arbitrary decision-making
4. **Temporal coordination** with opposing party's filings — Watson files, McNeill rules same day
5. **250-day separation** (from August 8, 2025 to April 15, 2026) maintained without a single hearing to evaluate whether it should continue

This is not a case of isolated judicial error. It is a pattern of proceedings in which Father was systematically denied meaningful participation, depriving him of his fundamental rights. Disqualification is required.

---

### V. EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS TAINTED ALL SUBSEQUENT ORDERS

Canon 3(A)(4) prohibits judges from initiating or considering "ex parte communications on the merits, or procedures affecting the merits, of a pending or impending matter." The pattern of procedural irregularity — Watson files → McNeill rules same day → Berry files within 48 hours — is circumstantial evidence of ex parte communication that violates this prohibition.

When ex parte communications occur, all subsequent orders are tainted and must be vacated. The appropriate remedy is disqualification and reassignment to a neutral judicial officer, with de novo review of all contested orders.
