# I. INTRODUCTION AND NATURE OF ACTION

Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors files this Complaint for Superintending Control pursuant to MCR 7.304 and Mich Const 1963, art 6, § 4, which provides:

> "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts; power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs . . ."

This is an original action seeking the exercise of this Court's constitutionally vested superintending authority over the 14th Judicial Circuit Court, Family Division, Muskegon County, and specifically over the conduct of Judge Jenny L. McNeill in Cases No. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP.

Father files this Complaint as an alternative to — and companion filing with — his Application for Leave to Appeal from the Court of Appeals (Case No. 366810). The extraordinary circumstances documented herein justify this Court's exercise of original jurisdiction because the normal appellate process cannot provide timely or adequate relief for an ongoing constitutional violation of the most fundamental character.

# IV. STATEMENT OF FACTS

## A. Background

The facts are fully set forth in the Application for Leave to Appeal and the Brief in Support, filed contemporaneously herewith, and are incorporated herein by reference. The following facts are emphasized for their relevance to the extraordinary relief sought:

## B. Extraordinary Circumstances Justifying Bypass of Normal Appeal

### 1. The 44% Ex Parte Rate

Of the 55 orders entered in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, 24 (44%) were entered on an ex parte basis — all without notice to Father, all presided over by Judge McNeill. This is not a case of occasional emergency orders; it is a systematic pattern of one-sided adjudication.

### 2. Five Ex Parte Orders in One Day

On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five (5) separate ex parte orders on a single day, all without notice to Father. The totality of the court's actions on that day:
- Suspended all parenting time.
- Modified custody arrangements.
- Entered additional orders on related matters.
- Created a fait accompli that shifted the burden entirely to Father.

### 3. 571+ Days of Denied Parenting Time

Father has been denied all contact with his son for over 571 consecutive days. This ongoing deprivation cannot be reversed — every day that passes is a day of the parent-child bond that can never be recovered. The child was approximately two years old when the separation began; she is now approximately four years old. These are critical developmental years.

### 4. No Evidentiary Hearing

In over two years of proceedings, the trial court has never conducted a full evidentiary hearing on custody or parenting time at which sworn testimony was taken and subject to cross-examination. The August 23, 2024 Final Judgment was entered without such a hearing. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders were entered without any hearing at all.

### 5. Disqualification Motion Denied

Father moved for disqualification of Judge McNeill under MCR 2.003(C)(1), citing the documented pattern of bias. The motion was denied, and Judge McNeill continues to preside. Under MCR 2.003(D), the judge herself ruled on the motion — creating the inherent conflict that this Court's superintending authority is designed to address.

### 6. Dismissed Motions Without Adjudication

The August 23, 2024 Final Judgment dismissed six (6) of Father's pending motions without hearing or adjudication on the merits, stating simply: "Plaintiff's six (6) motions . . . Dismissed." This blanket dismissal denied Father access to justice on multiple substantive issues.

# V. GROUNDS FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL

## A. No Adequate Legal Remedy

MCR 7.304(B) authorizes superintending control "to correct errors where there is no adequate legal remedy." The normal appellate process is inadequate here for the following reasons:

1. **Timing**: The Court of Appeals process takes months to years. Each day of delay is another day of irreparable harm to the parent-child relationship. The child's developmental window for bonding cannot be reopened.

2. **Scope**: The Court of Appeals can reverse specific orders but cannot address the systemic pattern of judicial conduct. Even if the August 8, 2025 orders are reversed, the same judge will continue to preside, and the pattern can resume.

3. **Remedy**: The Court of Appeals cannot order disqualification of a trial court judge as part of its standard appellate relief. Only this Court, exercising superintending control, can effectively address the structural bias documented here.

4. **Ongoing Violation**: Unlike a completed action that can be reviewed on appeal, the constitutional violation here is ongoing. Father is being deprived of his fundamental rights *right now*, and every day the deprivation continues.

## B. Prevention of Usurpation of Jurisdiction

MCR 7.304(B) also authorizes superintending control "to prevent usurpation of jurisdiction." The trial court has, in effect, accomplished the functional equivalent of a termination of parental rights — 571+ days of zero contact — without complying with any of the constitutional safeguards required for such a drastic action:

- No clear and convincing evidence standard applied (*Santosky v Kramer*).
- No evidentiary hearing with sworn testimony.
- No findings of parental unfitness.
- No exploration of less restrictive alternatives.
- No appointment of counsel or guardian ad litem.

By using ex parte custody orders to achieve what can constitutionally be accomplished only through termination proceedings with full due process protections, the trial court has exceeded its legitimate authority and usurped a function that requires the most rigorous procedural safeguards.

## C. Prevention of Action Contrary to Law

MCR 7.304(B) further authorizes superintending control to "prevent a court from acting contrary to law." The trial court has acted contrary to:

1. **MCR 3.207(C)(2)**: Requiring verified allegations of immediate and irreparable injury for ex parte orders.
2. **MCR 3.210**: Requiring evidentiary hearings in custody proceedings.
3. **MCL 722.27a(7)**: Requiring clear and convincing evidence before denying parenting time.
4. **MCR 2.003**: Requiring disqualification when actual bias is demonstrated.
5. **Due Process Clause**: US Const, Amend XIV; Mich Const 1963, art 1, § 17.
6. **Equal Protection Clause**: US Const, Amend XIV.

# VI. EVIDENCE OF ONGOING IRREPARABLE HARM

## A. Harm to the Parent-Child Bond

Child development research uniformly recognizes that prolonged separation between a parent and a young child causes profound and potentially irreversible damage to the attachment relationship. L.D.W. was approximately two years old when the separation began — a critical period for secure attachment formation.

At 571+ days of separation:
- L.D.W. may have difficulty recognizing Father.
- Attachment bonds formed during the first two years are being actively degraded.
- The child is being conditioned to a single-parent reality.
- Reunification becomes more difficult with each passing day.

## B. Constitutional Harm to Father

Father's fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his son is being violated on a daily and ongoing basis. This is not a past harm that can be remedied through monetary damages; it is a present and continuing constitutional violation.

## C. Harm to Judicial Integrity

The pattern documented here — if left uncorrected — undermines public confidence in the fairness and impartiality of Michigan's family courts. The integrity of the judicial system itself is harmed when a documented pattern of one-sided adjudication is permitted to continue.

# VII. RELIEF REQUESTED

Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors respectfully requests that this Honorable Court, exercising its constitutional superintending authority:

## A. Immediate Relief

1. **Issue an Order to Show Cause** directing Respondents to show cause why superintending control should not be exercised.

2. **Issue a Stay** of all proceedings before Judge McNeill in Cases No. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP pending resolution of this Complaint.

3. **Issue a Temporary Order Restoring Parenting Time** — at minimum, supervised parenting time — pending full resolution, unless Respondents can demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that such parenting time would endanger L.D.W.

## B. Final Relief

4. **Order Disqualification** of Judge McNeill from all further proceedings in Cases No. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP.

5. **Vacate** the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders and all subsequent continuation orders.

6. **Vacate** the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment to the extent it was entered without constitutionally adequate process.

7. **Remand** for a full evidentiary hearing before a different judge, with:
   - Sworn testimony subject to cross-examination.
   - Particularized findings on all twelve MCL 722.23 factors.
   - Application of the clear and convincing evidence standard under MCL 722.27a(7).
   - Consideration of Father's previously dismissed motions on their merits.

8. **Establish Supervisory Standards** for ex parte practice in family court proceedings, including:
   - Maximum permissible ex parte rate before presumption of bias triggers.
   - Mandatory verified statements under MCR 3.207(C)(2).
   - Automatic review by a different judge when ex parte orders are challenged.
   - Expedited hearing requirements (within 7 days) after any ex parte order affecting parenting time.

9. **Order Expedited Consideration** of this Complaint given the ongoing irreparable harm.

10. **Grant Any Other Relief** this Court deems just and appropriate.
