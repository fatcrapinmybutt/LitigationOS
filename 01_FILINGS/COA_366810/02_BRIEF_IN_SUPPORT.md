# IV. INTRODUCTION

This case asks the Michigan Supreme Court to address a fundamental question: whether a parent's constitutional rights can be entirely eliminated through a cascade of ex parte orders entered without notice, without evidentiary hearing, and without the procedural safeguards that the Constitution demands before the state may sever the parent-child bond.

Andrew J. Pigors has been denied all contact with his son, L.D.W., for over 571 consecutive days. This severance was accomplished not through a contested hearing with sworn testimony and cross-examination, but through a series of ex parte orders — 24 of 55 total orders (44%) — entered by a single judge who has demonstrated a documented pattern of asymmetric treatment of the parties.

The evidence compiled in this case is extraordinary in its scope: 663 documented mentions of the presiding judge in connection with procedural violations, 1,530 categorized harms attributable to judicial conduct, and a systematic pattern of denying Father's motions while granting Mother's. The statistical profile of this case — particularly the 44% ex parte rate — represents a structural failure in due process that transcends the facts of this individual case and implicates the constitutional rights of every parent who enters Michigan's family court system.

This Court's intervention is necessary because the Court of Appeals lacks the institutional authority to establish the statewide standards required to prevent recurrence. Only the Michigan Supreme Court can establish binding precedent defining the constitutional minimum standards for ex parte practice in family courts and the point at which asymmetric judicial treatment becomes constitutionally impermissible bias.

# V. CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK

## A. The Fundamental Liberty Interest in Parental Rights

The right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is among the most venerable rights recognized in American constitutional law. The Supreme Court of the United States has described this right in sweeping terms across nearly a century of jurisprudence:

> "The liberty interest at issue in this case — the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."

*Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

This principle traces through an unbroken line of precedent:

- *Meyer v Nebraska*, 262 US 390, 399 (1923): Liberty includes "the right of the individual . . . to establish a home and bring up children."
- *Prince v Massachusetts*, 321 US 158, 166 (1944): "[T]he custody, care and nurture of the child reside first in the parents."
- *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972): "[T]he interest of a parent in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children 'come[s] to this Court with a momentum for respect lacking when appeal is made to liberties which derive merely from shifting economic arrangements.'"
- *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982): "[F]reedom of personal choice in matters of family life is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment."

The Michigan Supreme Court has echoed these principles:

> "A parent has a significant interest in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children."

*Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

## B. Due Process Requirements When Fundamental Rights Are at Stake

Because the parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest, its deprivation triggers the full panoply of due process protections. The *Mathews v Eldridge* framework, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), requires consideration of:

1. **The private interest affected**: The interest here — the parent-child relationship — is among the weightiest recognized in constitutional law.

2. **The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used**: The risk is maximized when orders are entered ex parte, without notice, without sworn testimony, and without cross-examination.

3. **The government's interest**: The state's interest in child welfare is served by fair and accurate proceedings, not summary ones.

When the private interest is at its highest and the risk of error is at its greatest, due process demands the most rigorous procedural protections. *See Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US at 758-64 (requiring clear and convincing evidence before termination of parental rights); *Lassiter v Dep't of Social Services*, 452 US 18 (1981) (recognizing potential right to appointed counsel in termination proceedings).

## C. The Anti-Ambush Principle

Due process fundamentally requires notice and an opportunity to be heard *before* the state acts to deprive a person of a protected interest:

> "The fundamental requisite of due process of law is the opportunity to be heard . . . [and this] must be granted at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner."

*Armstrong v Manzo*, 380 US 545, 552 (1965) (emphasis added).

Ex parte proceedings are the antithesis of this principle. While recognized as necessary in narrow emergency circumstances, they are constitutionally suspect precisely because they deny the opposing party any opportunity to be heard. *See Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971); *Goldberg v Kelly*, 397 US 254 (1970).

## D. The Heightened Standard for Parenting Time Restrictions

Michigan law recognizes that restrictions on parenting time require heightened procedural protections. MCL 722.27a(7) provides:

> "The court may order that parenting time not be exercised . . . only if the court determines by clear and convincing evidence that the parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

This "clear and convincing evidence" standard — the same standard required for termination of parental rights under *Santosky* — cannot be satisfied through ex parte proceedings where no evidence is tested through cross-examination.

# VI. PATTERN OF JUDICIAL MISCONDUCT AS DUE PROCESS VIOLATION

## A. The Statistical Profile

| Period | Duration | Basis |

## B. The August 8, 2025 Orders as Constitutional Inflection Point

The entry of five separate ex parte orders on August 8, 2025 — all without notice to Father — represents a constitutional inflection point. The court's action on that date:

1. **Suspended all parenting time** without verified allegations of immediate and irreparable injury.
2. **Entered orders on matters beyond parenting time** without notice.
3. **Created a fait accompli** — by the time Father could respond, the suspension was already in effect and the burden had shifted to Father to seek restoration.
4. **Denied Father the ability to present exculpatory evidence** — including evidence that Mother's allegations were unverified and contradicted by available evidence.

The ex parte motion relied on Mother's unsworn allegations regarding Father's phone calls. These allegations did not meet the MCR 3.207(C)(2) standard of "immediate and irreparable injury" from the delay required to provide notice. The trial court's acceptance of these allegations without verification, and its entry of five orders based on them, represents judicial abdication of the gatekeeping function.

## C. Cumulative Effect Doctrine

Individual judicial errors, taken in isolation, might each fall within the range of judicial discretion. But when errors consistently favor one party and consistently prejudice the other, the cumulative pattern becomes constitutionally significant.

This Court should adopt a cumulative-pattern analysis for due process claims in family court, examining:

1. **Frequency of ex parte orders** relative to total orders.
2. **Asymmetry in motion dispositions** between the parties.
3. **Duration of fundamental rights deprivation** without constitutionally adequate process.
4. **Pattern of credibility determinations** consistently favoring one party.
5. **Responsiveness to self-correction** opportunities.

Applied to this case, every factor points toward a structural due process violation.

# VII. STRUCTURAL BIAS EVIDENCE

## A. The Impartiality Requirement

The Due Process Clause guarantees "a fair trial in a fair tribunal." *In re Murchison*, 349 US 133, 136 (1955). This guarantee requires not only the absence of actual bias but the absence of "even the probability of unfairness":

> "Every procedure which would offer a possible temptation to the average man as a judge . . . not to hold the balance nice, clear, and true between the State and the accused denies the latter due process of law."

*Tumey v Ohio*, 273 US 510, 532 (1927).

## B. Objective Indicators of Structural Bias

The record presents the following objective indicators of structural bias:

### 1. Ex Parte Rate

The 44% ex parte rate is, on information and belief, a statistical outlier for Michigan custody proceedings. In a properly functioning adversarial system, ex parte orders should constitute a small fraction of total orders — reserved for genuine emergencies where delay would cause irreparable harm. A rate approaching half of all orders indicates that the ex parte mechanism has been converted from an emergency tool into a routine instrument of one-sided adjudication.

### 2. Asymmetric Treatment of Parties

The record reveals systematic asymmetry:

- **Father's motions**: Consistently denied or dismissed without adjudication. Father's Motion for Contempt (regarding Mother's 37-day withholding of the child) was denied. Six pending motions were dismissed without hearing.
- **Mother's motions**: Consistently granted, including ex parte motions that did not meet the MCR 3.207(C)(2) standard.

### 3. Credibility Determinations

The trial court consistently:
- Credited Mother's unsworn allegations at face value.
- Excluded or disregarded Father's documentary evidence.
- Permitted Mother's counsel to present unverified claims.
- Subjected Father's pro se presentations to heightened scrutiny.

### 4. Evidentiary Hearing Avoidance

Despite the fundamental rights at stake, the trial court never conducted a full evidentiary hearing with sworn testimony subject to cross-examination on the merits of custody or parenting time. This avoidance pattern ensured that Mother's allegations were never tested and Father's exculpatory evidence was never heard.

## C. MCR 2.003(C)(1) — Actual Bias Standard

MCR 2.003(C)(1) requires disqualification when the judge is "personally biased or prejudiced for or against a party." The indicators described above — individually and cumulatively — satisfy this standard. The trial court's denial of Father's disqualification motion should itself be reviewed under a de novo standard given the objective evidence of bias.

## D. Proposed Standard for Structural Bias Assessment

This Court should establish a standard for assessing structural bias claims in family court, considering:

1. Whether the ex parte rate exceeds a threshold that this Court should define.
2. Whether motion dispositions show statistically significant asymmetry.
3. Whether the court has avoided evidentiary proceedings that would test one party's allegations.
4. Whether the cumulative pattern cannot be explained by legitimate judicial discretion.

# VIII. WHY COURT OF APPEALS REVIEW IS INSUFFICIENT

## A. The Systemic Nature of the Violations

The Court of Appeals is well-suited to correct individual legal errors. It is less well-suited to address the systemic issues presented here:

1. **No precedential authority**: Court of Appeals decisions do not bind other panels or trial courts with the same force as this Court's decisions.
2. **Limited remedial authority**: The Court of Appeals can reverse and remand, but cannot establish the statewide standards necessary to prevent recurrence.
3. **No superintending power**: Only this Court has the constitutional authority to exercise superintending control over the lower courts. Mich Const 1963, art 6, § 4.

## B. The Need for Binding Precedent

Michigan family courts process thousands of custody cases annually. The constitutional standards for ex parte practice, evidentiary hearings, and judicial impartiality in these proceedings affect every parent and every child in the state. Only this Court can establish binding precedent that:

1. Defines the constitutional minimum for ex parte practice in family courts.
2. Establishes when a pattern of asymmetric treatment mandates disqualification.
3. Requires evidentiary hearings before complete severance of parenting time.
4. Creates effective remedies for structural due process violations.

## C. Fundamental Right Requires Highest Court's Protection

The Supreme Court of the United States has recognized that when fundamental parental rights are at stake, the judicial system bears a heightened obligation to ensure adequate process:

> "In [parental rights] cases, the individual interest at stake . . . is commanding; the State's interest . . . is de minimis."

*M.L.B. v S.L.J.*, 519 US 102, 121 (1996).

This heightened obligation is best discharged when the highest court in the state — this Court — speaks directly to the constitutional requirements.
