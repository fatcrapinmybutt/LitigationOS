### II. BASIS FOR EMERGENCY RELIEF

#### A. Irreparable Harm — Each Day of Separation Causes Cumulative, Irreversible Developmental Damage

6. Michigan's Child Custody Act recognizes that the emotional bond between parent and child is a paramount consideration in custody determinations. MCL 722.23(a) ("the love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child"); MCL 722.23(c) ("the capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance").

7. The complete severance of parenting time for 204 days (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026) is not merely a temporary inconvenience — it is the functional termination of a parental relationship without the procedural protections required for such an extreme measure. Michigan law presumes that "it is in the best interests of a child for the child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents." MCL 722.27a(1).

8. Child development research uniformly establishes that prolonged parent-child separation during ages three through five causes measurable harm to attachment security, emotional regulation, and cognitive development. These harms are cumulative and, beyond a critical period, become irreversible. The child in this case has now passed through virtually the entire critical window of early childhood development without any contact with Father.

9. The trial court's August 8, 2025 suspension was based "solely on allegations and unsworn evidence" (Record, docket event dated August 8, 2025), without the required best-interest-factor analysis under MCL 722.23(a)-(l), and without the procedural protections mandated by MCR 3.207(A) for ex parte custody and parenting-time modifications.

10. Michigan law requires that a court may not suspend parenting time unless "the child's physical, mental, or emotional health would be endangered" by the continuation of parenting time. MCL 722.27a(3). No such finding was made. No such evidence was presented under oath. The ex parte motion "fails to allege that delaying for" a noticed hearing would cause irreparable harm — the very threshold MCR 3.207(B)(5)-(6) demands for ex parte relief.

11. The harm is irreparable because no monetary award, no future make-up parenting time, and no appellate reversal can restore 204 lost days of a child's developmental window. *See Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 56 (1992) (recognizing that delay in custody matters works irreparable harm). This Court's emergency intervention is the only remedy that can arrest the ongoing injury.

#### B. Likelihood of Success on the Merits — Five Preserved Issues Under *Carines* Plain Error

12. Father has preserved five issues for appeal, each independently warranting reversal under the *Carines* plain-error framework and *de novo* review:

13. **Issue 1: Ex Parte Due Process Violations.** The trial court entered 24 of 55 total orders (43.6%) ex parte, including the August 8, 2025 parenting-time suspension, without compliance with MCR 3.207(A) and (B)(5)-(6), which require (a) specific facts in a verified pleading or sworn testimony, (b) a showing that immediate and irreparable injury will result from delay, and (c) a hearing within 14 days. No verified pleading was filed. No sworn testimony was taken. No 14-day hearing was held before the suspension became indefinite.

14. **Issue 2: Failure to Conduct Best-Interest-Factor Analysis.** The trial court terminated Father's parenting time without analyzing the 12 best-interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)-(l). Michigan law requires that any parenting-time modification — even one that does not alter the established custodial environment — must be supported by a preponderance of evidence analyzed through the best-interest factors. *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 78-79 (2017). No such analysis was conducted before or after the August 8, 2025 suspension.

15. **Issue 3: Judicial Bias and Disqualification.** The record documents multiple judicial violations by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, including ex parte communications with Defendant, off-record handling of evidence, refusal to consider Father's exculpatory evidence, and a documented pattern of proceedings in which Father was denied meaningful participation. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)-(b) requires disqualification where a reasonable person would perceive bias. The combination of (a) 42 ex parte events, (b) refusal to review Father's photographic evidence, (c) willingness to rely on unsworn allegations, and (d) contempt sanctions against Father for asserting his rights creates an overwhelming appearance of partiality.

16. **Issue 4: Parental Alienation Under MCL 722.23(j).** Factor (j) requires the court to evaluate "the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." The record demonstrates that Defendant filed the ex parte motion to suspend Father's parenting time on August 8, 2025, and that the trial court granted the suspension without independent investigation. Defendant's pattern of withholding parenting time — including 37 consecutive days from March 26 to May 2, 2024, and additional withholding periods in October-November 2024 — was never addressed by the trial court.

17. **Issue 5: Violation of Fundamental Parental Rights.** The United States Supreme Court has recognized the "fundamental right of parents to make decisions concerning the care, custody, and control of their children" as "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The complete elimination of Father's parenting time for 204 days (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026), without a finding of danger and without due process protections, constitutes a deprivation of this fundamental right without due process of law, in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment.

#### C. Balance of Harms — Father Has Zero Parenting Time While Mother Has 100%

18. The current posture of this case presents the most extreme possible imbalance: Father has been allocated **zero** parenting time, while Mother has **100%** of all custody and parenting time.

19. Michigan law establishes a rebuttable presumption that parenting time with both parents is in the child's best interest. MCL 722.27a(1). The trial court's order completely eliminates this presumption without the requisite analysis and findings.

20. Restoring even supervised or limited parenting time pending appeal would serve the child's interests under MCL 722.23(a) (emotional ties), MCL 722.23(b) (guidance), MCL 722.23(c) (emotional needs), and MCL 722.23(j) (facilitation of the parent-child relationship), while creating no cognizable harm to Defendant's interests. Defendant has sole physical and legal custody; restoration of parenting time does not alter the custodial environment.

21. By contrast, denying emergency relief will cause 204 days of separation to become 300, 400, and beyond — each additional day deepening the developmental harm that is the very subject of this appeal.

#### D. Public Interest — Confidence in the Judiciary Is Undermined by Systematic Ex Parte Adjudication

22. The public has a vital interest in the integrity of the judicial process. When 43.6% of all orders in a custody case are entered ex parte — 24 of 55 orders — the appearance of impartial justice is destroyed. Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires judges to "avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." Canon 3(A)(4) prohibits ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters except under narrow, enumerated circumstances.

23. The trial court's systematic reliance on ex parte proceedings in a case involving a child's fundamental relationship with a parent undermines public confidence in the family court system. This Court's intervention would serve the public interest by ensuring that custody determinations — especially those that totally sever a parent-child bond — receive the procedural protections that Michigan law demands.

24. Additionally, the trial court's refusal to consider Father's exculpatory evidence (photographs from the July 29, 2025 exchange showing the child in good condition) while accepting Defendant's unsworn allegations creates a perception of predetermined outcomes that erodes the legitimacy of the judicial process.

#### D. This Court's Extraordinary Writ Power Under MCR 7.206

**Issue:** Whether this Court possesses authority to issue extraordinary writs to address the ongoing deprivation of Father's parental rights.

**Rule:** **MCR 7.206** vests the Court of Appeals with power to issue "extraordinary writs as necessary to effectuate its jurisdiction and to protect the rights of a party during the pendency of the appeal." MCR 7.206(A). This includes the power to issue writs of **mandamus** to compel a lower court to perform a clear legal duty, and writs of **habeas corpus** to compel the production of a person who is unlawfully detained or withheld. MCR 7.206(B) provides that such applications "may be submitted at any time" during the pendency of the appeal. The Court of Appeals' extraordinary writ authority is independent of and supplementary to its standard appellate powers.

**Application:** The trial court has failed to perform clear legal duties: it has not conducted a best-interest hearing under MCL 722.23(a)-(l) before suspending parenting time; it has not made the required endangerment finding under MCL 722.27a(3); and it has maintained a 204-day parent-child separation (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026) based on procedurally defective ex parte orders. Where the trial court refuses to comply with mandatory statutory and court-rule requirements, mandamus under MCR 7.206 is appropriate to compel performance. Additionally, the continued withholding of the minor child from Father without lawful authority — given the procedural defects in the underlying orders — supports the issuance of a writ of habeas corpus under MCR 7.206 to protect Father's fundamental parental rights during the pendency of this appeal.

**Conclusion:** MCR 7.206 provides this Court with independent authority to issue extraordinary writs — including mandamus and habeas corpus — to protect Father's rights and effectuate the Court's jurisdiction during the pendency of this appeal. Father respectfully invokes this authority.
