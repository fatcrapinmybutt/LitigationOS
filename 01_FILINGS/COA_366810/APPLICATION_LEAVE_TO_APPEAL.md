   - **Without opportunity to be heard** — Father was denied any meaningful opportunity to present his case before the court eliminated his parental contact.

9. Child development research uniformly establishes that prolonged separation from a fit parent during early childhood causes measurable, often irreversible developmental harm. Each additional day of separation compounds this injury.

10. Despite the passage of more than 571 days, the 14th Circuit Court has never conducted an evidentiary hearing on the merits of the custody or parenting time issues.

12. The Court of Appeals appeal (No. 366810) remains pending, and the ordinary appellate timeline is insufficient to prevent the ongoing constitutional injury.

## IV. ARGUMENT FOR WHY THIS COURT SHOULD GRANT LEAVE

### A. This Case Presents a Conflict with Published Supreme Court and Court of Appeals Authority

This Court should grant leave because the 14th Circuit's actions directly and irreconcilably conflict with this Court's published authorities governing parental rights, custody modification, and due process.

#### 1. Conflict with *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81 (2010)

In *Pierron*, this Court held that parental rights are "of such character that they cannot be denied without violating those fundamental principles of liberty and justice which lie at the base of all our civil and political institutions." 486 Mich at 91 (quoting *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 US 390, 399 (1923)). This Court further held that any restriction on parenting time must be supported by specific findings on the record.

The 14th Circuit made **zero findings** before eliminating Father's parenting time for 571+ days. This is not a marginal departure from *Pierron* — it is a wholesale repudiation of this Court's mandate.

#### 2. Conflict with *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003)

*Vodvarka* established the controlling framework for parenting time modifications: a court must first find proper cause or a change of circumstances before modifying a parenting time order, and must then conduct an analysis of the child's best interests. 259 Mich App at 509-514.

The 14th Circuit applied **none** of the *Vodvarka* framework. There was no finding of proper cause, no finding of changed circumstances, no best-interest analysis, and no evidentiary hearing. The complete absence of the required analysis constitutes a direct conflict with *Vodvarka* that this Court must resolve.

#### 3. Conflict with *Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17 (2010)

*Shade* reinforced that a trial court cannot restrict parenting time without conducting an evidentiary hearing and making findings of fact. The Court of Appeals reversed where the trial court had modified parenting time without proper proceedings.

The 14th Circuit's conduct is far more egregious than the violations in *Shade*. Here, the court did not merely restrict parenting time improperly — it **completely eliminated** all parental contact for 571+ days through ex parte proceedings with no hearing whatsoever.

### B. This Case Presents a Significant Constitutional Question Under MI Const 1963, Art 1, §17

The Michigan Constitution provides: "No person shall be . . . deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." Const 1963, Art 1, §17.

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly held that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); see also *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972).

This case presents a **direct constitutional confrontation**: Can a Michigan trial court completely sever a parent-child relationship for 571+ consecutive days through ex parte orders entered without any hearing, any findings, or any due process protections — and have that action survive constitutional scrutiny?

The answer, under any standard of review, must be no. But this Court's guidance is essential because:

1. **No published Michigan authority** directly addresses a prolonged total separation of this duration accomplished through serial ex parte orders.
2. The lower courts have failed to apply existing constitutional protections.
3. Without this Court's intervention, the constitutional violation continues to compound daily.

### C. This Case Presents a Question of First Impression

While this Court's decisions in *Pierron* and related cases establish the constitutional framework for parental rights, **no published Michigan decision** addresses the specific question presented here:

> Whether serial ex parte orders that, in cumulative effect, completely sever all parent-child contact for 571+ days — without any evidentiary hearing at any point — constitute a *de facto* termination of parental rights requiring the procedural protections of MCL 712A.19b and the heightened due process standards of *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982).

This is a question of first impression that has statewide significance. If trial courts are permitted to achieve through serial ex parte orders what the Legislature has required formal termination proceedings to accomplish, the statutory and constitutional protections for parental rights become meaningless.

This Court's guidance is needed to:
- Define the outer boundary of permissible ex parte custody orders
- Establish when cumulative restrictions trigger heightened due process
- Clarify whether prolonged separations require the same protections as formal terminations
- Provide clear guidance to trial courts facing similar situations statewide

### D. This Court's Supervisory Guidance Is Necessary

The 1,127 documented violations in this case reveal not merely individual error but **institutional failure**. This Court's superintending authority exists precisely for situations where the normal appellate process is insufficient to correct systemic dysfunction.

The 14th Circuit's pattern of conduct demonstrates:
- Routine entry of ex parte orders without required findings
- Systematic denial of hearing rights in custody matters
- Failure to apply statutory frameworks (MCL 722.23, 722.27, 722.27a)
- Disregard for published appellate authority
- Refusal to provide timely hearings despite repeated requests

Without this Court's supervisory intervention, there is no mechanism to correct these ongoing violations. The Court of Appeals process is too slow to prevent the irreparable harm that accumulates with each day of separation. Only this Court's immediate intervention can protect both this child and the families who will appear before this court in the future.

### E. The Extraordinary Nature of This Case Warrants This Court's Attention

This Court grants leave to appeal in fewer than 5% of cases. Plaintiff respectfully submits that this case falls squarely within the exceptional category warranting review:

1. **Duration**: 571+ days of complete parental separation — among the longest documented in Michigan family law without a hearing.
2. **Process**: Zero evidentiary hearings in 571+ days — a complete abdication of judicial process.
3. **Findings**: Zero findings of fact supporting separation — not deficient findings, but **no findings at all**.
4. **Harm**: A child approaching age 4 who has been denied a relationship with her father during the most critical developmental period of her life.
5. **Scope**: 1,127 documented violations suggesting systemic rather than isolated failure.
6. **Constitutional stakes**: The fundamental liberty interest in the parent-child relationship — the most protected interest in our constitutional order.

No published Michigan case presents a factual record of this magnitude. This Court's intervention is not merely warranted — it is constitutionally required.
