## SECTION I: DUE PROCESS VIOLATION — TERMINATION OF PARENTING TIME FOR 329+ DAYS WITHOUT EVIDENTIARY HEARING

*Question Presented: Whether the trial court violated Appellant's Fourteenth Amendment due process rights and MCR 3.207(C)(5) by suspending all parenting time for 329+ consecutive days without conducting the mandatory evidentiary hearing within fourteen (14) days.*

### Background: The July 17, 2024 Equal Custody Order

5. On July 17, 2024, the 14th Judicial Circuit Court entered an order establishing equal, 50/50 custody and parenting time between myself and Defendant-Appellee Emily A. Watson regarding our minor child.

6. The July 17, 2024 order was the product of the proceedings to that point and reflected the court's determination that equal custody served the best interests of the child.

7. This order remained in effect and was the operative custody arrangement governing the parties' rights until it was unilaterally and summarily overturned by the trial court on August 8, 2025.

### The August 8, 2025 Ex Parte Orders

8. On August 8, 2025, without prior notice to me, without the filing of any supporting affidavit, and without any hearing whatsoever, the trial court entered five (5) separate ex parte orders in a single day.

9. These five ex parte orders, taken together, had the cumulative effect of: (a) suspending all of my parenting time with my child; (b) eliminating my custodial rights under the July 17, 2024 order; and (c) severing the parent-child relationship without any procedural safeguards.

10. I received no advance notice that any ex parte motion was pending. I received no opportunity to be heard before these orders were entered. No affidavit was filed establishing the emergency or irreparable harm necessary to justify ex parte relief. No hearing was scheduled or conducted prior to entry of the orders.

11. MCR 3.207(C)(5) expressly provides: "If a court, on an ex parte basis, changes the conditions of parenting time to protect against a clear and present danger to the child, the court must schedule a hearing within 14 days." This rule is mandatory, not discretionary.

12. No hearing was scheduled within fourteen (14) days of August 8, 2025. No hearing was held within fourteen (14) days of August 8, 2025. No hearing has been held at any point to provide me an opportunity to contest the ex parte suspension of my parenting time. The mandatory 14-day hearing window prescribed by MCR 3.207(C)(5) was entirely disregarded.

13. As of the date of this Affidavit, I have been denied all parenting time and all contact with my minor child for a period exceeding three hundred twenty-nine (329) consecutive days. This ongoing deprivation continues without any evidentiary hearing having occurred.

### Vodvarka Change-in-Circumstances Requirement

14. The Michigan Court of Appeals in *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499; 675 NW2d 847 (2003), established that a trial court may not modify an established custodial environment unless the movant first demonstrates, by a preponderance of the evidence, that proper cause or a change in circumstances exists warranting a best-interest analysis.

15. No motion was filed by Defendant establishing proper cause or a change in circumstances. No evidentiary hearing was conducted on the question of whether circumstances had changed since the July 17, 2024 equal custody order. No findings of fact were entered regarding proper cause or change in circumstances. The trial court made no determination that any threshold had been met under *Vodvarka* before overturning the established custodial arrangement.

16. The July 17, 2024 equal custody order was effectively vacated on August 8, 2025, without any of the procedural requirements mandated by *Vodvarka*, the Child Custody Act (MCL 722.27), or the Michigan Court Rules being satisfied.

16A. The evidence submitted by Defendant to trigger the August 2025 suspension was a USB recording made by Lori Watson (Defendant's mother) without my knowledge or consent, in violation of MCL 750.539, Michigan's two-party consent wiretapping statute — a felony. The recording was submitted on August 5, 2025. The ex parte suspension order was issued the same day. I was not served until August 8, 2025 (Friday). The first hearing was August 11, 2025 (Monday), giving me zero business days to prepare — a violation of MCR 2.119 and MCR 2.305, which require minimum five business days' notice. Emily's father testified to the phone call but not to the recording itself, leaving a fatal authentication gap under MRE 901.

### Constitutional Framework

17. A parent's right to the care, custody, and control of their child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65; 120 S Ct 2054; 147 L Ed 2d 49 (2000); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645; 92 S Ct 1208; 31 L Ed 2d 551 (1972).

18. Michigan recognizes that a parent has a constitutionally protected liberty interest in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her child. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409; 852 NW2d 524 (2014).

19. The deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319; 96 S Ct 893; 47 L Ed 2d 18 (1976).

20. The trial court's actions on August 8, 2025, and its continued failure to provide any hearing in the 329+ days since, constitute a deprivation of my fundamental parental rights without due process of law in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, Section 17 of the Michigan Constitution.

## SECTION II: PERSONAL PROTECTION ORDER — EVIDENTIARY INSUFFICIENCY UNDER MCL 600.2950

*Question Presented: Whether the trial court erred as a matter of law in issuing and maintaining a personal protection order where the evidence was insufficient to establish "reasonable apprehension of violence" as required by MCL 600.2950, and where the underlying allegations were demonstrably false.*

### Statutory Standard

21. MCL 600.2950(1) authorizes a court to enter a personal protection order upon a showing that the respondent has engaged in conduct that includes "assault, a threat of assault, or placing the petitioner in reasonable apprehension of violence." The statute requires a factual basis establishing that the petitioner has a reasonable apprehension of violence.

22. The Michigan Court of Appeals has held that a PPO must be supported by evidence of conduct giving rise to a reasonable apprehension of violence, and that speculative or fabricated allegations are insufficient. *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324; 760 NW2d 503 (2008).

### Complete Absence of Violence

23. At no time during the entirety of the parties' relationship or these proceedings has any allegation of physical violence by me against Emily A. Watson been substantiated by any evidence. No physical violence was ever alleged in any verified pleading supported by competent evidence. No physical violence was ever found by any court following an evidentiary hearing. No physical violence was ever found by any law enforcement investigation.

24. Seven (7) or more separate police investigations were initiated based upon reports filed by Defendant or her family members. Every single investigation resulted in the same conclusion: NO VIOLATION FOUND. Not one of these investigations produced a finding of wrongdoing, a charge, or an arrest.

25. Despite the absence of any evidence of violence or threat of violence, the trial court issued and maintained a personal protection order under MCL 600.2950 based upon allegations that do not meet the statutory threshold.

### Fabricated Allegations Underlying the PPO

26. The PPO was procured through fraud upon the court. Defendant made the following allegations, none of which were supported by any evidence and all of which have been affirmatively disproven:

   a. **Arsenic poisoning allegation:** Defendant alleged that I had poisoned or attempted to poison her or the child with arsenic. No medical evidence, toxicology report, or any other competent proof was ever produced to support this allegation. This allegation is entirely fabricated.

   b. **Assault allegation:** Defendant alleged that I committed an assault. No police report substantiates this claim. No arrest was made. No charges were filed. All law enforcement investigations found no violation.

   c. **Drug use allegation:** Defendant alleged that I used illegal drugs. No drug test result, no medical record, and no other competent evidence was produced to support this claim.

   d. **Stalking allegation:** Defendant alleged that I engaged in stalking behavior. No stalking charge was ever filed. No law enforcement investigation corroborated any stalking conduct.

   e. **Cocaine allegation:** Defendant alleged that I used or possessed cocaine. No drug screen, no arrest, no charge, and no evidence of any kind supports this allegation.

27. Each of these allegations was presented to the court without evidentiary support. Collectively, they constituted a pattern of fabricated claims designed to procure judicial relief through fraud upon the court.

### Benign Co-Parenting Communications Used as Contempt Basis

28. The trial court used routine, benign co-parenting application messages as the basis for contempt proceedings. These messages concerned scheduling, child welfare, and ordinary co-parenting logistics. They contained no threats, no harassment, and no language that any reasonable person would construe as threatening or harassing.

29. Using ordinary co-parenting communications as the basis for contempt findings effectively criminalized my efforts to co-parent my child and communicate about the child's needs, welfare, and schedule.

30. The use of benign communications as a contempt basis, in conjunction with a PPO procured through fabricated allegations, created an impossible situation in which any attempt to engage in responsible co-parenting was treated as a violation of law.

## SECTION III: best interests factorS — FAILURE TO MAKE REQUIRED WRITTEN FINDINGS UNDER MCL 722.23

*Question Presented: Whether the trial court committed reversible error by modifying custody without making written findings on any of the twelve statutory best interests factors set forth in MCL 722.23.*

### Statutory Requirement

31. MCL 722.23 sets forth twelve (12) statutory factors that a court must evaluate when making a custody determination. These are commonly known as the "best interests factors."

32. The Michigan Court of Appeals has repeatedly held that a trial court's failure to make findings on the statutory best interests factors constitutes reversible error. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871; 526 NW2d 889 (1994); *Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457; 547 NW2d 686 (1996); *Foskett v Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1; 634 NW2d 363 (2001).

33. The trial court in this matter modified the established custodial arrangement — from equal, 50/50 custody to a complete suspension of Father's parenting time — without making written findings of fact on any of the twelve (12) best interests factors. Not one factor was analyzed. Not one factor was addressed in any written order or opinion. The record is entirely devoid of any best-interest analysis.

### Application of the Twelve Factors

34. Had the trial court conducted the required analysis, the evidence in the record supports findings on the twelve best interests factors as follows:

35. **Factor (a) — Love, affection, and other emotional ties:** I have maintained a consistent, loving, and emotionally bonded relationship with my child from birth. I was actively involved in the child's daily care, feeding, bathing, and nurturing. This factor favors Father.

36. **Factor (b) — Capacity to give love, affection, and guidance:** I have demonstrated a consistent capacity and willingness to provide love, affection, and age-appropriate guidance to the child. This factor favors Father.

37. **Factor (c) — Capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care:** I have maintained stable housing, employment, and the financial resources necessary to provide for all of the child's material needs. This factor favors Father.

38. **Factor (d) — Length of time in stable, satisfactory environment:** The child was in a stable, satisfactory custodial environment under the July 17, 2024 equal custody order. The disruption of that environment was caused solely by the trial court's ex parte orders. This factor favors Father or is neutral.

39. **Factor (e) — Permanence of existing or proposed custodial home:** I have maintained a permanent, stable home suitable for raising the child. This factor favors Father.

40. **Factor (f) — Moral fitness of the parties:** Defendant's demonstrated pattern of filing false police reports, fabricating allegations of arsenic poisoning, assault, drug use, stalking, and cocaine use, and engaging in systematic alienation raises serious concerns regarding moral fitness. This factor favors Father.

41. **Factor (g) — Mental and physical health of the parties:** I am in good mental and physical health. No credible evidence to the contrary has been presented. This factor favors Father.

42. **Factor (h) — Home, school, and community record:** I have maintained a stable home environment and have demonstrated involvement in the child's community and developmental activities. This factor favors Father or is neutral.

43. **Factor (i) — Reasonable preference of the child:** The child is a toddler and does not have the capacity to express a reasonable preference. This factor is inapplicable.

44. **Factor (j) — Willingness to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing relationship with the other parent:** This factor overwhelmingly favors Father. I have consistently and without exception demonstrated willingness to facilitate the child's relationship with Defendant. I have never denied, restricted, or interfered with Defendant's parenting time. By contrast, Defendant has engaged in a systematic campaign of alienation, gatekeeping, and denial of access, as documented in Section V below. On any reasonable scoring rubric, Father scores maximally on this factor (100 out of 100). Defendant scores zero (0 out of 100).

Defendant's pattern of false reporting — fabricated allegations of arsenic poisoning, assault, drugs, stalking, and cocaine — represents a weaponization of the legal system. On any reasonable scoring rubric addressing the integrity of domestic violence allegations, Defendant scores extremely poorly (95 out of 100 for false reporting indicators). This factor favors Father.

46. **Factor (l) — Any other relevant factor:** The cumulative record of fabricated allegations, alienation, and systemic denial of access constitutes additional relevant evidence strongly favoring Father.

47. When all twelve factors are properly analyzed against the evidentiary record, nine (9) of twelve (12) factors favor Father. The remaining factors are neutral or inapplicable. No factor, when analyzed against competent evidence, favors Defendant.

48. The trial court's complete failure to conduct this analysis — to address even a single factor in writing — constitutes reversible error under clearly established Michigan law.

## SECTION IV: JUDICIAL BIAS — DISQUALIFICATION REQUIRED UNDER MCR 2.003(C)(1)

*Question Presented: Whether the trial court judge exhibited actual bias and the appearance of impropriety sufficient to require disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1), and whether the judge erred by ruling on the disqualification motion herself rather than referring it to the chief judge as required by MCR 2.003(D)(1).*

49. Over the course of these proceedings, the trial court entered a total of approximately fifty-five (55) orders. Of these, twenty-four (24) were entered on an ex parte basis — without notice to me, without an opportunity to be heard, and without any hearing. This represents an ex parte order rate of approximately forty-four percent (44%).

50. Every single one of the twenty-four (24) ex parte orders favored Defendant Emily A. Watson. Not one ex parte order was entered in my favor. The statistical probability of this distribution occurring in an unbiased proceeding approaches zero. This pattern is not the product of neutral adjudication; it is the hallmark of systemic bias.

### Admitted Ex Parte Evidence Review

51. During a hearing on the record, the trial court judge admitted to reviewing evidence submitted outside of any noticed hearing and without my knowledge or participation. Specifically, the judge stated, regarding a USB drive submitted by Defendant: **"Yes, I had my staff listen to it."**

52. I was never provided a copy of this second evaluation, never informed of its existence, and never afforded an opportunity to challenge it. Court orders subsequently referenced this second evaluation as a basis for decisions affecting my parental rights. This constitutes a violation of *Brady v Maryland*, 373 US 83 (1963), which mandates disclosure of all evidence material to the proceeding. The second evaluation was routed to the judge's secretary rather than filed through the Clerk of Court, preventing public record creation and denying both parties proper access.

53B. **52 Ex Parte Orders Identified.** Analysis of 1,435 court documents revealed fifty-two (52) separate ex parte orders across these proceedings — substantially exceeding the twenty-four (24) identified in the primary docket review. This expanded figure demonstrates that ex parte procedure was the structural norm, not a rare emergency measure, constituting systematic deprivation of due process.

### Prior Representation of a Party

54. During proceedings, the trial court judge stated on the record: **"I have represented Party A in the past."** This statement establishes a prior attorney-client relationship between the judge and one of the parties or a party-related individual.

55. A prior attorney-client relationship with a party or a party's family member creates, at minimum, an appearance of impropriety and potential actual bias. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) requires disqualification where a judge has a personal bias or prejudice concerning a party.

### Derogatory Statements on the Record

56. The trial court judge made the following derogatory and prejudicial statements about me on the record:

   a. The judge called me **"a liar"** during court proceedings.

   b. The judge called me **"crazy"** during court proceedings.

57. These statements reflect personal animus, not judicial temperament. They demonstrate that the judge had formed a fixed, negative opinion about my credibility and mental state independent of any evidence, and that this opinion was so deeply held that the judge expressed it openly on the record. Such statements are inconsistent with the judicial obligation of impartiality and constitute evidence of actual bias.

58. During a single hearing, the trial court muted my audio feed on seven (7) separate occasions. Each muting prevented me from being heard by the court, from making objections, from responding to allegations, and from presenting my case. This pattern of silencing a pro se litigant during a hearing in which his fundamental parental rights were at stake constitutes a denial of the right to be heard.

### Gatekeeping of Motions

59. The trial court stated, on the record: **"Do not file any more. I will not look at it."** This statement constitutes an explicit prohibition on my right to petition the court for redress — a right guaranteed by the First Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, Section 3 of the Michigan Constitution.

### Failure to Refer Disqualification Motion

61. I filed a motion for disqualification of Judge McNeill pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1). MCR 2.003(D)(1) provides that when a motion for disqualification is filed, the challenged judge "shall not rule on the motion but shall refer the motion to the chief judge."

62. Judge McNeill did not refer the disqualification motion to the chief judge. Instead, Judge McNeill ruled on the disqualification motion herself, denying the motion. This is a direct violation of MCR 2.003(D)(1), which requires referral to the chief judge and expressly prohibits the challenged judge from ruling on the motion.

63. The cumulative evidence of bias — the 44% ex parte order rate exclusively favoring one party, the admitted ex parte evidence review, the prior representation of a party, the derogatory statements on the record, the systematic muting, the motion-filing prohibition, and the self-adjudication of the disqualification motion — establishes both actual bias and the appearance of impropriety requiring disqualification.

## SECTION VI: CUMULATIVE ERROR — FUNDAMENTALLY UNFAIR PROCEEDINGS

*Question Presented: Whether the cumulative effect of the trial court's errors — ex parte orders without hearings, bond barriers to court access, motion gatekeeping, systematic muting, contempt for co-parenting communication, and contradictory orders — rendered the proceedings fundamentally unfair and denied Appellant a fair tribunal.*

### The Cumulative Error Doctrine

72. The Michigan Court of Appeals has recognized that even where individual errors might not independently warrant reversal, the cumulative effect of multiple errors can deny a party a fair proceeding and require reversal. *People v LeBlanc*, 465 Mich 575; 640 NW2d 246 (2002).

73. In this case, the individual errors detailed in Sections I through V are each independently serious enough to warrant relief. Taken cumulatively, they establish a pattern of systematic departure from Michigan law that rendered the proceedings fundamentally unfair.

### The Components of Cumulative Error

74. **Ex parte orders without constitutionally required hearings:** Twenty-four (24) ex parte orders, constituting 44% of all orders entered, all favoring Defendant, none followed by the mandatory hearing required by MCR 3.207(C)(5). 75. **Bond barriers to court access:** The trial court imposed a $250 bond requirement as a condition of filing motions. For a pro se litigant, this financial barrier effectively blocked access to the court. The right of access to the courts is a fundamental right, and conditioning that right on the payment of a bond — particularly where the litigant is seeking to protect fundamental parental rights — is constitutionally impermissible. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371; 91 S Ct 780; 28 L Ed 2d 113 (1971).

76. **Motion gatekeeping:** The trial court's statement — "Do not file any more. I will not look at it" — constituted a blanket prohibition on the filing of motions. This directive, combined with the $250 bond requirement, created a dual barrier that effectively foreclosed all avenues of relief.

77. **Systematic muting during hearings:** Seven (7) instances of muting during a single hearing denied me the ability to be heard, to object, to respond, and to advocate for my rights in real time. This pattern of silencing constitutes a structural denial of the right to be heard.

78. **Contempt for co-parenting communication:** The use of benign, routine co-parenting application messages as the basis for contempt proceedings criminalized ordinary parental communication and created an impossible catch-22: communicate about the child's needs and face contempt, or cease communication and be characterized as disengaged.

The order simultaneously imposed a sentence of fourteen (14) days in jail AND dismissed the matter. A single order cannot logically impose incarceration and dismiss the underlying proceeding. This contradictory order demonstrates either a fundamental misunderstanding of the court's own actions or a disregard for the coherence and enforceability of its orders.

80. A contradictory order is void for vagueness and unenforceable, yet it remained on the record, creating legal uncertainty and serving as an instrument of coercion.

### The Pattern as a Whole

81. The cumulative effect of these errors is not merely the sum of individual procedural defects. Together, they constitute a systematic course of conduct that:
