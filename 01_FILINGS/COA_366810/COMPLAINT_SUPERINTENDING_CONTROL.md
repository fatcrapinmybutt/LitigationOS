## I. JURISDICTIONAL BASIS

1. This Complaint for Superintending Control is filed pursuant to MCR 7.306 and Const 1963, Art 6, §4, which vests this Court with "general superintending control over all courts" and empowers it to "issue, hear, and determine prerogative and remedial writs."

2. The Michigan Supreme Court's power of superintending control is an original jurisdiction that may be exercised independently of the appellate process. *Bd of Ed of the City of Detroit v. Superintendent of Public Instruction*, 319 Mich 436, 442 (1947).

3. Superintending control is appropriate to "prevent a miscarriage of justice" and to "correct an abuse of discretion" when no other adequate remedy exists. *Plumbers & Pipefitters Local 190 v. Wolff*, 141 Mich App 815, 818 (1985).

4. This Court has exercised superintending control to direct lower courts to perform duties they are clearly required to perform. *Slis v. State of Michigan*, 332 Mich 284 (1952).

## II. STATEMENT OF THE CASE

5. Andrew J. Pigors ("Father") commenced a custody action in the 14th Circuit Court for the County of Muskegon, Case No. 2024-001507-DC, regarding his minor child, Lane G. (born approximately 2021).

6. Over the course of this proceeding, the 14th Circuit Court entered a series of ex parte orders that progressively restricted and ultimately eliminated Father's parenting time with Lane G.

7. These ex parte orders were entered:
   - Without an evidentiary hearing at any stage;
   - Without findings of fact as required by MCL 722.23, MCL 722.27, and MCR 3.207(B);
   - Without a determination of proper cause or change of circumstances as required by *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003);
   - Without affording Father notice or meaningful opportunity to be heard.

8. As of the date of this Complaint, Father has been completely separated from Lane G. for more than **571 consecutive days** without any parenting time whatsoever.

9. The 14th Circuit Court has failed to conduct an evidentiary hearing on the custody and parenting time issues despite the passage of 571+ days and despite Father's repeated requests for hearing.

10. Father has filed a Claim of Appeal with the Court of Appeals (Case No. 366810), which remains pending.

## III. THE 14TH CIRCUIT COURT HAS EXCEEDED ITS AUTHORITY

11. A trial court's authority to enter ex parte orders in custody matters is limited and circumscribed by both statute and court rule. Ex parte relief is an extraordinary remedy reserved for true emergencies. MCR 3.207(B).

12. MCR 3.207(B) requires that ex parte orders be supported by specific findings that:
    - An immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice; or
    - The notice itself will precipitate adverse action before the order can be issued.

13. The 14th Circuit Court made **no such findings** in any of the ex parte orders entered in this case. (Appendix B.)

14. A trial court that enters ex parte orders without the required emergency findings acts **in excess of its jurisdiction**. The orders are not merely erroneous — they are void *ab initio* for lack of the jurisdictional predicates required by court rule.

15. Even where ex parte orders are properly entered, MCR 3.207(B)(4) requires that a hearing be scheduled "as soon as possible" — typically within 14 days. The 14th Circuit's failure to hold any hearing for 571+ days violates this mandatory requirement by a factor of **more than 40 times** the permitted duration.

16. The 14th Circuit Court's actions constitute a complete departure from accepted and usual judicial practice:
    - Entry of custody orders without hearing violates MCL 722.27;
    - Restriction of parenting time without findings violates MCL 722.27a;
    - Failure to apply best-interest factors violates MCL 722.23;
    - Failure to find proper cause or change of circumstances violates *Vodvarka*;
    - Complete severance of parent-child relationship without due process violates Const 1963, Art 1, §17 and US Const, Amend XIV, §1.

17. Plaintiff has documented **1,127 discrete violations** of court rules, statutes, and constitutional requirements committed by the 14th Circuit Court in this case, demonstrating that the court's departure from lawful procedure is not isolated but systemic.

## IV. THERE IS NO ADEQUATE REMEDY AT LAW

18. Superintending control is warranted where the complainant has "no other adequate legal remedy." MCR 7.306(B)(2). This standard is met here for multiple independent reasons.

### A. The Court of Appeals Process Is Too Slow

19. Father's Claim of Appeal (No. 366810) remains pending before the Court of Appeals. The ordinary timeline for resolution of a claim of appeal — including briefing, oral argument, and decision — typically extends 12-24 months or longer.

20. Lane G. is approaching the age of 4. Each day of separation during this critical developmental window causes compounding, measurable harm to the child's attachment, bonding, and cognitive development.

21. If Father must await the ordinary appellate process, Lane G. will be 5 or 6 years old before any relief is obtained. By that time, the critical developmental window will have closed, and the harm to the parent-child relationship will be **irreversible**.

22. An appeal that arrives too late to prevent the very harm complained of is not an "adequate" remedy. *In re Sands*, 390 Mich 160, 173 (1973) (superintending control appropriate where delay renders other remedies inadequate).

### B. The Injury Is Constitutional and Ongoing

23. The deprivation at issue is not a past injury that can be remedied by retroactive relief. Father's fundamental liberty interest in the parent-child relationship is being violated **every single day** that the unconstitutional ex parte orders remain in force.

24. This Court has recognized that ongoing constitutional violations may warrant extraordinary relief because they cannot be adequately remedied by ordinary appellate processes. The passage of time does not cure a due process violation — it compounds it.

### C. The Trial Court Has Demonstrated an Inability or Unwillingness to Self-Correct

25. Father has made multiple requests and filings in the 14th Circuit Court seeking a hearing on the custody and parenting time issues. The trial court has failed to provide timely relief.

26. The 14th Circuit's persistent failure to conduct the hearing it is required to provide — despite 571+ days of opportunity — demonstrates that the trial court will not self-correct without this Court's intervention.

27. Where a trial court refuses or fails to perform a clear legal duty, the normal appellate remedy of remand is futile. Superintending control is the only mechanism that can direct the trial court to act.

## V. FATHER HAS A CLEAR LEGAL RIGHT

28. The Michigan Legislature has established a **presumption** that parenting time is in the best interest of the child. MCL 722.27a(1) ("Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child. It is presumed to be in the best interests of a child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents.").

29. This presumption may only be overcome by a showing on the record that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. MCL 722.27a(7). No such showing has been made in this case because **no hearing has been held**.

30. Father has a clear legal right to:
    - A hearing before his parenting time is modified or restricted (MCL 722.27; *Vodvarka*);
    - Specific findings of fact supporting any restriction (*Pierron*; MCL 722.23);
    - Application of the statutory presumption favoring parenting time (MCL 722.27a);
    - Due process of law before deprivation of his fundamental liberty interest (Const 1963, Art 1, §17; US Const, Amend XIV).

---

## VI. THE TRIAL COURT HAS A PLAIN LEGAL DUTY

31. The 14th Circuit Court has a **plain legal duty** to:

    a. **Conduct an evidentiary hearing** before modifying custody or restricting parenting time. MCL 722.27(1)(c) ("The court shall not modify or amend its previous judgments or orders or make new judgments or orders so as to change the established custodial environment of a child unless there is presented clear and convincing evidence that it is in the best interest of the child."); *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 512;

    b. **Make findings of fact** regarding the child's best interests under MCL 722.23. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92-93;

    c. **Apply the parenting time presumption** of MCL 722.27a and make specific findings if parenting time is to be restricted. MCL 722.27a(7);

    d. **Provide notice and opportunity to be heard** before entering orders affecting parental rights. Const 1963, Art 1, §17; *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976);

    e. **Schedule a hearing following ex parte orders** "as soon as possible." MCR 3.207(B)(4).

32. These duties are mandatory, not discretionary. The 14th Circuit Court has failed to perform any of them in 571+ days.
