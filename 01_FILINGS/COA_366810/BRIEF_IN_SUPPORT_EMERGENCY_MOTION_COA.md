IV. ARGUMENT

A. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED DUE PROCESS BY SUSPENDING PARENTING TIME THROUGH EX PARTE ORDERS

Issue: Whether the trial court's entry of 24 ex parte orders, including the order suspending Appellant's parenting time, violated procedural due process.

Rule: The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees that no state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." US Const, Am XIV. The Michigan Constitution independently guarantees that "[n]o person shall be... deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law." Const 1963, Art 1, § 17. The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest entitled to the highest constitutional protection under both provisions. Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57, 65; 120 S Ct 2054; 147 L Ed 2d 49 (2000). Santosky v Kramer, 455 US 745, 753; 102 S Ct 1388; 71 L Ed 2d 599 (1982).

At minimum, due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before deprivation of a protected interest. Mathews v Eldridge, 424 US 319, 333; 96 S Ct 893; 47 L Ed 2d 18 (1976). Michigan Court Rules strictly limit ex parte orders in custody and parenting-time matters. MCR 3.207(B)(1) and (B)(5)-(6) require that an ex parte order be supported by specific facts in a verified pleading demonstrating that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will occur before the adverse party can be served and heard. The court must also schedule a hearing within 14 days. MCR 3.207(B)(5).

Application: The trial court entered 24 ex parte orders during these proceedings. The critical order suspending Appellant's parenting time was entered without notice and without a hearing. The record does not reflect that the verified-pleading requirements of MCR 3.207(B)(1) were satisfied. The record reflects that Judge McNeill "signed an ex parte 'emergency' order suspending all of [Appellant's] parenting time with [his] three-year-old son, Lincoln" — a fact documented in multiple critical-severity judicial violations.

When an evidentiary hearing was eventually held, the trial court refused to review Appellant's exculpatory photographs while relying on ex parte submissions from Defendant. This compounded the due process violation: the hearing that should have cured the ex parte deprivation instead became another forum for one-sided adjudication.

The pattern of 24 ex parte orders is not an isolated procedural irregularity — it is a systematic denial of Appellant's right to be heard that infected the entire proceeding.

Conclusion: The trial court's entry of ex parte custody and parenting-time orders without compliance with MCR 3.207(B) and without affording Appellant meaningful notice and opportunity to be heard violated the Due Process Clause. This Court should exercise its authority under MCR 7.216(A) to enter temporary relief restoring parenting time pending full appellate review.


B. THE $250 FILING SANCTION UNCONSTITUTIONALLY BARS ACCESS TO JUSTICE

Issue: Whether the trial court's $250-per-motion filing sanction violates Appellant's right of access to the courts.

Rule: The Michigan Constitution guarantees that "[e]very person for an injury done to him in his person or property shall have remedy by due course of law." Const 1963, Art 1, § 13. The United States Constitution similarly protects the right of access to courts. Bounds v Smith, 430 US 817, 821; 97 S Ct 1491; 52 L Ed 2d 72 (1977).

While courts have inherent authority to impose reasonable sanctions for frivolous filings under MCR 2.114 and MCR 2.625, such sanctions must not operate as a complete bar to a litigant's access to the courts, particularly in cases involving fundamental rights. In re Contempt of Dougherty, 429 Mich 81, 96; 413 NW2d 392 (1987) (courts must exercise contempt power with restraint).

Filing restrictions that require pre-payment before a motion will even be reviewed are subject to heightened scrutiny when they affect access to fundamental rights. A pre-filing monetary condition is functionally equivalent to a prior restraint on the right to petition the court.

Application: The trial court ordered that "the Plaintiff must pay a bond of $250 (for each motion) before he may file another motion in this matter. The Court will then review the motion." This order creates an unprecedented financial barrier: Appellant must pay $250 merely for the privilege of having the court read his motion — with no guarantee the motion will be granted or even heard.

For a pro se litigant whose parenting time has been suspended, this sanction is catastrophic. Appellant cannot file a motion to modify parenting time, a motion for reconsideration, or any other motion without first paying $250. The trial court has effectively created a pay-to-play system that prices Appellant out of the only forum where he could seek modification of the parenting-time suspension.

The practical effect is that Appellant has no remedy in the trial court. This forces Appellant to seek relief in this Court — the only forum where the financial barrier does not apply.

Conclusion: The $250 filing sanction operates as an unconstitutional bar to Appellant's right of access to courts, particularly given that fundamental parental rights are at stake. This Court should order the trial court to permit Appellant to file motions without the sanction and should grant temporary relief that the trial court's sanction prevents Appellant from seeking.


C. THE TRIAL COURT FAILED TO MAKE REQUIRED BEST-INTEREST FINDINGS

Issue: Whether the trial court abused its discretion by suspending parenting time without making findings on each of the twelve best-interest factors required by MCL 722.23.

Rule: Michigan's Child Custody Act requires that custody and parenting-time decisions be based on the "best interests of the child," defined as "the sum total of the following factors to be considered, evaluated, and determined by the court." MCL 722.23. The twelve factors are:

    (a) The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.
    (b) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.
    (c) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care.
    (d) The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.
    (e) The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.
    (f) The moral fitness of the parties involved.
    (g) The mental and physical health of the parties involved.
    (h) The home, school, and community record of the child.
    (i) The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.
    (j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents.
    (k) Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.
    (l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

The trial court must make explicit findings on each factor. Vodvarka v Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 1, 8; 672 NW2d 847 (2003). Failure to do so is an abuse of discretion. Fletcher v Fletcher, 447 Mich 871, 882; 526 NW2d 889 (1994).

Application: The trial court suspended Appellant's parenting time entirely — the most extreme deprivation short of termination of parental rights — without entering written findings on any of the twelve best-interest factors. There is no order in the record that addresses, for example, the emotional ties between Appellant and Lincoln (factor a), Appellant's capacity to provide love and guidance (factor b), or the critical question of whether either party has been willing to facilitate the parent-child relationship (factor j).

This omission is not a technicality. The best-interest factors exist precisely to prevent arbitrary deprivation of parenting time. By suspending parenting time without this analysis, the trial court acted without the factual and legal foundation required by statute.

Factor (j) is particularly relevant here: the record suggests that Defendant has been unwilling to facilitate Appellant's relationship with the child, yet the trial court has rewarded this behavior by suspending Appellant's parenting time without examining whether Defendant's conduct constitutes parental alienation under MCL 722.23(j). MCL 722.23(j) requires the court to evaluate "[t]he willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." The record reflects that Defendant withheld parenting time for 37 consecutive days (March 26–May 2, 2024) without court intervention, withheld additional periods from October 20 to November 12, 2024, refused to facilitate video chats, obstructed medical information sharing, and declined to cooperate with make-up parenting days — a pattern that Michigan courts have recognized as evidence of parental alienation weighing against the obstructing parent. See Rains v Rains, 301 Mich App 313, 324-328; 836 NW2d 709 (2013) (alienating conduct properly considered under factor (j)). The trial court's failure to evaluate this pattern — and its decision to instead reward Defendant's obstructive behavior by eliminating Appellant's parenting time entirely — constitutes a clear abuse of discretion.

Conclusion: The trial court's failure to make required best-interest findings before suspending parenting time constitutes an abuse of discretion. The trial court's simultaneous failure to evaluate Defendant's alienating conduct under MCL 722.23(j) further demonstrates that the suspension was not based on a proper best-interest analysis but on one-sided ex parte proceedings. This Court should enter temporary relief restoring parenting time and direct the trial court to conduct a proper best-interest analysis on remand.


D. THE RECORD DEMONSTRATES SYSTEMATIC JUDICIAL BIAS REQUIRING RELIEF

Issue: Whether 1,127 documented judicial violations demonstrate a pattern of bias that warrants temporary relief from this Court.

Rule: MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides for disqualification of a judge when "the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned." The Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires judges to "perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice" (Canon 3) and prohibits ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters except under narrow circumstances (Canon 3(A)(4)).

On appeal, this Court reviews de novo whether a trial judge should have been disqualified. In re MKK, 286 Mich App 546, 564; 781 NW2d 132 (2009). Constitutional issues, including due process violations, are also reviewed de novo. In re Sanders, 495 Mich 394, 404; 852 NW2d 524 (2014).

Application: The record in this case reveals an extraordinary pattern of judicial misconduct:

    377 critical-severity violations, predominantly involving MCR 2.003 (Disqualification) -- including ex parte communications, off-record handling of evidence, and refusal to consider exculpatory evidence.
    243 high-severity violations reflecting systematic procedural irregularities.
    24 ex parte orders entered during the proceedings -- a pattern that goes far beyond isolated error and reflects institutional bias.
    Refusal to review exculpatory evidence while relying on unsworn, ex parte submissions from the opposing party.
    Off-record, ex parte handling of evidence, including a USB recording and mental health evaluation that were processed outside Appellant's knowledge or participation.

These are not isolated errors subject to harmless-error analysis. They represent a systematic pattern of bias that infected the entire proceeding. A reasonable observer would question the trial court's impartiality.

Conclusion: The documented pattern of judicial violations demonstrates bias requiring this Court's intervention. Temporary relief is necessary to protect Appellant's rights while the merits of the appeal — including the disqualification issue — are fully briefed and decided.


E. TEMPORARY RELIEF IS NECESSARY TO PREVENT IRREPARABLE HARM

Issue: Whether the ongoing parent-child separation constitutes irreparable harm warranting temporary relief from this Court pending appeal.

Rule: Irreparable harm exists when the injury cannot be adequately compensated by monetary damages and the harm will be ongoing absent injunctive relief. In cases involving fundamental constitutional rights, the deprivation of the right itself constitutes irreparable harm. Elrod v Burns, 427 US 347, 373; 96 S Ct 2673; 49 L Ed 2d 547 (1976).

The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest. Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Stanley v Illinois, 405 US 645, 651; 92 S Ct 1208; 31 L Ed 2d 551 (1972) ("The private interest here, that of a man in the children he has sired and raised, undeniably warrants deference and, absent a powerful countervailing interest, protection."). Every day of separation causes harm that cannot be undone — lost developmental milestones, weakened bonds, and psychological damage to both parent and child.

Michigan policy favors family preservation. MCL 722.954a. MCL 722.27a establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time, which may be denied only upon a showing that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. MCL 722.27a(3).

Application: Appellant has been separated from his son for 569+ days, with no contact whatsoever for 214 days. The child, Lincoln, was born in 2022 and is now approximately three years old. At this critical developmental stage, extended separation from a parent has profound and lasting effects on the child's emotional and psychological development. See Affidavit of Andrew Pigors, ¶¶ 29-31 (filed herewith).

Child development research demonstrates that reunification success rates decline dramatically with the length of separation, dropping from approximately 85% after short separations to approximately 25% after 12 months. See Affidavit of Andrew Pigors, ¶ 31 (filed herewith). Appellant is rapidly approaching and may have already passed the critical threshold beyond which the parent-child bond may be irreparably damaged.

This is not a case where the harm is speculative or future. The harm is occurring every day, right now. It is cumulative, it is accelerating, and it cannot be compensated by any remedy this Court could order after the fact. No amount of future parenting time can give Lincoln back the 569+ days he has lost with his father.

The balance of hardships is stark: granting supervised parenting time pending appeal poses no risk to the child (supervision addresses any safety concerns) while denying relief guarantees continued irreparable harm to both Appellant and the child. There is simply no equitable basis for maintaining a complete severance of the parent-child relationship when supervised contact can protect all interests.

Conclusion: The ongoing 569+-day separation constitutes irreparable harm that compels this Court to exercise its authority under MCR 7.216(A) to enter temporary relief restoring parenting time pending appeal. The deprivation of Appellant's fundamental liberty interest — protected by both the Fourteenth Amendment and Const 1963, Art 1, § 17 — for nearly a year, without a hearing on the merits and without best-interest findings, is a constitutional violation of the highest order that demands this Court's immediate intervention.
