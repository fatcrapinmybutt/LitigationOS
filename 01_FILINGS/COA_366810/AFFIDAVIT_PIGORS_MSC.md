## IV. CONSTITUTIONAL MAGNITUDE OF THE VIOLATIONS

### A. Fundamental Parental Liberty

17. The right of a fit parent to the care, custody, and control of his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982).

18. This fundamental right cannot be terminated or substantially restricted without, at minimum: (a) notice; (b) an opportunity to be heard; and (c) a finding, supported by evidence, that restriction serves the child's best interest. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976); MCL 722.23; MCL 722.27.

19. None of these constitutional minimums were met. My parenting time was suspended in its entirety without a hearing, without an evidentiary finding, and without written best-interest analysis.

### B. Ex Parte Orders — August 8, 2025

20. On August 8, 2025, the trial court entered five (5) ex parte orders on a single day. I received no prior notice of any of these orders. I was given no opportunity to be heard before any of these orders were entered. No affidavit supporting emergency ex parte relief was filed or served upon me, as required by MCR 2.119(B) and MCR 3.207.

21. These five ex parte orders collectively suspended all of my parenting time with L.D.W. — the functional equivalent of terminating a fit father's parental rights without any process whatsoever.

22. The entry of five ex parte orders on a single day, without notice, without hearing, and without supporting affidavit, is not a procedural irregularity. It is a constitutional violation of the most fundamental kind.

22A. **Same-Day Evidence-to-Order Pipeline.** The USB recording that triggered the August 2025 suspension was submitted on August 5, 2025. The ex parte suspension order was issued the same day — before I was served. I was served on August 8, 2025 (Friday). The first objection hearing was August 11, 2025 (Monday), giving me zero business days to prepare. MCR 2.119 and MCR 2.305 require minimum five business days' notice. This was the fourth documented instance of inadequate service.

22B. **Criminally Obtained Evidence.** The USB recording was made by Lori Watson (Mother's mother) without my knowledge or consent, in violation of MCL 750.539, Michigan's two-party consent wiretapping statute — a felony. Emily's father testified to the phone call but not to the recording itself, leaving a fatal authentication gap under MRE 901. See *People v Bednarz*, 360 Mich 552 (1960); *People v Burge*, 722 NW2d 470 (2006).

22C. **52 Ex Parte Orders.** A comprehensive analysis of 1,435 court documents revealed fifty-two (52) separate ex parte orders — far exceeding the twenty-four (24) identified in the initial docket review. This expanded figure demonstrates that the 44% ex parte rate was, if anything, an undercount of the true scope of systematic due process deprivation.

### C. Statistical Pattern of Ex Parte Abuse

23. Across the life of these cases, I have documented that approximately forty-four percent (44%) of all orders entered against me have been ex parte orders — entered without notice, without hearing, and without opportunity to respond.

24. A forty-four percent ex parte order rate is a statistical impossibility if the court is operating within the bounds of due process. A rate of 44% demonstrates that ex parte procedure has become the norm, not the exception — a systematic deprivation of due process.

### D. Access to Courts

## V. SYSTEMATIC PROCEDURAL VIOLATIONS

### A. MCR 3.207(C)(5) — 14-Day Hearing Requirement

30. MCR 3.207(C)(5) requires that when a temporary custody order is entered ex parte, a hearing must be held within fourteen (14) days. This rule exists specifically to prevent the kind of prolonged deprivation of parental rights that has occurred here.

31. Zero hearings were held within the 14-day window required by MCR 3.207(C)(5) following any of the ex parte orders entered in these cases. Not one.

32. The first hearing permitted after the August 8, 2025 ex parte suspension of all parenting time did not occur until October 29, 2025 — eighty-two (82) days later. This is not a minor deviation from the 14-day rule. It is a wholesale abandonment of it.

### B. MCL 722.23 — best interests findings

33. MCL 722.23 enumerates the factors that a court must consider and make written findings on before making or modifying a custody or parenting time determination. This is not discretionary — it is mandatory.

34. Zero written best-interest findings under MCL 722.23 have been entered in connection with the suspension of my parenting time. The trial court has terminated a fit father's contact with his three-year-old child without ever putting pen to paper on the statutory best-interest factors.

### C. Vodvarka — Change of Circumstances

35. Under *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003), a court may not modify an established custodial environment without first finding, by clear and convincing evidence, that a change of circumstances has occurred and that modification is in the child's best interest.

36. On July 17, 2024, the court ordered equal 50/50 custody — establishing the custodial environment. Subsequent orders dramatically altered this custodial arrangement without any change-of-circumstances finding. No *Vodvarka* analysis was conducted. No clear and convincing evidence standard was applied.

### D. Order Entered "Without a Hearing"

37. The order entered on May 16, 2025 states on its face that it was entered "without a hearing." An order that modifies custody or parenting time entered without any hearing — not even a pro forma one — is void as a matter of due process.

### E. Evidence Handling Violations

38. A HealthWest evaluation was conducted and cleared me of the concerns raised. The trial court disregarded this evaluation without explanation.

39. A second evaluation was subsequently relied upon by the court, but this evaluation was never properly authenticated under MRE 901. It was presented without foundation, without the evaluator's testimony, and without any opportunity for cross-examination. Reliance on an unauthenticated evaluation to terminate a fundamental right violates both the Michigan Rules of Evidence and the Due Process Clause.

39A. **Brady v Maryland Violation.** The second evaluation constitutes a Brady violation. *Brady v Maryland*, 373 US 83 (1963), requires disclosure of all evidence material to the proceeding. The trial court obtained this second evaluation, relied upon it in orders, and never disclosed it to me. The trial court's role as both the obtaining party and the non-disclosing party makes this violation particularly egregious. I was specifically instructed to email the first evaluation to the judge's secretary rather than filing through the Clerk of Court — further circumventing proper disclosure and record-keeping requirements.
