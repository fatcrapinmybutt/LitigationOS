# JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT

This Court has jurisdiction over this appeal pursuant to MCR 7.203(A)(1), which grants the Michigan Court of Appeals jurisdiction over appeals of right from final judgments and final orders of the circuit court.

The 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County (Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, presiding) entered final orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC that determined custody and suspended Plaintiff-Father's parenting time. These orders constitute final orders as defined in MCR 7.202(6) because they resolved the substantive rights of the parties regarding custody and parenting time with the parties' minor child.

Plaintiff-Appellant Andrew J. Pigors filed a timely Claim of Appeal pursuant to MCR 7.204(A)(1). This appeal is properly before this Court as an appeal of right from a final order in a domestic relations proceeding involving child custody and parenting time.

This brief is filed within the time prescribed by MCR 7.212(A)(1)(a), as extended by order of this Court.

# ARGUMENT

## I. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY ENTERING EX PARTE ORDER (MCR 3.207(B))S SUSPENDING ALL PARENTING TIME WITHOUT VERIFIED PLEADING OR SPECIFIC FINDINGS AS REQUIRED BY MCR 3.207(B)(1) AND MCR 2.517(A)(1)

### A. Standard of Review

The trial court's compliance with court rule requirements is reviewed de novo as a question of law. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 85 (2010). Whether the trial court followed mandatory procedural requirements for ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s is a legal question that this Court reviews without deference. *Id.* The trial court's factual findings underlying custody and parenting time decisions are reviewed for clear error. *Id.* The overall custody decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion. See *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388; 719 NW2d 809 (2006). *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 325 (2006).

**Preservation:** This issue was preserved by Father's timely objection to the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) and motion to set aside the August 8, 2025 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)). (Lower Ct. Rec., pp. 50–51, Objection; Tr. 9/22/2025, pp. 5–8.) Father raised the lack of verified pleading and specific findings at the September 22, 2025 evidentiary hearing (Tr. 9/22/2025, pp. 5–8) and again at the November 26, 2025 hearing (Tr. 11/26/2025, pp. 4–7).

MCR 3.207(B)(1) governs the issuance of ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s in domestic relations cases. The rule provides:

> A party may obtain an ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) in a domestic relations action only if specific facts shown by verified complaint, petition, or affidavit clearly demonstrate that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice or that notice itself will precipitate adverse action before an order can be issued.

MCR 3.207(B)(1) (emphasis added).

This rule imposes three mandatory requirements before a trial court may enter an ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)): (1) the request must be supported by a **verified complaint, petition, or affidavit**; (2) the supporting document must contain **specific facts** — not conclusions, generalities, or speculation; and (3) those facts must **clearly demonstrate** immediate and irreparable injury. MCR 3.207(B)(1). Each requirement is conjunctive. Failure to satisfy any one is fatal to the order.

### C. The August 8, 2025 Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)) Failed All Three Requirements

The record demonstrates that the August 8, 2025 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) suspending all of Father's parenting time failed each of MCR 3.207(B)(1)'s requirements.

**First**, the ex parte motion was not supported by the requisite verified pleading. Mother's motion was filed the same day the order was signed and did not contain the verified factual showing required by the rule. (LC Ex Parte Motion, 8/8/2025; LC Docket.) MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires "specific facts shown by verified complaint, petition, or affidavit." A bare motion, unverified and unsupported by affidavit, does not satisfy this threshold. MCR 3.207(B)(1).

**Second**, the motion did not set forth specific facts demonstrating immediate and irreparable injury. (LC Ex Parte Motion, 8/8/2025.) The rule demands more than conclusions or allegations of general concern. The movant must identify particular, concrete, articulable facts that, if true, establish that the child faces immediate and irreparable harm absent ex parte relief. (LC Ex Parte Motion, 8/8/2025.) No such facts were presented.

**Third**, the motion did not clearly demonstrate that the delay required to effect notice would itself cause irreparable injury, or that notice would precipitate adverse action. MCR 3.207(B)(1). The filing was made on a scheduled parenting time exchange date. (LC Docket.) Yet the motion offered no explanation for why notice to Father — even same-day notice — was impracticable or would have caused harm.

### D. MCR 2.517(A)(1) Required Written Findings

MCR 2.517(A)(1) provides that "[i]n actions tried without a jury, the court shall find the facts specially, state separately its conclusions of law, and direct entry of the appropriate judgment." This rule applies to ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s affecting parenting time because such orders determine the substantive rights of the parties.

The August 8, 2025 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) contains no written findings of fact. (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 8/8/2025.) The order does not identify what specific harm the child faced. It does not explain why notice to Father was impracticable. It does not address any of the best interest factors (MCL 722.23) under MCL 722.23. It is, in substance, a blank suspension of a fundamental parental right — entered without the analytical foundation that MCR 2. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982).517(A)(1) and MCR 3.207(B)(1) require.

### E. The Prior Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)) Suffered the Same Deficiency

The August 8, 2025 order was not an isolated procedural lapse. The July 1, 2024 emergency ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) — which initially granted sole custody to Mother — likewise contained no specific findings of fact and was entered without notice to Father or opportunity to be heard. (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 7/1/2024.) The pattern of entering consequential ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s without the rule-required findings establishes a systemic failure to comply with MCR 3.207(B)(1).

### F. The Error Was Not Harmless

The ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) of August 8, 2025 eliminated all of Father's parenting time. That suspension has continued for 571+ days. During that time, Father has had no contact with his three-year-old child. This is not a case where a procedural deficiency produced an inconsequential result. The absence of required findings undergirds the total deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982). Under these circumstances, the error cannot be deemed harmless. The order must be reversed.

## II. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED FATHER'S DUE PROCESS RIGHTS UNDER US CONST AMEND XIV AND MI CONST ART 1 § 17 BY DEPRIVING HIM OF PARENTAL RIGHTS WITHOUT NOTICE, HEARING, OR OPPORTUNITY TO BE HEARD

### A. Standard of Review

Constitutional questions, including alleged violations of due process, are reviewed de novo. *Kubicki v Sharpe*, 306 Mich App 525, 538 (2014). This Court owes no deference to the trial court's resolution of constitutional issues. *Id.*

**Preservation:** Father raised the due process issue by objecting to the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))'s entry without notice or hearing (Lower Ct. Rec., pp. 50–51, Objection) and by arguing at the September 22, 2025 hearing that the deprivation of parental rights without notice violated due process. (Tr. 9/22/2025, pp. 6–8; Tr. 11/26/2025, pp. 5–7.) Additionally, constitutional challenges to the sufficiency of process need not be preserved where, as here, the deprivation of a fundamental right is at issue. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

The right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of his child is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982)." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This right is protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Id.* at 66; *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972) ("The rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed 'essential.'").

Michigan independently protects this right. MI Const 1963, Art 1, § 17 provides: "No person shall be . . . deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law." The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that parental rights are liberty interests protected by both the state and federal due process clauses. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

Because parental rights are fundamental, government action that deprives a parent of the care and custody of his child must satisfy heightened procedural and substantive requirements. *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982). The state may not sever the parent-child relationship based on a mere preponderance of the evidence; the clear and convincing evidence standard applies. *Id.* at 769.

### C. The Three-Factor Mathews v. Eldridge Analysis

The adequacy of procedural due process is evaluated under the three-factor framework of *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335 (1976):

> (1) the private interest affected by the official action;
> (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional or substitute procedural safeguards; and
> (3) the government's interest, including the fiscal and administrative burden of additional procedures.

*Mathews*, 424 US at 335.

Each factor weighs decisively in Father's favor.

**First — the private interest.** The interest at stake is a parent's right to maintain a relationship with his child — an interest the Supreme Court has described as "far more precious than any property right." *Santosky*, 455 US at 758–59. A 571+ day total deprivation of all parental contact implicates this interest at its maximum weight. This is not a case involving a temporary schedule adjustment. The trial court eliminated all contact between Father and a child in the earliest years of life.

**Second — the risk of erroneous deprivation.** The procedures used created an extreme risk of erroneous deprivation. The ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) was entered without: (a) notice to Father; (b) a hearing at which Father could be heard; (c) verified pleading or affidavit containing specific facts of harm; (d) written findings of fact; (e) application of the clear and convincing evidence standard; or (f) any time limitation or automatic review mechanism. (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 8/8/2025; LC Docket.) The absence of every procedural safeguard magnified the risk of error to the highest possible degree. Additional safeguards — notice, an opportunity to respond, an evidentiary hearing, and written findings — would have had substantial value in reducing this risk and were readily available.

**Third — the government's interest.** The government has a legitimate interest in protecting children from harm. But the government also has an interest in accurate adjudication and in maintaining public confidence in the judiciary. Providing Father with notice and a hearing before eliminating all contact with his child would have imposed minimal administrative burden. The trial court routinely conducts hearings on contested motions. There is no fiscal or logistical reason why a hearing could not have occurred before — or promptly after — the August 8, 2025 order was entered.

All three *Mathews* factors favor reversal.

### D. Substantive Due Process Was Also Violated

Due process has both procedural and substantive components. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65–66. Substantive due process prohibits the government from depriving a person of a fundamental right without adequate justification, regardless of the procedures followed. *Id.*

Here, the trial court deprived Father of all contact with his child for 571+ days without ever making a finding, by any evidentiary standard, that parenting time would endanger the child. MCL 722.27a(3) requires that before parenting time may be denied, "it [must be] shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(3). The trial court made no such finding. (LC Record.)

The total elimination of parental contact for 571+ days — without any finding of endangerment — violates substantive due process because there exists no rational basis, let alone a compelling justification, for the deprivation. *Santosky*, 455 US at 753 (the state must demonstrate "adequate justification" before severing parental ties); *Troxel*, 530 US at 68–69 (courts must give "special weight" to a fit parent's wishes regarding the care of his child).

### E. The Self-Reinforcing Cycle of Deprivation

The proceedings below created a constitutionally impermissible cycle. The trial court entered an ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) suspending parenting time. The suspension led to contempt proceedings. Contempt resulted in incarceration. Incarceration caused employment loss and housing instability. The court then cited Father's changed circumstances — circumstances the court's own orders produced — as further justification for continued restriction of parenting time.

A court may not manufacture the conditions it then relies upon to justify continued deprivation of a fundamental right. *Santosky*, 455 US at 764 ("The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982).").

### F. The Duration of Deprivation Compels Reversal

The Supreme Court in *Santosky* emphasized that the duration and finality of a deprivation of parental rights bear directly on the process that is due. 455 US at 758–59. A 571+ day complete severance of a parent-child relationship — imposed on a child who was three years old when the separation began — is, for practical purposes, permanent. At this developmental stage, 571 days constitutes a substantial fraction of the child's conscious memory. This Court should consider the duration of the deprivation as an independent basis for finding a due process violation.

## III. THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY FAILING TO MAKE ANY MCL 722.23 BEST INTEREST FINDINGS BEFORE SUSPENDING ALL PARENTING TIME FOR 571+ DAYS

### A. Standard of Review

Custody and parenting time decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. See *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388; 719 NW2d 809 (2006). *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 85 (2010). The trial court's factual findings are reviewed for clear error. *Id.* Questions of law — including whether the trial court applied the correct legal framework — are reviewed de novo. *Id.* A trial court abuses its discretion when it fails to select a principled outcome or when it makes an error of law. *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 325 (2006).

**Preservation:** Father raised the absence of best interest findings at the February 14, 2025 hearing (Tr. 2/14/2025, pp. 10–12), at the September 22, 2025 hearing (Tr. 9/22/2025, pp. 7–9), and at the November 26, 2025 hearing (Tr. 11/26/2025, pp. 6–8). Father specifically argued that the trial court was required to make MCL 722.23 factor-by-factor findings before modifying parenting time. (Lower Ct. Rec., pp. 50–51, Objection; Tr. 9/22/2025, pp. 7–9.)

Before modifying custody or substantially altering parenting time, the trial court must evaluate and make findings on each of the twelve best interest factors (MCL 722.23) enumerated in MCL 722.23(a)–(l). *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 889 (1994) ("The trial court must state its findings and conclusions with respect to each of the best interest factors (MCL 722.23)."); *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004) (reaffirming *Fletcher*'s factor-by-factor requirement).

This requirement is mandatory. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that it is "not enough for a trial court to simply state that it has considered the best interest factors (MCL 722.23)." *Id.* The court must make specific findings as to each factor. *Id.* Where the trial court fails to address all twelve factors, reversal and remand are required. *Id.*

### C. The Trial Court Made No Best Interest Findings

The trial court's orders reference "the best interest of the child (MCL 722.23)" in passing. (LC Order, 2/14/2025.) The February 14, 2025 order cites MCL 722.23 and MCL 722.27a(7). (LC Order, 2/14/2025.) But citation of a statute is not compliance with it. The orders contain no analysis of any individual factor. No factor is weighed. No factor is applied to the facts of this case. The trial court's orders are devoid of the particularized findings that *Fletcher* and *Harvey* require.

This deficiency persists across every order entered in the proceedings below. The August 8, 2025 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) contains no best interest analysis (MCL 722.23). (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 8/8/2025.) The September 22, 2025 order contains no best interest analysis (MCL 722.23). (LC Order, 9/22/2025.) The November 26, 2025 order contains no best interest analysis (MCL 722.23). (LC Order, 11/26/2025.) At no point did the trial court make findings on any of the twelve factors before or after eliminating all of Father's parenting time.

### D. The Vodvarka Threshold Was Never Satisfied

Before reaching the best interest factors (MCL 722.23), the trial court was required to determine whether a proper basis for modification existed. Under *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509 (2003), a party seeking custody modification (MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003)) must establish, by a preponderance of the evidence, either that proper cause exists or that a change of circumstances has occurred since the last custody order. MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 509.

The record does not reflect that the trial court made any threshold finding of changed circumstances or proper cause before entering orders that eliminated Father's parenting time. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 509. This is an independent error requiring reversal.

### E. The Absence of Findings Precludes Appellate Review

The purpose of the *Fletcher* requirement is to provide a reviewable record. *Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457, 462 (1996). Without factor-by-factor findings, this Court cannot determine whether the trial court's decision was within the range of principled outcomes or was arbitrary. *Id.* The complete absence of findings here makes meaningful appellate review impossible. This alone justifies remand with instructions to conduct a full best interest analysis (MCL 722.23).

### F. The Error Is Structural, Not Harmless

Where the trial court fails to analyze any of the twelve best interest factors (MCL 722.23) before making a custody or parenting time determination, the error is structural. It is not susceptible to harmless error analysis because the reviewing court has no findings to evaluate. The absence of any MCL 722.23 analysis before imposing a 571+ day suspension of all parenting time cannot be excused as a mere procedural irregularity. The trial court's failure deprived Father of the legal framework that Michigan law provides as a safeguard against arbitrary custody decisions. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889; *Harvey*, 470 Mich at 192.

## IV. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY IMPOSING THE MOST RESTRICTIVE REMEDY WITHOUT CONSIDERING LESS RESTRICTIVE ALTERNATIVES

### A. Standard of Review

Parenting time decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. See *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388; 719 NW2d 809 (2006). *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21 (2010). A trial court abuses its discretion when it fails to consider the applicable statutory framework or selects an outcome that is disproportionate to the circumstances. *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 600 (2004).

**Preservation:** Father preserved this issue by filing motions for restoration of parenting time (Lower Ct. Rec., pp. 22–26, Motion, 1/15/2025) and by arguing at each hearing that the trial court should consider graduated or supervised alternatives before total elimination. (Tr. 2/14/2025, pp. 11–13; Tr. 9/22/2025, pp. 8–10; Tr. 11/26/2025, pp. 7–9.) Father specifically objected that the court imposed the most restrictive remedy without considering less restrictive alternatives. (Lower Ct. Rec., pp. 50–51, Objection.)

Both constitutional and statutory law require that restrictions on parenting time be no more burdensome than necessary to address demonstrated concerns regarding the child's welfare.

Constitutionally, when the state restricts a fundamental right, it must employ the least restrictive means available. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 73 (2000). A total elimination of all parental contact is the most extreme restriction possible — exceeded only by formal termination of parental rights. The Due Process Clause requires a trial court to consider whether less restrictive alternatives — supervised visitation, therapeutic visitation, graduated reintroduction, or modified schedules — would adequately address any legitimate safety concern. *Santosky*, 455 US at 753.

Statutorily, MCL 722.27a provides a graduated framework of remedies. The statute presumes parenting time is in the child's best interest. MCL 722.27a(1). It requires clear and convincing evidence before parenting time may be denied entirely. MCL 722.27a(3). And when parenting time has been wrongfully denied, it mandates application of five statutory remedies. MCL 722.27a(9)(a)–(e).

### C. The Trial Court Imposed Total Elimination Without Intermediate Steps

The August 8, 2025 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) eliminated all of Father's parenting time. (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 8/8/2025.) The order did not impose supervised visitation. It did not order therapeutic visitation. It did not provide for phone or video contact. It did not establish a graduated reintroduction schedule. It imposed the maximum possible restriction — complete severance of the parent-child relationship — as a first resort.

The record does not reflect that the trial court considered any less restrictive alternative before entering the order. (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 8/8/2025; LC Docket.) The absence of any analysis of intermediate options is itself an abuse of discretion. See *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388; 719 NW2d 809 (2006). When multiple remedies are available and the court selects the most extreme without explaining why lesser alternatives are inadequate, the decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Shade*, 291 Mich App at 21.

### D. The Court Inverted the Statutory Framework

Rather than enforcing Father's parenting time rights under MCL 722.27a, the trial court:

1. Signed Mother's ex parte motion to *suspend* parenting time on August 8, 2025. (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B)), 8/8/2025.)
2. Ordered Father to undergo a mental health assessment as a *precondition* to any restoration of parenting time. (LC Order, 9/22/2025.)
3. Adjourned hearings repeatedly, extending the separation. (LC Docket.)
4. Denied Father's objection to the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)). (LC Order, 11/26/2025.)
5. Characterized Father's legal objections as "categorical rejection" of supervised visitation. (LC Order, 11/26/2025.)

This sequence inverts MCL 722.27a's framework. The statute places the burden on the party *denying* parenting time to show, by clear and convincing evidence, that the child would be endangered. MCL 722.27a(3). The trial court instead placed the burden on the *deprived* parent to prove his fitness before parenting time would be restored. This burden-shifting is reversible error. *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 600 (2004).

### E. The Five Statutory Remedies Were Never Applied Against Mother

MCL 722.27a(9) provides five mandatory remedies when a parent's parenting time has been wrongfully denied:

> (a) Compensating the aggrieved parent with additional parenting time.
> (b) Requiring the offending parent to pay costs and attorney fees.
> (c) Finding the offending parent in contempt.
> (d) Modifying the custody or parenting time order.
> (e) Imposing any other reasonable remedy.

MCL 722.27a(9)(a)–(e).

Despite 571+ days of denied parenting time, the trial court applied none of these remedies against Mother. The court did not order makeup parenting time under MCL 722.27a(7) — a remedy the statute makes mandatory through use of the word "shall." *Laffin v Laffin*, 280 Mich App 513, 517 (2008). The court did not find Mother in contempt. The court imposed no consequences for Mother's complete denial of the father-child relationship. *Pickering v Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694, 700 (2002) (trial court must fashion remedies that preserve the parent-child relationship).

### F. The Failure to Apply Factor (j) Is an Independent Error

MCL 722.23(j) requires the trial court to evaluate "[t]he willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." Despite 571+ days of total denial of Father's contact with the child, the trial court made no finding under Factor (j) regarding Mother's willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship. *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 715 (2008) (pattern of interference with the other parent's relationship is highly relevant under Factor (j)). This omission is an independent abuse of discretion that must be addressed on remand.

## V. THE TRIAL COURT'S PATTERN OF BIAS AND EX PARTE CONDUCT WARRANTS REVERSAL AND REASSIGNMENT TO A DIFFERENT JUDGE

### A. Standard of Review

Whether a judge should be disqualified is reviewed for abuse of discretion. See *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388; 719 NW2d 809 (2006), but the legal standards governing disqualification are reviewed de novo. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001). Where the question is whether due process requires disqualification under *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), the analysis is constitutional and subject to de novo review.

**Preservation:** Father preserved this issue by filing a Judicial Tenure Commission complaint against Hon. Jenny L. McNeill on or about September 1, 2025 (Lower Ct. Rec., p. 7, Register of Actions, Entry 9/1/2025), and by raising judicial conduct concerns on the record at the November 26, 2025 hearing. (Tr. 11/26/2025, pp. 3–5.) The pattern of ex parte conduct and off-record handling of evidence was documented in Father's filings. (Lower Ct. Rec., pp. 50–51, Objection.)

The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause requires "a fair trial in a fair tribunal." *In re Hathaway*, 464 Mich 672, 683 (2001). Disqualification is constitutionally required when "a serious risk of actual bias impact[s] the due process rights of a party." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b); *Caperton*, 556 US at 884.

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides two independent grounds for disqualification:

> (a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
> (b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton*, or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)–(b).

### C. The Record Establishes a Pattern Warranting Disqualification

This is not a case of isolated adverse rulings, which do not establish bias. *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9, 11 (1991). The record reveals a pattern of conduct that, viewed in its totality, demonstrates a serious risk of actual bias:

1. **Multiple ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s without required findings.** The trial court entered ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s on at least two occasions — July 1, 2024 and August 8, 2025 — suspending Father's parenting time without the specific findings required by MCR 3.207(B)(1). (LC Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(B))s, 7/1/2024 and 8/8/2025.)

2. **Off-record handling of evidence.** Evidence was submitted to and considered by the court outside Father's presence, including a USB recording and a mental health evaluation that were handled off the record. (LC Record.) Canon 3(A)(5) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct prohibits ex parte communication (MCR 3.207(B))s about pending matters.

3. **Denial of Father's opportunity to be heard.** Father's participation was restricted during proceedings. Father's legal objections were characterized as noncompliance rather than addressed on their merits. (LC Order, 11/26/2025; LC Transcripts.)

4. **Systematic one-sided application of procedural tools.** The PPO was used to restrict Father's parenting time and ability to communicate regarding the child. (LC Record.) Mother's ex parte motions were granted without the rule-required showing. (LC Docket.) Father's motions for restoration of parenting time were denied without best interest findings. (LC Record.)

5. **Failure to address Mother's parenting time denial.** Despite 571+ days of denied parenting time, the trial court never applied any of the five statutory remedies of MCL 722.27a(9) against Mother, while simultaneously holding Father in contempt. (LC Record.)

Viewed objectively, this pattern would cause a reasonable, informed observer to have serious concerns about the trial court's impartiality. *In re Hathaway*, 464 Mich at 683. The record demonstrates not merely adverse rulings, but a structural pattern in which one party's motions are granted without evidentiary foundation while the other party's rights are systematically curtailed without legal basis.

### D. Reassignment Is Warranted Under MCR 7.216(A)

MCR 7.216(A)(7) authorizes this Court to "enter any judgment or order or grant further or different relief as the case may require." This includes the authority to remand with instructions to reassign the case to a different judge. *Bayati v Bayati*, 264 Mich App 595, 602 (2004).

Reassignment is warranted where the record reveals a level of judicial conduct that calls into question the fundamental fairness of the proceedings and where remand to the same judge would be futile. *Bayati*, 264 Mich App at 602. The pattern documented in this record — entry of ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s without required findings, off-record handling of evidence, denial of Father's opportunity to be heard, and systematic failure to apply statutory protections to Father — satisfies this standard.

The remedy is proportionate to the harm. Father has been deprived of all contact with his child for 571+ days based on proceedings that lacked the procedural and substantive safeguards required by the Michigan constitution, the United States Constitution, and Michigan's Child Custody Act. Remand to the same judge would perpetuate the conditions that produced these errors. Reassignment is necessary to ensure fair adjudication on remand. *In re Haley*, 476 Mich 180, 193 (2006) (judicial conduct standards must be enforced to maintain public confidence in the judiciary); *Caperton*, 556 US at 884.
