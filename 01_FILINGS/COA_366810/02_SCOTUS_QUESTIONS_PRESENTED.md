### QUESTION 1: Due Process and Extended Custody Deprivation Without Hearing

> Whether the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment prohibits a state family court from effectively terminating a fit parent's custodial rights for a period exceeding 571 consecutive days through successive temporary orders, ex parte proceedings, and adjournments, without ever conducting an evidentiary hearing at which the parent has a meaningful opportunity to be heard, where this Court has recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court," *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

**Why This Question Warrants Review:**
- Directly implicates the fundamental liberty interest in parenting recognized in *Troxel*, *Santosky*, *Stanley v. Illinois*, and *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*
- State courts lack uniform standards for when temporary custody restrictions become de facto terminations requiring heightened procedural protections
- The *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test has never been squarely applied by this Court to prolonged temporary custody deprivations
- Affects thousands of parents nationwide subjected to extended "temporary" orders without meaningful review

### QUESTION 2: Structural Judicial Bias Through Systematic Ex Parte Communications

> Whether a state court judge's documented pattern of ex parte communications occurring in 44% of proceedings — including substantive contacts with one party's counsel, receipt of information outside the adversarial process, and off-record case discussions — constitutes a structural due process violation requiring automatic vacatur under the Fourteenth Amendment, without requiring a showing of actual prejudice, consistent with this Court's holding in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), that due process is violated where the "probability of actual bias" is "too high to be constitutionally tolerable."

**Why This Question Warrants Review:**
- *Caperton* established an objective "probability of bias" standard but left open its application to systematic ex parte communications
- Lower courts are divided on whether a pattern of ex parte contacts requires automatic vacatur or a showing of actual prejudice
- Family courts operate with less transparency and fewer procedural safeguards than other tribunals, making ex parte abuse more prevalent and harder to detect
- The question presents a Rule 10(c) departure from "accepted and usual course of judicial proceedings"

---

### QUESTION 3: Personal Protection Orders as Instruments of Custody Deprivation

> Whether the Due Process Clause requires heightened procedural safeguards — including a prompt adversarial hearing with a clear-and-convincing evidence standard — before a state court may issue or continue a Personal Protection Order (PPO) in a custody dispute, where the PPO operates to deprive the respondent parent of custodial rights, residential access, firearms rights under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(8), and other constitutionally protected interests, and where this Court recently addressed the intersection of protective orders and constitutional rights in *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. ___ (2024).

**Why This Question Warrants Review:**
- *Rahimi* addressed firearms restrictions under protective orders but did not resolve the broader due process requirements for issuance of protective orders that trigger cascading constitutional deprivations
- State standards for PPO issuance in custody disputes vary dramatically — some states require only a preponderance showing, others require clear and convincing evidence, and some permit issuance based on unsworn allegations
- PPOs have become a widely documented tactical tool in custody disputes nationwide, yet procedural protections for respondents have not kept pace
- The collateral consequences of PPOs (NICS entries, custody presumptions, housing restrictions, employment effects) aggregate into a deprivation far exceeding what minimal procedural protections contemplate
- The question intersects Due Process, Second Amendment (*Rahimi*), and First Amendment (where PPOs restrict speech or communication)

## PRESENTATION NOTES

### Framing Strategy:
1. **Lead with Question 1** — It is the most sympathetic and clearly grounded in established precedent (*Troxel*, *Santosky*, *Mathews v. Eldridge*)
2. **Question 2 extends *Caperton*** — The Court may be interested in defining the limits of its structural bias doctrine
3. **Question 3 follows *Rahimi*** — Timely, given the Court's recent engagement with protective order jurisprudence

### Circuit Split / State Conflict Indicators:
- **Q1:** Compare Michigan's approach with states that mandate periodic review hearings for temporary custody orders (e.g., California's 25-day review requirement)
- **Q2:** Compare jurisdictions requiring automatic vacatur for ex parte violations vs. harmless error review
- **Q3:** Compare PPO evidentiary standards across states (preponderance vs. clear and convincing; sworn vs. unsworn allegations)

### Tone and Style:
- Questions should read as **inevitable constitutional issues** the Court must eventually address
- Avoid case-specific facts in the question itself — save details for the Statement of the Case
- Each question should be understandable to a law clerk reading it for the first time in 30 seconds
