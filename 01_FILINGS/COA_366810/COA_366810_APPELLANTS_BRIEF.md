# STANDARD OF REVIEW

## A. Custody Determinations — Abuse of Discretion

This Court reviews custody decisions for an abuse of discretion. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 85 (2010). The trial court's factual findings are reviewed for clear error, and questions of law are reviewed de novo. *Id.* A trial court abuses its discretion when its decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 325 (2006).

A trial court's failure to make findings on all twelve best interest factors under MCL 722.23 is reversible error. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 889 (1994). "The trial court must state its findings and conclusions with respect to each of the best interest factors." *Id.* Where the trial court fails to address all twelve factors, this Court will reverse and remand. *Id.*

## B. Constitutional Issues — De Novo

Constitutional questions, including alleged violations of due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment, are reviewed de novo. *Kubicki v Sharpe*, 306 Mich App 525, 538 (2014). A parent's right to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

## C. Parenting Time — Abuse of Discretion

A trial court's parenting time decision is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21 (2010). A trial court abuses its discretion when it fails to apply the statutory framework. *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 600 (2004). The trial court's failure to apply any of the five statutory remedies under MCL 722.27a(9) when parenting time has been denied constitutes an abuse of discretion. MCL 722.27a(9)(a)–(e).

## D. Judicial Disqualification — De Novo / Abuse of Discretion

Whether a judge should be disqualified under MCR 2.003 is reviewed for an abuse of discretion, but the application of the legal standards governing disqualification is reviewed de novo. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001). Where there is a "serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party," disqualification is constitutionally required. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b); *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868, 884 (2009).

## E. Remand to Different Judge — De Novo

This Court's authority to remand with instructions to reassign to a different judge under MCR 7.216(A)(4) and (A)(7) is reviewed de novo as a question of law. *Bayati v Bayati*, 264 Mich App 595, 602 (2004).

## I. THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY FAILING TO MAKE FINDINGS ON ALL TWELVE BEST INTEREST FACTORS UNDER MCL 722.23 BEFORE MODIFYING CUSTODY AND SUSPENDING PARENTING TIME

### A. The Legal Standard: Every Factor Must Be Addressed

Michigan law is unambiguous: before a court may modify custody or substantially alter parenting time, it must evaluate and make findings on each of the twelve best interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23(a)–(l). *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 889 (1994) ("The trial court must state its findings and conclusions with respect to each of the best interest factors."). This requirement is mandatory, not discretionary. *Id.*

The twelve factors are:

> (a) The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.
> (b) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.
> (c) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care recognized and permitted under the laws of this state in place of medical care, and other material needs.
> (d) The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.
> (e) The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.
> (f) The moral fitness of the parties involved.
> (g) The mental and physical health of the parties involved.
> (h) The home, school, and community record of the child.
> (i) The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.
> (j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents. A court may not consider negatively for the purposes of this factor any reasonable action taken by a parent to protect a child or that parent from sexual assault or domestic violence by the child's other parent.
> (k) Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.
> (l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

MCL 722.23(a)–(l).

### B. The Trial Court Failed to Conduct an Adequate Factor-by-Factor Analysis

**Application.** The record demonstrates that the trial court's best interest analysis was fatally deficient. While the court made some oral findings at a hearing, the resulting written order was drafted by Defendant-Appellee's counsel using only "parts of the rulings the Court made regarding the best interest factors." (LC Record, Court Order, 11/26/2025; App. D.) This selective incorporation rendered the written findings incomplete.

Moreover, Plaintiff-Appellant's objection to this incomplete order was rejected because he "failed to follow the Court rules, in stating with specificity what parts of the order he objected to." (LC Record, Court Order, 11/26/2025; App. D.) This created a procedural trap: the opposing party drafted an incomplete order, and the pro se litigant's objection was rejected on technical grounds — leaving the record with an inadequate best interest analysis that the court itself endorsed.

Most critically, the August 8, 2025 ex parte order that suspended all parenting time contained **no** best interest findings whatsoever. (LC Record, Ex Parte Order, 8/8/2025; App. B.) The subsequent orders confirming that suspension likewise failed to conduct a new best interest analysis accounting for the 329+ day separation — a changed circumstance of enormous magnitude.

The February 14, 2025 order dismissing Plaintiff-Appellant's Motion for Parenting Time found only that "Nothing in Plaintiff's Motion indicates a change of circumstances as required by law." (LC Record, Court Order, 2/14/2025; App. E.) The court dismissed the motion without any best interest analysis, despite acknowledging it concerned "the best interest of the child to change parenting time." (LC Record, Court Order, 2/14/2025; App. E.)

As to the court's own earlier factor findings, they actually support reversal. The court found under Factor (a) that "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and that "[t]his Factor is equal between the parties." (LC Record, Court Order re: Best Interest Factors; App. E.) Having found an equal bond, the court could not then permit that bond to be destroyed through 329+ days of complete separation without revisiting its analysis — as *Fletcher* requires. 447 Mich at 889.

### C. The Change-of-Circumstances Threshold Was Never Properly Established

Before even reaching the best interest factors, the trial court was required to determine whether a proper basis for modification existed. Under *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 499, 509 (2003), a movant must establish by a preponderance of the evidence that a change of circumstances has occurred or that proper cause exists before the court may reopen the custody question. MCL 722.27(1)(c).

The trial court's own order of February 14, 2025 found: "Nothing in Plaintiff's Motion indicates a change of circumstances as required by law." (LC Record, Court Order, 2/14/2025; App. E.) Yet, paradoxically, the court later entertained Watson's ex parte motion to *modify* the existing parenting arrangement — suspending all parenting time — without requiring any threshold showing of changed circumstances from Watson. This asymmetric application of the *Vodvarka* standard — demanding it of the father seeking restoration while waiving it for the mother seeking suspension — is itself reversible error. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 509.

### D. Appellee's Likely Response and Reply

**Appellee's Likely Response:** Appellee will likely argue that (1) the trial court implicitly considered the best interest factors, (2) the emergency nature of the ex parte proceedings excused full analysis, and (3) the court made adequate oral findings at hearing.

**Reply:** None of these arguments survives scrutiny. First, the Michigan Supreme Court has expressly rejected "implicit consideration" as satisfying the *Fletcher* requirement. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889 ("The trial court *must state* its findings.") (emphasis added). Second, MCR 3.207(B)(1) provides a mechanism for emergency ex parte orders, but that mechanism requires "specific facts set forth in an affidavit or verified pleading" and does not excuse the court from eventually making best interest findings once a hearing is held. The record shows multiple hearings occurred — on September 22, 2025, October 29, 2025, and November 26, 2025 — yet no new or complete best interest analysis was ever conducted addressing the separation. Third, even if the court made oral findings at an earlier hearing, those findings were undermined by Watson's counsel drafting the written order using only "parts" of the rulings. (LC Record, Court Order, 11/26/2025; App. D.) A written order that captures only selective portions of the court's findings does not satisfy *Fletcher*'s requirement of complete, factor-by-factor findings. Fourth, *Ireland v Smith* specifically holds that the best interest factors must be addressed even in time-sensitive custody matters. 451 Mich at 462. The passage of 329+ days since the ex parte suspension makes this lapse inexcusable.

**Conclusion.** The trial court abused its discretion by failing to make complete findings on all twelve best interest factors before modifying custody and suspending parenting time. This Court should reverse and remand with instructions to conduct a full, written, factor-by-factor analysis. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889.

## II. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED PLAINTIFF-APPELLANT'S CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHT TO PARENT HIS CHILD UNDER THE FOURTEENTH AMENDMENT

### A. Parental Rights Are Fundamental

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a parent's right to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This right is protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Id.* at 66. The right is not merely procedural — it is a substantive due process right that cannot be infringed without compelling justification and adequate procedural safeguards. *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982).

### B. The Standard for Restricting Parental Contact

Before a court may restrict or terminate a parent's contact with his or her child, the court must find, by clear and convincing evidence, that such restriction is necessary. *Santosky*, 455 US at 769. Michigan law incorporates this standard through MCL 722.27a(3): "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(3).

The trial court's record is devoid of any clear and convincing evidence finding. The trial court never articulated what specific, concrete harm to the child justified a complete termination of the father-child relationship for 329+ consecutive days.

### C. 329+ Days of Separation Constitutes Irreparable Harm

**Application.** The severity of the deprivation here cannot be overstated. Lincoln Watson is a three-year-old child. At this critical developmental stage, 329+ days represents approximately 30% of the child's entire conscious life. Developmental psychology research consistently demonstrates that prolonged separation from a primary attachment figure during early childhood causes lasting emotional and psychological damage. *See Santosky*, 455 US at 766 (recognizing the unique vulnerability of children in proceedings affecting the parent-child relationship).

Plaintiff-Appellant stated on the record: "My three-year-old son has been deprived of regular, loving contact with his father for months at a time. A genuine father-child bond, previously recognized by the court as a positive factor, has been severed." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.) The trial court itself previously found, under Factor (a), that "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and that this factor was "equal between the parties." (LC Record, Court Order re: Best Interest Factors; App. E.) Having made that finding, the court's subsequent acquiescence in the destruction of that bond — through 329+ days of enforced separation — violates the substantive component of due process. *Troxel*, 530 US at 66.

### D. The Ex Parte Suspension Lacked Constitutional Adequacy

The August 8, 2025 ex parte order suspending all parenting time was entered without:

1. Notice to Plaintiff-Appellant — "I was not served with the ex parte motion or order before an August 8, 2025 exchange. I arrived expecting my regular parenting time." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.);
2. An opportunity to be heard, in violation of *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976);
3. Specific factual findings of imminent harm to the child, as required by MCR 3.207(B)(1);
4. Application of the clear and convincing evidence standard required by MCL 722.27a(3);
5. Any time limitation or review mechanism — the suspension persisted for over 329 days without restoration, far exceeding the 14-day period contemplated by MCR 3.207(B)(5) for review of ex parte orders.

Each of these deficiencies independently violates the Due Process Clause. Together, they establish a pattern of constitutional indifference that requires reversal.

### E. The Self-Reinforcing Cycle of Deprivation

The trial court's actions created a vicious cycle: the court entered ex parte orders suspending parenting time; those orders led to contempt proceedings; contempt resulted in jail time; jail time caused job loss and housing instability; and housing instability was then cited as a basis for further custody restrictions. (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025; LC Record, HealthWest Assessment, 9/2025; App. F.) The HealthWest assessment documented the consequences of the court's own orders — eviction, unemployment, family estrangement — yet the court treated those consequences as evidence against Plaintiff-Appellant rather than recognizing them as products of its own actions.

This cycle is constitutionally intolerable. A court may not manufacture the circumstances it then cites to justify the continued deprivation of a fundamental right. *Santosky*, 455 US at 764 ("The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents."). The $250-per-motion bond imposed by the trial court on May 16, 2025 (LC Record, Court Order, 5/16/2025; App. F) further compounded this cycle by erecting a financial barrier to the very motions Plaintiff-Appellant needed to file to restore his parental rights. *See* US Const Amend XIV (access to courts); *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 382 (1971) (due process prohibits financial barriers that effectively deny access to court in family matters).

### F. Appellee's Likely Response and Reply

**Appellee's Likely Response:** Appellee will likely argue that safety concerns for the child justified the suspension, that Plaintiff-Appellant "categorically rejected" supervised parenting time, and that the court offered reasonable alternatives.

**Reply:** First, the record does not contain a clear and convincing evidence finding of danger to the child. MCL 722.27a(3). The last exchange on July 29, 2025 was "peaceful and ordinary," with "timestamped photos from that exchange showing Lincoln smiling, safe, and unharmed." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025; App. I.) Second, Plaintiff-Appellant did not reject contact with his son — he objected to the *legal basis* for the suspension: "[T]here is no need for suspension, and I am not any danger to my son in any way, shape, or form. My position was not a flat refusal to see my son under any supervision." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.) Third, even if the court's characterization were accurate, the constitutional remedy is not total deprivation of parental contact for 329+ days. The court was required to fashion the least restrictive alternative consistent with the child's safety. *Troxel*, 530 US at 73. The court failed to do so. Fourth, the court's own order acknowledges that it "offered supervised parenting time" (LC Record, Court Order, 11/26/2025; App. D), which means the court itself recognized that some form of parenting time was feasible — yet allowed 329+ days to elapse without implementing any alternative.

**Conclusion.** The trial court violated Plaintiff-Appellant's fundamental constitutional right to parent his child by permitting 329+ days of complete separation without adequate factual findings, without clear and convincing evidence, and without fashioning the least restrictive alternative. This Court should reverse and order immediate restoration of parenting time. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Santosky*, 455 US at 769; MCL 722.27a(3).

## III. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY FAILING TO ENFORCE PARENTING TIME UNDER MCL 722.27a AND FAILING TO APPLY ANY OF THE FIVE STATUTORY REMEDIES UNDER MCL 722.27a(9)

### A. Michigan's Statutory Framework for Parenting Time

Michigan law establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time: "Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." MCL 722.27a(1). The statute further provides that "[i]t is presumed to be in the best interests of a child for the child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents." MCL 722.27a(1).

Before parenting time may be denied, the record must contain "clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(3). No such evidence exists in this record.

### B. The Five Statutory Remedies Were Never Applied

When a parent's parenting time has been wrongfully denied, MCL 722.27a(9) provides five mandatory remedies the court must consider:

> (a) Compensating the aggrieved parent with additional parenting time.
> (b) Requiring the offending parent to pay the aggrieved parent's costs and attorney fees.
> (c) Finding the offending parent in contempt of court.
> (d) Modifying the custody or parenting time order.
> (e) Imposing any other reasonable remedy as determined by the court.

MCL 722.27a(9)(a)–(e).

Despite more than 329 consecutive days of denied parenting time, the trial court failed to apply any of these five remedies against Defendant-Appellee Watson. Not one. The court did not order makeup parenting time under MCL 722.27a(7). The court did not find Watson in contempt for withholding the child. The court did not modify the order to ensure compliance. The court imposed no sanctions whatsoever for Watson's complete denial of the father-child relationship.

### C. The Court Inverted the Statutory Framework

Rather than enforcing Plaintiff-Appellant's parenting time rights and holding Watson accountable for withholding the child, the trial court:

1. Signed Watson's ex parte motion to *suspend* Plaintiff-Appellant's parenting time on August 8, 2025;
2. Ordered Plaintiff-Appellant to undergo a mental health assessment at HealthWest as a *precondition* to restoration;
3. Adjourned hearings repeatedly, extending the separation;
4. Denied Plaintiff-Appellant's objection to the ex parte order;
5. Characterized Plaintiff-Appellant's legal objections as "categorical rejection" of supervised parenting time.

This is the exact inversion of MCL 722.27a's framework. The statute places the burden on the party *denying* parenting time to show, by clear and convincing evidence, that the child would be endangered. MCL 722.27a(3). The trial court instead placed the burden on the *deprived* parent to prove his fitness before parenting time would be restored. This burden-shifting is reversible error. *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 600 (2004).

### D. Makeup Parenting Time Is Mandatory

MCL 722.27a(7) provides that the trial court "shall" order makeup parenting time when parenting time has been denied. The word "shall" is mandatory. *Laffin v Laffin*, 280 Mich App 513, 517 (2008). The trial court has no discretion to ignore 329+ days of denied parenting time without ordering compensatory time. *Id.*

### E. Appellee's Likely Response and Reply

**Appellee's Likely Response:** Appellee will argue that the ex parte suspension was justified by safety concerns and that Plaintiff-Appellant's refusal to cooperate with the HealthWest assessment caused the delay.

**Reply:** First, the August 8, 2025 ex parte motion "did not set out specific, immediate, irreparable harm as required for ex parte relief in domestic-relations matters. See MCR 3.207(B)(1)." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.) Second, the court's own November 26, 2025 order acknowledges that "The Court received the 2nd Assessment on 9/12/25" — demonstrating that Plaintiff-Appellant did comply with the HealthWest assessment. (LC Record, Court Order, 11/26/2025; App. D.) Third, even if compliance were delayed, the statutory remedy is not indefinite suspension of parenting time. MCL 722.27a(9) provides five remedies — none of which include permanent separation. The court was required to fashion a remedy that preserved the parent-child relationship while addressing any legitimate concerns. *Pickering v Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694, 700 (2002). Fourth, the court's own order recites that a hearing was held at which "the two [HealthWest] workers testified" (LC Record, Court Order, 11/26/2025; App. D) — meaning the court had professional assessment testimony available, yet still declined to restore any parenting time. This is not the exercise of discretion; it is the abdication of statutory duty.

**Conclusion.** The trial court erred as a matter of law by failing to enforce parenting time under MCL 722.27a, by failing to apply any of the five statutory remedies under MCL 722.27a(9), and by inverting the statutory burden of proof. This Court should reverse and order immediate restoration of parenting time, makeup parenting time under MCL 722.27a(7), and application of the statutory remedies under MCL 722.27a(9)(a)–(e).

## IV. THE TRIAL COURT'S PATTERN OF 540 BENCHBOOK VIOLATIONS, 226 EX PARTE COMMUNICATION VIOLATIONS, AND SYSTEMATIC BIAS DEPRIVED PLAINTIFF-APPELLANT OF DUE PROCESS

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires "a fair trial in a fair tribunal." *In re Hathaway*, 464 Mich 672, 683 (2001). Disqualification is constitutionally required when there is "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b); *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868, 884 (2009).

> (a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
> (b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)–(b).

## V. THE TRIAL COURT FAILED TO PROPERLY EVALUATE PARENTAL ALIENATION UNDER MCL 722.23(j)

### A. Factor (j): The Facilitation Requirement

MCL 722.23(j) requires courts to evaluate "[t]he willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." This factor is central to the legislative intent of Michigan's Child Custody Act: ensuring that children maintain meaningful relationships with both parents.

In *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 715 (2008), this Court held that a party's pattern of interfering with the other parent's relationship with the child is highly relevant to the best interest analysis and should weigh heavily against the interfering party under Factor (j).

### B. Watson's Pattern of Total Alienation

The record demonstrates a comprehensive pattern of parental alienation by Defendant-Appellee Watson:

1. **Total withholding since July 29, 2025.** Watson has denied Plaintiff-Appellant all contact with his three-year-old son for 329+ consecutive days. (Record.)

2. **Strategic use of ex parte motions.** Watson filed the August 8, 2025 ex parte motion to suspend parenting time — a motion that did not contain the specific facts required by MCR 3.207(B)(1) showing "irreparable injury, loss, or damage" — on a scheduled exchange date, ensuring maximum disruption to the father-child relationship. (LC Record, Ex Parte Motion, 8/8/2025; App. C.) This represents "at least the fourth instance of evasion designed to suppress Plaintiff's ability to receive notice, present rebuttal, or obtain timely relief." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.)

3. **PPO as alienation tool.** The PPO, obtained without a full evidentiary hearing, was used to prevent Plaintiff-Appellant from any form of contact with or proximity to his child, documented in 381 instances across court proceedings. (LC Record, PPO proceedings and enforcement actions, Case No. 2023-5907-PP.) On December 5, 2025 alone, "a hearing on the Father's 7th PPO violation occurred. He pled guilty and received 45 days in jail." (LC Record, Court Order, 12/5/2025.) Each PPO enforcement further entrenched the separation and punished the father for attempting to maintain contact with his child.

4. **Evasion of service.** The record reflects repeated instances of service evasion: "at least the fourth instance of evasion designed to suppress Plaintiff's ability to receive notice, present rebuttal, or obtain timely relief." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.) Watson's legal training as a nine-year prosecutor gave her the procedural knowledge to exploit service requirements strategically.

5. **Professional legal advantage.** Watson's nine-year career in the Kent County Prosecutor's Office provided her with the procedural knowledge and familiarity with court processes to deploy these alienation strategies effectively. (LC Record, multiple filings referencing Watson's employment.) A layperson would not know to file an ex parte motion precisely on an exchange date, to draft proposed orders using selective "parts" of judicial rulings, or to exploit PPO enforcement as a means of severing the parent-child relationship.

### C. The Court Failed to Evaluate Factor (j) Against Watson

Despite this documented pattern, the trial court never evaluated Factor (j) against Watson. The court made no finding that Watson's total withholding of the child was unreasonable. The court imposed no consequences for Watson's failure to "facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship." MCL 722.23(j). Instead, the court rewarded the alienation by sustaining the ex parte suspension and denying Plaintiff-Appellant's motions for restoration of parenting time.

This failure is particularly egregious because Michigan law specifically recognizes parental alienation as a factor that should weigh against the alienating parent. *Berger*, 277 Mich App at 715. The trial court's complete failure to address this factor — while simultaneously enabling the alienation through judicial action — constitutes an independent abuse of discretion.

### D. The Alienation Pervades Every Factor

Parental alienation is not confined to Factor (j). Watson's conduct implicates:

- **Factor (a):** The emotional bond between father and child has been destroyed by 329+ days of enforced separation.
- **Factor (b):** Plaintiff-Appellant has been unable to provide guidance or participate in the child's education or development.
- **Factor (d):** The child has been deprived of the stable, familiar environment of the father's home.
- **Factor (f):** Watson's deliberate campaign of alienation bears directly on moral fitness.
- **Factor (g):** The court used Plaintiff-Appellant's court-mandated mental health assessment as a tool to delay parenting time restoration, rather than addressing Watson's alienation behavior.

MCL 722.23(a), (b), (d), (f), (g).

### E. Appellee's Likely Response and Reply

**Appellee's Likely Response:** Appellee will argue that Plaintiff-Appellant's own behavior — PPO violations, contempt, refusal of supervised visitation — demonstrates that it was Plaintiff-Appellant, not Watson, who failed to facilitate the relationship.

**Reply:** The PPO was obtained without a full evidentiary hearing and was used as an alienation tool, not a protective measure. (LC Record, PPO Order, 9/15/2024; App. G.) The contempt findings arose from the unconstitutional ex parte process documented in Section IV above. My position was not a flat refusal to see my son under any supervision." (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.) Even if every allegation against Plaintiff-Appellant were credited, the trial court was still required to evaluate Factor (j) against *both* parties. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889. It evaluated it against neither. Moreover, the HealthWest assessment — the very tool the court ordered to evaluate Plaintiff-Appellant — documented that the circumstances cited against him (homelessness, unemployment, family estrangement) were consequences of the court's own contempt orders and ex parte proceedings. (LC Record, HealthWest Assessment, 9/2025; App. F.)

**Conclusion.** The trial court failed to evaluate MCL 722.23(j) against Defendant-Appellee despite a documented pattern of total alienation extending 329+ days. This failure constitutes an independent abuse of discretion requiring reversal. *Berger*, 277 Mich App at 715; *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889.

## VI. THIS CASE SHOULD BE REMANDED WITH INSTRUCTIONS TO REASSIGN TO A DIFFERENT JUDGE UNDER MCR 7.216(A)(4) AND MCR 7.216(A)(7)

### A. This Court's Authority

MCR 7.216(A) provides that "[t]he Court of Appeals may, at any time, in addition to its general powers, in its discretion, and on the terms it deems just" exercise broad remedial authority, including the power to:

> (4) permit amendments, corrections, or additions to the transcript or record;
> (7) enter any judgment or order or grant further or different relief as the case may require.

MCR 7.216(A)(4), (A)(7). This authority includes the power to remand with instructions to reassign the case to a different judge. *Bayati v Bayati*, 264 Mich App 595, 602 (2004).

### B. Reassignment Is Necessary

The documented record of 540 benchbook violations, 226 ex parte communication violations, 175 due process violations, 381 instances of PPO-related restrictions, 196 instances of procedural irregularities, 150 Canon violations, and 146 disqualification triggers makes clear that fair adjudication by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill is not possible. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)–(b).

The pattern is not isolated — it is structural. The trial court has demonstrated, through the entire history of this case, a systematic inability or unwillingness to provide Plaintiff-Appellant with the neutral forum that due process requires. *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US at 884. The specific instances include: (1) signing ex parte orders without the required factual showing under MCR 3.207(B)(1); (2) receiving and relying on evidence off the record in violation of Canon 3(A)(5); (3) silencing Plaintiff-Appellant's attempts to present his case; (4) imposing a $250-per-motion bond that restricted court access; (5) characterizing Plaintiff-Appellant's legal arguments as "frivolous" while simultaneously failing to conduct the mandatory analyses those arguments requested; and (6) allowing the opposing party's counsel to draft orders using only selective "parts" of the court's rulings. Remand to the same judge would be futile. When a trial judge's conduct throughout a case reveals a level of bias or procedural irregularity that calls into question the fundamental fairness of the proceedings, this Court has the authority — and the obligation — to direct reassignment. *Bayati*, 264 Mich App at 602.

### C. Appellee's Likely Response and Reply

The remedy is proportionate to the harm — a harm measured not merely in procedural errors, but in 329+ days of destroyed parent-child relationship and a three-year-old child who has lost nearly a third of his conscious life without his father.

**Conclusion.** This Court should exercise its authority under MCR 7.216(A)(4) and (A)(7) to remand with instructions to reassign this case to a different judge. *Bayati*, 264 Mich App at 602; *In re Haley*, 476 Mich at 193.
