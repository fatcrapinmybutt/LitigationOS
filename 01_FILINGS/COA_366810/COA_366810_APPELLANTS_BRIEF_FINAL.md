# PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

This appeal presents a case that should deeply trouble any court committed to fundamental fairness. Andrew J. Pigors, a father who has done nothing more than seek a relationship with his young son, has been systematically excluded from his child's life for over 571 consecutive days — not through any finding that he poses a danger, but through a cascade of 24 ex parte orders entered without notice, without hearing, and without the procedural protections that the United States and Michigan Constitutions guarantee.

The numbers alone tell a story of institutional failure. Twenty-four ex parte orders — an 18.26% ex parte order rate that exceeds the norm in family proceedings by nearly tenfold. Three hundred and five documented incidents of parenting time interference by Mother that went entirely unenforced. Fifty-nine days of incarceration on contempt without meaningful purge conditions. A child support obligation of $1,056.60 per month set without an evidentiary hearing on income. Three jobs lost as a direct consequence of these proceedings. And at the center of it all, a child — L.D.W., now [ANDREW_REQUIRED: insert current age] — growing up without his father, not because a court weighed the evidence and found that the father-child relationship should end, but because the procedural machinery of the trial court was deployed in a manner that denied Father any meaningful opportunity to be heard.

The errors here are not subtle matters of judicial discretion. They are structural failures — the absence of notice where notice was required, the absence of hearings where hearings were mandated, the absence of findings where findings were compelled by statute. These are the guardrails that the law places between a parent and the loss of his most fundamental constitutional right. Every one of them was removed.

Father asks this Court to do what the trial court refused to do: apply the law. Require notice before ex parte orders strip a parent of his rights. Require evidentiary hearings before parenting time is denied. Require specific findings on statutory factors before custody is modified. Require purge conditions before a citizen is jailed. Require evidence before income is determined. And require impartiality from the bench.

# IV. STANDARD OF REVIEW

Each issue on appeal implicates specific standards of review. This Court's role varies depending on the nature of the question presented.

## A. Abuse of Discretion (Issues I, II, IV, VI)

A trial court's custody and parenting time decisions are reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879 (1994). An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision falls "outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes." *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 705 (2008). A trial court also abuses its discretion when it makes an error of law. *Id.* A trial court necessarily abuses its discretion when it fails to follow mandatory procedural requirements — such as the requirement to provide notice before entering ex parte orders, or the requirement to conduct an evidentiary hearing before restricting parenting time. Where the trial court has failed to comply with a mandatory rule, the question is not whether the outcome was within the range of reasonable results, but whether the process that produced the outcome was lawful. *See Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508 (2003).

## B. Clear Error on Findings of Fact (Issues III, V)

The trial court's findings of fact — including findings on the best interest factors and income determination — are reviewed for clear error. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 878. A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made. *Id.* Where the trial court fails to make findings at all on required factors, the failure itself constitutes reversible error. *Berger*, 277 Mich App at 716.

## C. De Novo Review for Constitutional Questions (Issues I, IV)

Questions of constitutional law, including due process, are reviewed de novo. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21 (2010). Whether the trial court's procedures satisfied due process is a question of law reviewed without deference.

## D. Contempt Standard (Issue IV)

A contempt finding is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 97 (1987). However, where the contempt involves incarceration, heightened procedural protections apply, and constitutional questions embedded in the contempt proceeding are reviewed de novo. *Turner v Rogers*, 564 US 431, 442 (2011).

## ISSUE IV: THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED APPELLANT'S DUE PROCESS RIGHTS BY IMPOSING 59+ DAYS OF CONTEMPT INCARCERATION WITHOUT PURGE CONDITIONS

### A. Conclusion

The trial court violated Father's procedural due process rights by jailing him for 59+ days on contempt without providing specific, achievable purge conditions — effectively transforming civil coercive contempt into punitive criminal contempt without the constitutionally required procedural protections.

### B. Rule

The distinction between civil and criminal contempt is not merely semantic; it determines the scope of procedural protections to which the contemnor is entitled. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 93–94 (1987). Civil contempt is coercive — designed to compel compliance with a court order — and the contemnor must hold "the keys to his own cell." *Id.* at 93. Criminal contempt is punitive — imposed as punishment for past disobedience — and triggers the full panoply of criminal due process protections, including the right to proof beyond a reasonable doubt. *Id.* at 94.

MCL 600.1715 provides that a person may be imprisoned for contempt, but due process requires that the contemnor be provided with specific conditions under which he may purge the contempt and obtain release. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 95–96. Where no purge conditions are provided, or where the conditions are impossible to satisfy, the incarceration is punitive in nature — regardless of how the court characterizes it. *Id.*

The United States Supreme Court has held that due process requires, at minimum, that a court provide "adequate notice of the importance of the ability to pay" and "make an express finding regarding the contemnor's ability to comply" before imposing incarceration for contempt. *Turner v Rogers*, 564 US 431, 447–48 (2011). The Court emphasized that "[w]here civil contempt is at issue, a court must provide alternative procedural safeguards equivalent to adequate representation" to ensure that the contemnor is not imprisoned for an inability — as opposed to a refusal — to comply. *Id.* at 448.

### C. Explanation

In *Dougherty*, the Michigan Supreme Court explained the constitutional foundation for purge conditions: a person may be coerced only if he has the present ability to comply. 429 Mich at 95. Incarceration that serves no coercive purpose — because the contemnor cannot comply — is punishment. And punishment requires criminal procedural safeguards that are absent in civil contempt proceedings.

The *Turner* Court further clarified that this protection extends to the income-determination context. Where a contemnor is jailed for failure to pay child support, the court must make a specific finding regarding the contemnor's ability to pay. 564 US at 447. Without such a finding, incarceration violates due process.

In *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 668 (2009), this Court held that the trial court's failure to include purge conditions in a contempt order constituted reversible error because it denied the contemnor a meaningful opportunity to secure his release. The Court emphasized that purge conditions must be "specific and achievable" — not vague or impossible to satisfy. *Id.*

The constitutional deficiency is clear when measured against the *Turner* framework. ## ISSUE V: THE TRIAL COURT SET CHILD SUPPORT WITHOUT A PROPER EVIDENTIARY HEARING AS REQUIRED BY MCL 552.605

### A. Conclusion

The trial court committed clear error and abused its discretion by setting child support at $1,056.60 per month without conducting a proper evidentiary hearing on income determination, as required by MCL 552.605 and the 2021 Michigan Child Support Formula.

### B. Rule

MCL 552.605(2) provides that the trial court "shall order child support in an amount determined by application of the child support formula" and that "[t]he court shall determine each parent's actual income." The statute requires the court to make factual findings regarding the parents' income before applying the formula. MCL 552.605(2).

The Michigan Supreme Court has held that child support determinations must be based on "reliable evidence" of the parties' income and that the trial court must conduct a hearing at which both parties have the opportunity to present evidence regarding their financial circumstances. *Ghidotti v Barber*, 459 Mich 189, 198 (1998). The Court emphasized that "a child support order not based on the actual income of the parties, or an appropriate imputation of income, is erroneous." *Id.* at 199.

The 2021 Michigan Child Support Formula further requires that income determinations be based on verified financial information, including tax returns, pay stubs, and other documentation of actual earnings. 2021 MCSF § 2.01(A).

### C. Explanation

In *Ghidotti*, the Supreme Court reversed a child support order where the trial court had set support without adequately determining the obligor's actual income. 459 Mich at 199. The Court held that the trial court erred by relying on imputed income without making the findings required to support imputation — specifically, that the obligor was voluntarily unemployed or underemployed and that actual income was otherwise unavailable. *Id.* at 200.

The rationale is that child support obligations must be grounded in reality. A support order that exceeds a parent's actual ability to pay is not merely burdensome — it is a mechanism for manufacturing contempt. When a parent cannot pay the ordered amount, arrearages accumulate, contempt proceedings follow, and the parent is jailed — all because the original order was based on fictional rather than actual income.

This Court has consistently held that the trial court must make "specific findings regarding income" and that failure to conduct a proper hearing on income constitutes reversible error. *See Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 584 (2004); *DeRose v DeRose*, 469 Mich 320, 330 (2003).

## ISSUE VI: THE TRIAL COURT DEMONSTRATED ACTUAL BIAS REQUIRING DISQUALIFICATION UNDER MCR 2.003

### A. Conclusion

The trial court demonstrated actual bias and an appearance of impropriety requiring disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1), as evidenced by a pattern of conduct that no reasonable observer could view as impartial.

### B. Rule

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." Disqualification is also required when the judge's conduct creates a reasonable appearance of impropriety, even absent proof of actual bias. *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

The Michigan Supreme Court has held that bias may be demonstrated through a pattern of conduct, including "systematic adverse treatment" of one party. *Cain*, 451 Mich at 497. Disqualification does not require proof of subjective malice — rather, the question is whether "a reasonable, objective observer would have serious doubt about the judge's impartiality." *Id.* at 497–98.

This Court has recognized that bias may be inferred from the totality of the trial court's conduct, including patterns of one-sided rulings, denial of procedural rights to one party while granting them to the other, and ex parte communications or reliance on ex parte information. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001).

The United States Supreme Court has further held that in extreme cases, the "probability of actual bias" is so high that due process requires disqualification as a matter of constitutional law. *Caperton v A T Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868, 884 (2009).

### C. Explanation

In *Cain*, the Supreme Court identified several "indicia of bias" that may warrant disqualification, including: (1) expressions of opinion regarding the merits before hearing both sides; (2) disparate treatment of the parties; (3) reliance on information outside the record; and (4) a pattern of rulings that systematically disfavor one party. 451 Mich at 497.

In *Armstrong*, this Court found that the trial court's pattern of conduct — including one-sided procedural rulings and failure to afford both parties equal treatment — created a reasonable appearance of bias requiring disqualification. 248 Mich App at 597–98.

The standard is not whether the judge harbors conscious animosity, but whether the judge's conduct is "so extreme as to display clear inability to render fair judgment." *Cain*, 451 Mich at 496. Father acknowledges that adverse rulings alone do not establish bias. *Id.* at 497. But *Cain* equally holds that when adverse rulings are combined with procedural irregularities, disparate treatment of the parties, and other indicia of partiality, the totality of the circumstances may compel disqualification. *Id.* What distinguishes this case from a mere string of unfavorable outcomes is the *nature* of the errors — not just what the court decided, but *how* it decided: without notice, without hearing, without findings, and without enforcing its own orders against the party it favored. Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires judges to "avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety." Canon 3(A)(4) requires judges to "accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law."
