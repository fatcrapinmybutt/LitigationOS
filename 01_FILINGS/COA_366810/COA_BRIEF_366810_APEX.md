# PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

This appeal presents a case that should deeply trouble any court committed to fundamental fairness. Andrew J. Pigors, a father who has done nothing more than seek a relationship with his young son, has been systematically excluded from his child's life for over 571 consecutive days — not through any finding that he poses a danger, but through a cascade of ex parte orders entered without notice, without hearing, and without the procedural protections that the United States and Michigan Constitutions guarantee.

The numbers tell the story of institutional failure. Twenty-four of fifty-five orders — 44% — were entered ex parte, all adverse to Father, all presided over by Judge McNeill. Five ex parte orders were entered on a single day, August 8, 2025, suspending all parenting time without notice. Father was jailed for 59 days for sending birthday messages to his own child through AppClose. A favorable mental health evaluation (HealthWest Report H0002, September 5, 2025) was rejected by the trial court, which then sent an off-record letter to the evaluator — outside the record and in violation of Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct. Seven separate factual allegations by Mother — including claims of arsenic poisoning, assault, sexual assault, cocaine use, methamphetamine use, surveillance, and mental instability — were debunked, yet the trial court continued to restrict Father's rights based on the same discredited narrative. And throughout, a USB drive submitted by Mother was admitted and relied upon without being clerk-filed per MCR 8.119 — placing evidence beyond Father's ability to examine or challenge.

Most troubling of all, the presiding judge was subject to mandatory disqualification that was never disclosed. Judge McNeill's husband, Cavan Berry, shares a familial relationship with Ronald Berry, Mother's domestic partner. Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), this nexus required disqualification. It was not disclosed. Moreover, Judge McNeill, Judge Hoopes, and Judge Ladas-Hoopes — whose rulings collectively shaped this case — all originated from the same Muskegon law firm, creating a structural bias that no litigant should be forced to navigate.

The errors here are not subtle matters of judicial discretion. They are structural failures — the absence of notice where notice was required, the absence of hearings where hearings were mandated, the absence of findings where findings were compelled by statute, and the absence of impartiality where the Constitution demands it. Father asks this Court to apply the law.

# IV. ARGUMENT

## A. ISSUE I: THE TRIAL COURT COMMITTED A STRUCTURAL DUE PROCESS VIOLATION THROUGH A 44% EX PARTE ORDER RATE

### Standard of Review

Structural constitutional errors are reviewed de novo. *People v Carines*, 460 Mich 750, 763 (1999). Compliance with court rules is reviewed de novo. *Haliw v Sterling Heights*, 471 Mich 700, 704 (2005). A trial court abuses its discretion when its decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388 (2006).

### Preservation

Father objected to the ex parte orders through multiple filings in the trial court, including his Motion to Set Aside Ex Parte Order and his Motion for Disqualification. (LCR, Father's Motions.) To the extent any specific order was not preserved through contemporaneous objection, Father had no opportunity to object to proceedings of which he was never informed — the structural nature of the violation makes preservation requirements inapplicable.

### Argument

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and Mich Const 1963, art 1, § 17 require, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a person is deprived of a protected liberty interest. *Mullane v Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co*, 339 US 306, 314 (1950). A parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is a fundamental liberty interest. *Meyer v Nebraska*, 262 US 390, 399 (1923); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982).

MCR 3.207(C)(2) provides strict requirements for ex parte orders in domestic relations proceedings. A party may obtain an ex parte order "only on a showing, by verified statement of facts, that (a) immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice, or (b) the notice itself will precipitate adverse action before the order can be issued." Ex parte relief is an "extraordinary remedy" warranted only in "exceptional circumstances." *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508 (2003).

The trial court's entry of 24 of 55 orders (44%) on an ex parte basis — all adverse to Father — transforms what should be isolated exceptions into a systemic practice. When nearly half of all orders in a case are entered ex parte, the proceedings can no longer be characterized as adversarial. They become, in substance, inquisitorial proceedings in which one party presents her version of events and the court acts without ever hearing from the other side.

The August 8, 2025 event underscores the structural nature of the violation: five separate ex parte orders entered on a single day, suspending all parenting time, adding restrictions, and modifying custody arrangements — all without Father's knowledge or participation. Father arrived at the scheduled exchange expecting to see his child and discovered only afterward, by contacting the court, that his rights had been terminated by ex parte order. As the Supreme Court observed in *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 379 (1971), "[t]he right to be heard does not depend on an advance showing that one will surely prevail at the hearing." Father was entitled to be heard *before* his parenting time was suspended, not after.

Not a single ex parte motion in the record alleged — much less demonstrated — that delaying for the time required to provide notice would result in immediate and irreparable injury as MCR 3.207(C)(2) requires. No emergency circumstances were articulated. The August 8, 2025 motion was based on a report from three men at a public library who claimed Father appeared agitated during a phone call — not on any allegation of imminent harm to L.D.W.

The cumulative effect of this structural violation cannot be remedied by reviewing individual orders in isolation. Each ex parte order compounded the harm of those that preceded it, creating a cascade of adverse rulings that Father could not challenge in real time because he did not know they existed. The remedy for a structural due process violation of this magnitude is reversal and reassignment to a different judge. *See In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 680-81 (2009).

## B. ISSUE II: THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY DENYING ALL PARENTING TIME FOR 571+ CONSECUTIVE DAYS WITHOUT AN EVIDENTIARY HEARING

### Standard of Review

A trial court's decision to restrict parenting time is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 507-08. Whether a party has been afforded due process is reviewed de novo. *Bonner v Brighton*, 495 Mich 209, 221 (2014). The adequacy of procedures in custody proceedings is reviewed de novo. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

### Preservation

Father raised this issue through multiple motions to reinstate parenting time and objections to the ex parte orders. (LCR, Father's Motions.)

### Argument

Michigan's Child Custody Act establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time. MCL 722.27a(1) provides that parenting time shall be granted "in accordance with the best interests of the child." MCL 722.27a(3) creates a statutory presumption that "it is in the best interest of a child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents."

Parenting time may be restricted only when the court determines, based on evidence, that parenting time "would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(7). This determination must be made in an evidentiary hearing. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 26-27 (2010); *Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 80-81 (2017).

In *Shade*, this Court reversed a parenting time restriction where the trial court failed to conduct a proper evidentiary hearing. 291 Mich App at 31. In *Lieberman*, this Court held that a parent's right to parenting time cannot be terminated based on "allegations, arguments of counsel, or the court's own impressions" — it requires evidence. 319 Mich App at 81. The *Lieberman* Court specifically held that the trial court erred by suspending parenting time without first conducting an evidentiary hearing, even where serious allegations had been made. *Id.* at 82. In *Marik v Marik*, 325 Mich App 353, 368 (2018), this Court clarified that even when legitimate concerns exist, the proper response is to conduct a hearing — not to suspend parenting time indefinitely.

Father has been denied all contact with L.D.W. for 571+ consecutive days. During this period, no evidentiary hearing has been conducted. No witness has testified under oath. No evidence has been subjected to cross-examination. No finding has been made — by any standard of proof — that parenting time with Father "would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health" under MCL 722.27a(7).

The complete denial of all parenting time represents the most extreme deprivation short of termination of parental rights. As the Michigan Court of Appeals recognized in *Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich App 142, 150 (2001), "a parent has a due process right to a hearing before being deprived of custody of his or her child."

The *Mathews v Eldridge* analysis, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), compels the same conclusion: (1) the private interest — Father's fundamental liberty interest in his child — is among the most substantial the Constitution protects; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through ex parte proceedings without an evidentiary hearing is severe; and (3) the government's interest in protecting children is served, not undermined, by fair proceedings. *Santosky*, 455 US at 766-67 ("the State registers no gain towards its declared goals when it separates children from the custody of fit parents").

The trial court's failure to hold an evidentiary hearing before denying Father all contact with his child for 571+ days requires reversal.

## C. ISSUE III: THE TRIAL COURT'S BEST INTEREST FINDINGS WERE AGAINST THE GREAT WEIGHT OF THE EVIDENCE

### Standard of Review

This Court reviews findings on each best interest factor under the great weight of the evidence standard. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879 (1994). A finding is against the great weight of the evidence when "the evidence clearly preponderates in the opposite direction." *Id.* Discretionary rulings are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 507-08. The failure to make required findings is itself reversible error. *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192-93 (2004).

### Preservation

Father raised the best interest factors through his motions and objections in the trial court, including his Motion Re: Best Interest Factors filed April 11, 2024. (LCR, Motion, 04/11/2024.)

### Argument

MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve factors that the trial court "shall consider" in custody disputes. The Michigan Supreme Court held in *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004), that "a trial court must consider and explicitly state its findings" on each factor. The failure to make such findings is reversible error. *Id.* at 193; MCR 2.517(A)(1)-(2).

The August 23, 2024 Final Judgment granted Mother sole custody without particularized findings on all twelve factors. The record does not reflect that the trial court conducted the required analysis. This alone constitutes reversible error under *Harvey*.

The evidence that was before the trial court preponderates against the custody determination:

**Factor (a) — Love, affection, and emotional ties.** Father maintained a loving bond with L.D.W. during periods when parenting time was exercised and consistently sought to maintain contact. The trial court's implicit finding favoring Mother was against the great weight of the evidence.

**Factor (c) — Capacity to provide.** Father demonstrated ability and willingness to provide for L.D.W.'s physical needs. No evidence in the record supports a finding that Father was unable to provide food, clothing, medical care, or housing.

**Factor (j) — Willingness to facilitate relationship.** This is the most critical factor. The record is replete with evidence that Mother systematically obstructed Father's parenting time: 37 consecutive days of withholding (March-May 2024); 23 consecutive days of denial (October-November 2024); repeated refusal of video chats; vacation scheduling during Father's time. Despite this extensive record of interference, the trial court failed to address parental alienation under Factor (j) and instead rewarded Mother's obstruction by granting her sole custody. *See Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556, 565 (2015) (recognizing parental alienation as relevant to Factor (j)).

**Factor (f) — Moral fitness.** Mother's seven debunked allegations — arsenic, assault, sexual assault, cocaine, methamphetamine, surveillance, and mental instability — each constitute a false statement to the court. A pattern of fabricating allegations to gain advantage in custody proceedings is directly relevant to moral fitness. The trial court failed to weigh this evidence against Mother.

**MCL 722.27a(7) — Clear and Convincing Evidence.** MCL 722.27a(7) provides: "Parenting time shall be granted in specific terms . . . unless the court determines on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the mental, physical, or emotional health of the child." The trial court made no such finding on the record. The absence of this required finding renders the denial of Father's parenting time reversible error.

## D. ISSUE IV: THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY WEAPONIZING THE PPO TO CIRCUMVENT THE CHILD CUSTODY ACT

### Standard of Review

Statutory interpretation is reviewed de novo. *Klooster v City of Charlevoix*, 488 Mich 289, 295-96 (2011). Whether a trial court's custody determination complied with statutory requirements is reviewed de novo. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92.

### Preservation

Father raised this issue through his motions and objections regarding the interaction between the PPO and custody proceedings. (LCR, Father's Motions.)

### Argument

MCL 600.2950(4) expressly provides:

> "A personal protection order shall not be made a condition of release of a minor child to a parent and **shall not in any manner affect the provisions of a custody or parenting time order** issued by the circuit court under the Child Custody Act of 1970 . . . ."

(Emphasis added.)

Despite this clear statutory prohibition, the trial court permitted the PPO entered in Case No. 2023-5907-PP to function as the primary mechanism for restricting Father's custody and parenting time. The PPO was treated not as an independent protective measure but as a de facto custody order — entered without the procedural protections of the Child Custody Act, without a best interest analysis under MCL 722.23, and without the evidentiary threshold required by MCL 722.27a(7).

The chronology demonstrates the weaponization:

1. **December 4, 2023**: PPO issued against Father. (LCR-PPO, Order.)
2. **April 11, 2024**: PPO amended ex parte — the same day custody complaint activity occurred. (LCR-PPO, Ex Parte Order; LCR, ROA.)
3. **April 29, 2024**: Ex parte order in custody case entered, citing the PPO proceeding. (LCR, Ex Parte Order.)
4. **June 15, 2024**: PPO violation hearing resulted in 45-day jail sentence — Father's liberty restricted through a proceeding that MCL 600.2950(4) prohibits from affecting custody.
5. **August 23, 2024**: Final Judgment grants sole custody to Mother, with the PPO as a background factor. (LCR, Final Judgment.)
6. **August 8, 2025**: Ex parte order suspends all parenting time. (LCR, Ex Parte Order.)

Michigan courts have recognized that PPOs cannot be used as a substitute for custody proceedings. *See Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31 (2010); *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 598-99 (2004). The PPO itself was entered without sufficient evidence meeting the standards of MCL 600.2950(1). The allegations consisted primarily of unverified claims not subjected to rigorous evidentiary scrutiny. *See Laffin v Laffin*, 280 Mich App 513, 517 (2008).

The trial court's use of the PPO to accomplish what MCL 600.2950(4) expressly forbids requires reversal.

## E. ISSUE V: THE TRIAL COURT WAS SUBJECT TO MANDATORY DISQUALIFICATION UNDER MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — THE BERRY-McNEILL NEXUS AND THREE-COURT CARTEL

### Standard of Review

A trial court's decision on a motion for disqualification is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001). Whether a judge's conduct rises to the level requiring disqualification is reviewed de novo. *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868, 884 (2009). Whether facts establish mandatory disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) is reviewed de novo as a question of law.

### Preservation

Father filed a Motion for Disqualification of Judge McNeill pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1). (LCR, Motion for Disqualification.) The motion was denied. (LCR, Order Denying Disqualification.)

### Argument

#### 1. Mandatory Disqualification — The Berry-McNeill Familial Nexus

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides for disqualification where the judge or the judge's spouse has a familial relationship with a party or a person associated with a party that creates a "serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party."

Judge McNeill's husband is Cavan Berry. Ronald Berry — Mother's boyfriend and domestic partner, who has been present in L.D.W.'s household throughout these proceedings — shares the Berry surname and familial connection with the judge's husband. This nexus between the trial judge's family and the opposing party's household creates exactly the kind of "serious risk of actual bias" that MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) is designed to prevent.

The Berrys' familial relationship was never disclosed to Father on the record. The appearance of impropriety alone would warrant disqualification under Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which requires judges to "avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." When the judge's spouse is related to the domestic partner of a party — and that relationship is not disclosed — the proceeding is tainted from inception.

#### 2. The Three-Court Cartel — Structural Bias

The structural bias concern deepens when the professional origins of the judges involved are examined. Judge McNeill, Judge Hoopes, and Judge Ladas-Hoopes — whose rulings collectively shaped both the PPO and custody proceedings — all originated from the same Muskegon County law firm. This common professional origin raises serious questions about whether Father received adjudication by independent judicial officers or whether the proceedings were shaped by a network of former colleagues.

While collegial relationships among members of a bar do not ordinarily require disqualification, the combination of (a) a familial nexus between the presiding judge's spouse and the opposing party's partner, (b) three judges from the same firm handling interrelated aspects of the same litigation, and (c) a 44% ex parte rate exclusively adverse to Father creates a totality that no reasonable observer could characterize as impartial.

#### 3. Demonstrated Pattern of Actual Bias

Even absent the familial nexus, the record independently establishes actual bias under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a):

- **24 of 55 orders (44%)** entered ex parte — all by Judge McNeill, all adverse to Father. (LCR, ROA.)
- **Five ex parte orders on a single day** (August 8, 2025). (LCR, ROA, 08/08/2025.)
- **Systematic denial of Father's motions** while Mother's motions were routinely granted. Father's six pending motions were dismissed without adjudication by the Final Judgment.
- **Selective enforcement**: Mother withheld L.D.W. for 37 consecutive days without consequence; Father was jailed for 59 days for sending birthday messages through AppClose.
- **Rejection of favorable evidence**: HealthWest Report H0002 favorable to Father was rejected; the trial judge sent an off-record letter to the evaluator.

The Supreme Court recognized in *Caperton*, 556 US at 884, that due process requires recusal where "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." The Berry-McNeill nexus, the Three-Court Cartel, and the demonstrated pattern of asymmetric treatment collectively satisfy this standard. *See also Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996) (discussing the constitutional dimensions of judicial bias).

## F. ISSUE VI: EVIDENTIARY MANIPULATION — REJECTION OF FAVORABLE EVALUATION AND ACCEPTANCE OF OFF-RECORD EVIDENCE

### Standard of Review

Evidentiary rulings in custody proceedings are reviewed for abuse of discretion. The trial court's obligation to base custody decisions on evidence presented through proper proceedings is reviewed de novo. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92. Compliance with the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct is reviewed de novo.

### Preservation

Father raised these issues through his post-hearing filings and motion for reconsideration. (LCR, Father's Filings.)

### Argument

#### 1. Rejection of HealthWest Report H0002

On September 5, 2025, HealthWest issued Report H0002 following a mental health evaluation of Father. The evaluation produced findings favorable to Father — directly contradicting Mother's allegation of "mental instability," which was one of her seven debunked allegations. (HealthWest Report H0002, 09/05/2025.)

Rather than weighing this evidence as the best interest analysis requires, the trial court rejected the evaluation. A trial court must consider all relevant evidence bearing on the best interest factors. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 879; MCL 722.23(g) (mental and physical health of the parties). The rejection of a professional evaluation that contradicted the court's predetermined narrative is inconsistent with the duty to weigh evidence impartially and constitutes an abuse of discretion.

#### 2. Off-Record Letter to the Evaluator

After rejecting the HealthWest evaluation, Judge McNeill sent a letter to the HealthWest evaluator — a communication that occurred outside the court record and outside the presence of the parties. This off-record communication violated Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which provides:

> "A judge should not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

The letter to the evaluator was not disclosed to Father, was not filed on the record, and was not subject to adversarial testing. Whatever the letter's contents, the act of communicating with a professional evaluator off the record in a pending custody case — particularly after rejecting that evaluator's favorable findings — creates an irremediable appearance of impropriety.

#### 3. The Unfiled USB Drive

During the proceedings, Mother submitted a USB drive to the court as evidence. This USB was not clerk-filed per MCR 8.119, which governs court records and requires that all evidence submitted to the court be properly filed and made part of the official record. (LCR, ROA — absence of USB filing entry.)

MCR 8.119(C) provides that "the clerk shall maintain . . . all papers and records pertaining to cases and proceedings in that court." Evidence that is submitted to and relied upon by the trial court but not clerk-filed is effectively invisible to appellate review — and to the opposing party's ability to examine and challenge it.

The trial court's reliance on a USB drive that was never clerk-filed denied Father due process in two ways: (1) he could not examine or challenge the contents through normal discovery and cross-examination, and (2) the evidence is not part of the appellate record, depriving this Court of the ability to assess its contents. Evidence that exists outside the record cannot form the basis for judicial action.

## G. ISSUE VII: DISPROPORTIONATE CONTEMPT SANCTIONS AND FRAUD ON THE COURT

### Standard of Review

A contempt finding is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 97 (1987). Where contempt involves incarceration, heightened procedural protections apply, and constitutional questions are reviewed de novo. *Turner v Rogers*, 564 US 431, 442 (2011). Whether conduct constitutes fraud on the court is reviewed de novo.

### Preservation

Father raised these issues through his responses to contempt proceedings and subsequent motions. (LCR, Father's Filings.)

### Argument

#### 1. Disproportionate Sanctions — 59 Days for Birthday Messages

Father was incarcerated for 59+ days on contempt. The predicate conduct included Father's sending birthday messages to his own child through AppClose — a co-parenting communication application. (LCR, Contempt Proceedings.)

The disproportion is stark: Father used a court-approved platform to wish his child happy birthday and was jailed for nearly two months. Meanwhile, Mother withheld L.D.W. for 37 consecutive days (March-May 2024) and 23 consecutive days (October-November 2024) in violation of court-ordered parenting time, and faced zero enforcement.

Civil contempt must be coercive, not punitive. The contemnor must have the present ability to comply with the court's order — "the keys to his prison in his own pocket." *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 95. The contempt orders in this case did not provide Father with specific, achievable purge conditions that, if satisfied, would result in his immediate release. Without purge conditions, what the trial court characterized as civil contempt became, in substance, criminal punishment — imposed without the procedural protections required for criminal proceedings, including the right to counsel. *Turner*, 564 US at 447-48; *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480, 490 (1990).

The consequences extended beyond jail. Father lost three jobs as a direct result of his incarceration, diminishing his ability to pay child support, generating additional arrearages, and fueling additional contempt proceedings. This self-reinforcing cycle was foreseeable and avoidable.

#### 2. Fraud on the Court — Seven Debunked Allegations

Mother made seven separate factual allegations against Father — arsenic poisoning, assault, sexual assault, cocaine use, methamphetamine use, surveillance, and mental instability — each of which was investigated and debunked. (LCR, Investigation Results; LCR-PPO, Hearing Records.)

These allegations were not innocent errors. They formed a systematic pattern of false statements to the court designed to obtain ex parte orders, PPO violations, contempt findings, and ultimately the complete severance of the father-child relationship. When a party makes seven false factual allegations to a court in a custody proceeding, and each allegation is debunked, the proceeding has been infected by fraud.

The trial court's failure to address — or even acknowledge — the pattern of debunked allegations is itself an indicator of bias. A neutral tribunal, confronted with evidence that seven separate factual claims by one party had been disproven, would at minimum subject that party's subsequent representations to heightened scrutiny. Instead, the trial court continued to credit Mother's narrative and restrict Father's rights.

The cumulative effect of the fraud — combined with the 44% ex parte rate, the rejection of favorable evidence, the off-record communications, and the disproportionate sanctions — rendered the proceedings below fundamentally unfair. The remedy is reversal and reassignment.
