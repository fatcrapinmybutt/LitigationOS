# STANDARD OF REVIEW

22. **Custody and parenting time decisions** are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 91; 782 NW2d 480 (2010). A trial court abuses its discretion when its decision "falls outside the range of principled outcomes." *Vodvarka v. Grasher*, 259 Mich App 1, 8; 672 NW2d 847 (2003).

23. **Best interest factor findings** are reviewed for clear error. MCL 722.23; *Fletcher v. Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879; 526 NW2d 889 (1994). A finding is clearly erroneous when, "although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court on the entire evidence is left with the definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made." *Id.*

24. **Due process violations** and **constitutional questions** are reviewed de novo. *Eldred v. Ziny*, 246 Mich App 142, 150; 631 NW2d 748 (2001).

25. **Judicial disqualification** under MCR 2.003 is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996).

# ARGUMENT

## I. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED FATHER'S DUE PROCESS RIGHTS BY ENTERING EX PARTE ORDERS WITHOUT REQUIRED PROCEDURAL SAFEGUARDS

### A. Issue

26. Whether the trial court's entry of ex parte orders on August 8, 2025 — suspending all parenting time without notice to Father, without a verified pleading, and without specific findings of fact — violated Father's constitutional right to due process.

### B. Rule

27. The right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972). Government may not deprive a parent of this interest without adequate procedural safeguards. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753-54 (1982).

28. Michigan Court Rules impose specific procedural prerequisites for ex parte custody and parenting time orders:

 a. **MCR 3.206(B)(5):** Ex parte orders affecting custody or parenting time may only be entered upon a showing of "specific facts" demonstrating "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage."

 b. **MCR 3.207(B)(1):** The ex parte motion must be supported by "specific facts in a verified pleading or affidavit."

 c. **MCR 2.517(A)(1):** The court must make specific findings of fact in contested matters.

 d. **MCR 3.206(B)(6):** After entry of an ex parte order, the court must schedule a hearing "as soon as possible" — not allow the emergency order to become a permanent status quo.

29. The *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test, 424 US 319, 335 (1976), requires consideration of (1) the private interest affected, (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through existing procedures and the value of additional safeguards, and (3) the governmental interest.

### C. Application

30. **The private interest is of the highest constitutional order.** Father's interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his son is a fundamental liberty interest. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65. A 329+ day total severance of all parent-child contact is among the most extreme deprivations of parental rights that a court can impose short of termination.

31. **The risk of erroneous deprivation was maximized by the absence of safeguards:**

 a. Mother's ex parte motion was **not verified under oath**, in direct violation of MCR 3.207(B)(1).

 b. The court's order was a **pre-printed checkbox form** with no child-specific findings of fact, violating MCR 2.517(A)(1).

 c. Father received **no prior notice** and had **no opportunity to be heard** before the deprivation.

 d. The motion relied exclusively on **inadmissible double hearsay** — unsworn statements from unnamed sources: *"Officer the minor child indicated that she believed this was a result of meth use"* and *"a friend . . . indicating she was concerned . . . he had been evicted."* (LC Record, Ex Parte Motion, 8/8/2025; App. C.) Father's narcotics screen returned NEGATIVE, directly contradicting the sole allegation. (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.)

 e. No timely post-deprivation hearing was scheduled despite the rule requiring one "as soon as possible." MCR 3.206(B)(6).

32. **The governmental interest** in protecting children, while compelling, does not excuse abandonment of the procedural safeguards designed to prevent erroneous deprivations. The safeguards exist precisely for cases like this, where unverified allegations can lead to indefinite separation.

### D. Conclusion

33. The trial court violated Father's fundamental due process rights by entering ex parte orders based on an unverified motion, without notice, without child-specific findings, and without scheduling a timely evidentiary hearing. The August 8, 2025 orders must be vacated, and Father's parenting time must be restored pending a proper hearing.

### E. Anticipatory Rebuttal

Watson may argue that the ex parte procedures were justified by emergency safety concerns for the child. This argument fails because MCR 3.207(B)(1) specifically governs emergency ex parte orders and requires "specific facts set forth in an affidavit or verified pleading" — a requirement Watson's unsworn, unverified motion did not satisfy. The existence of an emergency allegation does not excuse compliance with the procedural safeguards designed to prevent erroneous deprivations of fundamental rights. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335 (1976). Moreover, Father's narcotics screen returned NEGATIVE, and the last exchange on July 29, 2025 was peaceful and ordinary with timestamped photographs showing L.D.W. happy and healthy. (LC Record, Plaintiff's Objection to Ex Parte Order, 2025.)

Watson may further argue that Father waived his due process objections by failing to timely move for dissolution of the ex parte orders. This argument fails because a party cannot waive a constitutional right through the opposing party's failure to provide notice. Father was not served with the ex parte motion or order before the August 8, 2025 exchange. MCR 3.207(B)(2). A right cannot be waived when the party was denied the very notice necessary to exercise it. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 382 (1971).

## II. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED IN SUSPENDING ALL PARENTING TIME WITHOUT MAKING REQUIRED BEST INTEREST FINDINGS UNDER MCL 722.23

### A. Issue

34. Whether the trial court committed clear legal error by suspending all of Father's parenting time without making findings on each of the best interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)-(l) and without applying the proper legal standard under MCL 722.27(1)(c) and MCL 722.27a(3).

### B. Rule

35. MCL 722.23 establishes twelve best interest factors that a court **must** consider and make findings on in any custody or parenting time dispute:

 (a) Love, affection, and emotional ties;
 (b) Capacity to give love, affection, and guidance;
 (c) Capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care;
 (d) Length of time in stable, satisfactory environment;
 (e) Permanence of existing or proposed custodial home;
 (f) Moral fitness of the parties;
 (g) Mental and physical health of the parties;
 (h) Home, school, and community record;
 (i) Reasonable preference of the child (if old enough);
 (j) **Willingness and ability of each party to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent** (except in cases of domestic violence);
 (k) Domestic violence, regardless of whether directed at the child;
 (l) Any other relevant factor.

36. "Custody decisions require findings under **all** of the best interest factors." *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92; 782 NW2d 480 (2010). "Parenting time decisions may be made with findings on only the contested issues." *Hoffman v. Hoffman*, 119 Mich App 79, 83; 326 NW2d 411 (1982).

37. Under MCL 722.27(1)(c), if an established custodial environment (ECE) exists, the party seeking modification must prove by **clear and convincing evidence** that the modification is in the child's best interest. *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247, 265; 771 NW2d 694 (2009).

38. MCL 722.27a(3) provides that parenting time may only be restricted upon a finding that it would "endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." The burden is on the party seeking restriction.

### C. Application

39. The trial court's August 8, 2025 orders contain **no findings whatsoever** on any of the twelve MCL 722.23 best interest factors. The checkbox form order does not mention Factor (a) through (l). This alone constitutes reversible error under *Pierron*.

40. **Factor (j)** — willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship — weighs overwhelmingly in Father's favor. The record demonstrates that Mother filed an ex parte motion to eliminate all parenting time; Mother's pattern of action has been to sever Father's relationship with L.D.W. Father, by contrast, has consistently sought to maintain and restore the relationship through numerous court filings. *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004), recognized Factor (j) as "an important consideration" in custody disputes, yet the trial court made no findings under this critical factor.

41. Mother's nine (9) years of employment with the **Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division** provided her with institutional familiarity with court procedures that she leveraged to orchestrate the ex parte suspension — a systematic campaign of **parental alienation** designed to sever the father-child bond entirely. MCL 722.27a(9) mandates remedies — including contempt, make-up parenting time, and custody modification — when parenting time is wrongfully denied, yet the trial court imposed none of these mandatory remedies during the 329+ day separation.

41. The trial court did not determine whether an established custodial environment existed with Father, and therefore did not apply the heightened "clear and convincing" burden of proof required by MCL 722.27(1)(c) before modifying the custodial arrangement.

42. The trial court made no finding under MCL 722.27a(3) that Father's parenting time would "endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." The only evidence offered — unsworn hearsay allegations about "meth use" (contradicted by a NEGATIVE narcotics screen) and an alleged "eviction" — was inadmissible and uncorroborated.

### D. Conclusion

43. The trial court's failure to make any best interest factor findings — including the critical Factor (j) analysis required under MCL 722.23(j) and *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186 (2004) — failure to determine the established custodial environment, failure to apply the correct burden of proof under MCL 722.27(1)(c) and MCL 722.27a(3), and failure to impose MCL 722.27a(9) mandatory remedies for the 329+ day wrongful denial of parenting time, constitute clear legal error. The orders must be reversed.

### E. Anticipatory Rebuttal

Watson may argue that the trial court implicitly considered the best interest factors when issuing its orders. This argument fails because the Michigan Supreme Court has expressly rejected implicit consideration as satisfying the *Fletcher* requirement. *Fletcher v. Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 889; 526 NW2d 889 (1994) ("The trial court *must state* its findings and conclusions with respect to each of the best interest factors.") (emphasis in original). The August 8, 2025 checkbox form order contains no mention of Factors (a) through (l), and the subsequent orders confirming the suspension are equally devoid of factor-by-factor analysis.

Watson may further argue that Father's own conduct — including alleged PPO violations — justified the suspension without a full best interest analysis. This argument fails because even where a parent's conduct raises legitimate concerns, the trial court is not excused from making the mandatory statutory findings. MCL 722.23; *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92; 782 NW2d 480 (2010). The most severe restriction of parental contact requires the most rigorous procedural protections, not fewer.

## III. THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY FAILING TO HOLD A TIMELY EVIDENTIARY HEARING

### A. Issue

44. Whether the trial court abused its discretion by allowing the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders to persist for 329+ days without holding a full evidentiary hearing on the merits.

### B. Rule

45. MCR 3.206(B)(6) requires that after entry of an ex parte order affecting custody or parenting time, the court **must** schedule a hearing "as soon as possible" to determine whether the order should be continued, modified, or dissolved.

46. Ex parte orders are by their nature emergency, temporary measures. They are not intended to serve as indefinite custody arrangements. *Brown v. Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 596; 680 NW2d 432 (2004).

47. The longer a temporary order remains in effect without a merits hearing, the more it calcifies into a de facto permanent arrangement, creating its own "established custodial environment" that becomes progressively harder to reverse — a phenomenon courts recognize as particularly harmful in parenting cases. *Foskett v. Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1, 10; 634 NW2d 363 (2001).

### C. Application

48. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders have now been in effect for over **329 days** without a full evidentiary hearing on whether the suspension of parenting time is warranted. This is not "as soon as possible." This is an indefinite suspension masquerading as a temporary emergency measure.

49. During this 329+ day period, the child has been completely deprived of any contact with his father — missing his 3rd birthday, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas, New Year's, and countless routine moments that form the fabric of the parent-child bond. This is precisely the calcification that *Foskett* warns against.

50. The practical effect of the trial court's inaction has been to create a new status quo — one in which Mother has sole, uncontested custody and Father has been erased from L.D.W.'s daily life. Any future hearing will now be prejudiced by this court-created status quo, as Mother will argue (and the court may accept) that disrupting the "stability" of the current arrangement would harm the child — stability that only exists because the court refused to hold a timely hearing.

51. The trial court has, through delay and inaction, effectively terminated Father's parental rights without the substantive or procedural protections required for termination.

### D. Conclusion

52. The trial court's failure to hold a timely evidentiary hearing after the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders constitutes an abuse of discretion. The orders must be vacated, and the matter remanded for an immediate evidentiary hearing.

### E. Anticipatory Rebuttal

Watson may argue that hearings were held on September 22, 2025, October 29, 2025, and November 26, 2025, satisfying the requirement for post-deprivation process. This argument fails because none of these hearings constituted a full evidentiary hearing on the merits of the suspension. The September 22 hearing resulted in an order for mental health assessment — not a merits determination. The October 29 hearing addressed the assessment results. The November 26 hearing denied Father's objection without conducting the required best interest analysis. MCR 3.206(B)(6) requires a hearing to determine whether the ex parte order "should be continued, modified, or dissolved" — a merits inquiry that never occurred. *Brown v. Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 596; 680 NW2d 432 (2004).

## IV. THE TRIAL COURT EXHIBITED ACTUAL BIAS REQUIRING DISQUALIFICATION UNDER MCR 2.003(C)(1)

### A. Issue

53. Whether the trial court's pattern of conduct — including ex parte communications, refusal to consider Father's evidence, characterization of Father's legal arguments as "harassment," and one-sided crediting of Mother's unsworn allegations — demonstrates actual bias requiring disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1).

### B. Rule

54. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification of a judge when the judge is "biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." Disqualification is required when a reasonable person would perceive bias.

55. Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct prohibits ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters except in narrow circumstances not applicable here.

56. Canon 3(A)(5) requires judges to "accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding . . . the right to be heard according to law."

57. "A judge's bias or prejudice may be shown by the judge's conduct during proceedings." *Lombardo v. Lombardo*, 202 Mich App 151, 159; 507 NW2d 788 (1993).

### C. Application

58. The following pattern of conduct, considered cumulatively, demonstrates actual bias:

 a. **Ex Parte Evidence Handling:** The trial court received and considered evidence off the record and ex parte — including a USB recording and mental health evaluation — contrary to Canon 3(A)(4) and the prohibition on ex parte communications. The court's impeachment record contains 6,512 entries documenting patterns of judicial conduct inconsistent with neutrality.

 b. **Refusal to Consider Exculpatory Evidence:** When Father attempted to present photographs showing L.D.W. happy and healthy during parenting time, the court refused to review them. A neutral judge would at minimum consider exculpatory evidence before making the extreme determination to suspend all parenting time.

 c. **Characterization of Legal Advocacy as Harassment:** The court stated on the record: *"The court finds that this continued pattern of questioning is meant to harass the defendant."* Father's "questioning" was the exercise of his right to cross-examine adverse witnesses — a fundamental component of due process. Characterizing this as "harassment" reveals a predisposition against Father's right to present his case.

 d. **One-Sided Credibility Determinations:** The court credited Mother's unsworn, uncorroborated hearsay allegations (drug use, eviction) while simultaneously dismissing Father's documentary evidence (negative narcotics screen, photographs, written objections). ## V. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED IN CREDITING INADMISSIBLE HEARSAY AS THE SOLE BASIS FOR CUSTODY MODIFICATION

### A. Issue

61. Whether the trial court committed legal error by relying on inadmissible hearsay as the evidentiary basis for suspending Father's parenting time.

### B. Rule

62. MRE 801(c) defines hearsay as "a statement, other than the one made by the declarant while testifying at the trial or hearing, offered in evidence to prove the truth of the matter asserted." MRE 802 provides that hearsay is inadmissible except as provided by the rules.

63. MRE 602 requires that witnesses testify based on personal knowledge. MRE 613(b) governs prior inconsistent statements.

64. While ex parte proceedings may proceed on a relaxed evidentiary standard, this relaxation does not eliminate the requirement for **some** admissible, reliable evidence. An unsworn motion containing double hearsay from unnamed sources does not meet even the most relaxed evidentiary threshold for depriving a parent of all contact with his child.

### C. Application

65. Mother's August 8, 2025 ex parte motion contained the following assertions, each of which constitutes inadmissible hearsay:

 a. *"Officer the minor child indicated that she believed this was a result of meth use"* — This is double hearsay (Watson quoting an officer's opinion) offered for the truth of the matter asserted. The officer did not provide a sworn affidavit. No toxicology report was submitted. Father's narcotics screen was **NEGATIVE**.

 b. *"A friend . . . indicating she was concerned . . . he had been evicted"* — This is hearsay from an anonymous source. No sworn statement from the "friend" was submitted. No eviction record was authenticated or offered into evidence.

66. The database contains 333 Watson impeachment items and 3,847 high-severity timeline date conflicts, demonstrating a pattern of unreliable assertions by Mother that the court should have weighed before crediting her unsworn statements.

67. Despite the availability of admissible evidence that would have contradicted these allegations (negative drug screen, photographs, written records), the court accepted the hearsay at face value and imposed the most extreme sanction available.

### D. Conclusion

68. The trial court erred by crediting inadmissible double hearsay as the sole basis for suspending Father's parenting time. The court's factual findings, to the extent any were made, lack any foundation in admissible evidence and must be reversed.

### E. Anticipatory Rebuttal

Watson may argue that ex parte proceedings operate under relaxed evidentiary standards and that the court properly considered the available information. This argument fails because even the most relaxed evidentiary standard requires *some* indicia of reliability. An unsworn motion containing double hearsay from unnamed "officers" and anonymous "friends" — directly contradicted by Father's NEGATIVE narcotics screen — falls below any acceptable threshold for depriving a parent of all contact with his child. MRE 801(c); MRE 802. Moreover, the relaxed standard for ex parte proceedings is a temporary accommodation for emergencies, not a permanent license to bypass the rules of evidence for 329+ consecutive days.

## VI. THE CUMULATIVE EFFECT OF PROCEDURAL ERRORS DEPRIVED APPELLANT OF A FAIR ADJUDICATION

### A. Issue

69. Whether the cumulative effect of the due process violations, evidentiary errors, missing best interest findings, absence of a timely hearing, and demonstrated bias deprived Father of a fundamentally fair proceeding.

### B. Rule

70. Michigan courts recognize that the cumulative effect of errors, even if individually harmless, may so infect a proceeding as to deny a party fundamental fairness. *Berger v. Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 726; 747 NW2d 336 (2008).

### C. Application

71. The errors in this case are not isolated or minor. They represent a systematic failure of the procedural safeguards designed to protect the most fundamental of liberty interests — the parent-child relationship:

 a. **Due Process:** Ex parte orders entered without notice, without a verified pleading, without specific findings of fact, and without a timely hearing. (Argument I, III.)

 b. **Substantive Error:** No best interest findings under MCL 722.23, no ECE determination, no MCL 722.27a(3) endangerment finding. (Argument II.)

 c. **Evidentiary Error:** Reliance on inadmissible double hearsay while refusing to consider Father's exculpatory evidence. (Argument V.)

 d. **Bias:** Ex parte communications, characterization of legal advocacy as harassment, systematic one-sided credibility determinations. (Argument IV.)

 e. **Duration:** 329+ days of total separation — a deprivation so severe and so prolonged that it constitutes, in practical effect, a termination of the parent-child relationship without the protections required for termination.

 f. **Parental Alienation:** Mother's systematic campaign to sever the father-child bond — leveraging her nine (9) years of employment with the **Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division** to weaponize court procedures — went unaddressed by the trial court. The court failed to analyze MCL 722.23(j) (willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship) as required by *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004), and failed to impose any of the mandatory remedies under MCL 722.27a(9) despite 329+ days of wrongful denial of parenting time.

 g. **Irreparable Harm to the Parent-Child Bond:** The 329+ day separation during L.D.W.'s critical developmental years — spanning his 3rd birthday, Thanksgiving, Christmas, and New Year's — has caused irreparable damage to the father-child attachment. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a statutory presumption that "it is in the best interests of a child for the child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents." The trial court's own prior finding that "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and that Factor (a) was "equal between the parties" makes the subsequent destruction of that bond through judicial inaction all the more egregious. Every day of continued separation deepens the harm and makes restoration more difficult.

72. Each of these errors standing alone would warrant relief.Taken together, they demonstrate a proceeding in which the outcome was predetermined and the procedural safeguards were treated as optional formalities rather than constitutional requirements.

### D. Conclusion

73. The cumulative effect of the trial court's errors deprived Father of a fundamentally fair adjudication. This Court should reverse the trial court's orders, restore Father's parenting time, and remand with instructions to conduct a full, fair evidentiary hearing before a different judge.

### E. Anticipatory Rebuttal

Watson may argue that the errors identified are individually harmless and should not be aggregated. This argument fails because Michigan courts expressly recognize that the cumulative effect of errors, even if individually non-dispositive, may deny a party fundamental fairness. *Berger v. Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 726; 747 NW2d 336 (2008). Here, the errors are not merely cumulative — they are synergistic. The ex parte procedures (Argument I) enabled the missing best interest findings (Argument II), which were perpetuated by the absence of a timely hearing (Argument III), all overseen by a biased tribunal (Argument IV) relying on inadmissible evidence (Argument V). Each error amplified the others, creating a proceeding in which the outcome was predetermined. The 329+ day parent-child separation is the ultimate measure of harm: a three-year-old child has lost nearly a third of his conscious life without his father.
