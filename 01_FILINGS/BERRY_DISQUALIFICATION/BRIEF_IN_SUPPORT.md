## I. INTRODUCTION

This Brief accompanies Plaintiff's Motion for Disqualification and Affidavit of Bias, both filed pursuant to MCR 2.003. The central issue is not a close call. When a judge's husband is related to the domestic partner of a party — and the judge never discloses that connection over more than 290 days of contested litigation involving parental rights, custody, child support, and contempt proceedings resulting in incarceration — the question is not whether disqualification is warranted. The question is why it took this long.

The Berry-McNeill conflict is not a matter of judicial temperament or disputed legal interpretation. It is a **structural conflict of interest** — a familial link between the bench and a party that, once established, renders every prior ruling suspect and every future ruling indefensible. No amount of good faith can cure a structural conflict. The only remedy is disqualification and reassignment.

## III. CONTROLLING AUTHORITY

The applicable standard is established by MCR 2.003(C)(1) and the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, as interpreted by the Michigan Supreme Court in *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347 (1975), and its progeny. The test is objective:

> "The question is whether the conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired."

*Crampton*, 395 Mich at 351.

The reviewing standard is not whether the judge is actually biased, but whether a **"reasonable, fully informed observer"** would have significant doubt about the judge's impartiality. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996). Where the ground for disqualification is an extrajudicial familial relationship, the inquiry focuses on the nature and closeness of the relationship, the extent of the interest at stake, and the adequacy (or absence) of disclosure.

## V. ARGUMENT

### A. The Berry-McNeill Connection Mandates Disqualification Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) establishes a bright-line rule: a judge is disqualified when a person within the third degree of relationship to the judge or the judge's spouse:

> (i) is a party to the proceeding, or an officer, director, or trustee of a party;
> (ii) is acting as a lawyer in the proceeding;
> (iii) is known by the judge to have a more than de minimis interest that could be substantially affected by the proceeding; or
> (iv) is to the judge's knowledge likely to be a material witness in the proceeding.

The chain here is direct: **Judge McNeill → married to Cavan Berry → (on information and belief) related to Ronald Berry → domestic partner of Defendant Watson**. If the Cavan-Ronald Berry relationship is within the third degree (brothers, half-brothers, or first cousins), then Ronald Berry is a "person within the third degree of relationship" to the judge's spouse.

Ronald Berry's domestic relationship with the Defendant gives him a **more than de minimis interest** in the outcome of these proceedings. Every custody order, parenting time schedule, and child support determination directly affects the household in which Ronald Berry lives. The interest is not speculative; it is concrete, daily, and ongoing. This satisfies MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iii).

Further, to the extent Ronald Berry has personal knowledge of conditions in the Watson household — and a domestic partner living with a party and the child necessarily does — he is **likely to be a material witness** in future custody or parenting time proceedings. This satisfies MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iv).

The federal parallel confirms this analysis. **28 U.S.C. § 455(b)(5)(iii)** requires disqualification where a spouse's relative "is known by the judge to have an interest that could be substantially affected by the outcome of the proceeding." While the federal statute is not binding in state court, it reflects the same principle: **familial relationships between the bench and a party's household are categorically disqualifying**.

### B. The Undisclosed Conflict Satisfies the Extrajudicial Source Doctrine

The United States Supreme Court in *Liteky v. United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 554-55 (1994), distinguished between:

- **Judicial source bias**: opinions formed during the proceedings (generally insufficient for disqualification); and
- **Extrajudicial source bias**: predispositions arising from the judge's personal relationships, knowledge, or interests outside the proceedings (classic ground for disqualification).

The Berry-McNeill familial connection is an **extrajudicial source** in its purest form. The connection existed before these proceedings began, operates outside the courtroom, and provides the judge with a personal, familial motive to favor one party. This is precisely the scenario the disqualification rules were designed to address.

Under *Liteky*, once an extrajudicial source of potential bias is identified, the inquiry shifts from whether the judge's individual rulings were legally defensible to whether the **source of the potential bias is sufficient to require recusal**. Here, the source — a direct familial link to a party's household — is more than sufficient.

### C. Canon 3(C)(1) — Impartiality Might Reasonably Be Questioned

Canon 3(C)(1) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides the broadest disqualification standard:

> "A judge should disqualify himself or herself in a proceeding in which the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."

This is a "reasonable person" standard. The question is not whether Judge McNeill believes herself to be impartial, but whether a **reasonable, fully informed observer** would harbor "significant doubt" about her impartiality. *Cain*, 451 Mich at 497.

Consider what that fully informed observer would know:

1. The judge's husband is an attorney who practices **family law** in the **same county** where the judge presides over family cases.

2. The judge's husband is **related to the domestic partner of the Defendant** in the case before the judge.

3. The judge and the Defendant **live in the same small city** of Norton Shores.

4. The judge **never disclosed** any of these connections over 290+ days of contested litigation.

5. The judge entered **ex parte orders** suspending the Plaintiff's parenting time without the required factual findings.

6. The judge **incarcerated the Plaintiff for 59 days** on contempt without the procedural protections required by *Dougherty* and *Mead*.

7. The judge **consistently granted the Defendant's motions** while **denying or ignoring the Plaintiff's**.

A reasonable observer would not merely harbor "significant doubt." A reasonable observer would conclude that the proceedings were compromised from the outset. Canon 3(C)(1) demands disqualification.

Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i) provides additional support: disqualification is required when a **member of the judge's spouse's family** has "an interest that could be substantially affected by the outcome of the proceeding." Ronald Berry is a member of Judge McNeill's spouse's family (on information and belief), and his domestic relationship with the Defendant gives him a direct interest in the outcome. This is a textbook application of Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i).

### D. The 290-Day Non-Disclosure Independently Warrants Disqualification

MCR 2.003(B) imposes an **affirmative, self-executing duty** on judges to disqualify themselves whenever disqualification is required. This duty does not depend on a party filing a motion. The judge must act **sua sponte**.

Canon 2(A) requires judges to "act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary." Canon 2(B) requires that "a judge shall not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment."

Judge McNeill knew or should have known that her husband is related to Ronald Berry. A judge is presumed to know the family members of her own spouse. She knew or should have known that Ronald Berry was in a domestic relationship with a party appearing before her. She had an obligation to disclose this connection and recuse herself at the earliest opportunity.

Instead, she said nothing. For **290+ days**. Through hearings, orders, contempt proceedings, incarceration, and ex parte actions that fundamentally altered the Plaintiff's relationship with his child.

The non-disclosure is an **independent ground** for disqualification, separate from the underlying conflict. Even if the Court were to find the familial connection insufficient to mandate automatic disqualification (which Plaintiff disputes), the **failure to disclose** — for nearly a year, while presiding over a case involving the liberty and parental rights of a self-represented litigant — independently satisfies the "appearance of impropriety" standard of Canon 2(A) and the "reasonably questioned impartiality" standard of Canon 3(C)(1).

The silence speaks. A judge who has nothing to hide has nothing to conceal.

### E. Caperton and Due Process — The Constitutional Floor

The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause provides an independent constitutional basis for disqualification. In *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), the Supreme Court held:

> "[T]here is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions — when a person with a personal stake in a particular case had a significant and disproportionate influence in placing the judge on the case." *Id.* at 884.

While *Caperton* involved campaign contributions, the Court's due process analysis is not limited to that context. The principle is general: **due process requires recusal whenever the probability of actual bias is "too high to be constitutionally tolerable."** *Id.* at 872.

Here, the "person with a personal stake" is Ronald Berry — who lives with the Defendant and whose daily life is directly affected by every custody, parenting time, and support order in this case. The connection to the judge flows through the judge's husband, creating a **familial pipeline** between the bench and a party's household.

*Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510, 523 (1927), established that "it certainly violates the Fourteenth Amendment . . . to subject [a party's] liberty or property to the judgment of a court the judge of which has a direct, personal, substantial, pecuniary interest in reaching a conclusion against him." While the interest here is familial rather than pecuniary, the principle is the same: a judge with a personal connection to one party's household cannot provide the "fair trial in a fair tribunal" that due process demands. *Withrow v. Larkin*, 421 U.S. 35, 47 (1975).

The due process argument is especially compelling here because the proceedings resulted in **Plaintiff's incarceration for 59 days** — a deprivation of physical liberty that triggers the highest level of due process protection. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 95 (1987). A litigant incarcerated by a judge who harbored an undisclosed familial connection to the opposing party's household has been denied the most basic guarantee of American justice: an impartial tribunal.

### F. The Pattern of Rulings Corroborates Structural Bias

Plaintiff acknowledges the general principle that "judicial rulings alone almost never constitute a valid basis for a bias or partiality motion." *Liteky*, 510 U.S. at 555. However, *Liteky* also recognized that rulings **can** support a disqualification motion when they "reveal such a high degree of favoritism or antagonism as to make fair judgment impossible." *Id.*

Here, the pattern of rulings does not stand alone — it is read against the backdrop of the now-discovered structural conflict of interest. When viewed through that lens, the pattern is probative:

- **Ex parte orders favoring Defendant**: Multiple ex parte orders suspending Plaintiff's parenting time without the MCR 3.207(B) findings.
- **Contempt and incarceration**: 59 days of jail time without the *Dougherty*/*Mead* protections.
- **Differential treatment**: Defendant's motions promptly addressed and granted; Plaintiff's delayed, denied, or ignored.
- **Child support without hearing**: $1,056.60/month set without evidentiary hearing on income.
- **Cumulative parenting time deprivation**: 569+ days lost through a combination of ex parte orders, non-enforcement, and inaction.

*Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573 (2001), holds that a pattern of conduct can establish bias. The pattern here — read in context — is consistent with a predisposition toward the Defendant arising from the familial connection, and inconsistent with the neutral, impartial adjudication that MCR 2.003 and the Judicial Canons demand.

### G. All Prior Orders Are Subject to Vacatur

If Judge McNeill was disqualified ab initio — as Plaintiff contends — then every order she entered in this case was entered by a judge who lacked the impartiality required by due process and the Michigan Court Rules. Such orders are not merely erroneous; they are **structurally defective**.

The appropriate remedy is not automatic vacatur of every order, but rather **referral to the successor judge** with full authority to review, modify, or vacate any order that may have been affected by the conflict. Plaintiff specifically requests review of:

1. All ex parte orders suspending parenting time;
2. The contempt findings and incarceration orders;
3. The child support order;
4. Any credibility determinations adverse to Plaintiff;
5. Any order entered without a hearing or without adequate notice.

The successor judge, free from the structural conflict, can determine whether each order would have been entered by an impartial tribunal, and can fashion appropriate relief.
