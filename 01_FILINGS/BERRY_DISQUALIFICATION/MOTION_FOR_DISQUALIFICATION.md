## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — Third-Degree Familial Relationship Mandates Disqualification

23. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that a judge is disqualified when:

> *"[A] person within the third degree of relationship to either [the judge or the judge's spouse] . . . (i) is a party to the proceeding, or an officer, director, or trustee of a party; (ii) is acting as a lawyer in the proceeding; (iii) is known by the judge to have a more than de minimis interest that could be substantially affected by the proceeding; or (iv) is to the judge's knowledge likely to be a material witness in the proceeding."*

24. If Ronald Berry is related to Cavan John Berry (Judge McNeill's husband) within the third degree — as brother, half-brother, or first cousin — then Ronald Berry is a "person within the third degree of relationship" to the judge's spouse.

25. Ronald Berry, through his domestic relationship with Defendant Emily A. Watson, has a **more than de minimis interest** in the outcome of these proceedings. Custody, parenting time, and child support determinations directly affect the household in which Ronald Berry resides with the Defendant and, upon information and belief, with the minor child L.D.W. This satisfies MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iii).

26. Further, to the extent Ronald Berry has personal knowledge of the circumstances in the Watson household — where he resides with Defendant and L.D.W. — he is **likely to be a material witness** in future proceedings. This satisfies MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iv).

27. Any one of these sub-provisions independently mandates disqualification. Together, they make continued presidence by Judge McNeill constitutionally untenable.

### B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

28. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) provides that a judge is disqualified when **"[t]he judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney."**

29. The undisclosed familial connection between Judge McNeill's household and the Defendant's household, combined with the pattern of rulings detailed in Section II.F above, satisfies the objective test for bias articulated in *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975): whether the judge's conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired."

30. The extrajudicial source doctrine, as articulated in *Liteky v. United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 554-55 (1994), is directly applicable here. The Berry familial connection is a quintessential **extrajudicial source** of potential bias — it arises not from the proceedings themselves but from the judge's personal family relationships. The Supreme Court in *Liteky* held that "opinions formed by the judge on the basis of facts introduced or events occurring in the course of the current proceedings" are generally insufficient, but "a favorable or unfavorable predisposition" from an extrajudicial source is precisely what the disqualification standards target.

31. *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573 (2001), holds that a pattern of conduct can establish bias. Here, the pattern of one-sided rulings — read against the backdrop of the undisclosed family connection — is consistent with, and reasonably explained by, a predisposition toward the Defendant arising from the judge's family ties.

### C. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(c) and (d) — Spouse's Interest and Financial Interest

32. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(c) provides for disqualification when the judge or the judge's spouse "has a more than de minimis interest that could be substantially affected by the proceeding."

33. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(d) provides for disqualification when the judge or the judge's spouse "has a financial interest in the subject matter in controversy or in a party to the proceeding."

34. The outcomes of this custody and support proceeding directly affect the household of a family member of Judge McNeill's husband. To the extent that custody arrangements, parenting time schedules, and child support payments affect the living situation, financial circumstances, and daily life of the Berry-Watson household — and they plainly do — Judge McNeill's spouse has a familial interest that satisfies subsection (c). Any direct or indirect financial consequences flowing through the Berry family relationship satisfy subsection (d).

### D. Canon 3(C)(1) — Impartiality Might Reasonably Be Questioned

35. Canon 3(C)(1) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides:

> *"A judge should disqualify himself or herself in a proceeding in which the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."*

36. Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i) specifically addresses situations where a "member of [the judge's spouse's] family" has "an interest that could be substantially affected by the outcome of the proceeding."

37. The United States Supreme Court's decision in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 881 (2009), provides the governing constitutional framework: **"[T]here is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions — when a person with a personal stake in a particular case had a significant and disproportionate influence in placing the judge on the case."** While *Caperton* involved campaign contributions, the principle applies with equal force where a judge's family member has a personal stake in the outcome.

38. The test is objective. The question is not whether Judge McNeill actually harbors bias, but whether "a reasonable, fully informed observer would entertain significant doubt" about her impartiality. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996). A reasonable observer, learning that the presiding judge's husband is related to the domestic partner of one of the parties, would entertain more than "significant doubt" — such an observer would view continued presidence as a structural violation of due process.

### E. MCR 2.003(B) — Duty to Disclose and Disqualify Sua Sponte

39. MCR 2.003(B) provides: **"A judge shall disqualify himself or herself in any proceeding in which the judge cannot impartially hear a case."** This provision imposes an **affirmative, self-executing duty** to identify and disclose potential conflicts.

40. Judge McNeill knew or should have known of her husband's relationship to Ronald Berry. She knew or should have known that Ronald Berry was in a domestic relationship with a party appearing before her. She had an obligation to disclose this connection and recuse herself, and she failed to do so for **290+ days** — during which she entered orders materially affecting Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights.

41. The failure to disclose is not a neutral omission. Canon 2(A) requires judges to "act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary." Canon 2(B) requires that a judge "not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment." A 290-day silence on a known (or knowable) familial connection to a party's domestic partner is irreconcilable with these canons.

### F. Appearance of Impropriety — Canons 1, 2(A), and 2(B)

42. Canon 1 demands that judges "uphold the integrity and independence of the judiciary." Canon 2(A) requires conduct that "promotes public confidence" in judicial impartiality. Canon 2(B) prohibits family relationships from influencing judicial conduct.

43. The facts here present a textbook appearance of impropriety:

    - The judge's husband is an attorney practicing **family law** in the **same county** where the judge presides over family cases;
    - The judge's husband is, on information and belief, related to the **domestic partner of the Defendant**;
    - The judge and the Defendant reside in the **same city**;
    - The judge **never disclosed** any of these connections;
    - The judge's rulings have **systematically favored the Defendant** over 290+ days.

44. Even if every ruling were defensible in isolation, the **cumulative appearance** — undisclosed family connection, same-city residence, family-law-practicing spouse, and one-sided pattern of rulings — is devastating to public confidence. A reasonable, fully informed member of the public would conclude that the fix was in.

### G. Federal Parallel — 28 U.S.C. § 455 and Constitutional Due Process

45. While this Court applies Michigan standards, the federal parallel standard under **28 U.S.C. § 455** is instructive and supports the same result. Section 455(b)(5)(iii) requires disqualification where a judge's spouse's relative "is known by the judge to have an interest that could be substantially affected by the outcome of the proceeding."

46. The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause provides an independent constitutional floor. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), holds that due process may require recusal even absent a specific court rule violation, where "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 872.

47. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987), establishes that due process demands heightened procedural protections in contempt proceedings resulting in incarceration. The fact that Judge McNeill presided over contempt proceedings that resulted in Plaintiff's 59-day incarceration — while harboring an undisclosed familial connection to the opposing party's household — implicates the most fundamental due process concerns.

### H. All Prior Orders Are Subject to Challenge

48. If this Motion is granted, Plaintiff respectfully submits that **all prior orders entered by Judge McNeill in this matter are subject to review for potential vacatur**, on the ground that they were entered by a judge who was disqualified ab initio.

49. A judge who should have been disqualified from the outset lacks the impartiality required by due process. Orders entered under such circumstances are not merely erroneous — they are structurally defective. While the successor judge will determine the appropriate scope of review, Plaintiff preserves his right to challenge each order on this basis.
