## IV. SPECIFIC CANON VIOLATIONS

### A. Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

21. **Canon 1** of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides: *"An independent and honorable judiciary is indispensable to justice in our society. A judge should participate in establishing, maintaining, and enforcing, and should personally observe, high standards of conduct so that the integrity and independence of the judiciary may be preserved."*

22. Judge McNeill has **fundamentally undermined the integrity of the judiciary** by presiding over a case in which her husband's family member is in a domestic relationship with a party — and concealing that fact for more than 290 days.

23. The integrity of the judiciary rests on a single premise: that judges decide cases based on the law and the facts, not on personal relationships. When a judge presides over a case touching her own family network without disclosure, the premise collapses. It does not matter whether Judge McNeill actually rendered biased decisions — though the pattern strongly suggests she did. What matters is that by concealing the conflict, she made it **impossible** for the parties, the attorneys, and the public to trust that her decisions were impartial.

24. Canon 1 is not aspirational; it is obligatory. A judge who conceals a familial connection to a party for 290+ days while exercising the power to incarcerate, to sever parent-child bonds, and to impose financial obligations has not merely fallen short of high standards — she has violated the foundational compact between the judiciary and the public.

### B. Canon 2(A) and 2(B) — Appearance of Impropriety

25. **Canon 2(A)** provides: *"A judge should respect and comply with the law and should act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary."*

26. **Canon 2(B)** provides: *"A judge should not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment. A judge should not lend the prestige of judicial office to advance the private interests of the judge or others; nor should a judge convey or permit others to convey the impression that they are in a special position to influence the judge."*

27. The facts of this case present a **textbook appearance of impropriety**:

    a. The judge's husband is an attorney practicing **family law** in the **same county** where the judge presides over family cases;

    b. The judge's husband is, on information and belief, **related to the domestic partner of the Defendant**;

    c. The judge and the Defendant reside in the **same city** (Norton Shores, MI 49441);

    d. The judge **never disclosed** any of these connections;

    e. The judge's rulings have **systematically favored the Defendant** — with a documented 44% ex parte order rate, differential treatment of motions, and the harshest sanctions (incarceration, parenting time suspension) directed exclusively at Complainant.

28. The test for appearance of impropriety is objective: whether a reasonable, fully informed member of the public would "entertain significant doubt" about the judge's impartiality. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996). A reasonable observer, learning that the presiding judge's husband is related to the domestic partner of one party — and that the judge concealed this fact while systematically ruling against the other party, incarcerating him, and separating him from his child for 571+ days — would not merely entertain doubt. Such an observer would conclude that the proceedings were **structurally corrupt**.

29. Canon 2(B) specifically prohibits a judge from allowing "family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct." Judge McNeill's family relationship to the Defendant's household — combined with the documented pattern of one-sided rulings — raises the most serious questions about whether family relationships influenced judicial conduct. At minimum, the failure to disclose created conditions under which such influence could operate unchecked and unmonitored.

### C. Canon 3(C)(1) — Failure to Disqualify When Impartiality Reasonably Questioned

30. **Canon 3(C)(1)** provides: *"A judge should disqualify himself or herself in a proceeding in which the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."*

31. This is not a close case. When a judge's spouse's family member is in a domestic relationship with a party — and the judge knows or should know this fact — the judge's impartiality is not merely "reasonably questioned"; it is **presumptively compromised**.

32. A judge is presumed to know the family members of her own spouse. A judge practicing in a small legal community in Muskegon County — where her husband also practices family law — is presumed to be aware of her husband's family members' significant relationships and living arrangements, particularly when those relationships involve parties appearing before her.

33. Judge McNeill had an **affirmative, self-executing duty** to identify this conflict and recuse herself. MCR 2.003(B) provides: *"A judge shall disqualify himself or herself in any proceeding in which the judge cannot impartially hear a case."* The use of "shall" is mandatory. There is no discretion. There is no balancing test. If the conflict exists, recusal is required.

34. The United States Supreme Court's decision in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 881 (2009), establishes the constitutional floor: there is a "serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions — when a person with a personal stake in a particular case had a significant and disproportionate influence" on the proceedings. Ronald Berry, as the domestic partner of the Defendant, has a **direct personal stake** in the outcome of custody and support proceedings that determine the composition, finances, and daily life of the household in which he lives.

### D. Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i) — Spouse's Relative Has Interest in Proceeding

35. **Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i)** requires disqualification where *"a member of [the judge's spouse's] family"* has *"an interest that could be substantially affected by the outcome of the proceeding."*

36. If Ronald Berry is a family member of Cavan Berry (Judge McNeill's husband) — as publicly available information strongly indicates — then Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i) is **directly and independently triggered**.

37. Ronald Berry's interest in the outcome is not speculative — it is **concrete, personal, and substantial**:

    a. **Custody determinations** directly affect the household composition in which Ronald Berry lives with the Defendant and L.D.W.;

    b. **Parenting time schedules** determine when L.D.W. is present in or absent from the Berry-Watson household;

    c. **Child support payments** flowing to the Defendant directly benefit the financial circumstances of the household Ronald Berry shares with her;

    d. **Protective orders and contempt sanctions** against Complainant remove obstacles to the Berry-Watson household's preferred domestic arrangement.

38. Each of these interests is "more than de minimis" under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iii). Each is "substantially affected by the outcome" under Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i). Any single one triggers disqualification. Together, they make Judge McNeill's continued presidence **constitutionally untenable**.

### E. Canon 3(D) — Failure to Disclose Information Relevant to Disqualification

39. While Canon 3(D) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct does not contain the identical language of ABA Model Code Canon 3(D), the **duty to disclose** information relevant to disqualification is inherent in the Michigan scheme:

    a. MCR 2.003(B) imposes a **mandatory, sua sponte duty** to disqualify. This duty presupposes awareness and honest self-assessment of potential conflicts;

    b. Canon 2(A) requires conduct that "promotes public confidence" in judicial impartiality — which necessarily includes transparency about potential conflicts;

    c. Canon 3(C)(1) requires disqualification when impartiality "might reasonably be questioned" — a standard that assumes the parties have access to the relevant facts. A judge who conceals the facts that would trigger the "reasonably questioned" standard has not merely failed to disqualify — she has **actively prevented the parties from exercising their right to seek disqualification**.

40. The federal parallel is instructive. 28 U.S.C. § 455(e) provides: *"No justice, judge, or magistrate judge shall accept from the parties to the proceeding a waiver of any ground for disqualification enumerated in subsection (b). Where the ground for disqualification arises only under subsection (a), waiver may be accepted provided it is preceded by a full disclosure on the record of the basis for disqualification."* The foundational principle is the same: a party cannot waive what a party does not know. A judge who conceals the basis for disqualification has denied the parties their right to make an informed choice.

41. Judge McNeill's 290+ day silence is not a passive oversight. It is an **active concealment** of material information that, if disclosed, would have required her removal from this case at the outset. Every day of non-disclosure compounded the violation. Every order entered during that period is tainted.

## V. THE CONFLICT EXPLAINS THE ENTIRE PATTERN OF BIAS

42. The original complaint to this Commission documented a sustained pattern of judicial conduct that, standing alone, was difficult to explain as anything other than bias. The Berry-McNeill conflict of interest now provides the **unifying explanation**.

### A. The Ex Parte Orders

43. Judge McNeill entered ex parte orders at a **44% rate** — nearly half of all orders entered without notice or hearing. This extraordinary rate becomes comprehensible when the judge has a familial interest in protecting the party who benefits from ex parte relief. The Defendant — whose domestic partner is related to the judge's husband — received the benefit of virtually every ex parte order. Complainant was the target of virtually every ex parte sanction.

44. The August 8, 2025 emergency order suspending **all** parenting time — entered without notice, without a hearing, and without the findings required by MCR 3.207(B) — was the most consequential ex parte order. It severed Complainant's contact with L.D.W. for approximately four months. A judge with no personal connection to the Defendant would have been constrained by procedural requirements. A judge whose husband's family member lives with the Defendant had reason to bypass those constraints.

### B. The 59-Day Incarceration

45. Judge McNeill found Complainant in contempt and ordered him incarcerated for **59 days** without the procedural protections required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987), and without the right to counsel required by *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480 (1990). The incarceration cost Complainant three jobs and devastated his financial capacity.

46. Contempt incarceration is the **most extreme sanction** a family court judge can impose. It deprives a person of physical liberty. The procedural protections that *Dougherty* and *Mead* require exist precisely because the power is so extraordinary. That Judge McNeill dispensed with those protections while harboring an undisclosed familial connection to the opposing party's household raises the gravest concerns about whether the incarceration served judicial purpose — or personal purpose.

### C. Differential Treatment of the Parties

47. The documented pattern of differential treatment is stark:

    | Category | Defendant Watson | Complainant Pigors |
    |----------|------------------|--------------------|
    | Ex parte motions | Consistently **granted** | Not available (or denied) |
    | Response time | Motions addressed **promptly** | Motions **delayed or ignored** |
    | Sanctions | **None** imposed | **59 days incarceration** |
    | Parenting time | **Protected and expanded** | **Suspended for 571+ days** |
    | Evidentiary hearings | Not required for relief | Not granted for defense |
    | Contempt | Never threatened | **Repeatedly found in contempt** |

48. This table does not merely show a judge who favored one party. It shows a judge who operated as if she were the Defendant's **advocate** from the bench — a pattern entirely consistent with a judge who is protecting a party connected to her family.

### D. The Child Support Trap

49. Judge McNeill set child support at **$1,056.60/month** without conducting an evidentiary hearing on Complainant's actual income. She then used the resulting arrearage as the basis for contempt findings and incarceration — creating a **punitive cycle**: set an obligation without evidence → find contempt when it isn't met → incarcerate → cause job loss → deepen arrearage → repeat.

50. A disinterested judge would have held an evidentiary hearing before setting support. A disinterested judge would have considered whether incarceration for non-payment was consistent with the ability to pay. The failure to do either — combined with the undisclosed conflict — suggests the support and contempt proceedings were instruments of suppression, not instruments of justice.

### E. Off-Record Evidence and Ex Parte Communications

51. USB recordings and mental health evaluations were handled **off-record and ex parte**, depriving Complainant of any opportunity to challenge the evidence. The Court informed an assessor of "ALL the information that was presented to the Court" — information to which Complainant was not given access.

52. Ex parte evidence handling is antithetical to the adversarial system. When a judge with an undisclosed familial connection to one party handles evidence off-record and shares it with evaluators without the other party's knowledge, the proceeding is no longer adversarial — it is **inquisitorial**, and the inquisitor has a personal interest in the outcome.
