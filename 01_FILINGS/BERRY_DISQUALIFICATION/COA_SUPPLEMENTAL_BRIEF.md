# IV. ARGUMENT

## A. The Undisclosed Berry-McNeill Conflict Is Structural Error Requiring Automatic Reversal

### 1. The Constitutional Requirement of an Impartial Tribunal

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees every litigant the right to an impartial tribunal. This guarantee is not aspirational; it is structural. "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." *In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955). This right "applies with equal force whether the proceeding is criminal or civil, and whether the parties are represented by counsel or not." *Withrow v. Larkin*, 421 U.S. 35, 46 (1975).

The United States Supreme Court has consistently held that judicial disqualification is "a matter of constitutional dimension" that goes to the very foundation of the proceeding. *Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510, 523 (1927); *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 876 (2009); *Williams v. Pennsylvania*, 579 U.S. 1, 8 (2016).

### 2. Structural Error Defined

Structural errors are constitutional defects that "affect the framework within which the trial proceeds, rather than simply an error in the trial process itself." *Williams*, 579 U.S. at 12. They are "defects in the constitution of the trial mechanism" that defy harmless-error analysis because they "infect the entire trial process." *Id.*

The Supreme Court has specifically identified the participation of a biased or conflicted judge as a structural error:

> "The appearance of bias demeans the reputation and integrity of the judiciary and is as injurious to the system of justice as actual bias. . . . When the judicial officer presiding over a case is not combatively impartial—when that officer is biased—all bets are off: any outcome is suspect, any conviction is potentially unfair, and no amount of after-the-fact rationalization can cure the structural injury to due process."

*Williams*, 579 U.S. at 14.

In *Tumey*, the Court held that proceedings before a financially interested judge violated due process regardless of whether actual bias was proven, because "it certainly violates the Fourteenth Amendment . . . to subject his liberty or property to the judgment of a court the judge of which has a direct, personal, substantial, pecuniary interest in reaching a conclusion against him." 273 U.S. at 523.

### 3. The Berry-McNeill Conflict Is Structural

The undisclosed Berry-McNeill conflict is a textbook structural defect. It is not an error that occurred at one point during the trial; it is a condition that existed before the first order was entered and persisted through every hearing, every ruling, and every order for more than 290 days. It did not affect one isolated decision — it pervaded the entire proceeding.

When the presiding judge's spouse is related to the domestic partner of a party, the impartiality of every ruling is compromised — not because actual bias is proven, but because the structural conditions for impartiality are absent. A tribunal is not "fair" in the constitutional sense when the judge has an undisclosed familial connection to a party, regardless of whether the judge's individual decisions can be independently justified. *See Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 884 ("[T]he inquiry is an objective one. The Court asks not whether the judge is actually, subjectively biased, but whether the average judge in his position is 'likely' to be neutral.").

### 4. Automatic Reversal Is the Only Remedy

Because the participation of a disqualified judge is structural error, the remedy is automatic reversal without a showing of specific prejudice. *Williams*, 579 U.S. at 12; *Tumey*, 273 U.S. at 535. The Court need not examine whether each individual order would have been different before a different judge. The error is the proceeding itself, not any particular ruling within it.

As the Supreme Court explained in *In re Murchison*:

> "Our system of law has always endeavored to prevent even the probability of unfairness. . . . [T]o perform its high function in the best way, justice must satisfy the appearance of justice."

349 U.S. at 136 (internal quotation marks omitted).

The 290+ days of proceedings below cannot satisfy the appearance of justice when the presiding judge's husband's family member was in a domestic relationship with one of the parties — and the judge never said a word.

### 5. The *Caperton* Framework Requires Reversal

In *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), the Supreme Court held that due process requires recusal when "there is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions." *Id.* at 884. The *Caperton* Court emphasized that the inquiry is prophylactic and structural:

> "The difficulties of inquiring into actual bias, and the fact that the inquiry is often a private one, simply underscore the need for objective rules. Otherwise there may be no adequate protection against a judge who simply misreads or misapprehends the real motives at work in deciding the case."

*Id.* at 883.

Here, the objective facts create far more than a "serious risk" of bias — they create a structural condition in which impartiality cannot be presumed:

- The judge's husband is related to the domestic partner of a party;
- The judge and the Defendant reside in the same city;
- The judge's husband practices family law in the same county and appears before the same courts;
- The judge never disclosed any of these connections;
- The pattern of rulings overwhelmingly favored the party connected to the judge's family: 52 ex parte orders, 100% one-sided, 44% of all orders entered without notice to Father.

Under *Caperton*, reversal is required.

## B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) Mandates Disqualification

### 1. The Rule

MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that a judge is disqualified when:

> "[A] person within the third degree of relationship to either [the judge or the judge's spouse] . . . (i) is a party to the proceeding, or an officer, director, or trustee of a party; (ii) is acting as a lawyer in the proceeding; (iii) is known by the judge to have a more than de minimis interest that could be substantially affected by the proceeding; or (iv) is to the judge's knowledge likely to be a material witness in the proceeding."

This rule establishes a **bright-line, mandatory disqualification** that does not require a showing of actual bias. If the familial relationship exists and any of the four sub-conditions is met, disqualification is automatic.

### 2. Application

**The familial link.** Judge McNeill's husband is Cavan John Berry. On information and belief, Ronald Berry is related to Cavan John Berry within the third degree (as brother, half-brother, or first cousin). If so, Ronald Berry is "a person within the third degree of relationship to . . . the judge's spouse."

**Sub-condition (iii): More than de minimis interest.** Ronald Berry, through his domestic relationship with Defendant Emily A. Watson, has a *more than de minimis interest* in the outcome of these proceedings. Custody and parenting time determinations directly affect the household in which Ronald Berry resides with the Defendant and, on information and belief, with the minor child L.D.W. Child support payments flow into that household. The daily logistics of custody — pick-ups, drop-offs, supervised visitation schedules, residential arrangements — all directly implicate the living situation that Ronald Berry shares with Defendant. This is not a remote or speculative interest; it is immediate and substantial. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iii) is satisfied.

**Sub-condition (iv): Material witness.** To the extent Ronald Berry has personal knowledge of the circumstances in the Watson household — where he resides with Defendant and L.D.W. — he is *likely to be a material witness* in future proceedings concerning custody, parenting time, and the best interests of L.D.W. under MCL 722.23. The best-interest factors explicitly require inquiry into the home environment. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iv) is satisfied.

**Sub-condition (i): Ronald Berry's role as de facto household member.** While Ronald Berry is not technically a "party" in the legal sense, his domestic relationship with the named Defendant means that the practical effects of every custody, parenting time, and support order entered below directly implicate his daily life, his household, and his relationship. This further supports disqualification under the broader appearance-of-impropriety standard.

### 3. Standard of Review

The Michigan Supreme Court in *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975), established the controlling standard: "whether the conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception that the judge's ability to carry out judicial responsibilities with integrity, impartiality and competence is impaired." The *Cain* Court refined this as whether "a reasonable, fully informed observer would entertain significant doubt" about the judge's impartiality. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

The standard is objective. It does not require proof that Judge McNeill actually harbored bias. It requires only that the facts — a judge's husband's relative in a domestic relationship with a party, undisclosed for 290+ days, in a case featuring 52 one-sided ex parte orders — would cause a reasonable, fully informed observer to doubt the judge's impartiality.

No reasonable observer, knowing these facts, could conclude that these proceedings were conducted by an impartial tribunal.

### 4. The Pattern of Rulings Corroborates the Conflict

While the structural conflict alone mandates disqualification, the pattern of rulings is confirmatory:

- **52 ex parte orders**, 100% favoring the party connected to the judge's family;
- **44% ex parte rate** — extraordinary by any measure;
- **Five ex parte orders entered on a single day** (August 8, 2025) without notice to Father;
- **59 days of incarceration** for civil contempt without right-to-counsel protections;
- **571+ days of parenting time deprivation** without an evidentiary hearing on the merits;
- **Judicial interference** with the HealthWest independent evaluation after it returned findings favorable to Father (initial "ALL ZEROS" scores changed to "Rule Out" after judicial communication with the evaluator);
- **Systematic denial** of Father's motions while granting Mother's;
- **Child support of $1,056.60/month** imposed without an evidentiary hearing on income.

In *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573 (2001), this Court held that a *pattern of conduct* can establish bias sufficient for disqualification. Here, the pattern of conduct — viewed in the light of the now-discovered conflict — is not merely consistent with bias; it is explained by it.

## C. Non-Disclosure for 290+ Days Violates Canon 3(D) and Due Process

### 1. The Duty to Disclose

MCR 2.003(B) provides: "A judge shall disqualify himself or herself in any proceeding in which the judge cannot impartially hear a case." This provision imposes an **affirmative, self-executing duty** to identify and disclose potential grounds for disqualification.

Canon 3(D) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires:

> "A judge should disclose on the record information that the judge believes the parties or their lawyers might consider relevant to the question of disqualification, even if the judge believes there is no real basis for disqualification."

The duty runs from the judge to the litigant — not the reverse. A party should not have to investigate the judge's family tree to secure the right to a fair trial. *See* Canon 2(A) (judges must "act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary").

### 2. 290+ Days of Silence

Judge McNeill knew or should have known that her husband, Cavan John Berry, is related to Ronald Berry. She knew or should have known that Ronald Berry is in a domestic relationship with Defendant Emily A. Watson — a party appearing before her in a contested family law matter. She had a duty to disclose this connection on the record. She did not.

For more than 290 days, Judge McNeill:
- Conducted hearings without disclosing the connection;
- Entered orders — including ex parte orders — without disclosing;
- Made credibility determinations without disclosing;
- Set child support without disclosing;
- Adjudicated contempt proceedings resulting in Father's incarceration without disclosing;
- Entered a Final Judgment of Custody stripping Father of his parental rights without disclosing.

### 3. Non-Disclosure as an Independent Due Process Violation

The failure to disclose is not a neutral omission. It is an affirmative deprivation of due process. When a judge fails to disclose a known or knowable conflict, the litigant is denied the information necessary to exercise his right to seek disqualification under MCR 2.003(D). The litigant cannot challenge what he does not know. The judge's silence becomes a shield against accountability and a sword against the litigant's rights.

Canon 2(A) requires judges to "promote public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary." Canon 2(B) requires that "family, social, or other relationships" shall not "influence judicial conduct or judgment." A 290-day silence on a known or knowable familial connection to a party's domestic partner is irreconcilable with these commands.

The constitutional dimension is equally clear. Due process requires not merely an impartial judge, but one "whose impartiality is not reasonably questioned." *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 884. A judge who conceals a familial connection to a party for 290 days has not merely failed to satisfy this standard — she has affirmatively undermined it.

### 4. The Self-Represented Litigant's Disadvantage

Father appeared *in propria persona* throughout these proceedings. He did not have access to the investigative resources of a law firm. He was not in a position to conduct background research on the presiding judge's family relationships. He was incarcerated for 59 days, further limiting his ability to investigate.

The obligation to disclose conflicts of interest falls on the judge precisely because litigants — and especially self-represented litigants — should not bear the burden of policing judicial impartiality. That Father eventually discovered the conflict through independent research does not excuse the 290-day silence. It underscores the unfairness of a system in which the judge's duty to disclose is honored in the breach.

## D. All Prior Orders Are Voidable Ab Initio

### 1. The Principle

When a judge was disqualified at the time an order was entered, that order is **voidable ab initio** — that is, it may be set aside as having been entered by a tribunal that lacked the authority of impartiality required by due process.

The United States Supreme Court addressed this principle in *Aetna Life Ins. Co. v. Lavoie*, 475 U.S. 813, 825–27 (1986), vacating a judgment entered by a court where one justice had a conflict of interest, even though the other justices were not disqualified. The Court held that the participation of a conflicted judicial officer taints the entire proceeding.

In *Williams v. Pennsylvania*, 579 U.S. 1, 12 (2016), the Court reaffirmed that the remedy for judicial disqualification is vacatur and remand, because the structural defect "infect[s] the entire trial process."

The disqualification was not a one-time event; it existed from the beginning and persisted throughout. Accordingly, the remedy is not limited to a single order — it extends to every order entered by the disqualified judge.

### 3. The Void/Voidable Distinction

Father acknowledges the distinction between void and voidable orders. A judgment entered by a judge without subject-matter jurisdiction is void; a judgment entered by a disqualified judge is voidable. The practical consequence is that the voidable orders remain in effect until set aside by a court of competent jurisdiction — which is precisely the relief sought herein.

## E. Relief From Judgment Under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(b) — Newly Discovered Evidence

### 1. The Rule

MCR 2.612(C)(1)(b) provides that a court may relieve a party from a final judgment or order on the basis of:

> "Newly discovered evidence which by due diligence could not have been discovered in time to move for a new trial under MCR 2.611(B)."

### 2. The Berry-McNeill Conflict Is Newly Discovered Evidence

The Berry-McNeill familial conflict is quintessential "newly discovered evidence" within the meaning of MCR 2.612(C)(1)(b):

**(a) The evidence is newly discovered.** Appellant did not know, and had no reason to know, of the familial connection between Judge McNeill's husband (Cavan John Berry) and Defendant's domestic partner (Ronald Berry) until Appellant's independent investigation of publicly available records revealed the connection during the pendency of this appeal.

**(b) The evidence could not have been discovered through due diligence.** A self-represented litigant is not expected to investigate the presiding judge's family relationships. The duty to disclose conflicts rests on the judge, not the litigant. MCR 2.003(B); Canon 3(D). Judge McNeill's 290-day silence on the Berry family connection effectively concealed the evidence from Appellant. Due diligence does not require a litigant to overcome the judge's own concealment.

**(c) The evidence is not merely cumulative or impeaching.** The Berry-McNeill conflict does not merely impeach Judge McNeill's credibility or bolster a point already made. It reveals a structural defect in the tribunal itself — a condition that, had it been known, would have required disqualification before the first order was entered.

**(d) The evidence would probably change the outcome.** If Judge McNeill had been disqualified — as she should have been — the entire case would have been heard by a different judge. Every order, every ruling, every credibility determination would have been made by a different judicial officer. The evidence does not merely suggest that the outcome *might* have been different; it establishes that the *proceeding itself* would have been different in the most fundamental sense.

### 3. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(f) — Any Other Reason Justifying Relief

In the alternative, MCR 2.612(C)(1)(f) permits relief from judgment for "any other reason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment." An undisclosed judicial conflict of interest that pervades a 290-day proceeding, resulting in the deprivation of a father's fundamental right to the care and custody of his child, is precisely the kind of extraordinary circumstance that subsection (f) was designed to address.
