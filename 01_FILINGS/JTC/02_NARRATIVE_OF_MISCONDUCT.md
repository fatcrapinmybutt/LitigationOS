## CATEGORY A: EX PARTE CONTACTS AND COMMUNICATIONS

**150 Documented Violations**
**Applicable Standards: MCJC Canon 3(B)(8); MCR 3.703; MCR 2.107**

### Legal Standard

Canon 3(B)(8) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides: *"A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."*

MCR 3.703 establishes specific procedural requirements for ex parte PPOs, including evidentiary standards that must be met before entry without hearing. MCR 2.107 governs service and notice requirements, ensuring all parties receive adequate notice before judicial action.

### The 44% Ex Parte Rate — Statistical Impossibility

Judge McNeill entered substantive orders at an **ex parte rate of 44%** — meaning nearly half of all orders in these cases were entered without notice to or hearing from Complainant. To contextualize:

| Metric | Judge McNeill | Expected Baseline | Deviation |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Ex parte order rate | **44%** | < 10% | **4.4× baseline** |
| Expected ex parte orders (if baseline) | ~34 | < 10% of orders | — |
| Actual ex parte orders | **150** | — | **+116 excess** |

Ex parte relief is an extraordinary remedy reserved for genuine emergencies where irreparable harm would result from the delay necessary to provide notice. A sustained 44% rate across two related cases over multiple years is not attributable to emergency circumstances. It is facially indicative of systematic abuse of ex parte procedures.

### Specific Instances

#### A-1: Ex Parte PPO Entries Without Statutory Findings

Judge McNeill entered Personal Protection Orders without conducting the evidentiary analysis required by MCL 600.2950 and MCL 600.2950a. These orders were entered based solely on the petition of one party, without affording Complainant the opportunity to be heard, present evidence, or cross-examine. **See Exhibits A-1 through A-6.**

The 20 "mcneillexparte" scanned documents in the exhibit package document specific ex parte orders entered by Judge McNeill, each representing a substantive judicial action taken without Complainant's knowledge or participation.

#### A-2: Ex Parte Custody Modifications

Substantive modifications to custody arrangements were entered without hearing, without motion practice by either party, and without the best-interest analysis required by MCL 722.23. Custody — a fundamental liberty interest under *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000) — was modified through ex parte action, denying Complainant the procedural due process to which he is constitutionally entitled.

#### A-3: August 8, 2025 — The Catastrophic Ex Parte Suspension

On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill issued ex parte orders suspending **all parenting time** based on an emergency motion filed by Emily Watson. This order:

- Was entered without notice to Complainant
- Was based on an **unsworn motion** containing inadmissible hearsay
- Made no findings under MCL 722.23 (best-interest factors)
- Made no findings under MCL 600.2950 (PPO statutory standard)
- Resulted in the complete deprivation of Complainant's parental rights
- Was entered **after** the effective date of the September 1, 2025 Canon 3 amendment strengthening anti-bias protections

This single order exemplifies the pattern: a fundamental right was extinguished through an ex parte proceeding that did not meet the minimum evidentiary and procedural standards required by Michigan law.

#### A-4: Ex Parte Sanctions

Financial sanctions of $250 were imposed against Complainant without prior notice that sanctions were being considered and without opportunity to respond — a due process violation compounding the retaliatory nature of the sanctions.

#### A-5: Communications Outside Presence of Opposing Party

The record reflects instances where information from one party was received and acted upon outside the presence of the other party, including an email-to-secretary directive that resulted in orders based on one-sided information. **See Exhibit A-2 (docket entries showing orders without corresponding hearings).**

## CATEGORY B: BIAS AND PREJUDICE

**167 MCR 2.003 Disqualification Violations**
**Applicable Standards: MCJC Canon 2; Canon 3(B)(5); MCR 2.003(C)(1); *Cain v. Michigan Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996); *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009)**

### Legal Standard

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification where the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party. Canon 3(B)(5) prohibits a judge from manifesting bias or prejudice by words or conduct.

The test is objective: whether a reasonable person would have a well-grounded fear of receiving unfair treatment. *Cain v. Michigan Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996). Under *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), due process requires disqualification where there is a serious risk of actual bias.

### Specific Instances

#### B-1: Derogatory Language in Official Court Orders

Judge McNeill used the following language in official court orders — not in the heat of oral proceedings, but in written, deliberated judicial documents:

- **"Crazy"** — Used to characterize Complainant's legal positions. This language has no place in judicial writing and evidences personal animus rather than legal analysis. **See Exhibit B-1.**
- **"Liar"** — Used to label Complainant in official court orders. A judicial determination that testimony is not credible is substantively different from a personal attack labeling a party. The latter reflects prejudice, not adjudication. **See Exhibit B-2.**

These words violate Canon 3(B)(5) (bias manifested by words) and the 2025 amendment to Canon 3 (prohibition on intentional or reckless manifestation of bias).

#### B-2: One-Sided Application of Legal Standards

- **Selective evidentiary scrutiny:** Judge McNeill applied heightened scrutiny to Complainant's submissions while accepting opposing party's representations without similar scrutiny
- **Negative drug screen ignored:** Complainant submitted a negative drug screen — exculpatory evidence directly relevant to custody and fitness — which Judge McNeill disregarded without explanation. **See Exhibits B-5, B-6.**
- **Asymmetric motion practice:** Complainant's motions were denied without analysis or met with sanctions; opposing party's requests were granted routinely, often ex parte

#### B-3: Predetermined Outcomes

- **Checkbox orders without findings:** Judge McNeill issued custody orders in checkbox format without the factual findings or legal analysis required by MCR 2.517. The use of checkbox forms in complex custody matters — without any written findings — suggests outcomes predetermined rather than adjudicated. **See Exhibit B-8.**
- **12/12 best interest factors favor Complainant, yet ruled against:** A comprehensive analysis of the MCL 722.23 factors demonstrates that all 12 favor Complainant, yet Judge McNeill ruled against him without analyzing a single factor.
- **305 documented interference incidents ignored:** Defendant Emily Watson's family conducted 305 documented intimidation and interference incidents against Complainant's parenting time. This evidence, presented to the court, was disregarded without explanation. *See* MCL 722.23(j) (willingness to facilitate close relationship).

#### B-4: Refusal to Disqualify

Complainant filed a Motion to Disqualify Judge McNeill on or about March 4, 2025, citing documented bias and prejudice under MCR 2.003. The motion was denied despite the evidence of 167 disqualification-warranting violations documented herein. **See Exhibit B-7.**

#### B-5: Watson Employment Connection

Emily Watson's employment at the **Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division** creates an appearance of impropriety under Canon 2(B) (relationship influence on judicial conduct). This connection warrants investigation by the Commission to determine whether any improper relationship or influence existed.

## CATEGORY C: DUE PROCESS DENIAL

**175+ Documented Violations**
**Applicable Standards: US Const Amend XIV; MI Const 1963 Art 1 §17; MCR 2.517; MCL 722.23; *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (2014); *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971)**

### Legal Standard

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and Article 1, §17 of the Michigan Constitution require that no person be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. A parent's right to the care and custody of a child is a **fundamental liberty interest** of the highest constitutional order. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (2014).

*Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971), holds that the state cannot condition access to judicial proceedings that affect fundamental rights on the ability to pay fees or costs.

### Legal Standard

The First Amendment and Article 1, §§3 and 13 of the Michigan Constitution protect the right of access to courts. Judicial retaliation against a party for exercising legal rights is misconduct *per se*. *BE&K Construction Co. v. NLRB*, 536 US 516 (2002).

The data reveals four distinct phases of escalating misconduct correlated with Complainant's exercise of legal rights:

**Phase 1 — Initial Proceedings:**
- Violations present but sporadic
- Primarily procedural in nature (MEDIUM severity)
- Ex parte orders begin appearing

**Phase 2 — Post-Motion Escalation:**
- Following Complainant's first substantive motions
- Sharp increase in CRITICAL and HIGH severity violations
- Ex parte order rate increases significantly
- Retaliatory sanctions appear ($250)

**Phase 3 — Sustained Misconduct:**
- Violations become routine across all categories
- Derogatory language appears in orders ("crazy," "liar")
- Custody deprivation without MCL 722.23 findings
- Negative drug screen ignored

**Phase 4 — Catastrophic Escalation (August 2025–Present):**
- August 8, 2025: Ex parte suspension of ALL parenting time
- 571+ days of denied contact continues
- Complainant's access to meaningful judicial review effectively foreclosed
- Pattern continues unabated

| Complainant Action | Judicial Response | Temporal Gap |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
