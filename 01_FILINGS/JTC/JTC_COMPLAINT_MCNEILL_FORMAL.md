## II. Chronological Narrative of Misconduct

### 2023: PPO Proceedings Commence

5. On or about December 3, 2023, a Personal Protection Order petition was filed and granted on the same day in Case No. 2023-5907-PP, without prior notice to Father and without an evidentiary hearing.

### 2024: Custody Proceedings and Trial

6. On May 14, 2024, Judge McNeill entered an Order for Custody, Parenting Time, and Child Support in Case No. 2024-001507-DC.

7. On July 16, 2024, Father filed six separate motions, all of which were denied by Judge McNeill in a single summary order without individual analysis or statement of reasons, in violation of MCR 2.517.

8. On July 17, 2024, a non-jury trial was held on custody, parenting time, and child support. The court found that "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child." Following trial, Father exercised equal (50/50) parenting time for 438 consecutive days.

9. On September 25, 2024, Father filed a Motion to Disqualify Judge McNeill under MCR 2.003. Judge McNeill denied the motion herself, writing: "DENIED. No bias shown." Under MCR 2.003(D)(1), when a judge does not voluntarily disqualify, the motion *shall* be referred to the chief judge for determination. No referral was made. This denied Father the independent review the rule was designed to ensure.

### 2025: Escalation

#### February–March 2025

10. On February 28, 2025, Father's court-appointed attorney, Mandi Martini, emailed Father before a hearing: "I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked. I'm sure you do not want to spend the next week in jail on a contempt charge for speaking out in court when you weren't asked." This communication demonstrates that the appearance of bias was so entrenched that an officer of the court anticipated a predetermined outcome and effectively warned Father that speaking in his own defense would result in incarceration.

11. On February 28, 2025, Judge McNeill entered an order simultaneously imposing 14 days jail for contempt AND dismissing the underlying matter — dispositions that are mutually exclusive, as dismissal extinguishes the court's enforcement authority.

12. On March 3, 2025, at 8:51 AM, Pamela Rusco (Judge McNeill's judicial secretary) emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker: "Good Morning Jamie, Andrew Pigors was ordered to turn himself in to the Jail by 5pm on Friday to serve his 14 days in jail for the PPO Violation. I confirmed with the jail that he is not currently lodged. Can you please issue a warrant for him? Attached are both the order after hearing and the jail mittimus." This email constitutes an ex parte communication: chambers initiated a prosecutorial action — a warrant request — with the prosecution outside the presence of the parties. Additionally, Rusco independently investigated Father's incarceration status by calling the jail — a substantive investigative act, not a ministerial function.

13. On March 28, 2025, a Friend of the Court recommendation was issued.

#### August 2025: The Catastrophic Ex Parte Suspension

14. On August 5, 2025, Mother submitted a USB recording to the clerk's office. Judge McNeill later stated on the record: "Yes, I had my staff listen to it." The judge received and considered substantive evidence — an audio recording — outside the presence of the parties, in direct violation of Canon 3(A)(4). The recording was made by Lori Watson (Mother's mother) without Father's consent, potentially a felony under MCL 750.539c. No authentication under MRE 901 was established.

15. On August 7, 2025, Albert Watson (Mother's father) told North Shores Police Department Officer Ella Randall (Badge #47437, Case NS2505044) that "they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son." This statement, memorialized in a police report, demonstrates advance knowledge of the following day's ex parte orders — knowledge inconsistent with a spontaneous judicial response.

16. On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five separate ex parte orders in a single day in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, all without prior notice to Father, all suspending his parenting time, and all favoring Mother. Father received no advance notice of any filing, motion, or request that precipitated these orders. This date represents the complete termination of Father's contact with L.D.W.

17. On September 5, 2025, Father attended a HealthWest mental health evaluation as directed by the court. The evaluation was favorable to Father. Rather than directing the evaluation to be filed with the Clerk of Court per MCR 8.119, Judge McNeill directed Father to email the evaluation results to her secretary, Pamela Rusco. This routing ensured the favorable evaluation bypassed the public record and was never accessible to the opposing party through normal channels. Rusco subsequently stated: "I do not accept filings via e-mail."

18. Prior to the September 11, 2025 HealthWest assessment, Judge McNeill provided a letter to HealthWest describing Father's behaviors as "several incidents over the past two years of court proceedings which are suggestive of a mental health disorder." This letter was sent without Father's knowledge or consent, constituting an ex parte communication with a prospective evaluator that colored the evaluation before it began.

19. On September 25, 2025, administrative orders were filed referencing MCR 8.110(C) regarding fees and record integrity, including an amended ex parte administrative petition.

#### October–November 2025: Continued Suppression

20. On October 20–November 15, 2025, Judge McNeill granted Mother permission to take L.D.W. out of the country during a period when Father's parenting time had been suspended and no contact of any kind was permitted with his child.

21. On October 29, 2025, Complainant filed a JTC complaint package (Exhibits Binder v5) with the Commission.

22. A HealthWest clinical chart memo documents that "Pamela Rusco, Judicial Secretary for the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill . . . called to request if this clinician could testify . . . will email us the subpoenas." Subpoenas for clinical witness testimony were arranged via telephone and email between chambers staff and the clinical provider — not through formal court filings served on the parties as required by MCR 2.506. Neither party received notice of this coordination.

23. During the Show Cause #5 hearing on November 15, 2025, Father appeared remotely and was muted three or more times while attempting to present argument and objections. Court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini sat silent, offering no advocacy, objections, or defense. Father was prevented from presenting a police report concerning L.D.W.'s condition. Judge McNeill stated "quit nitpicking" when Father raised that police had misattributed a statement. Muting a party during a hearing is the functional equivalent of removing that party from the courtroom.

24. On November 26, 2025, Judge McNeill imposed 45 additional days of jail for sending birthday and holiday messages to L.D.W. through AppClose, the court-sanctioned co-parenting application. Combined with prior sentences, Father has been incarcerated for a cumulative 59 days in these proceedings.

25. On November 29, 2025, Pamela Rusco transmitted emails regarding PPO and jail status — substantive case matters communicated outside the presence of the parties and not filed on the record.

### Ongoing Pattern

She further characterized Father's motions as "harassment to Defendant and to the Court, along with an abuse of the Court system." No vexatious-litigant finding was made under MCL 600.2591. This constitutes a blanket refusal to perform the judicial function.

27. Judge McNeill rejected the favorable HealthWest evaluation (document H0002, dated September 5, 2025) and characterized it as a "ghost" mental health evaluation — a prejudicial characterization without evidentiary basis.

28. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "I have represented Party A in the past." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge has been counsel or adviser to a party. Despite this admission, Judge McNeill did not recuse herself and continued to preside.

29. Father is a pro se litigant of limited means. *See Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971).

30. Asymmetric enforcement is documented throughout: zero enforcement actions against Mother for documented parenting-time violations; maximum punishment against Father for messages sent through a court-sanctioned application.

# PART 5: CANON VIOLATION ANALYSIS

## I. Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

**Canon 1 provides:** *"An independent and honorable judiciary is indispensable to justice in our society. A judge should participate in establishing, maintaining, and enforcing, and should personally observe, high standards of conduct so that the integrity and independence of the judiciary may be preserved."*

### Violation 1.1: Undisclosed Conflicts of Interest

Judge McNeill was previously a partner at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill. Chief Judge Timothy Hicks and Judge Maria Ladas Hoopes are also former partners of the same firm. Three judges on the same circuit bench originating from the same law firm creates, at minimum, an appearance that the bench operates as a unified entity rather than as independent judicial officers. When Father's MCR 2.003 disqualification motion was denied without referral to the chief judge, the motion should have gone to a judge from the same firm — a structural conflict.

**Applicable Standard:** Canon 1; MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)–(b); *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009) (due process requires recusal where probability of actual bias is too high).

### Violation 1.2: Prior Representation Without Recusal

Judge McNeill admitted on the record: "I have represented Party A in the past." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge has served as counsel or adviser to a party. This admission alone required recusal under the court rules.

**Supporting Evidence:** Hearing transcript (see Exhibit B-6).

### Violation 1.3: Berry Family Connection

Ronald Berry, Mother's boyfriend/domestic partner, has been present throughout the proceedings. Any undisclosed connection between the Berry family and Judge McNeill or her former law firm would constitute an additional conflict requiring disclosure and potential recusal. The Commission is respectfully requested to investigate whether any such connection exists.

**Note:** This allegation is presented as a request for investigation, not as a proven fact. The Commission has subpoena power to obtain relevant records.

---

## II. Canon 2 — Avoidance of Impropriety and Appearance of Impropriety

**Canon 2 provides:** *"A judge shall avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities."*

### Violation 2.1: Extraordinary Ex Parte Order Rate

Judge McNeill entered substantive orders in the referenced cases at an ex parte rate of approximately 44%, compared to an expected baseline of less than 10–12% for family division proceedings. This rate is approximately 3.65 times the expected baseline. Ex parte relief is an extraordinary remedy reserved for genuine emergencies; a sustained 44% rate across two related cases is inconsistent with legitimate emergency practice.

**Supporting Evidence:** Docket sheet analysis (Exhibit A-2); statistical compilation (Exhibit A-4 series).

### Violation 2.2: Same-Day Ex Parte Orders on Critical Matters

On August 8, 2025, five ex parte orders were entered in a single day, suspending all parenting time — a fundamental constitutional right — without notice and without hearing. On December 3, 2023, a PPO was filed and granted on the same day. These same-day dispositions on matters of constitutional dimension create a severe appearance of impropriety.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibits A-1, A-3, A-4, A-5.

### Violation 2.3: Off-Record Evidence Handling

Judge McNeill directed Father to route a favorable mental health evaluation through her secretary rather than filing it with the Clerk of Court as required by MCR 8.119. When Father attempted to comply, the secretary stated: "I do not accept filings via e-mail." This routing ensured the favorable evaluation never became part of the public record. The judge also had her staff review a USB recording submitted by Mother outside the presence of the parties.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibits D-2, E-1, E-2.

### Violation 2.4: Chambers-Prosecution Coordination

Judge McNeill's judicial secretary, Pamela Rusco, initiated a warrant request directly to Prosecutor Jamie Hooker by email, attaching court orders as enforcement instruments directed to one side only. This coordination between chambers and the prosecution, outside the presence of the defense, creates a severe appearance of impropriety. *See Ethics Opinion JI-134* (judge responsible for improper ex parte contacts by staff).

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibit A-6, D-1.

## III. Canon 3 — Impartial Performance of Judicial Duties

**Canon 3(A)(4) provides:** *"A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."*

**Canon 3(A)(3) provides:** *"A judge shall be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants."*

**Canon 3(A)(7) provides:** *"A judge shall accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law."*

### Violation 3.1: Muting Father During Hearings

During the Show Cause #5 hearing (November 15, 2025), Father was muted three or more times while attempting to present argument and evidence. Father was prevented from presenting a police report regarding L.D.W.'s condition. Muting a party during a hearing is functionally equivalent to removing that party from the courtroom and violates Canon 3(A)(7).

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibit B-2.

### Violation 3.2: Hostile On-Record Statements

Judge McNeill stated "quit nitpicking" when Father raised a legitimate evidentiary concern (police misattribution of a statement). She characterized the favorable HealthWest evaluation as a "ghost" mental health evaluation. She told Father: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." These statements demonstrate hostility toward a party-litigant, not the patient and dignified demeanor required by Canon 3(A)(3).

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibits B-4, B-5, C-6.

### Violation 3.3: Rejection of Favorable Court-Ordered Evaluation

Father obtained a favorable HealthWest evaluation (document H0002, September 5, 2025) as directed by the court. Judge McNeill rejected this evaluation and directed a second evaluation — but sent a letter to the second evaluator characterizing Father as exhibiting behaviors "suggestive of a mental health disorder," thereby biasing the replacement evaluation before it began.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibits A-8, C-6.

### Violation 3.4: Disproportionate Incarceration

Father was incarcerated for a cumulative 59 days — 14 days following the November 15, 2025 hearing and 45 days following the November 26, 2025 hearing. The 45-day sentence was imposed for sending birthday and holiday messages to L.D.W. through AppClose, the court-sanctioned co-parenting application. Meanwhile, documented violations by Mother resulted in zero enforcement actions — no show-cause orders, no contempt findings, no sanctions.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibits C-1, C-2.
**Applicable Standard:** *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987) (contempt power must be exercised with restraint).

### Violation 3.5: Asymmetric Grant/Denial Pattern

Analysis of Judge McNeill's rulings demonstrates a grant ratio of approximately 1.9:1 favoring Mother and a denial ratio of approximately 1.7:1 disfavoring Father. This asymmetry, combined with zero enforcement of Mother's documented violations and maximum enforcement against Father, demonstrates outcome-determinative bias.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibits B-3, B-4; docket analysis.

### Violation 3.6: International Travel Permission During Contact Suspension

While Father had zero contact with L.D.W. — no visits, no phone calls, no communication of any kind — Judge McNeill granted Mother permission to take L.D.W. out of the country for October 20–November 15, 2025. The asymmetry is stark: Father is denied all contact while Mother is granted international travel authority.

**Supporting Evidence:** Exhibit B-8.

## IV. Procedural Rule Violations

### MCR 2.003 — Disqualification

Father's September 25, 2024 disqualification motion was denied by Judge McNeill herself without referral to the chief judge as mandated by MCR 2.003(D)(1). The rule uses the word "shall" — referral is not discretionary.

### MCR 3.210 — Custody Proceedings

Custody modifications were entered without the notice and hearing procedures required by MCR 3.210. The August 8, 2025 orders modified custody without notice.

### MCR 8.119 — Record Requirements

Evidence was routed through the judge's secretary rather than the Clerk of Court, ensuring it was never filed on the public record as required by MCR 8.119.

### MCR 2.506 — Subpoenas

Subpoenas for clinical witness testimony were arranged through chambers staff by telephone and email rather than through formal court filings served on the parties, violating MCR 2.506.

### MCR 2.107 — Service and Notice

Orders were entered before the expiration of the response period required by MCR 2.107, denying Father the opportunity to respond.
