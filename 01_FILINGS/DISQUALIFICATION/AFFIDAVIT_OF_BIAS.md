### II. SPECIFIC INCIDENTS OF BIAS

#### A. Ex Parte Order — PPO Issuance Without Full Hearing (November 1, 2023)

4. On or about November 1, 2023, the Court issued a Personal Protection Order (domestic) against me in Case No. 2023-5907-PP without conducting a full evidentiary hearing.

5. I was not afforded the opportunity to present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, or challenge the allegations before the order issued. The PPO was based on Defendant's petition, which I believe contained false and misleading statements about my conduct and parenting fitness.

6. Specifically, Defendant's allegations included claims that I was "being homeless and couch surfing," "monitoring her with a webcam," and "throwing her off of a table" — allegations that are contradicted by contemporaneous evidence in my possession.

#### B. PPO Modification Hearing — Inadequate Consideration (December 4, 2023)

7. On December 4, 2023, a hearing was held on modification of the PPO before Judge McNeill.

8. During this hearing, I attempted to present evidence contradicting Defendant's allegations, but the Court's consideration of my evidence appeared cursory compared to the weight given to Defendant's claims.

9. The resulting order continued restrictions on me without adequate findings addressing my evidence.

#### C. PPO Violation Show Cause — Acceptance of Uncorroborated Allegations (January 7, 2024)

10. On January 7, 2024, a Show Cause hearing was held regarding alleged PPO violations under MCR 3.708.

11. The Court credited Defendant's allegations regarding violations without requiring corroborating evidence, while discounting my explanations and documentary evidence.

#### D. Consolidated Hearings — Insufficient Separation (April 11, 2024)

12. On April 11, 2024, Judge McNeill conducted proceedings simultaneously addressing: (a) an ex parte order amending the PPO; (b) an Order After Hearing on Violation of PPO; and (c) related custody matters.

13. The consolidation of these distinct proceedings, each requiring different legal standards, prejudiced my ability to address each issue with the specificity required.

14. An ex parte order was entered at this hearing amending the PPO without prior notice to me of the specific modifications sought.

#### E. Ex Parte Emergency Order — Total Suspension of Parenting Time (August 2025)

15. On or about August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill signed an ex parte "emergency" order suspending **all** of my parenting time with my three-year-old son, Lincoln.

16. This order was entered without:
   - Advance notice to me of the emergency motion;
   - An opportunity for me to respond before the order issued;
   - Specific factual findings of immediate and irreparable harm as required by MCR 3.207(B);
   - A prompt post-deprivation hearing.

17. The effect of this order was devastating. It completely stopped all parenting time, including:
   - Regular weekly/biweekly parenting time;
   - Scheduled Labor Day 2025 holiday parenting time;
   - My son's 3rd birthday celebration;
   - All special-event and holiday time through at least November 2025.

18. I timely filed a written objection and motion to modify or rescind the ex parte order and requested a hearing, as instructed by the order itself. The order stated that the "ex parte order will automatically become a temporary order if you do not file a written objection or motion to modify or rescind the ex parte order and a request for a hearing. Even if an objection" is filed, additional procedures must follow. Despite my timely compliance, the order was not promptly addressed.

19. The ex parte order remained in effect for approximately **four months** (August through November 2025) before any meaningful evidentiary hearing was held. During this period, I had zero contact with my son.

#### F. Off-Record Evidence Handling

20. During these proceedings, a USB recording was submitted to the Court and handled off-record, without my knowledge or opportunity to challenge its contents, authenticity, or admissibility.

21. A mental-health evaluation was ordered, conducted, and received by the Court without adequate transparency. The Court informed the assessor of "ALL the information that was presented to the Court at the hearing, including the incident at the library, alleged following by the same individuals, eviction from his housing, his Mother's" involvement — yet I was not provided with the same information packet or an opportunity to supplement it with my perspective.

22. This off-record handling of evidence that materially affected the Court's decisions about my parental rights violated my right to confrontation and meaningful participation.

#### G. Denial of Make-Up Parenting Time

23. Despite multiple requests, the Court failed to order or enforce make-up parenting time when Defendant refused to facilitate court-ordered parenting exchanges.

24. On approximately April 15, 2025, Defendant explicitly refused to facilitate make-up parenting time. The Court took no enforcement action despite MCL 552.511b, which requires enforcement of missed parenting time.

25. Defendant's conduct demonstrated "willful obstruction of court-ordered parenting time, undermining" the parenting time order, yet the Court's response was consistently inadequate.

#### H. Incarceration Without Proper Contempt Process

26. Judge McNeill found me in contempt and ordered me jailed for a total of **59 days** without affording me the procedural protections required for contempt proceedings resulting in incarceration. Specifically:

   a. I was not provided adequate notice of the specific charges constituting contempt;
   b. I was denied my right to counsel under *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480; 460 NW2d 493 (1990), despite facing incarceration;
   c. I was not afforded a meaningful opportunity to present a defense or purge the contempt;
   d. The findings did not meet the evidentiary standards required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987).

27. As a direct result of this incarceration, I lost three (3) jobs, compounding the financial harm and undermining my ability to meet the child support obligation of $1,056.60 per month that the Court set without an evidentiary hearing on my actual income.

#### I. Child Support Set Without Evidentiary Hearing

28. The Court set child support at **$1,056.60 per month** without conducting an evidentiary hearing to determine my actual income, expenses, or ability to pay. This amount was imposed and then used as a basis for further contempt proceedings when I could not pay — creating a cycle of incarceration that the Court itself set in motion.

#### J. Custody Evaluator Ordered Then Reversed

29. The Court ordered a custody evaluation, raising my reasonable expectation that an independent professional assessment would inform the custody determination. The Court then reversed course and set aside the evaluation without explanation, demonstrating outcome-driven adjudication rather than neutral fact-finding.

#### K. The Court's Own Acknowledgments

26. The Court itself noted that it "offered supervised parenting time to the Father and he categorically rejected that. The Court will consider a motion for supervised parenting time once" certain conditions were met. However, this framing obscured the fact that supervised parenting time was offered only after total suspension — not as an initial measured response — and placed the burden on me to petition for partial restoration of rights that should never have been fully revoked without proper findings.

27. The Court acknowledged ordering assessments and receiving results, yet allowed the total parenting-time suspension to persist throughout the assessment period, contrary to the principle that restrictions on fundamental rights must be narrowly tailored.

### IV. DIFFERENTIAL TREATMENT EVIDENCE

29. Throughout these proceedings, I have observed consistent differential treatment:

   a. **Defendant's ex parte motions**: Consistently granted, often without notice to me and without requiring legal prerequisites (sworn facts, findings of immediate harm). Defendant Emily Watson "filed the Emergency Ex Parte Motion to Suspend Parenting Time" and the Court signed it.

   b. **Plaintiff's motions**: Denied or not ruled upon, including motions for enforcement of parenting time, make-up parenting time, modification or rescission of ex parte orders, and reconsideration of adverse rulings.

   c. **Defendant's credibility**: Accepted without cross-examination or corroboration, even when contradicted by documentary evidence. Emily Watson filed **32 objections** against the custody arrangements, making false claims about my living conditions, finances, and parenting ability.

   d. **Plaintiff's credibility**: Questioned despite documentary support, with adverse inferences drawn from ex parte materials I had no opportunity to rebut.

30. Emily "falsely accused Andrew of assaulting her during an argument" (October 15, 2023), among other false allegations that were credited by the Court without independent verification.

### V. IMPACT STATEMENTS

37. Judge McNeill's conduct has had the following impacts:

   a. **Parent-child separation**: I was completely separated from my three-year-old son Lincoln for approximately four months (August–November 2025) based on an ex parte order entered without proper findings. In total, I have been denied parenting time for **569+ cumulative days** through a combination of ex parte suspensions, Defendant's willful obstruction, and the Court's refusal to enforce its own orders.

   b. **91% parental alienation score**: Forensic analysis documents that the Court's systematic removal of me from my child's life has produced a parental alienation score of **91%**, reflecting near-total destruction of the father-child relationship.

   c. **Missed developmental milestones**: I missed my son's 3rd birthday, Labor Day 2025, and numerous developmental milestones during the separation.

   d. **59 days of incarceration**: I was jailed for 59 days on contempt findings without proper process, without the right to counsel under *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480; 460 NW2d 493 (1990), and without the procedural safeguards required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987).

   e. **Three jobs lost**: As a direct result of my incarceration, I lost three (3) jobs, destroying my ability to maintain employment and meet the very financial obligations the Court imposed.

   f. **Financial burden**: The Court set child support at $1,056.60 per month without an evidentiary hearing on my income. Filing fees and costs of responding to repeated ex parte filings, combined with the inability to maintain employment due to incarceration, created severe financial hardship.

   g. **Emotional harm**: The uncertainty, lack of contact with my son, and perception that the Court is not providing fair process has caused significant emotional distress to both me and my child.

   h. **Erosion of parent-child bond**: Extended separation from a child between ages 2–3 threatens the long-term parent-child bond that Michigan law (MCL 722.23, Factor J) and constitutional law protect.

   i. **Access to justice denied**: As the evidence quotes reflect, "the ex parte order or [failure] to restore parenting time . . . when you combine that financial barrier with the ex parte shutdown, it leaves my son without his father and leaves me with no realistic written [remedy]."
