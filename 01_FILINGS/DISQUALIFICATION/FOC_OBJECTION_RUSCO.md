    NOW COMES Plaintiff, ANDREW J. PIGORS, appearing in propria

as Friend of the Court officer in the above-captioned matter, and

14th Amendment to the United States Constitution. In support of this
Objection and Motion, Plaintiff states as follows:


I. INTRODUCTION

    1. The Friend of the Court is required by statute and court rule
to serve as a neutral, independent officer of the court. MCL 552.503;
MCR 3.208. The FOC's function is to investigate, recommend, and
enforce domestic relations orders in an impartial manner, free from
the influence of the presiding judge or either party.

    2. Pamela Rusco cannot fulfill this independent role because she
simultaneously serves as Judge McNeill's personal secretary and
administrative staff. This dual role creates an inherent, structural
conflict of interest that renders any recommendation, investigation,
or enforcement action she undertakes in this case fundamentally
unreliable.

    3. Beyond the structural conflict, specific conduct by Rusco
demonstrates that she has actively used her gatekeeping position to
disadvantage Plaintiff and advantage Defendant, including blocking
Plaintiff's access to the clerk's office while facilitating
Defendant's ex parte submissions to the judge.


II. LEGAL STANDARD

A. MCR 3.218 — Objection to FOC Recommendation

    4. MCR 3.218(1) provides that a party may object to any
recommendation of the friend of the court by filing a written
objection.

    5. MCR 3.218(4) provides that upon filing of an objection, the
court shall hold a de novo hearing. The de novo hearing requirement
reflects the Legislature's recognition that FOC recommendations are
not final determinations — they are subject to independent judicial
review when challenged.

    6. The right to object under MCR 3.218 necessarily encompasses
the right to challenge the qualifications and impartiality of the FOC
officer making the recommendation. A recommendation from a biased
source cannot form the basis for a just determination.

B. Due Process — Impartial Decision-Maker

    7. The 14th Amendment to the United States Constitution guarantees
that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without
due process of law. A parent's right to the care, custody, and
companionship of his child is a fundamental liberty interest. Troxel v
Granville, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

    8. Due process requires, at minimum, that decisions affecting
fundamental rights be made by impartial decision-makers. Withrow v
Larkin, 421 US 35, 46 (1975). This includes officers who make
recommendations directly influencing judicial determinations.

    9. An FOC officer who is simultaneously employed as the presiding
judge's personal secretary is not an impartial decision-maker. She
is structurally beholden to the judge whose decisions she is supposed
to independently inform.

C. MCL 552.507 — FOC Independence

    10. MCL 552.507 establishes the Friend of the Court as an arm of
the court but requires that its functions be carried out independently.
The legislative design contemplates an office that operates at arm's
length from the judge, providing independent investigation and
recommendation — not an extension of the judge's personal staff.


III. FACTUAL BASIS

A. Dual Role: Secretary and FOC

    11. Pamela Rusco serves as Judge McNeill's secretary and personal
administrative staff in the 14th Circuit Court, Family Division.

    12. Pamela Rusco simultaneously acts in a Friend of the Court
capacity in this case, making recommendations, facilitating
communications, and participating in enforcement actions.

    13. These dual roles are irreconcilable. As judge's secretary,
Rusco's professional obligation is to serve the judge's needs and
directives. As FOC officer, her obligation is to serve as an
independent, neutral officer of the court. She cannot serve two
masters — particularly when one master is the judge whose decisions
she is supposed to independently evaluate and inform.

B. Blocking Plaintiff's Access to the Clerk

    14. Pamela Rusco explicitly told Plaintiff that he was NOT ALLOWED
to submit evidence or filings to the clerk's office.

    15. This directive was not based on any court rule, local
administrative order, or legitimate procedural requirement. There is
no provision in the Michigan Court Rules that authorizes a judge's
secretary to block a party from filing documents with the clerk.

    16. The clerk's office is the constitutionally and procedurally
designated point of access for all parties to file documents with the
court. MCR 8.119 governs the filing of documents and vests authority
in the clerk — not in the judge's personal staff.

    17. Rusco's directive effectively denied Plaintiff access to the
court system itself — a denial of the most basic due process right:
the right to be heard.

C. Facilitating Defendant's Ex Parte Submissions

    18. While blocking Plaintiff's access, Rusco facilitated — or at
minimum failed to prevent — Defendant's direct ex parte submissions
to Judge McNeill, including but not limited to the USB drive submitted
on or about August 8, 2025.

    19. This USB drive was not filed through the clerk's office, was
not entered on the docket, and was not provided to Plaintiff before
the Court acted on its contents to suspend all parenting time.

    20. The asymmetry is stark and unconstitutional:

        — Plaintiff: Told by the judge's secretary that he CANNOT
          file evidence through the clerk.

        — Defendant: Permitted to submit evidence directly to the
          judge, bypassing the clerk entirely.

    21. This asymmetric access, facilitated by Rusco's dual role,
constitutes a violation of equal protection under the 14th Amendment
and a fundamental breach of procedural due process.

D. Communications with Prior Attorney

    22. Pamela Rusco communicated via email with Mandi Martini,
Plaintiff's prior attorney, regarding matters in this case.

    23. The content and extent of these communications are at issue.
Plaintiff does not have full access to the communications between
Rusco and Martini and is unable to assess whether they included ex
parte discussions of case substance, judicial preferences, or
strategic guidance that prejudiced Plaintiff's position.

    24. These communications are relevant to both the disqualification
of Rusco as FOC and the broader pattern of ex parte conduct in this
case. Their preservation and production is essential.

E. Pattern of Conduct Consistent with Judge's Interests

    25. Every action taken by Rusco in her dual capacity has been
consistent with Judge McNeill's apparent preference for Defendant's
position:

        (a) Blocking Plaintiff's filings → preventing Plaintiff
            from building his record;
        (b) Facilitating Defendant's ex parte submissions →
            enabling the judge to act on one-sided information;
        (c) Communicating with Plaintiff's attorney → potential
            channel for judicial influence outside the record.

    26. This pattern is not coincidental. It is the natural and
predictable result of allowing the judge's personal secretary to act
as the neutral FOC officer. The structural conflict produces the
biased outcome.


IV. PREJUDICE TO PLAINTIFF

    27. Plaintiff has been concretely prejudiced by Rusco's conduct:

        (a) Plaintiff was unable to file evidence with the clerk
            that would have contested Defendant's false allegations;

        (b) Defendant's ex parte submissions — facilitated outside
            normal filing channels — resulted in the suspension of
            Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights;

        (c) Plaintiff has been denied parenting time with L.D.W.
            for an extended period, and the FOC's involvement in
            this deprivation is tainted by Rusco's structural
            conflict;

        (d) Any FOC recommendation issued by Rusco cannot be
            relied upon as independent or impartial, calling into
            question the foundation of the Court's prior orders
            that relied on such recommendations.

    5. Order disclosure to Plaintiff of all communications between

    6. Pending appointment of an independent FOC officer, stay all

    7. Grant such other and further relief as the Court deems just
and equitable.
