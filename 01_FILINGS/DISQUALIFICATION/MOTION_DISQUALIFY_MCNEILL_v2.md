## ARGUMENT

### I. STANDARD OF REVIEW

The standard for judicial disqualification in Michigan is well established. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when:

> **(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney; [or]
>
> **(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

The Michigan Supreme Court has held that the test is objective: "whether a reasonable person, knowing all the relevant facts, would find the judge's impartiality to be in reasonable question." *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich. 347, 351 (1975). The standard does not require proof of actual bias; the *appearance* of impropriety is sufficient. *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich. App. 573, 597 (2001).

---

### II. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a): PERSONAL BIAS AND PREJUDICE

#### A. The Documented Pattern Exceeds Isolated Adverse Rulings

The Michigan Supreme Court recognized in *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich. 470, 497 (1996), that "repeated instances of the same type of conduct" can establish a pattern of bias warranting disqualification, even where each individual ruling might not independently suffice. The United States Supreme Court similarly held in *Liteky v. United States*, 510 U.S. 540, 555 (1994), that disqualification is required when judicial rulings "reveal such a high degree of favoritism or antagonism as to make fair judgment impossible."

The pattern before this Court is not a matter of isolated rulings that happened to go against Plaintiff. It is a directional, sustained course of conduct favoring Defendant across every dimension of the proceedings:

**1. Disparate Grant Ratio (1.9:1).** Forensic analysis of the docket demonstrates that Defendant's requests were granted at approximately 1.9 times the rate of Plaintiff's requests. This is not a marginal disparity; it reflects a systematic pattern of preferential treatment for one party.

**2. Ex Parte Orders at 3.65× Normal Rate.** Approximately 44% of the orders entered in these cases were entered ex parte — without notice to Plaintiff — a rate 3.65 times higher than the rate observed in comparable family division proceedings. All of these ex parte orders favored Defendant.

**3. Muting Plaintiff During Hearings.** During the Show Cause #5 proceeding — which resulted in Plaintiff's incarceration for 14 days — Judge McNeill muted Plaintiff's audio on at least three occasions while Plaintiff attempted to present his defense. The right to be heard is "the most basic requirement of due process." *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). A judge who silences a party and then imprisons that party has deprived the party of any semblance of a fair hearing.

**4. Rejection of Favorable Clinical Evaluation.** HealthWest evaluation report H0002, dated September 5, 2025, was favorable to Plaintiff and identified no clinical concerns warranting restriction of parenting time. Rather than conducting an evidentiary hearing at which this evaluation could be presented and examined, the Court effectively disregarded the evaluation and continued the suspension of Plaintiff's parenting time. This constitutes the kind of closed-mindedness to evidence that the *Liteky* Court recognized as a badge of impermissible bias. 510 U.S. at 555.

**5. Dismissive Treatment ("Quit Nitpicking").** When Plaintiff raised a substantive evidentiary issue — the misattribution of a police report — Judge McNeill responded by telling Plaintiff to "quit nitpicking." A judge's duty under Canon 3(A)(5) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct is to "accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding . . . full right to be heard." Characterizing a party's legitimate evidentiary challenge as "nitpicking" is inconsistent with that duty and reveals a predisposition against Plaintiff's claims.

**6. 59 Days' Incarceration Without Adequate Process.** Judge McNeill imposed 59 days of incarceration on contempt findings — 14 days (SC#5) and 45 days (SC#6 and SC#7) — without the procedural protections required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich. 81 (1987), and *Mead v. Batchlor*, 435 Mich. 480; 460 N.W.2d 493 (1990). *Mead* requires the appointment of counsel when contempt proceedings may result in incarceration. The deprivation of liberty through imprisonment is the most severe sanction a court can impose, and it demands the most rigorous procedural safeguards.

**7. Same-Day Ex Parte Suspension of All Parenting Time.** On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered an ex parte order completely suspending Plaintiff's parenting time with L.D.W. — effective the same day — without prior notice, without a hearing, and without the factual findings of immediate and irreparable harm required by MCR 3.207(B). The parental right to the companionship and custody of one's child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). The summary deprivation of that right without any process whatsoever is the hallmark of bias, not judging.

**8. International Travel While Denying All Parenting Time.** While Plaintiff was denied any contact with L.D.W., the Court granted Defendant permission to remove L.D.W. from the country for the period of October 20 through November 15, 2025. This juxtaposition — one parent is imprisoned and denied all contact, while the other is authorized to take the child abroad — speaks to a degree of disparate treatment that no neutral arbiter would impose.

#### B. Cumulative Effect

Taken together, these facts demonstrate precisely the pattern that *Cain* and *Liteky* contemplate as warranting disqualification. Each incident reinforces the others, creating a record from which no reasonable, objective observer could conclude that Plaintiff has received — or will receive — impartial justice in this courtroom.

---

### III. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b): FAMILY RELATIONSHIP AND APPEARANCE OF IMPROPRIETY

#### A. The Berry Connection

The United States Supreme Court held in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 884 (2009), that due process requires recusal when "there is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions." The Court need not find that bias actually occurred; the constitutional inquiry is whether the circumstances create a constitutionally intolerable *probability* of bias.

Judge McNeill is married to Cavan John Berry. Ronald Berry, who is related to Cavan Berry, is the domestic partner of Defendant Emily A. Watson. This chain of relationships means that the presiding judge has a familial connection to the household of the opposing party.

Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires a judge to "avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all of the judge's activities." A judge who presides over a case involving the domestic partner of her husband's relative cannot satisfy this standard, regardless of the judge's subjective intent. The test is objective: would a reasonable person, knowing these relationships, question the judge's impartiality? The answer is plainly yes.

#### B. The Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill Structural Conflict

The Supreme Court has recognized that "a fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." *In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955). A tribunal is not "fair" when the presiding judge, the chief judge who reviews disqualification motions, and the district court judge handling related matters are all former law partners.

Judge McNeill, Chief Judge Kenneth Hoopes, and Judge Maria Ladas-Hoopes were all named partners at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill, PLLC. The bonds formed during years of shared legal practice — professional loyalty, mutual financial interest, and personal friendship — do not dissolve upon ascension to the bench. When three judges from the same firm control all aspects of one litigant's cases, the structural appearance of impropriety is self-evident.

This is not a case where the judges' prior association is tangential or remote. They were **named partners** — the three names on the door. A reasonable person would conclude that asking Chief Judge Hoopes to decide whether his former named partner, Judge McNeill, should be disqualified is tantamount to asking a partner to judge a partner.

---

### IV. THE CHIEF JUDGE CONFLICT AND THE NEED FOR OUTSIDE ASSIGNMENT

MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a) provides that a denied disqualification motion is referred to the Chief Judge. But MCR 2.003(D) presupposes that the Chief Judge can act impartially. Where the Chief Judge is the subject judge's former named law partner, this presumption fails.

The Michigan Constitution vests in the Supreme Court "general superintending control over all courts." Const. 1963, Art. VI, § 4. MCR 7.304 implements this authority. Where structural conflicts within a circuit court prevent fair adjudication, superintending control is the appropriate remedy.

Plaintiff requests that if this Motion is denied by Judge McNeill, and if Chief Judge Hoopes is asked to review, the Chief Judge either: (a) recuse himself and request SCAO assignment of an outside judge; or (b) the record be preserved for an immediate Complaint for Superintending Control to the Michigan Supreme Court under MCR 7.304.

---

### V. CONCLUSION

The record before this Court establishes every element required for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b). The Berry family connection creates a disqualifying relationship. The Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill law firm connection creates a structural conflict that pervades the entire 14th Circuit. And the documented pattern of bias — the 1.9:1 grant ratio, the muting, the rejected evaluation, the 59 days of incarceration, the same-day ex parte orders, the "quit nitpicking" dismissal — demonstrates a sustained course of conduct that no objective observer could characterize as impartial.

Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court:

3. **Alternatively**, if this Motion is denied, refer the matter directly to the Michigan Supreme Court or the SCAO for assignment of an independent judge, bypassing Chief Judge Hoopes on the ground that his former law partnership with Judge McNeill creates an appearance of impropriety equal to that which disqualifies Judge McNeill herself; and

4. **Grant** such other and further relief as this Court deems just and equitable.

## Critical Note on the Hoopes Problem

If Judge McNeill denies this Motion and it is referred to Chief Judge Hoopes under MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a), Plaintiff should immediately file a separate motion objecting to Chief Judge Hoopes's participation on the ground of his former law partnership with Judge McNeill. If both judges refuse to step aside, the next step is a **Complaint for Superintending Control** to the Michigan Supreme Court under MCR 7.304, or an **Application for Leave to Appeal** under MCR 7.305, requesting assignment of an outside judge.

The key argument: MCR 2.003(D) presupposes that the Chief Judge is a disinterested party. Where the Chief Judge is the subject judge's former named law partner, the presumption of disinterestedness fails, and the Supreme Court's superintending control authority is the only remedy that can ensure a fair adjudication.
