## I. DEFENSE THEORY SUMMARY

Andrew James Pigors acted in lawful self-defense during an incident at the Muskegon County Jail. Body camera footage from a responding or present officer captures the officer stating that **five inmates had planned to kill Andrew**. Under Michigan's Self-Defense Act (MCL 780.972), a person who uses force in the honest and reasonable belief that imminent use of unlawful force is necessary to defend themselves has committed no crime.

The prosecution's failure to disclose this body camera footage constitutes a **Brady violation** under *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), as the footage is plainly exculpatory — it corroborates the defendant's reasonable fear of imminent death or great bodily harm.

## III. OFFICER STATEMENT: FIVE-INMATE THREAT

### A. What the Officer Said

An officer present during or after the jail incident stated, on body camera, that **five inmates had planned to kill Andrew Pigors**.

### B. Evidentiary Significance

This statement is significant because:

1. **It establishes the threat was real and known to law enforcement** — not fabricated by the defendant after the fact
2. **It corroborates Andrew's reasonable fear** — the "reasonable person" standard under MCL 780.972(1) is satisfied when law enforcement itself acknowledges the threat
3. **It negates the "aggressor" argument** — if Andrew was the target of a planned attack by five people, he cannot be the initial aggressor
4. **It supports proportional force** — facing five attackers with a plan to kill justifies the use of force, including potentially deadly force, under M Crim JI 7.16

### C. Admissibility

- **Present sense impression** — MRE 803(1): Statement describing an event made while or immediately after perceiving it
- **Excited utterance** — MRE 803(2): Statement relating to a startling event made while under the stress of excitement
- **Statement by law enforcement** — The officer's statement is based on the officer's investigation, observation, or information received from jail staff/inmates
- **Not hearsay if offered for effect on listener** — If offered to show why Andrew reasonably believed he was in danger, it is not offered for truth of the matter asserted

## V. MICHIGAN SELF-DEFENSE LAW

### A. MCL 780.972 — Self-Defense Act (Use of Force in Self-Defense)

**MCL 780.972(1):** An individual who has not or is not engaged in the commission of a crime at the time he or she uses deadly force or force other than deadly force may use deadly force or force other than deadly force against another individual anywhere he or she has the legal right to be with no duty to retreat if either of the following applies:

> **(a)** The individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent death of or imminent great bodily harm to himself or herself or to another individual.
>
> **(b)** The individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent sexual assault of himself or herself or of another individual.

**MCL 780.972(2):** An individual who has not or is not engaged in the commission of a crime at the time he or she uses force other than deadly force may use force other than deadly force against another individual anywhere he or she has the legal right to be with no duty to retreat if he or she honestly and reasonably believes that the use of that force is necessary to defend himself or herself or another individual from the imminent unlawful use of force by another individual.

### Application to This Case

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| **Not engaged in crime** | Andrew was an inmate — being incarcerated does not strip the right to self-defense. *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich 116, 127 (2002) ("the right of self-defense is available to all persons") |
| **Honest belief** | Andrew believed five inmates intended to kill him — subjective element satisfied |
| **Reasonable belief** | An officer confirmed on body cam that five inmates planned to kill Andrew — objective element satisfied |
| **Imminent threat** | A coordinated plan by five people to kill constitutes an imminent threat |
| **Proportional force** | Force used against five attackers with lethal intent is proportional |
| **No duty to retreat** | MCL 780.972 eliminates the duty to retreat; even in a jail setting, an inmate cannot retreat from a housing unit |

### B. MCL 780.971 — Definitions (Castle Doctrine)

**MCL 780.971(3):** Provides a rebuttable presumption that the defendant had an honest and reasonable belief in imminent death or great bodily harm when force is used against a person who is in the process of committing a home invasion or who has already committed one.

> **Note:** The Castle Doctrine's home-invasion presumption does not directly apply in a jail setting. However, the broader Self-Defense Act (MCL 780.972) applies everywhere the defendant has a legal right to be, and an inmate is legally placed in a jail facility.

### C. Key Michigan Self-Defense Cases

| Case | Holding | Relevance |
|------|---------|-----------|
| *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich 116 (2002) | Self-defense is available to all persons; once raised, prosecution must disprove beyond reasonable doubt | Core framework — prosecution bears burden |
| *People v. Dupree*, 486 Mich 693 (2010) | Defendant need not be "faultless" — imperfect self-defense can negate specific intent | Even if Andrew's response was imperfect, it may negate intent |
| *People v. Guajardo*, 300 Mich App 26 (2013) | Self-defense applies even when defendant initiated contact, if force escalation by other party was disproportionate | Addresses potential "aggressor" argument |
| *People v. Heflin*, 434 Mich 482 (1990) | Self-defense instruction required when any evidence supports it | Body cam is "evidence" requiring instruction |

## VI. JURY INSTRUCTIONS

### A. M Crim JI 7.15 — Self-Defense

Standard self-defense instruction. Tells jury to consider whether:
1. The defendant honestly and reasonably believed use of force was necessary
2. The threatened harm was imminent
3. The force used was proportional to the threat

**Application:** The body cam footage showing an officer confirming 5 inmates planned to kill Andrew directly supports elements (1) and (2). Five-on-one disparity supports element (3).

### B. M Crim JI 7.16 — Use of Deadly Force in Self-Defense

Instructs jury that deadly force is justified when defendant honestly and reasonably believes deadly force is necessary to prevent **imminent death or great bodily harm**.

**Application:** A planned attack by five people to kill one person meets the "imminent death" threshold. This instruction should be requested if the charge involves allegations of deadly force.

### C. M Crim JI 7.20 — Aggressor's Right to Self-Defense

Instructs jury that even an initial aggressor may regain the right to self-defense if:
1. The aggressor withdraws from the conflict and clearly communicates withdrawal
2. The other party escalates to deadly force disproportionate to the initial aggression

**Application:** This instruction should be requested prophylactically. If the prosecution argues Andrew was the aggressor, this instruction preserves the self-defense claim even under that theory. However, the primary defense is that Andrew was the **target**, not the aggressor — the five-inmate plan proves this.

### D. M Crim JI 7.21 — Duty to Retreat (No Longer Required)

Michigan's Self-Defense Act (MCL 780.972) **eliminated** the duty to retreat. The jury should be instructed that Andrew had **no duty to retreat** before using force.

### E. M Crim JI 7.22 — Burden of Proof on Self-Defense

Once the defendant raises self-defense, the **prosecution bears the burden** of disproving it beyond a reasonable doubt. *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich 116 (2002).
