                          I. INTRODUCTION

    1.  Defendant is charged in connection with an incident that

occurred on December 7, 2025, at the Muskegon County Jail, 996

Laketon Avenue, Muskegon, Michigan 49442.

    2.  Trial in this matter is currently scheduled for April 7,

    3.  Despite the imminent trial date, Defendant has not received

has the prosecution responded to Defendant's prior requests for

exculpatory evidence.

    4.  Critical to Defendant's case is evidence that a corrections

officer stated, on or about December 7, 2025, that five (5) inmates

were "getting ready to kill" the Defendant — establishing the

factual foundation for Defendant's claim of self-defense.

    5.  This evidence, along with body camera footage, incident

reports, and witness statements, constitutes Brady material that the

prosecution is constitutionally and procedurally obligated to

disclose.


                    II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

    6.  On December 7, 2025, Defendant Andrew J. Pigors was an

inmate at the Muskegon County Jail.

    7.  Prior to the incident giving rise to the criminal charge,

a corrections officer made a statement to the effect that five (5)

inmates were "getting ready to kill" the Defendant.

    8.  This statement demonstrates that corrections staff had

knowledge of an imminent threat to Defendant's life and physical

safety before the incident occurred.

    9.  The charged incident arose in the context of this known and

documented threat against Defendant's life.

    10. Defendant acted in lawful self-defense in response to a

reasonable and well-founded fear of imminent serious bodily harm or

death, as recognized under Michigan law. People v. Dupree, 486

Mich. 693, 707 (2010); MCL 780.972.

    11. A FOIA request for body camera footage and related records

was submitted to the Muskegon County Sheriff's Office on March 19,

2026. As of the date of this motion, no response has been received.


              III. LEGAL AUTHORITY — MCR 6.201

A.  Mandatory Disclosure (MCR 6.201(A))

    12. MCR 6.201(A) requires the prosecution to provide mandatory

discovery disclosures in criminal cases. Upon request, the

prosecution must provide, without court order:

        (a)  Any exculpatory information or evidence known to the
             prosecutor or any police agency involved in the
             investigation of the charged offense (MCR 6.201(A));

        (b)  The names and addresses of all prosecution witnesses
             whom the prosecutor intends to call at trial
             (MCR 6.201(A)(1));

        (c)  Any written or recorded statement of a witness whom
             the prosecution intends to call at trial, including
             police officer reports (MCR 6.201(A)(2));

        (d)  Any criminal history of the defendant known to the
             prosecution (MCR 6.201(A)(3));

        (e)  Any physical evidence, documents, photographs, or
             tangible objects that the prosecution intends to
             introduce at trial or that were obtained from or
             belonging to the defendant (MCR 6.201(A)(6)).

B.  Defendant's Right to Discovery (MCR 6.201(B))

    13. Under MCR 6.201(B), Defendant is additionally entitled

to request, and the prosecution is obligated to provide:

        (a)  Any written or recorded statements by any person with
             knowledge of the charged incident (MCR 6.201(B)(1));

        (b)  Any report or results of scientific tests, experiments,
             or comparisons, including medical reports
             (MCR 6.201(B)(2));

        (c)  Any police report or internal investigative report
             related to the charged offense (MCR 6.201(B)(3));

        (d)  The opportunity to inspect, photograph, or copy any
             tangible evidence in the prosecution's possession
             (MCR 6.201(B)(5)).

C.  Constitutional Obligation — Brady v. Maryland

    14. Independent of MCR 6.201, the prosecution has an

affirmative, ongoing constitutional obligation under Brady v.

Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), and its progeny to disclose all

evidence favorable to the accused that is material to guilt or

punishment. See also:

        (a)  United States v. Bagley, 473 U.S. 667, 682 (1985)
             (Brady applies to impeachment evidence);

        (b)  Kyles v. Whitley, 514 U.S. 419, 437 (1995)
             (prosecution must learn of favorable evidence known
             to others acting on behalf of the government);

        (c)  Strickler v. Greene, 527 U.S. 263, 281-82 (1999)
             (three components: favorable, suppressed, material);

        (d)  People v. Lester, 232 Mich. App. 262, 281 (1998)
             (Michigan adoption of Brady standards).

    15. The corrections officer's statement regarding five inmates

threatening Defendant's life is, by definition, exculpatory evidence

that directly supports the defense theory. Suppression of this

evidence would constitute a due process violation under the

Fourteenth Amendment and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan

Constitution.

        (c)  Reflects favorably on the credibility of the Defendant;

    26. An affirmative representation from the prosecution that all

              V. GROUNDS FOR COURT INTERVENTION

    27. Defendant has been unable to obtain this evidence through

informal request or FOIA. Trial is imminent. Without this evidence,

Defendant cannot prepare an adequate defense, in violation of the

Sixth Amendment right to effective assistance (even for pro se

defendants, see Faretta v. California, 422 U.S. 806 (1975)) and

the Fourteenth Amendment right to due process.

    28. MCR 6.201(J) provides that if a party fails to comply with

discovery rules, the court may order compliance, grant a continuance,

prohibit the party from introducing undisclosed evidence, or enter

such other order as it deems just under the circumstances.

    29. The interests of justice require disclosure. The evidence

requested goes directly to whether Defendant acted in lawful self-

defense. Suppression of the officer's statement and supporting

evidence would be fundamentally unfair.
