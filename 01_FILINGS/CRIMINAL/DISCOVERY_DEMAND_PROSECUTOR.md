## II. LEGAL AUTHORITY

### A. Michigan Court Rule 6.201 — Discovery

**MCR 6.201(A)** provides that, upon request, the prosecution must provide the defendant with discovery of the materials and information within its control. The rule mandates disclosure as a matter of right and does not condition disclosure upon a showing of materiality.

**MCR 6.201(B)** provides that, upon request, the party must provide:

> (1) Any written or recorded statement, including electronically recorded statements, pertaining to the case by a lay witness whom the party may call at trial, except that a defendant is not obliged to provide the party's own statement;
>
> (2) A description of and an opportunity to inspect any tangible physical evidence that the party may introduce at trial, including any document, photograph, or other paper;
>
> (3) A list of witnesses whom the party may call at trial;
>
> (4) Any criminal record that the party may use at trial to impeach a witness.

**MCR 6.201(C)** provides additional discovery for the defendant upon request, including:

> The prosecution must, upon request, provide the defendant with the following, which are in the possession or control of the prosecution, law enforcement, or other government agencies:
>
> (1) Any exculpatory information or evidence known to the prosecution;
>
> (2) Any police report and crime scene evidence pertaining to the case;
>
> (3) Any written or recorded statements by a defendant, co-defendant, or accomplice, including any electronically recorded statement.

### B. Constitutional Brady/Giglio Obligations

Under *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), the prosecution has an affirmative constitutional duty to disclose evidence favorable to the accused that is material to guilt or punishment. This obligation extends to:

- **Exculpatory evidence** — evidence tending to show the defendant's innocence or negate an element of the offense. *Brady*, 373 U.S. at 87.

- **Impeachment evidence** — evidence that could be used to impeach prosecution witnesses. *Giglio v. United States*, 405 U.S. 150 (1972).

- **Evidence known to law enforcement** — the prosecution's duty extends to evidence in the possession of law enforcement, whether or not the prosecutor has actual knowledge of the evidence. *Kyles v. Whitley*, 514 U.S. 419, 437 (1995).

Evidence is "material" under *Brady* if "there is a reasonable probability that, had the evidence been disclosed to the defense, the result of the proceeding would have been different." *United States v. Bagley*, 473 U.S. 667, 682 (1985).

### C. MCR 6.201(J) — Sanctions for Noncompliance

**MCR 6.201(J)** provides that if a party fails to comply with a discovery order or request:

> The court may order the party to provide the discovery, grant a continuance, prohibit the party from introducing in evidence the material not disclosed, or enter such other order as it deems just under the circumstances.

The court may also **dismiss the case** where the prosecution's failure to disclose is willful, prejudicial, and no lesser sanction will cure the harm.
