## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. Right to Effective Assistance of Counsel

12. The Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides: "In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right . . . to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence."

13. Article 1, § 20 of the Michigan Constitution provides: "In every criminal prosecution, the accused shall have the right . . . to have the assistance of counsel for his or her defense."

14. The right to counsel means the right to **effective** counsel, not merely the formal appointment of an attorney. *Strickland v. Washington*, 466 U.S. 668 (1984); *People v. Pickens*, 446 Mich 298 (1994).

### B. Ginther Hearing Required

15. Under *People v. Ginther*, 390 Mich 436 (1973), a defendant is entitled to an evidentiary hearing on claims of ineffective assistance of counsel. The *Ginther* hearing allows the court to:
    - Hear testimony from the defendant about counsel's failures
    - Hear testimony from counsel regarding her performance
    - Make a factual determination about whether the attorney-client relationship has broken down
    - Determine appropriate relief

16. The Defendant requests a *Ginther* hearing on the record, at which the Court may inquire into:
    - Whether Ms. Campanelli has communicated with the Defendant
    - Whether Ms. Campanelli has investigated the self-defense claim
    - Whether Ms. Campanelli has obtained or reviewed body camera footage
    - Whether Ms. Campanelli has filed any pretrial motions
    - Whether Ms. Campanelli has prepared for trial

### C. Standard for Substitution of Counsel

17. A criminal defendant's right to appointed counsel includes the right to have counsel who will provide effective assistance. When appointed counsel fails to communicate with the client, the court should appoint substitute counsel. *People v. Mack*, 190 Mich App 7, 14 (1991).

18. Michigan courts consider the following factors when evaluating a request for new counsel:
    - Whether the attorney has failed to communicate with the defendant
    - Whether the breakdown in communication prejudices the defense
    - Whether the defendant has identified a legitimate complaint (not merely a disagreement over strategy)
    - Whether substitution will cause undue delay

    See *People v. Traylor*, 245 Mich App 460 (2001); *People v. Strickland*, 293 Mich App 393 (2011).

19. Here, the Defendant's complaint is not a disagreement about strategy — it is a **complete failure to communicate**. Counsel cannot develop a strategy if counsel will not speak to the client. This is the most fundamental breakdown possible.

### D. Constructive Denial of Counsel

20. A complete failure by counsel to communicate with the client, investigate the case, or prepare for trial constitutes a **constructive denial of the right to counsel** — which is presumptively prejudicial and requires no further showing under *Strickland*.

21. *United States v. Cronic*, 466 U.S. 648, 659 (1984): When counsel "entirely fails to subject the prosecution's case to meaningful adversarial testing," the defendant is constructively denied the right to counsel, and prejudice is presumed.

22. *People v. Frazier*, 478 Mich 231 (2007): The Michigan Supreme Court recognized that constructive denial of counsel is a basis for relief without the standard *Strickland* prejudice showing.

23. Ms. Campanelli's failure to communicate, investigate, or file motions in a self-defense case where exculpatory body camera footage exists constitutes a constructive denial of the right to counsel.

---

### E. Alternative: Right to Self-Representation

24. If the Court declines to appoint substitute counsel, the Defendant invokes his right to self-representation under the Sixth Amendment. *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806 (1975).

25. In *Faretta*, the United States Supreme Court held:

> "The Sixth Amendment does not provide merely that a defense shall be made for the accused; it grants to the accused personally the right to make his defense." 422 U.S. at 819.

26. A defendant who chooses self-representation must make the choice: (a) knowingly, (b) intelligently, and (c) voluntarily. *Faretta*, 422 U.S. at 835; *People v. Anderson*, 398 Mich 361 (1976).

27. Michigan Court Rule MCR 6.005(D) provides the procedure for waiver of counsel:

> "Before accepting a waiver [of appointed counsel], the court must advise the defendant of the charge, the maximum possible prison sentence for the offense, any mandatory minimum sentence required by law, and the risk involved in self-representation, and must satisfy itself that the waiver is voluntarily and understandingly made."

### F. Request for Standby Counsel

30. If the Court grants the Defendant's request to proceed *in propria persona*, the Defendant requests the appointment of **standby counsel** to assist with procedural questions, discovery, and courtroom procedure.

31. The appointment of standby counsel does not infringe on the right to self-representation so long as standby counsel does not interfere with the defendant's control of the defense. *McKaskle v. Wiggins*, 465 U.S. 168 (1984).
