## III. ARGUMENT

### A. Self-Defense Immunity Under MCL 780.972

10. Michigan's Self-Defense Act, MCL 780.972, provides:

> "An individual who has not or is not engaged in the commission of a crime at the time he or she uses deadly force or force other than deadly force may use deadly force or force other than deadly force against another individual anywhere he or she has the legal right to be with no duty to retreat if . . . [t]he individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent death of or imminent great bodily harm to himself or herself or to another individual."

11. The Defendant satisfies every element of this statute:

    **(a) Not engaged in the commission of a crime.** The Defendant was an inmate lawfully housed in the jail facility. Being incarcerated does not strip a person of the fundamental right to self-defense. *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich 116, 127 (2002) ("the right of self-defense is available to all persons").

    **(b) Honest belief of imminent death.** The Defendant learned that five inmates had formed a plan to kill him. This knowledge created an honest belief that his life was in imminent danger.

    **(c) Reasonable belief.** The reasonableness of the Defendant's belief is corroborated by the body camera footage in which a law enforcement officer — not the Defendant — states that five inmates planned to kill the Defendant. When law enforcement itself confirms the threat, the objective reasonableness of the Defendant's belief is beyond dispute.

    **(d) No duty to retreat.** MCL 780.972 eliminates the duty to retreat. In a jail setting, retreat is physically impossible — inmates cannot leave their housing units at will.

    **(e) Proportional force.** The Defendant faced five individuals acting in concert with the intent to kill. Any force used by the Defendant was proportional to the threat of death at the hands of five attackers.

12. Under MCL 780.972, a person who acts in lawful self-defense "has committed no crime." This Court should dismiss the charges.

### B. Michigan Case Law Supports Dismissal

13. *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich 116 (2002): Once the defendant raises self-defense, the prosecution bears the burden of disproving it beyond a reasonable doubt. Here, the prosecution's own evidence (body camera footage) corroborates the defense rather than disproving it.

14. *People v. Dupree*, 486 Mich 693 (2010): A defendant need not be entirely "faultless" to claim self-defense. Even if the Defendant's response was imperfect, the defense applies if his belief was honest and reasonable.

15. *People v. Heflin*, 434 Mich 482 (1990): A self-defense instruction must be given when any evidence supports it. Here, the evidence not only supports the instruction — it compels dismissal.

16. *People v. Guajardo*, 300 Mich App 26 (2013): Self-defense applies even when the defendant initiated contact, if the other party's escalation was disproportionate. Here, the Defendant did not initiate — he was the target of a planned attack.

### C. Brady Violation — Failure to Disclose Exculpatory Evidence

17. The prosecution has a constitutional obligation to disclose all evidence favorable to the defense. *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963).

18. This obligation extends to:
    - Evidence that tends to exculpate the defendant: *Brady*, 373 U.S. at 87
    - Evidence that impeaches prosecution witnesses: *Giglio v. United States*, 405 U.S. 150 (1972)
    - Evidence in the possession of law enforcement: *Kyles v. Whitley*, 514 U.S. 419 (1995)

19. Under Michigan law, the prosecution's Brady obligation is codified in MCR 6.201 and extends to all evidence in the possession of any law enforcement agency involved in the case. *People v. Lester*, 232 Mich App 262 (1998).

20. The body camera footage is **plainly exculpatory**:
    - It captures a law enforcement officer stating that five inmates planned to kill the Defendant
    - It directly corroborates the Defendant's self-defense claim
    - It undermines any theory that the Defendant was the aggressor
    - It establishes that law enforcement was aware of the threat

21. The prosecution's failure to disclose this footage is a Brady violation. The appropriate remedy is dismissal. See *People v. Chenault*, 495 Mich 142 (2014) (discussing remedies for Brady violations); *Arizona v. Youngblood*, 488 U.S. 51 (1988) (bad faith destruction of evidence requires dismissal).

22. At minimum, this Court should:
    - **Order immediate production** of all body camera footage from all officers present during or after the incident
    - **Sanction the prosecution** under MCR 6.201(J) for discovery violations
    - **Dismiss the charges** if the footage has been destroyed or cannot be produced

### D. Denial of Effective Assistance of Counsel

23. The Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, § 20 of the Michigan Constitution guarantee the right to effective assistance of counsel.

24. Assigned public defender Amy Campanelli has failed to communicate with the Defendant. Specifically:
    - [VERIFY — insert specific dates and methods of attempted contact]
    - [VERIFY — insert duration of non-communication]
    - [VERIFY — insert any other facts demonstrating Campanelli's failure to act]

25. A complete failure to communicate with the client constitutes a constructive denial of the right to counsel. *United States v. Cronic*, 466 U.S. 648, 659 (1984) (complete denial of counsel at a critical stage is presumptively prejudicial).

26. Under Michigan law, a defendant is entitled to a *Ginther* hearing to establish ineffective assistance. *People v. Ginther*, 390 Mich 436 (1973). The Defendant has filed a separate Motion for New Counsel (or Self-Representation) addressing this issue.

27. The Defendant's inability to prepare a defense due to counsel's abandonment constitutes a violation of due process. The charges should be dismissed, or in the alternative, trial should be adjourned until the Defendant has effective representation.

## IV. DUE PROCESS — CUMULATIVE EFFECT

28. The combination of the prosecution's Brady violation, the failure to provide discovery, and the denial of effective assistance of counsel creates a cumulative due process violation that deprives the Defendant of a fair trial.

29. The Due Process Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment (U.S. Const. amend. XIV) and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution require fundamental fairness. *People v. Aceval*, 282 Mich App 379 (2009).

30. Here, the Defendant faces trial without: (a) access to the most critical piece of exculpatory evidence; (b) meaningful communication with counsel; and (c) adequate time and resources to prepare a self-defense case. This is fundamentally unfair.
