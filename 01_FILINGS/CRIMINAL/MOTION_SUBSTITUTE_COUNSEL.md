## II. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. Right to Effective Assistance of Counsel — Sixth Amendment and MCR 6.005

The Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution guarantees every criminal defendant the right to "the Assistance of Counsel for his defence." U.S. Const. amend. VI. This right is applicable to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. *Gideon v. Wainwright*, 372 U.S. 335 (1963).

**MCR 6.005(A)** provides:

> "An indigent defendant is entitled to have an attorney appointed at public expense in all criminal proceedings, at all stages of the proceedings."

The right to counsel means the right to **effective** counsel. *Strickland v. Washington*, 466 U.S. 668 (1984). Under *Strickland*, a defendant claiming ineffective assistance must show:

1. **Deficient performance** — that counsel's representation fell below an objective standard of reasonableness; **and**

2. **Prejudice** — that there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel's unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.

*Id.* at 687–694.

### B. Complete Failure to Communicate Constitutes Deficient Performance

Counsel's complete failure to communicate with a defendant regarding the case, strategy, and discovery constitutes performance that falls below any objective standard of reasonableness. A criminal defense attorney has a **duty to consult with the defendant** on important decisions and to keep the defendant informed of the case. *Strickland*, 466 U.S. at 688.

When counsel fails to communicate with the client, the client is deprived of the ability to:

- Assist in his own defense;
- Provide relevant facts and identify witnesses;
- Make informed decisions about plea offers, trial strategy, and the exercise of constitutional rights;
- Review discovery and identify exculpatory evidence.

This failure is particularly prejudicial when, as here, the Defendant has information about specific exculpatory evidence (body-cam footage) that counsel has apparently failed to obtain or communicate about.

### C. Substitute Counsel or Pro Se — MCR 6.005

**MCR 6.005(E)** provides:

> "The court shall not permit the defendant to make an initial waiver of the right to be represented by a lawyer at arraignment on the information or indictment or at any later proceeding without first:
>
> (1) advising the defendant of the charge, the maximum possible prison sentence for the offense, any mandatory minimum sentence required by law, and the risk involved in self-representation, and
>
> (2) offering the defendant the opportunity to consult with a retained lawyer or, if the defendant is indigent, the opportunity to consult with an appointed lawyer."

The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that a defendant may waive the right to counsel and proceed pro se, provided the waiver is knowing, intelligent, and voluntary. *People v. Anderson*, 398 Mich. 361, 367–368 (1976).

### D. Right to Self-Representation — *Faretta v. California*

The United States Supreme Court held in *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806 (1975), that a criminal defendant has a constitutional right to self-representation. The Court stated:

> "The Sixth Amendment does not provide merely that a defense shall be made for the accused; it grants to the accused personally the right to make his defense." *Id.* at 819.

A defendant who knowingly and intelligently waives the right to counsel must be permitted to represent himself. *Id.* at 835.

## III. ARGUMENT

### A. Substitute Counsel Should Be Appointed

The attorney-client relationship between the Defendant and Ms. Campanelli has irretrievably broken down. Counsel has failed to communicate about the case, failed to discuss strategy, failed to keep the Defendant informed of discovery, and has engaged in hostile conduct toward the Defendant.

This Court has the authority and the obligation to ensure that the Defendant receives effective assistance of counsel. Where, as here, the breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is **complete and irremediable**, the appropriate remedy is the appointment of substitute counsel.

The Defendant respectfully requests that this Court:

1. Relieve Amy Campanelli as counsel of record;
2. Appoint qualified substitute counsel; and
3. Grant a reasonable continuance to allow substitute counsel to prepare for trial.

### B. In the Alternative, the Defendant Should Be Permitted to Proceed Pro Se

If this Court determines that substitute counsel cannot be timely appointed, the Defendant requests permission to proceed in propria persona (pro se) pursuant to *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806 (1975), and MCR 6.005(E).

The Defendant understands and acknowledges:

- The charges against him and the maximum possible sentence;
- The risks and disadvantages of self-representation;
- That the Court will hold him to the same standards of procedure and substantive law as a licensed attorney;
- That the Court will not provide him with legal advice.

The Defendant nonetheless wishes to exercise his constitutional right to self-representation rather than proceed with counsel who is not communicating with him and is not preparing his defense.

If the Defendant proceeds pro se, he respectfully requests that this Court appoint **standby counsel** to be available for consultation, as is customary in Michigan courts.

### C. A Continuance Is Necessary to Protect the Defendant's Rights

Whether the Court appoints substitute counsel or permits the Defendant to proceed pro se, a **continuance is necessary** to ensure adequate preparation for trial.

Trial is scheduled for **April 7, 2026 — seventeen (17) days** from the date of this motion. The Defendant has been unable to determine what discovery has been obtained, what trial preparation has been done, or whether exculpatory evidence has been secured. Neither new counsel nor the Defendant proceeding pro se can adequately prepare for trial in this timeframe.

The Defendant's right to a fair trial under the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments outweighs any interest in maintaining the current trial date. The Defendant does not request a continuance for purposes of delay, but solely to protect his constitutional right to a meaningful defense.
