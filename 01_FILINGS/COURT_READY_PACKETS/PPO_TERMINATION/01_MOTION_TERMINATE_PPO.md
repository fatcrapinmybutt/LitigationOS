## 3. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. PPO History and Procedural Background

The procedural history of this PPO reveals a pattern of abuse from inception:

**December 3, 2023 — PPO Petition Filed and Granted Same Day (Ex Parte)**

Petitioner filed a petition for a Personal Protection Order under MCL 600.2950a. The PPO was **issued the same day** — ex parte, without notice to Respondent, and without any evidentiary hearing. Respondent had no opportunity to be heard before the order took effect.

**December 4, 2024 — PPO Extended for Full Year**

The PPO was extended for an additional full year. The extension was triggered by Petitioner's strategic filing of a fourth alleged PPO violation on November 19, 2024 — timed to ensure automatic extension under Muskegon County policy without requiring new evidence of any actual threat. Respondent was notified of the extension on December 5, 2024 — after it was already entered, in violation of MCR 3.707(B)'s notice requirement.

**Show Cause Proceedings and Escalating Sentences**

Petitioner filed six or more show cause motions alleging PPO violations. **None of the alleged violations involved physical proximity, threats, stalking, or any behavior suggesting danger to Petitioner's safety.** Every alleged violation consisted of attempted communication — including birthday messages to the parties' minor child through AppClose. The pattern of escalating punishment:

| Date | Proceeding | Allegation | Result |
|------|-----------|------------|--------|
| Jan. 7, 2024 | Show Cause Hearing | PPO violation — exchange | Hearing per MCR 3.708 |
| Apr. 11, 2024 | PPO Amended Ex Parte | Additional restrictions | Order entered without hearing |
| Nov. 15, 2025 | Show Cause #5 | Alleged communication | **14 days jail** |
| Nov. 26, 2025 | Show Cause #6 & #7 | Birthday messages on AppClose | **45 days jail** |

**Total Incarceration: 59 Days for Birthday Messages**

Respondent was sentenced to a combined **59 days of incarceration** for sending birthday messages and other innocuous communications to his minor child through AppClose — an application specifically designed for co-parent communication.

**Prior Guilty Plea — Does Not Immunize PPO From Challenge**

Respondent acknowledges entering a guilty plea to PPO violations on October 18, 2025 (CC 382a). Respondent does not seek to withdraw that plea. However, the *existence* of a guilty plea to violating a PPO does not immunize the underlying PPO from challenge. A PPO that was obtained through fraud and maintained through abuse of process remains subject to termination. The plea addressed the technical question of whether the PPO's terms were violated — it did not adjudicate whether the PPO should continue to exist. MCL 600.2950(5) expressly provides for modification/termination; if a guilty plea foreclosed all subsequent challenges, the statutory mechanism would be nullified — an absurd result. *See In re Certified Question (Bankey v. Storer Broadcasting)*, 432 Mich 438, 455 (1989).

## 4. LEGAL STANDARD FOR TERMINATION

### A. MCL 600.2950 and MCR 3.707

Michigan law establishes that a PPO is appropriate only where the petitioner demonstrates a reasonable apprehension of violence or threatening behavior. *Pickering v Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694 (2002); *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 (2008). MCR 3.707(A) provides that "[a] respondent who is subject to a personal protection order may file a motion to modify or rescind the personal protection order." MCR 3.717 authorizes the court to terminate a PPO upon a showing that the order is no longer necessary.

### B. Element-by-Element Analysis Under MCL 600.2950(1)

| MCL 600.2950(1) Element | Required Showing | Evidence in Record |
|---|---|---|
| **(a) Assault or battery** | Actual or attempted physical harm | **None.** Seven police investigations: zero findings. |
| **(b) Sexual assault** | Forced or coerced sexual contact | **None.** Not alleged. |
| **(c) Stalking** | Course of conduct causing fear of harm | **Not supported.** Police found: "No PPO violation occurred." |
| **(d) Destruction of property** | Malicious damage to property | **None.** Not alleged. |
| **(e) Threats of violence** | Credible threats of physical harm | **Not supported.** HealthWest: Danger to Others = **0**. |
| **(f) Interference with removal** | Preventing departure from premises | **None.** Not alleged. |
| **(g) Firearms** | After specific legal prohibition | **None.** Not alleged. |
| **(h) Trespass** | Entering property without permission | **Not supported.** Approaches were for court-ordered exchanges. |
| **(i) Reasonable apprehension** | Objective basis for fear of violence | **Affirmatively refuted.** Seven investigations found no threat. |

Not a single statutory element is met.

### C. *Hayford v Hayford* Requires an Evidence-Based Finding

In *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 (2008), the Michigan Court of Appeals held that issuance and continuation of a PPO requires evidence demonstrating a reasonable apprehension of violence — not merely a subjective claim of fear. The court recognized that PPOs are powerful legal instruments that restrict liberty, and must be grounded in objective evidence, not speculation. *Id.* at 330-31.

Here, not only is the evidence insufficient — it is affirmatively **exculpatory**. Seven independent investigations demonstrate that Respondent is not a threat.

## 5. ARGUMENT

### I. THE PPO MUST BE TERMINATED — STATUTORY STANDARD NOT MET

MCL 600.2950(1) authorizes a PPO only upon a showing of specific prohibited conduct. As shown above, no element is satisfied. Seven separate police investigations concluded with the same finding: no violation, no threat, no harm. Norton Shores Police Report MU2422249 (November 15, 2024) explicitly states: **"No violation of PPO occurred."**

If seven independent investigations by neutral, trained law enforcement professionals cannot find a single instance of threatening or harmful conduct, this Court cannot reasonably conclude that Petitioner has a "reasonable apprehension of violence" within the meaning of MCL 600.2950(1).

*See Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 512–13 (2003); *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 (2008).

### II. THE PPO VIOLATES FUNDAMENTAL CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHTS

The United States Supreme Court has consistently recognized that parents have a fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and companionship of their children. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65-66 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982).

As currently enforced, the PPO operates as a **complete bar** to Respondent's exercise of parenting time with L.D.W. Respondent has been denied all meaningful contact, jailed for birthday messages, and threatened with contempt each time he attempts any form of parenting.

Because the right to parent is fundamental, strict scrutiny applies. *See Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Washington v Glucksberg*, 521 US 702, 720 (1997). The PPO fails both prongs:

1. **No compelling interest.** Seven investigations found no threat. No violence has ever been alleged.
2. **Not narrowly tailored.** A complete bar on all parental contact is the most extreme restriction possible.

### III. WEAPONIZATION: TACTICAL ABUSE OF PROCESS

The timing of Petitioner's PPO enforcement actions reveals calculated strategy:

| Show Cause Filing | Proximity to Custody Event |
|---|---|
| Show Cause #7 (Oct. 27, 2025) | **2 days** before Oct. 29, 2025 custody hearing |

This pattern is consistent throughout — PPO filings timed to prejudice custody proceedings. The PPO-custody cross-reference analysis reveals same-day coordination between PPO filings and custody actions on multiple occasions (December 4, 2023; March 29, 2024; April 1, 2024), each carrying maximum weaponization correlation.

MCR 3.704(A) establishes that a PPO shall not be used as a substitute for other remedies available under law. Petitioner's use of the PPO to accomplish what would otherwise require a custody modification under MCL 722.27 violates this limitation.

Michigan recognizes abuse of process where a party uses legal process "for a purpose not proper in the regular prosecution of the proceeding." *Friedman v Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1, 30 (1981). MCR 1.109(E) and MCL 600.2591 authorize sanctions for frivolous filings.

### IV. DISPROPORTIONATE ENFORCEMENT

The enforcement disparity is constitutionally intolerable:

| Party | Conduct | Consequence |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Andrew Pigors (Respondent) | Birthday messages to L.D.W. on AppClose | **59 days incarceration** |
| Albert Watson (Petitioner's father) | Forcible child removal from Respondent's arms; throwing papers through car window | **Zero** |
| Cody Watson (Petitioner's brother) | Road harassment; threats; intimidation (MCSD-2024-02101) | **Zero** |
| Lori Watson (Petitioner's mother) | Withholding child from court-ordered exchange; alleged eavesdropping (MCL 750.539c) | **Zero** |
| Emily A. Watson (Petitioner) | Filing false allegations under oath; serial show cause abuse | **Zero** |

The Equal Protection Clause requires evenhanded enforcement. *Village of Willowbrook v Olech*, 528 U.S. 562 (2000). A PPO enforced exclusively against one party while the other party and her agents commit objectively more serious conduct with impunity is not a protective order — it is a punitive instrument.

### V. FEDERAL FIREARMS CONSEQUENCE — 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(8)

The continued existence of this PPO strips Respondent of his Second Amendment right to possess firearms under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(8). The Supreme Court in *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024), upheld § 922(g)(8) but emphasized the provision applies to individuals who pose a **"credible threat"** — not individuals subject to PPOs obtained through fabricated allegations. Where no credible threat has ever been substantiated, the indefinite deprivation of a constitutional right through a fraudulently obtained PPO raises serious due process concerns.

### VI. CHILLING EFFECT ON PARENTING

The PPO prevents Respondent from exercising any contact whatsoever with L.D.W. Even communicating through AppClose — specifically designed for co-parent communication — was treated as a criminal violation resulting in incarceration. This chilling effect violates parental rights protected by MCL 722.27a(1) and the Fourteenth Amendment.

The PPO also rests in part on illegally obtained evidence. The USB recording that formed the basis for the August 5, 2025 ex parte suspension order was made by Lori Watson without Respondent's knowledge or consent — a felony under MCL 750.539c. Under *People v Burge*, 722 NW2d 470 (Mich App 2006), such evidence must be excluded. The recording was admitted without proper authentication under MRE 901.
