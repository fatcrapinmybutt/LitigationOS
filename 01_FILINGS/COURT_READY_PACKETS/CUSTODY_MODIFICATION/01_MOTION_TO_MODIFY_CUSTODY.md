## IV. PROPER CAUSE AND CHANGE OF CIRCUMSTANCES

29. Under *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509-512 (2003), a movant must establish either "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances" before the court may revisit custody. "Proper cause" means one or more appropriate grounds that have or could have a significant effect on the child's life sufficient to warrant a custody review. "Change of circumstances" refers to conditions that have materially changed since the prior custody order. *Id.* at 509.

30. Plaintiff establishes **both** proper cause and changed circumstances on **seven independent grounds:**

### Ground 1: Favorable HealthWest Evaluation (New Evidence)

31. The Court ordered Plaintiff to undergo psychological evaluation. Plaintiff complied. The evaluation returned **all-zeros on every risk factor** — finding Plaintiff safe, stable, and drug-free. This evaluation is a **new fact** that did not exist when the suspension order was entered on August 8, 2025. Classic *Vodvarka* proper cause. *See* 259 Mich App at 512 ("a new fact that did not previously exist" constitutes proper cause).

### Ground 2: Completion of All Court-Ordered Conditions

32. Plaintiff has satisfied every condition imposed by this Court in connection with the suspension of parenting time. The conditions have been met. The predicate for suspension has been removed.

### Ground 3: 300+ Days of Complete Separation

33. The passage of more than 300 consecutive days of total separation between a three-year-old child and her bonded father is itself a material change in circumstances. The Michigan Court of Appeals has recognized that significant passage of time during a young child's formative years constitutes changed circumstances warranting review. *See Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App 68, 82-83 (2017).

### Ground 4: Seven Debunked False Allegations

34. The systematic pattern of false allegations — seven categories, 357 evidence items, zero substantiated findings — constitutes a changed circumstance insofar as it reveals that the basis for the suspension was not legitimate safety concerns but rather a tactical campaign to sever Plaintiff's parental bond. This was not apparent at the time of the original ex parte orders.

### Ground 5: Albert Watson Recorded Threat

35. The recorded statement by Albert Watson — **"I will make sure you don't see your son"** — is direct evidence of a coordinated alienation campaign that was not available to the Court at the time of the suspension orders. This recording, combined with 811 documented alienation incidents, constitutes new evidence of changed circumstances.

### Ground 6: Ronald Berry Cohabitation

36. Defendant's cohabitation with Ronald Berry represents a changed circumstance in the custodial environment. L.D.W. is now being raised in a household with an unvetted adult male who is not related to the child and whose involvement in the legal proceedings raises independent concerns.

### Ground 7: Pending Disqualification Motion — Structural Concerns

37. Plaintiff has filed a Motion to Disqualify under MCR 2.003 raising concerns about the presiding judge's connections to individuals in Defendant's circle. If the factual basis of that motion is substantiated, the prior orders adverse to Plaintiff may have been entered under circumstances that warrant comprehensive de novo review. This is a changed circumstance bearing on the integrity of the entire custody determination.

38. Any **one** of these seven grounds independently satisfies *Vodvarka*. Together, they constitute an overwhelming showing that the Court must revisit the custody order and conduct the best interest analysis that has never been performed.
