IV. SPECIFIC INCIDENTS OF BIAS AND PREJUDICE

A. Ex Parte Orders — Systematic and One-Sided

    12. I have reviewed every order entered in Case Nos.
2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP. Of the fifty-five (55) total orders
entered as of the date of this Affidavit, twenty-four (24) —
representing 44% — were entered ex parte, without prior notice to me
and without affording me an opportunity to be heard.

    13. Every single one of these twenty-four (24) ex parte orders
favored Defendant Emily A. Watson. Not one ex parte order has been
entered in my favor.

    14. On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five (5) separate ex
parte orders on a single day, none of which were served on me prior to
entry, and none of which provided me advance notice or opportunity to
respond.


B. Ex Parte Order — Total Suspension of Parenting Time

    15. On or about August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill signed an ex parte
"emergency" order suspending all of my parenting time with L.D.W.

    16. This order was entered without:
        - Advance notice to me of the emergency motion;
        - An opportunity for me to respond before the order issued;
        - Specific factual findings of immediate and irreparable harm
          as required by MCR 3.207(B); or
        - A prompt post-deprivation hearing.

    17. The effect of this order was devastating. It completely stopped
all parenting time, including regular weekly/biweekly parenting time,
scheduled Labor Day 2025 holiday parenting time, my child's third
birthday celebration, and all special-event and holiday time.

    18. I timely filed a written objection and motion to modify or
rescind the ex parte order and requested a hearing. The ex parte order
remained in effect for approximately four months (August through
November 2025) before any meaningful evidentiary hearing was held.
During this period, I had zero contact with L.D.W.


C. Ex Parte Review of Evidence

    19. During these proceedings, a USB recording was submitted to the
Court and handled off-record, without my knowledge or opportunity to
challenge its contents, authenticity, or admissibility.

    20. On August 5, 2025, Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Yes, I
had my staff listen to it," referring to this USB evidence. This
admission establishes that the Court reviewed substantive, disputed
evidence outside any noticed hearing, without either party present, and
without opportunity for objection or cross-examination.


D. Off-Record Evidence Handling — HealthWest Evaluation

    21. A mental-health evaluation was ordered. Judge McNeill
specifically instructed me to email my HealthWest assessment to her
secretary rather than filing through the Clerk's office. The Court
subsequently acknowledged receipt:

        "The Court notes that the Plaintiff did email a HW assessment
        to the Court's secretary on Friday, September 5, 2025, before
        this order was complete. However, the Plaintiff did not follow
        the Court's instructions."

    22. This off-docket evidence handling prevented: (a) the opposing
party from accessing the evaluation; (b) creation of a public record;
and (c) proper filing in the court system per MCR 8.119.

    23. HealthWest evaluation report H0002, dated September 5, 2025,
was favorable to me (Psychosis = 0, Substance Abuse = 0, Danger to
Self/Others = 0, LOCUS Level One). The Court rejected this favorable
evaluation without conducting an evidentiary hearing, and instead
continued the suspension of my parenting time.


E. On-the-Record Statements Demonstrating Bias

    24. Judge McNeill stated on the record that I am "a liar" and that
I am "crazy." These are personal characterizations reflecting animus
and prejudgment, not neutral judicial assessments.

    25. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Do not file any more. I
will not look at it." No vexatious-litigant finding has been made under
MCR 2.625.

    26. Judge McNeill referenced and denounced a "ghost" second mental
health evaluation — an evaluation I never took. This demonstrates
either reliance on extrajudicial information or a fundamental failure
to ascertain the facts of the case.


F. Muting During Hearings

    27. During a single hearing, I was muted seven (7) separate times
by the Court without warning, without stated basis, and without
providing me an alternative means to present my position. In a separate
Zoom hearing, I was muted three (3) additional times while attempting
to present defense evidence. At the February 28, 2025 hearing, the
Court muted my microphone four (4) separate times. Muting a pro se
litigant repeatedly across multiple hearings is not legitimate
courtroom management; it is suppression of one party's voice.


G. Dismissive Treatment of Legal Arguments

    28. When I raised the issue of police report misattribution — a
factual error in a report upon which the Court had relied — Judge
McNeill responded by stating words to the effect of "quit nitpicking."
This dismissal of a substantive evidentiary challenge demonstrates
prejudgment.


H. Incarceration Without Proper Contempt Process

    29. Judge McNeill found me in contempt and ordered me jailed for a
total of 59 days without affording me the procedural protections
required for contempt proceedings resulting in incarceration:

        a. I was not provided adequate notice of the specific charges;
        b. I was denied my right to counsel under Mead v Batchlor, 435
           Mich 480; 460 NW2d 493 (1990), despite facing incarceration;
        c. I was not afforded a meaningful opportunity to present a
           defense or purge the contempt; and
        d. The findings did not meet the evidentiary standards required
           by In re Contempt of Dougherty, 429 Mich 81 (1987).

    30. As a direct result of this incarceration, I lost three (3)
jobs, compounding the financial harm and undermining my ability to meet
the child support obligation of $1,056.60 per month that the Court set
without an evidentiary hearing on my actual income.


I. Financial Barrier — $250 Bond

    31. Judge McNeill imposed a $250 bond as a condition for me to file
motions. No determination was made under MCR 2.625 that I am a
vexatious litigant. This functions as a financial barrier preventing me
from accessing the court.


J. Failure to Enforce Parenting Time

    32. Defendant has committed twenty-seven-plus (27+) documented
parenting time violations. Judge McNeill has taken zero (0) enforcement
actions on any of these violations — no show-cause orders, no contempt
findings, no make-up parenting time orders.

    33. On approximately April 15, 2025, Defendant explicitly refused
to facilitate make-up parenting time. The Court took no enforcement
action despite MCL 552.511b, which requires enforcement of missed
parenting time.


K. Child Support Set Without Evidentiary Hearing

    34. The Court set child support at $1,056.60 per month without
conducting an evidentiary hearing to determine my actual income,
expenses, or ability to pay. This amount was imposed and then used as a
basis for further contempt proceedings when I could not pay — creating
a cycle of incarceration that the Court itself set in motion.


L. Prior Disqualification Motion — Procedural Violation

    35. On September 25, 2024, I filed a prior Motion for
Disqualification. Judge McNeill denied this motion herself, stating:
"DENIED. No bias shown."

    36. Under MCR 2.003(D)(1), when a challenged judge denies a motion
for disqualification, the motion "shall be referred to the chief
judge" for a hearing and de novo determination. Judge McNeill did not
refer the motion. This procedural violation is independently
disqualifying.


M. Prior Representation of a Party

    37. During a hearing, Judge McNeill stated on the record: "I have
represented Party A in the past," establishing a prior professional
relationship with a party to this action. No recusal followed; no
waiver was sought.

differential treatment:

        a. Defendant's ex parte motions: Consistently granted, often

        b. Plaintiff's motions: Denied or not ruled upon, including

        c. Defendant's credibility: Accepted without cross-examination
