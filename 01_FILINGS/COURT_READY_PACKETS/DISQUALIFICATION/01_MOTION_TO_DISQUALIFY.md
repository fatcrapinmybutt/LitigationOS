IV. LEGAL GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

    45. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) provides that a judge is disqualified when
the judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney."

    46. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that the test is objective:
"whether a reasonable person, knowing all the relevant facts, would
find the judge's impartiality to be in reasonable question." Crampton v
Dep't of State, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975). The standard does not
require proof of actual bias; the appearance of impropriety is
sufficient. Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp, 248 Mich App 573, 597
(2001).

    47. The Supreme Court recognized in Cain v Dep't of Corrections,
451 Mich 470, 497 (1996), that "repeated instances of the same type of
conduct" can establish a pattern of bias warranting disqualification.
The United States Supreme Court held in Liteky v United States, 510 US
540, 555 (1994), that disqualification is required when judicial
rulings "reveal such a high degree of favoritism or antagonism as to
make fair judgment impossible."

    48. The pattern before this Court is not a matter of isolated
rulings. It is a directional, sustained course of conduct:

        DOCUMENTED PATTERN SUMMARY:
        ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
        | Category                                   | Finding            |
        |--------------------------------------------|--------------------|
        | Ex parte orders                            | 24 of 55 (44%),   |
        |                                            | all favor Def.     |
        | Ex parte orders on single day (Aug 8, 2025)| 5                  |
        | Disparate grant ratio                      | 1.9:1 favoring Def.|
        | On-record statements of hostility          | "liar," "crazy"    |
        | Blanket refusal to consider filings        | "Do not file any   |
        |                                            |  more"             |
        | Muting incidents (single hearing)          | 7                  |
        | Additional muting incidents                | 7+ across multiple |
        |                                            |  proceedings       |
        | Incarceration without adequate process     | 59 days total      |
        | Enforcement of 27+ violations by Def.      | 0                  |
        | Written best-interest findings (MCL 722.23)| 0                  |
        | Hearings in MCR 3.207(C)(5) 14-day window  | 0                  |
        | Bond imposed without MCR 2.625 finding     | $250               |
        ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

    49. Any one of these facts independently supports a finding of bias
under Crampton. Together, they compel the conclusion that Plaintiff
cannot receive fair and impartial treatment before Judge McNeill.


B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — Personal Knowledge / Appearance of Impropriety

    50. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) disqualifies a judge who has "personal
knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding" or
whose conduct creates a "serious risk of actual bias" under Caperton v
AT Massey Coal Co, 556 US 868 (2009), or fails to adhere to the
appearance of impropriety standard of Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of
Judicial Conduct.

    51. The Berry Family Connection. Judge McNeill is married to Cavan
John Berry. Ronald Berry, who is related to Cavan Berry, is the
domestic partner of Defendant Emily A. Watson. This chain of
relationships means the presiding judge has a familial connection to
the household of the opposing party. Canon 2 requires a judge to
"avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all of the
judge's activities." A judge who presides over a case involving the
domestic partner of her husband's relative cannot satisfy this
standard. The test is objective: would a reasonable person, knowing
these relationships, question the judge's impartiality? The answer is
plainly yes.

    52. The Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill Structural Conflict. Three judges
from the same law firm now control all aspects of one litigant's cases.
As the Supreme Court stated in In re Murchison, 349 US 133, 136
(1955): "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due
process." A tribunal is not "fair" when the presiding judge, the chief
judge who reviews disqualification motions, and the district court
judge handling related matters are all former law partners. They were
named partners — the three names on the door. A reasonable person would
conclude that asking Chief Judge Hoopes to decide whether his former
named partner should be disqualified is tantamount to asking a partner
to judge a partner.

    53. Ex Parte Review of Evidence. By directing staff to review USB
evidence outside a hearing (see ¶ 34), Judge McNeill acquired —
through her agents — personal knowledge of the contents of disputed
evidence, untested by cross-examination. This is precisely the kind of
extrajudicial knowledge acquisition that MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) was
designed to prevent.

    54. Prior Representation of a Party. Judge McNeill's on-the-record
disclosure — "I have represented Party A in the past" (see ¶ 36) —
establishes a prior attorney-client relationship with a party. Even if
remote in time, the appearance of impropriety standard does not require
proof of actual bias. Crampton, 395 Mich at 351.


C. Canon Violations as Independent Grounds

    55. Canon 1 — Integrity of the Judiciary. A judge who calls a party
"a liar" and "crazy," enters 44% of orders ex parte and uniformly
favoring one party, and refuses to read a party's filings does not
uphold the integrity of the judiciary.

    56. Canon 2 / Rule 2.11 — Appearance of Impropriety. The Berry
family connection and the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill law firm connection each
independently create an appearance of impropriety that "reasonable
minds" would perceive as compromising impartiality. In re Haley, 476
Mich 180, 193 (2006).

    57. Canon 3(A)(4) — Ex Parte Communications. Directing staff to
listen to USB evidence outside a hearing is an ex parte communication
concerning a pending proceeding, in direct violation of this Canon.

    58. Canon 3(B)(2) — Right to Be Heard. Muting a party seven times
in one hearing and imposing a blanket prohibition on future filings
denies the right to be heard according to law.


D. Due Process Violations

    59. In Caperton v AT Massey Coal Co, 556 US 868, 884 (2009), the
United States Supreme Court held that due process requires recusal when
"there is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and
reasonable perceptions." The Court adopted an objective standard: the
question is whether the probability of bias is "too high to be
constitutionally tolerable." Id. at 872.

    60. The record here establishes far more than a "serious risk." It
establishes a documented pattern of uniformly one-sided rulings, ex
parte conduct, hostility on the record, and procedural violations, all
directed against a single party. Under Caperton's objective standard,
this compels disqualification.

    61. The Michigan Constitution provides protection at least
coextensive with the Fourteenth Amendment. Mich Const 1963, Art 1,
§ 17. The totality of circumstances here — twenty-four ex parte
orders, zero enforcement of the other party's violations, on-the-record
hostility, seven mutings, a bond without procedural basis, a refusal
to even read future filings, 59 days incarceration — is overwhelming.
