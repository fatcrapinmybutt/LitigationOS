# STANDARD OF REVIEW

## Custody Determinations — Abuse of Discretion

This Court reviews custody and parenting time determinations for an abuse of discretion. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21 (2010); *Vodvarka v Grashorn*, 259 Mich App 559, 564 (2003). An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Kubicki v Sharpe*, 306 Mich App 525, 538 (2014); *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 85 (2010).

A trial court necessarily abuses its discretion when it fails to evaluate all twelve best interest factors under MCL 722.23 before modifying an established custodial environment. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879 (1994); *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004).

## Factual Findings — Clear Error

The trial court's factual findings are reviewed for clear error. A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to support it, the reviewing court on the entire record is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake was made. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 878; *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 323 (2006).

## Constitutional Questions — De Novo

Constitutional questions, including whether a party was deprived of due process or whether a fundamental right was violated, are reviewed de novo. *Harvey*, 470 Mich at 191. Whether the trial court's procedures satisfied the requirements of due process is a question of law reviewed de novo. *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 353 (1975).

## Judicial Disqualification — De Novo (Legal Standard) / Abuse of Discretion (Application)

Whether the legal standard for disqualification under MCR 2.003 was met is reviewed de novo. The application of the standard to the facts is reviewed for abuse of discretion. *In re Hathaway*, 464 Mich 672, 683 (2001); *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868, 881 (2009).

# ARGUMENT

## I. THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY SUSPENDING ALL PARENTING TIME VIA EX PARTE ORDER WITHOUT FINDINGS OF IMMINENT HARM

### A. The Legal Framework for Ex Parte Custody and Parenting Time Orders

Under Michigan law, a court may enter an ex parte order affecting custody or parenting time only upon a showing of specific facts demonstrating that there is an imminent and substantial risk of harm to the child's health, safety, or welfare. MCR 3.207(B)(1). The movant must present specific, verified facts — not conclusory allegations — establishing the emergency. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 23 (2010).

The rule requires more than general allegations of unfitness or vague safety concerns. *Id.* at 24. The court must make specific findings on the record as to why the emergency necessitates acting without notice and an opportunity to be heard. MCR 3.207(B)(1).

This procedural safeguard exists because "a parent's right to the custody and control of [his or her] children is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Before the government may deprive a parent of this fundamental right — even temporarily — due process requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard. *Id.*; *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976).

### B. The Ex Parte Orders of August 8, 2025 Failed to Satisfy MCR 3.207(B)

On August 8, 2025, the trial court entered five separate ex parte orders in a single day suspending all of Father's parenting time. (App-K.) These orders:

1. **Were entered without prior notice to Father.** Father received no advance notice that any ex parte proceeding would occur. He had no opportunity to be heard before the court eliminated his fundamental right to parent L.D.W. (App-K.)

2. **Were not supported by specific factual findings of imminent harm.** The orders do not contain the specific factual findings required by MCR 3.207(B)(1). There is no finding that L.D.W. faced any imminent danger to her health, safety, or welfare. (App-K.)

3. **Were not based on sworn affidavits or verified pleadings.** MCR 3.207(B)(1) contemplates that ex parte relief will be supported by specific facts presented under oath. The record does not reflect that any sworn affidavit or verified pleading was presented to support these orders. (App-K.)

### C. Vodvarka and Shade Require Reversal

In *Vodvarka v Grashorn*, 259 Mich App 559, 566 (2003), this Court held that before a court may modify parenting time, it must first determine whether the movant has established proper cause or a change of circumstances. The court must then conduct a best interest analysis under MCL 722.23. *Id.* at 567.

In *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17 (2010), this Court specifically addressed the requirements for ex parte orders affecting parenting time. The Court held that the trial court erred in entering an ex parte order modifying parenting time without specific factual findings of an emergency. *Id.* at 23–24.

Here, the trial court's August 8, 2025 ex parte orders suspended all of Father's parenting time without any of the procedural protections required by *Shade* and *Vodvarka*. This constitutes a clear abuse of discretion.

### D. The Ongoing Suspension Compounds the Error

The trial court's failure to promptly restore parenting time after the initial ex parte orders compounds the error. More than 227 days have passed since August 8, 2025, and Father has been denied all contact with L.D.W. This prolonged deprivation — without a proper evidentiary hearing establishing that Father poses any danger to L.D.W. — violates both MCL 722.27a and Father's fundamental constitutional rights.

MCL 722.27a(1) provides that "[p]arenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." MCL 722.27a(3) provides that parenting time "shall not be denied" except upon a showing by clear and convincing evidence that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. No such showing has been made here.

## II. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED DUE PROCESS BY CONDUCTING PROCEEDINGS WITHOUT PROPER NOTICE AND SERVICE UNDER MCR 2.107

### A. Due Process Requires Notice and an Opportunity to Be Heard

The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution provide that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. US Const Amend XIV; Mich Const 1963, Art 1, § 17.

At its core, due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976); *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 378 (1971).

In child custody cases, where the fundamental liberty interest in the parent-child relationship is at stake, the due process requirements are at their apex. *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982); *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

### B. Michigan Court Rules Mandate Specific Service Requirements

MCR 2.107 establishes the service requirements for pleadings and papers in Michigan courts. Under MCR 2.107(C)(1), service of papers must be made upon the party. MCR 2.107(C)(3) specifies the methods of service. These rules are not mere technicalities — they are the procedural mechanism by which due process is implemented.

### C. The Trial Court Failed to Ensure Proper Service

The record reflects that multiple orders were entered and proceedings conducted without Father receiving proper notice and service as required by MCR 2.107.

Most critically, the five ex parte orders of August 8, 2025 suspending all parenting time were entered without Father receiving any prior notice. (App-K.) The orders took effect immediately, depriving Father of all contact with L.D.W. before he had any knowledge of the proceedings.

## III. THE TRIAL COURT ERRED BY FAILING TO CONDUCT A BEST INTEREST ANALYSIS UNDER MCL 722.23 BEFORE MODIFYING CUSTODY

### A. The Mandatory Best Interest Analysis

Under Michigan law, before a court may modify a custody or parenting time order, it must evaluate all twelve best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23(a)–(l). *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 879 (1994); *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004). This is a mandatory requirement, not a discretionary one. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 879 ("The trial court should consider all the factors listed in MCL 722.23, and explicitly state its findings and conclusions with respect to each").

The twelve statutory factors are:

(a) The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.
(b) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.
(c) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care recognized and permitted under the laws of this state in place of medical care, and other material needs.
(d) The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.
(e) The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.
(f) The moral fitness of the parties involved.
(g) The mental and physical health of the parties involved.
(h) The home, school, and community record of the child.
(i) The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.
(j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents. A court may not consider negatively for the purposes of this factor any reasonable action taken by a parent to protect a child or that parent from sexual assault or domestic violence by the child's other parent.
(k) Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.
(l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

### B. The Trial Court Failed to Make Required Findings

The trial court's orders modifying custody and suspending parenting time do not contain specific findings on all twelve best interest factors. (App-A; App-K.) The Final Judgment of August 23, 2024, which awarded sole legal and sole physical custody to Mother, does not reflect a factor-by-factor analysis as required by *Fletcher*. (App-A.)

The ex parte orders of August 8, 2025 suspending all parenting time likewise contain no best interest analysis whatsoever. (App-K.)

### C. This Error Requires Reversal and Remand

The Michigan Supreme Court has been clear: "It is an error of law, necessarily an abuse of discretion, for a trial court to fail to explicitly consider each [best interest] factor." *Harvey*, 470 Mich at 192. When the trial court fails to make the required findings, the proper remedy is reversal and remand for a proper best interest hearing. *Fletcher*, 447 Mich at 889; *Demski v Petlick*, 309 Mich App 404, 427 (2015).

### D. Proper Cause / Change of Circumstances Threshold

Before reaching the best interest analysis, the trial court must first determine whether the movant has established proper cause or a change of circumstances sufficient to revisit the custody order. MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 566. A court that proceeds to modify custody without this threshold determination commits reversible error. *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 591 (2004).

The record does not reflect that the trial court made a proper cause or change of circumstances finding before entering the ex parte orders of August 8, 2025. (App-K.)

On September 22, 2025, the trial court ordered Father to undergo a HealthWest mental health assessment. (Order, Sept. 22, 2025, App-M.) Father complied with this order and completed the evaluation.

### B. The Evaluation Was Favorable

The HealthWest evaluation did not identify any safety concerns, mental health conditions, or factors that would preclude Father from safely exercising parenting time with L.D.W. (HealthWest Evaluation Report, [ANDREW_REQUIRED — provide date of HealthWest report and key findings].)

### C. The Court Disregarded Its Own Evaluation

Despite ordering the evaluation and receiving favorable results, the trial court continued to deny Father all parenting time. The court offered no explanation on the record for disregarding the evaluation it had itself ordered.

A trial court abuses its discretion when it disregards competent, relevant evidence without explanation. *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 716 (2008). Where the court orders an evaluation to assist in determining whether a parent poses a risk to the child, and the evaluation reveals no such risk, the court cannot simply ignore the results and continue depriving the parent of contact with the child.

Instead of crediting the professional HealthWest evaluation, the trial court appears to have relied on unsworn, unsubstantiated allegations to justify the continuing suspension of parenting time. Due process requires that custody and parenting time determinations be based on competent evidence, not speculation or untested allegations. MCR 3.210(C); *Santosky*, 455 US at 764.

## V. THE TRIAL JUDGE SHOULD BE DISQUALIFIED UNDER MCR 2.003 BASED ON THE BERRY-McNEILL FAMILY RELATIONSHIP

### A. The Legal Standard for Disqualification

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge:

> (a) Is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
>
> (b) Based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as combated in *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

The United States Supreme Court held in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), that due process is violated when the risk of bias is so substantial that it creates an unconstitutional "probability of bias." *Id.* at 884.

In *In re Hathaway*, 464 Mich 672, 683 (2001), the Michigan Supreme Court held that a judge must be disqualified under MCR 2.003 when a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would harbor legitimate doubts about the judge's ability to be impartial.

## VI. THE CUMULATIVE ERRORS DEPRIVED APPELLANT OF HIS FUNDAMENTAL CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHT TO PARENT

### A. The Fundamental Right to Parent

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This right is "far more precious than property rights." *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 758–59 (1982).

### B. Cumulative Error Doctrine

When multiple trial court errors are individually insufficient for reversal but collectively undermine the fairness of the proceedings, the cumulative error doctrine requires reversal. Each of the errors identified in this brief — the ex parte orders without required findings, the service deficiencies, the failure to conduct a best interest analysis, the disregard of favorable evaluations, and the disqualifying conflict of interest — compounds the others.

### C. The Combined Effect

The cumulative effect of the trial court's errors has been devastating. Father has been completely separated from his child L.D.W. for over 227 days. This separation was accomplished through:

1. **Ex parte orders** entered without the findings of imminent harm required by MCR 3.207(B) (Issue I);
2. **Service failures** that deprived Father of notice and an opportunity to be heard (Issue II);
3. **Failure to conduct a best interest analysis** as mandated by MCL 722.23 and *Fletcher* (Issue III);
4. **Disregard of favorable professional evaluations** ordered by the court itself (Issue IV);
5. **A presiding judge with an undisclosed family connection** to the opposing party's domestic partner (Issue V).

No single one of these errors, standing alone, might be dismissed as harmless. Together, they paint a picture of proceedings that were fundamentally unfair and that deprived Father of his constitutionally protected right to parent his child.

### D. Constitutional Analysis

Under the *Mathews v Eldridge* balancing test, the private interest at stake — the parent-child relationship — is "commanding." *Santosky*, 455 US at 758–59. The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used — ex parte orders, service failures, no best interest analysis, conflicted judge — is extremely high. The government's interest in protecting children, while legitimate, does not justify the wholesale deprivation of parental rights without the procedural safeguards required by law.
