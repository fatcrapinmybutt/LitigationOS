## VII. CONSTITUTIONAL FOUNDATION

### A. Substantive Due Process — Fundamental Parental Rights

33. The Fourteenth Amendment protects against governmental deprivation of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The United States Supreme Court has long recognized that parental rights are among the most fundamental of all liberty interests.

34. In *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000), the Supreme Court reaffirmed that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." The Court traced this principle through a century of precedent, from *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923), through *Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510 (1925), to *Wisconsin v. Yoder*, 406 U.S. 205 (1972).

35. In *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982), the Court held that "the fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State." A deprivation of all parenting time exceeding 230 consecutive days, in the face of overwhelming exculpatory evidence, constitutes a de facto severance of parental rights without the constitutional protections required by *Santosky*.

### B. Procedural Due Process

36. Before the government may deprive a person of a fundamental constitutional right, procedural due process requires, at minimum: (a) adequate notice of the allegations and proceedings; (b) an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner; (c) the right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses; and (d) a decision by a neutral and detached adjudicator. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).

37. None of these protections were afforded to Plaintiff before his parenting time was suspended. The suspension was entered ex parte on August 8, 2025, without notice, without hearing, and without any opportunity for Plaintiff to respond. This procedural failure renders the deprivation constitutionally infirm from its inception.

### C. Equal Protection

38. The Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires that similarly situated individuals be treated alike. The record in this case demonstrates a pattern of disparate treatment in the entry of ex parte orders that raises equal protection concerns under *Craig v. Boren*, 429 U.S. 190 (1976), and its progeny.

## VIII. MICHIGAN STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

### A. MCL 722.27a — Parenting Time Presumption

39. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time, providing that "[p]arenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." The statute creates a presumption that parenting time is in the child's best interest, which may be overcome only by clear and convincing evidence that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health.

40. MCL 722.27a(3) provides: "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

41. No such showing has been made. Every objective metric confirms that parenting time with Plaintiff poses no danger to L.D.W. The statutory presumption has not been rebutted and cannot be rebutted on this record.

### B. MCR 3.207 — Mandatory Parenting Time Procedures

42. MCR 3.207(B) requires that when parenting time is contested, the court shall consider the factors in MCL 722.27a(7). Michigan courts have consistently held that ex parte custody and parenting time orders are disfavored and may only be entered upon a showing of immediate and irreparable harm to the child. *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247 (2009). The procedures mandated by MCR 3.207 were not followed in this case.

### C. MCL 722.27a(7) — Parenting Time Factors

43. Application of the parenting time factors uniformly supports restoration:
