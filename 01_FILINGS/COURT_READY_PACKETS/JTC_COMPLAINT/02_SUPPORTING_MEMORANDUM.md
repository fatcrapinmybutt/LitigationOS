## III. DETAILED ANALYSIS: EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS

### A. The March 3, 2025 Rusco-Hooker Warrant Request

On March 3, 2025, at 8:51 AM, Pamela Rusco sent the following email to Prosecutor Jamie Hooker:

> "Good Morning Jamie, Andrew Pigors was ordered to turn himself in to the Jail by 5pm on Friday to serve his 14 days in jail for the PPO Violation. I confirmed with the jail that he is not currently lodged. Can you please issue a warrant for him? Attached are both the order after hearing and the jail mittimus."

This communication violates Canon 3(A)(4) for multiple independent reasons:

1. **Chambers-prosecution contact.** The communication was between the judge's staff and the prosecution, outside the presence of the defense. *See Ethics Opinion JI-134* (a judge is responsible for the improper ex parte contacts of court staff).

2. **Investigative function.** Rusco "confirmed with the jail" that Father was not lodged — a substantive investigative act. Judicial staff verifying a defendant's custody status and then requesting law enforcement action based on that verification is prosecutorial, not ministerial.

3. **Warrant instigation.** Chambers staff initiated a warrant request — a prosecutorial action — against one party. The judge's office functioned as an extension of the prosecution.

### B. The August 5, 2025 USB Recording

Judge McNeill admitted on the record: "Yes, I had my staff listen to it." The "it" was a USB recording submitted by Mother through the clerk's office. The recording was made by Lori Watson (Mother's mother) without Father's consent, potentially violating MCL 750.539c (eavesdropping).

The ex parte handling is dispositive: substantive evidence was received and evaluated by the judge's staff outside the presence of both parties, without authentication under MRE 901, without chain-of-custody verification, and without opportunity for objection. Canon 3(A)(4) prohibits the judge from even "consider[ing]" ex parte communications, let alone directing staff to analyze them.

### C. The HealthWest Evaluator Letter

Prior to the September 11, 2025 HealthWest assessment, Judge McNeill sent a letter describing Father's behaviors as "several incidents over the past two years of court proceedings which are suggestive of a mental health disorder." This constitutes:

1. An **ex parte communication** with a prospective evaluator — sent without Father's knowledge or opportunity to respond;
2. **Prejudicial framing** — the letter characterized Father negatively before the evaluation began;
3. An attempt to **override** the favorable September 5, 2025 HealthWest evaluation (H0002) by biasing the replacement evaluation.

### D. The Subpoena Coordination

A HealthWest clinical chart memo documents that Rusco called to request clinical testimony and would "email us the subpoenas." MCR 2.506 requires formal service of subpoenas on all parties. Arranging clinical testimony through telephone calls and emails between chambers staff and the provider — without notice to the parties — constitutes ex parte coordination of evidence presentation.

### E. Advance Knowledge of Ex Parte Orders

On August 7, 2025, Albert Watson told NSPD Officer Ella Randall (Badge #47437, Case NS2505044): "they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son."

This statement demonstrates:

1. **Advance knowledge** of the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders — knowledge that the orders would be entered before they were requested or entered;
2. **Pre-coordination** — the Watson family was staging the factual predicate for ex parte relief they already knew would be granted;
3. **Police report as manufactured predicate** — the "incident" was being documented specifically to support an ex parte motion.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS: DENIAL OF DUE PROCESS

### A. The August 8, 2025 Catastrophe

On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five separate ex parte orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. The orders:

- Suspended all parenting time;
- Were entered without any notice to Father;
- Were entered without any hearing;
- Were entered without findings of immediate and irreparable harm as required by MCR 3.207(B);
- Were based on Mother's emergency motion — which was unsworn and predicated on inadmissible hearsay;
- Were entered the day after Albert Watson told police "they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order."

Prior to August 8, Father had exercised equal (50/50) parenting time for **438 consecutive days** following the July 17, 2024 trial. The court itself had found "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child." The complete termination of contact without notice or hearing — and the premeditated nature revealed by Albert Watson's statement — constitutes a profound denial of due process.

### B. The 59-Day Incarceration

| Date | Sentence | Basis | Procedural Deficiency |
|------|----------|-------|----------------------|
| Feb. 28, 2025 | 14 days | PPO violation | Simultaneously dismissed underlying matter; mutually exclusive dispositions |
| Nov. 26, 2025 | 45 days | Sending birthday/holiday messages via AppClose | Disproportionate; AppClose is court-sanctioned app; no *Dougherty* protections |
| **Total** | **59 days** | | |

The 45-day sentence is particularly egregious: Father was incarcerated for sending birthday and holiday messages to L.D.W. through AppClose — the court-sanctioned co-parenting application. The messages were sent by a father to his child on the child's birthday. The sentence was imposed without:

- The procedural protections of *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987);
- The right to counsel required by *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480 (1990);
- Any assessment of proportionality;
- Any consideration of purge conditions.

The incarceration cost Father three jobs and deepened a child support arrearage that was itself based on income set without an evidentiary hearing.

### C. The Child Support Trap

The punitive cycle operates as follows:

1. Judge McNeill set child support at **$1,056.60/month** without an evidentiary hearing on actual income;
2. Father could not pay the amount because it exceeded his actual earnings;
3. Arrearage accumulated;
4. Judge McNeill found contempt based on the arrearage;
5. Father was incarcerated;
6. Incarceration caused job loss (three jobs);
7. Job loss deepened the arrearage;
8. The cycle repeats.

A disinterested judge would have held an evidentiary hearing before setting support. A disinterested judge would have considered ability to pay before incarcerating for non-payment. The failure to do either — combined with the undisclosed conflict — reveals a punitive mechanism, not a child support mechanism.

### D. Orders Before Response Period

Multiple orders were entered before the MCR 2.107 response period expired. Under MCR 2.107, a party has 21 days to respond to a motion (or 28 days if served by mail). Entering orders before this period expires denies the non-moving party the opportunity to respond — a fundamental due process violation.
