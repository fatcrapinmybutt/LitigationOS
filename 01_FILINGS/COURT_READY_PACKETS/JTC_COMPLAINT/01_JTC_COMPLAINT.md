## PART II: NATURE OF COMPLAINT — SIX CATEGORIES OF MISCONDUCT

I allege that Judge Jenny L. McNeill has engaged in a sustained pattern of judicial misconduct in violation of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct (Canons 1–3), the Michigan Constitution (Const. 1963, art. 6, § 30), and the Michigan Court Rules (MCR 9.104, 9.205), organized into the following six categories:

### Category 1: Failure to Disclose Conflicts of Interest

Judge McNeill has failed to disclose multiple disqualifying conflicts of interest in violation of Canon 1, Canon 2(B), Canon 3(C)(1), and MCR 2.003:

**A. The Berry-McNeill Familial Connection.**

Judge McNeill is married to attorney Cavan John Berry, a licensed Michigan attorney who practices family law in Muskegon County. Ronald Berry — on information and belief, a family member of Cavan Berry — is in a domestic relationship with Defendant Emily A. Watson. This creates a direct familial chain between the presiding judge and a party:

```
JUDGE JENNY L. McNEILL (P58235)
          │
      [married to]
          │
CAVAN JOHN BERRY (attorney, family law, Muskegon County)
          │
      [family member — on information and belief]
          │
RONALD BERRY (non-attorney, Muskegon County)
          │
      [romantic/domestic partner — on information and belief]
          │
EMILY A. WATSON (DEFENDANT — party in Case No. 2024-001507-DC)
```

Judge McNeill never disclosed any aspect of this relationship during 290+ days of proceedings — not when she suspended all parenting time, not when she ordered Complainant incarcerated for 59 days, not when she set child support without an evidentiary hearing.

**B. The Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill Law Firm Connection.**

Judge McNeill was a named partner at "Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill," located at 435 Whitehall Road, North Muskegon. Her former law partners now hold the following judicial positions:

- **Kenneth Hoopes** — Current Chief Judge, 14th Circuit Court. As Chief Judge, Hoopes controls judicial assignments for the circuit.
- **Maria Ladas-Hoopes** (Kenneth Hoopes' wife) — Judge, 60th District Court, Muskegon County.

Three judges from the same small law firm now occupy the bench in Muskegon County — two in the 14th Circuit and one in the 60th District Court. When Complainant's MCR 2.003(D)(1) disqualification motion was denied by Judge McNeill without referral to the Chief Judge, the referral would have gone to Kenneth Hoopes — her former law partner. This represents a structural conflict that makes the MCR 2.003 disqualification process non-functional within the 14th Circuit.

**C. Prior Representation Admission.**

Judge McNeill stated on the record: "I have represented Party A in the past." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge has been counsel or adviser to a party. Despite this on-record admission, Judge McNeill did not recuse herself.

### Category 2: Ex Parte Communications

Judge McNeill engaged in a pattern of ex parte communications and off-record evidence handling in violation of Canon 3(A)(4):

- **Warrant request through chambers.** On March 3, 2025, Pamela Rusco (Judge McNeill's judicial secretary) emailed Prosecutor Jamie Hooker: "Good Morning Jamie, Andrew Pigors was ordered to turn himself in to the Jail by 5pm on Friday to serve his 14 days in jail for the PPO Violation. I confirmed with the jail that he is not currently lodged. Can you please issue a warrant for him?" Rusco independently investigated Father's incarceration status by calling the jail — a substantive investigative act, not a ministerial function. This constitutes an ex parte communication between chambers and the prosecution outside the presence of the defense.

- **Off-record USB evidence.** On August 5, 2025, Mother submitted a USB recording to the clerk's office. Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Yes, I had my staff listen to it." The judge received and considered substantive evidence outside the presence of the parties. The recording was made by Lori Watson (Mother's mother) without Father's consent, potentially violating MCL 750.539c.

- **Evaluator bias letter.** Prior to the September 11, 2025 HealthWest assessment, Judge McNeill sent a letter to HealthWest describing Father's behaviors as "several incidents over the past two years of court proceedings which are suggestive of a mental health disorder." This letter was sent without Father's knowledge or consent.

- **Subpoena coordination through chambers.** A HealthWest clinical chart memo documents that Rusco called to arrange clinical witness testimony and would "email us the subpoenas." Subpoena arrangements were made via telephone and email between chambers staff and the clinical provider — not through formal court filings served on the parties per MCR 2.506.

- **Advance coordination of ex parte relief.** On August 7, 2025, Albert Watson (Mother's father) told NSPD Officer Ella Randall (Badge #47437, Case NS2505044) that "they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son." This statement demonstrates advance knowledge of the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders.

### Category 3: Denial of Due Process

Judge McNeill systematically denied Father's right to due process in violation of Canon 3(A)(7) and the Fourteenth Amendment:

- **Five ex parte orders in one day.** On August 8, 2025, five separate ex parte orders were entered in a single day suspending all parenting time, without notice to Father and without the findings of immediate and irreparable harm required by MCR 3.207(B).

- **59 days incarceration.** Father was incarcerated for a cumulative 59 days — 14 days (February 28, 2025) and 45 days (November 26, 2025) — without the procedural protections required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987), or the right to counsel required by *Mead v Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480 (1990). The 45-day sentence was for sending birthday and holiday messages through AppClose, the court-sanctioned co-parenting application.

- **Mutually exclusive orders.** On February 28, 2025, Judge McNeill simultaneously imposed 14 days jail for contempt AND dismissed the underlying matter — dispositions that are mutually exclusive, as dismissal extinguishes the court's enforcement authority.

- **571+ days parent-child separation.** Through a combination of ex parte suspensions, non-enforcement of parenting-time orders, and judicial inaction on Plaintiff's motions, Father has been separated from L.D.W. for a cumulative period exceeding 571 days.

- **Child support without hearing.** Child support was set at $1,056.60/month without an evidentiary hearing on actual income. The resulting arrearage was then used as the predicate for contempt and incarceration — creating a punitive cycle.

- **Orders before response period.** Orders were entered before the expiration of the MCR 2.107 response period, denying Father the opportunity to respond.

### Category 4: Bias and Prejudgment

Judge McNeill demonstrated systematic bias favoring Defendant Watson in violation of Canon 3(A)(1) and Canon 3(B)(5):

- **Blanket motion denial.** On July 16, 2024, Judge McNeill denied six separate motions filed by Father in a single summary order without individual analysis or statement of reasons, violating MCR 2.517.

- **Attorney warning of predetermined outcome.** On February 28, 2025, court-appointed attorney Mandi Martini emailed Father: "I'm pretty sure Judge McNeil is not in a good mood today and she doesn't want to hear from you unless asked. I'm sure you do not want to spend the next week in jail on a contempt charge for speaking out in court when you weren't asked." An officer of the court anticipated a predetermined outcome and warned Father that speaking in his own defense would result in incarceration.

- **Muting Father during hearings.** During the Show Cause #5 hearing (November 15, 2025), Father was muted three or more times while attempting to present argument and evidence. Father was prevented from presenting a police report regarding L.D.W.'s condition.

- **Rejection of favorable evaluation.** Father obtained a favorable HealthWest evaluation (H0002) as directed by the court. Judge McNeill rejected the evaluation and characterized it as a "ghost" mental health evaluation — a prejudicial characterization without evidentiary basis.

- **Asymmetric enforcement.** Zero enforcement actions against Mother for documented parenting-time violations. Maximum punishment against Father for co-parenting messages. Defendant's motions promptly addressed and typically granted; Complainant's motions delayed, denied, or left without ruling.

- **MCR 2.003 self-denial.** Father's September 25, 2024 disqualification motion under MCR 2.003 was denied by Judge McNeill herself with only "DENIED. No bias shown." Under MCR 2.003(D)(1), when a judge does not voluntarily disqualify, the motion *shall* be referred to the chief judge. No referral was made.

### Category 5: Improper Judicial Conduct

Judge McNeill engaged in conduct unbecoming a judicial officer in violation of Canon 3(A)(3):

- **"Quit nitpicking."** When Father raised that police had misattributed a statement, Judge McNeill stated "quit nitpicking" — dismissing a legitimate evidentiary concern.

- **"Do not file any more."** Judge McNeill stated on the record: "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." She further characterized Father's motions as "harassment to Defendant and to the Court, along with an abuse of the Court system." No vexatious-litigant finding was made under MCL 600.2591. This constitutes a blanket refusal to perform the judicial function.

- **$250 filing barrier.** A $250 bond/deposit requirement was imposed as a condition for Father to file motions, without statutory authority, without a vexatious-litigant finding, and without assessment of ability to pay. *See Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971).

- **International travel during contact suspension.** While Father had zero contact with L.D.W., Judge McNeill granted Mother permission to take L.D.W. out of the country (October 20–November 15, 2025).

### Category 6: Failure to Follow Law

Judge McNeill failed to apply mandatory legal provisions:

- **MCL 722.23 best-interest factors.** Custody modifications were entered without the 12-factor best-interest analysis required by MCL 722.23.

- **MCR 3.210 custody proceedings.** Custody modifications were entered without the notice and hearing procedures required by MCR 3.210.

- **MCR 3.207(B) emergency relief.** The August 8, 2025 suspension was entered without findings of immediate and irreparable harm.

- **MCR 2.003(D)(1) disqualification referral.** The disqualification motion was denied without mandatory referral to the chief judge.

- **MCR 8.119 record requirements.** Evidence was routed through chambers rather than the Clerk of Court.

- **MCR 2.517 findings of fact.** Multiple orders were entered without required findings of fact or conclusions of law.

- **MCL 600.2950 PPO factors.** PPO orders were entered without the statutory findings required by MCL 600.2950.

- **MCR 2.107 service and notice.** Orders were entered before the expiration of response periods.

## PART IV: CANON VIOLATION ANALYSIS

### I. Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

**Canon 1 provides:** *"An independent and honorable judiciary is indispensable to justice in our society. A judge should participate in establishing, maintaining, and enforcing, and should personally observe, high standards of conduct so that the integrity and independence of the judiciary may be preserved."*

**Violation 1.1: Berry-McNeill Undisclosed Conflict.**
Judge McNeill is married to Cavan John Berry. Ronald Berry — on information and belief, Cavan Berry's family member — is in a domestic relationship with Defendant. Judge McNeill never disclosed this connection during 290+ days of proceedings. The concealment undermines the foundational premise that judges decide cases on law and facts, not personal relationships.

**Violation 1.2: Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill Judicial Network.**
Three judges from the same small law firm now occupy the Muskegon County bench. This creates, at minimum, an appearance that the bench operates as a unified entity. The MCR 2.003 disqualification process is structurally non-functional because referral goes to Chief Judge Kenneth Hoopes — McNeill's former law partner.

**Violation 1.3: Prior Representation Without Recusal.**
Judge McNeill admitted on the record: "I have represented Party A in the past." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) required recusal. None occurred.

**Applicable Standards:** Canon 1; MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)–(b); *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009).

### II. Canon 2 — Avoidance of Impropriety and Appearance of Impropriety

**Canon 2(A) provides:** *"A judge should respect and comply with the law and should act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary."*

**Canon 2(B) provides:** *"A judge should not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment."*

**Violation 2.1: Ex Parte Order Rate.**
Judge McNeill's ex parte order rate of approximately 44% — compared to a family division baseline of less than 10–12% — is approximately 3.65 times the expected rate. The database documents 3,697 ex parte violations. Ex parte relief is extraordinary; a sustained 44% rate is inconsistent with legitimate emergency practice.

**Violation 2.2: Same-Day Ex Parte Orders.**
Five ex parte orders on August 8, 2025 suspending a fundamental constitutional right. Same-day PPO on December 3, 2023. These dispositions on matters of constitutional dimension create a severe appearance of impropriety.

**Violation 2.3: Off-Record Evidence Handling.**
Evidence routed through chambers staff rather than the Clerk of Court per MCR 8.119. USB recordings reviewed ex parte. Favorable mental health evaluation suppressed from the record.

**Violation 2.4: Chambers-Prosecution Coordination.**
Rusco's March 3, 2025 warrant request to Prosecutor Hooker — outside the presence of the defense — creates a severe appearance of impropriety. *See Ethics Opinion JI-134.*

**Applicable Standards:** Canon 2(A), (B); *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996).

### III. Canon 3 — Impartial Performance of Judicial Duties

**Canon 3(A)(4) provides:** *"A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications."*

**Canon 3(A)(7) provides:** *"A judge shall accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding . . . the right to be heard according to law."*

**Canon 3(C)(1) provides:** *"A judge should disqualify himself or herself in a proceeding in which the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."*

**Violation 3.1: Muting Father During Hearings.**
Father was muted three or more times during the November 15, 2025 hearing. This is functionally equivalent to removing a party from the courtroom. Canon 3(A)(7).

**Violation 3.2: Hostile On-Record Statements.**
"Quit nitpicking." "Ghost" mental health evaluation. "Do not file any more." These demonstrate hostility toward a party-litigant, not the patient and dignified demeanor required by Canon 3(A)(3).

**Violation 3.3: Rejection of Court-Ordered Evaluation.**
Judge McNeill rejected the favorable HealthWest evaluation (H0002) and biased the replacement evaluation with a prejudicial letter to the evaluator.

**Violation 3.4: Disproportionate Incarceration.**
59 cumulative days of incarceration — including 45 days for birthday messages through a court-sanctioned app — while zero enforcement against Mother for documented violations. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987).

**Violation 3.5: Failure to Disqualify.**
The Berry-McNeill connection alone triggers Canon 3(C)(1). Combined with the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill firm network, MCR 2.003(B)'s mandatory sua sponte disqualification duty was triggered. *See Caperton*, 556 US at 881.

**Violation 3.6: Canon 3(C)(1)(d)(i) — Spouse's Family Interest.**
Ronald Berry's interest in the outcome is concrete and substantial: custody determinations affect household composition; parenting-time schedules determine child's presence; child support payments benefit the household; protective orders remove obstacles to the Berry-Watson household's preferred arrangement.

## PART V: GROUNDS FOR DISCIPLINE

The described conduct constitutes grounds for discipline under MCR 9.104, which provides that discipline may be imposed for:

**(a) Misconduct in office** — The pattern of undisclosed conflicts, ex parte communications, and systematic due process violations constitutes misconduct in the performance of judicial duties.

**(b) Persistent failure to perform judicial duties** — The blanket refusal to consider filings ("I will not look at it"), the failure to apply mandatory statutory factors (MCL 722.23), and the failure to refer the disqualification motion constitute persistent failure to perform duties.

**(c) Conduct clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice** — A judge who conceals a familial connection to a party for 290+ days while exercising the power to incarcerate, sever parent-child bonds, and impose financial obligations engages in conduct that undermines public confidence in the judiciary.

**(d) Habitual intemperance** — The pattern of hostile statements, retaliatory escalation, and exercise of contempt power as a punitive rather than remedial tool constitutes an abuse of judicial authority.
