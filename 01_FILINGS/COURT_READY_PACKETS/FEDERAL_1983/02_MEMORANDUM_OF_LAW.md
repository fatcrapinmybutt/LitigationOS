## III. ARGUMENT

### A. Judicial Immunity Does Not Protect Defendant McNeill

Defendants will undoubtedly raise judicial immunity as a defense to liability under § 1983. This defense fails for two independent reasons.

#### 1. Non-Judicial Acts: Failure to Disclose a Mandatory Conflict

Judicial immunity attaches only to acts that are "judicial in nature" — that is, acts performed in the judge's "judicial capacity." *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11 (1991). The Supreme Court has drawn a clear line between judicial acts and administrative/ministerial acts:

> "[A]dministrative decisions, even though they may be essential to the very functioning of the courts, have not similarly been regarded as judicial acts." *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 229 (1988).

The duty to disclose a disqualifying conflict of interest under MCR 2.003(C)(1) and Canons 2 and 3 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct is a **mandatory, ministerial, and administrative duty** — not a judicial act. It does not involve the resolution of disputes between parties. It does not require the exercise of judicial discretion. It is a precondition to the lawful exercise of judicial authority, not an exercise of that authority itself.

Judge McNeill's failure to disclose her spousal relationship to Cavan John Berry — a relative of the opposing party's domestic partner — is therefore not protected by judicial immunity.

#### 2. Acts in the Clear Absence of All Jurisdiction

Even for acts that are judicial in nature, immunity is overcome where "the judge acts in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Mireles*, 502 U.S. at 12; *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 357 (1978).

A judge who is mandatorily disqualified from a case due to a familial conflict of interest acts in the **clear absence of all jurisdiction** when she proceeds to adjudicate that case without disclosure. The disqualification is not discretionary — it is automatic upon the existence of the disqualifying relationship. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) provides that disqualification is required where the judge has "a personal bias or prejudice concerning a party or a party's lawyer." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) further provides that disqualification is required where the judge's impartiality "might reasonably be questioned," including where the judge's spouse has a relationship to a party.

Judge McNeill was mandatorily disqualified. She had no jurisdiction to preside. Every order she entered — including the five ex parte orders of August 8, 2025, and the contempt orders resulting in 59 days of incarceration — was entered in the clear absence of all jurisdiction.

#### 3. The Conspiracy Exception

When a judge conspires with private parties to deprive a person of constitutional rights, the judge loses immunity for acts taken in furtherance of the conspiracy. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 28 (1980) ("[A] judicial officer who acts in concert with others who do not have judicial immunity is also not immune from suit."). While this exception is most directly applicable to the private co-conspirators (it allows § 1983 claims against them), the conspiracy itself demonstrates that McNeill's actions were not the detached, impartial exercises of judgment that immunity is designed to protect.

### B. Private Defendants Acted Under Color of State Law

A private party acts under color of state law when she is a "willful participant in joint activity with the State or its agents." *Adickes v. S.H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144, 152 (1970). More specifically, "private persons, jointly engaged with state officials in [a] challenged action, are acting 'under color' of law for purposes of § 1983 actions." *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. at 27-28.

The Supreme Court's framework in *Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 U.S. 922 (1982), establishes a two-part test: (1) the deprivation must be caused by the exercise of some right or privilege created by the state, or by a person for whom the state is responsible; and (2) the party charged with the deprivation must be a person who may fairly be said to be a state actor.

**Defendant Watson** satisfies both prongs. She invoked and manipulated state judicial power — filing fabricated PPO and custody motions, submitting ex parte evidence, and coordinating with judicial officers — to deprive Plaintiff of fundamental rights. She did not merely invoke a legal process; she corrupted it through conspiracy with a conflicted judge.

**Defendant Berry** satisfies both prongs. He made direct ex parte contacts with the court, exploiting his familial relationship with the presiding judge's spouse. The temporal coordination pattern — Watson files, McNeill rules, Berry files within 48 hours — demonstrates joint activity with the state.

**Defendant Barnes** (P55406) satisfies both prongs as Watson's attorney who facilitated the ex parte strategy and weaponized court filings. An attorney who conspires with a judge to deprive a party of due process acts under color of state law. *Tower v. Glover*, 467 U.S. 914 (1984) (holding that public defenders can be liable under § 1983 for conspiring to deprive rights); *see also Dennis*, 449 U.S. at 27 (private parties jointly engaged with state officials are state actors).

### C. The Berry-McNeill Familial Conflict Establishes §1985 Conspiracy

To state a claim under 42 U.S.C. § 1985(3), Plaintiff must allege: (1) a conspiracy; (2) for the purpose of depriving a person of the equal protection of the laws; (3) an act in furtherance of the conspiracy; and (4) an injury or deprivation of rights. *Griffin v. Breckenridge*, 403 U.S. 88, 102-03 (1971).

In the Sixth Circuit, a § 1985(3) claim also requires "some racial, or perhaps otherwise class-based, invidiously discriminatory animus behind the conspirators' action." *Id.* at 102. Plaintiff satisfies this requirement as an unmarried, *pro se* father — a class that has been treated with systematic hostility in the family court system, subjected to differential treatment, heightened scrutiny, and punitive measures not applied to similarly situated custodial mothers.

The conspiracy is established by:

1. **The familial link**: Judge McNeill → Cavan Berry (spouse) → Ronald Berry (relative) → Emily Watson (domestic partner). This chain creates both motive and opportunity.

2. **Temporal coordination**: The documented pattern — Watson files → McNeill rules same day → Berry files within 48 hours — demonstrates concerted action, not coincidence.

3. **Premeditated fraud**: Albert Watson's August 7, 2025 statement to police — "they want this documented so Emily can get Ex Parte order" — one day before five ex parte orders issued.

4. **Ex parte channels**: Watson's submission of a USB drive through the FOC referee, bypassing Plaintiff's right to notice and response, with immediate judicial action.

5. **Outcome asymmetry**: Every ruling favored Watson/Berry; every ruling harmed Plaintiff. Seven PPO show causes initiated, four dismissed (demonstrating baseless filings), with escalating punishment on later ones.

### D. Fundamental Parental Rights Are a Protected Liberty Interest

The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment:

- *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000) ("[T]he interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court.");
- *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982) (parental rights are a "fundamental liberty interest");
- *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972) (father's interest in children "undeniably warrants deference and, absent a powerful countervailing interest, protection").

Because a fundamental liberty interest is at stake, the deprivation must be narrowly tailored to serve a compelling state interest and accompanied by adequate procedural protections. Here, Plaintiff received **no process at all** for the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders — no notice, no hearing, no findings, and no evidence of endangerment. The court-ordered HealthWest evaluation subsequently confirmed what no process could have determined: Plaintiff posed zero danger.

Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), the procedural due process analysis weighs: (1) the private interest (the parent-child relationship — maximum weight); (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation (maximum — no process was provided); and (3) the government's interest (minimal — no emergency existed, and the HealthWest evaluation confirmed no danger). All three factors favor Plaintiff.

42 U.S.C. § 1986 imposes liability on any person who, "having knowledge that any of the wrongs conspired to be done, and mentioned in [§ 1985], are about to be committed, and having power to prevent or aid in preventing the commission of the same, neglects or refuses so to do."

Chief Judge Hoopes satisfies each element:

1. **Knowledge**: Hoopes was McNeill's former law partner at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill. In the small Muskegon County legal community, the Berry family connections were known or readily ascertainable. Plaintiff also raised concerns about the conflict in court filings accessible to the Chief Judge.

2. **Power to prevent**: As Chief Judge, Hoopes has the statutory and administrative authority to: (a) investigate judicial conflicts of interest; (b) reassign cases when a judge is disqualified; (c) implement conflict screening procedures; and (d) take disciplinary or corrective action against judges who fail to disclose conflicts.

3. **Neglect or refusal**: Despite knowledge and power, Hoopes did nothing. He did not investigate. He did not reassign. He did not implement conflict screening. He allowed the conspiracy to continue and the deprivation of Plaintiff's rights to escalate.

Moreover, Hoopes's inaction was not neutral — he had a personal stake in preserving the status quo, as he is McNeill's former law partner and the disclosure of her conflict could reflect adversely on the shared firm history. His own wife, Judge Ladas-Hoopes, was simultaneously presiding over a proceeding against Plaintiff, creating a multilayered structural conflict.

### F. Younger Abstention Does Not Apply

Younger abstention, which counsels federal courts to abstain from interfering with ongoing state proceedings, is subject to three recognized exceptions. All three apply here.

#### 1. Bad Faith and Harassment (*Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124 (1975))

The state proceedings are infected with bad faith: a judge with an undisclosed familial conflict entered ex parte orders without notice, incarcerated Plaintiff for constitutionally protected speech, and suppressed favorable evidence. The premeditated coordination — Albert Watson's admission to police one day before five ex parte orders — demonstrates that the proceedings were used as instruments of harassment, not legitimate adjudication.

#### 2. Patently Unconstitutional State Proceedings

The state proceedings are patently unconstitutional because:
- (a) The presiding judge has a mandatory disqualifying conflict that renders all her orders void for want of jurisdiction;
- (b) Plaintiff was deprived of fundamental liberty interests through ex parte orders without any notice or hearing;
- (c) Constitutionally protected speech was punished with incarceration;
- (d) Favorable, court-ordered evidence was suppressed.

#### 3. No Adequate Opportunity to Raise Federal Claims

The structural conflict — three judges from one law firm controlling all courts in Muskegon County — eliminates any adequate opportunity to raise federal claims in state court. Plaintiff cannot obtain a disinterested hearing because:
- (a) Judge McNeill has the conflict;
- (b) Chief Judge Hoopes, who would reassign the case, is McNeill's former law partner;
- (c) Judge Ladas-Hoopes, Hoopes's wife, is presiding over a related case against Plaintiff;
- (d) A $250 bond requirement blocks motion practice;
- (e) Plaintiff's motions to disqualify have been denied or ignored.

The Sixth Circuit has recognized that abstention is inappropriate where "the state proceeding was brought in bad faith or with the purpose of harassing the federal plaintiff." *Fieger v. Thomas*, 74 F.3d 740, 750 (6th Cir. 1996).

### G. The Domestic Relations Exception Does Not Bar This Action

The domestic relations exception is narrowly construed and does not bar § 1983 claims arising from state family court proceedings. The Sixth Circuit has squarely held:

> "Section 1983 claims based on the constitutionality of the processes and procedures employed by state courts in domestic relations cases are not barred by the domestic relations exception." *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279, 291 (6th Cir. 1998).

Plaintiff does not seek a custody determination. He does not ask this Court to determine who should have custody of L.D.W. Rather, Plaintiff challenges the **process** — or complete absence thereof — by which his constitutional rights were violated. This is precisely the type of claim the Sixth Circuit has held is cognizable in federal court. *See also Pittman v. Cuyahoga Cnty. Dep't of Children & Family Servs.*, 640 F.3d 716, 724 (6th Cir. 2011); *Ankenbrandt v. Richards*, 504 U.S. 689, 704 (1992).

### H. Rooker-Feldman Does Not Divest This Court of Jurisdiction

The Rooker-Feldman doctrine bars federal district courts from exercising appellate jurisdiction over state court judgments. *Rooker v. Fidelity Trust Co.*, 263 U.S. 413 (1923); *District of Columbia Court of Appeals v. Feldman*, 460 U.S. 462 (1983).

This doctrine does not apply here because Plaintiff does not seek to overturn any state court judgment. He challenges the **constitutional adequacy of the process** used to reach those judgments — specifically, the undisclosed conflict of interest, the ex parte orders without notice, and the suppression of evidence. These are **independent federal claims**, not disguised appeals.

The Supreme Court clarified in *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp.*, 544 U.S. 280, 284 (2005), that Rooker-Feldman is "confined to cases" brought by "state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments" and "inviting district court review and rejection of those judgments." Plaintiff does not invite this Court to review the merits of any custody determination. He asserts that the entire process was constitutionally defective from inception due to the structural conflict, and he seeks damages and prospective relief under federal civil rights statutes.
