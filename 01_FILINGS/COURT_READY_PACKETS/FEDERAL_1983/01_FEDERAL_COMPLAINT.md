6\. This Court has subject matter jurisdiction pursuant to **28 U.S.C. § 1331** (federal question jurisdiction) because this action arises under the Constitution and laws of the United States, specifically 42 U.S.C. §§ 1983, 1985(3), and 1986.

7\. 8\. This Court has supplemental jurisdiction over any state-law claims pursuant to **28 U.S.C. § 1367**, as they arise from the same nucleus of operative facts as the federal claims.

9\. 10\. **Rooker-Feldman Doctrine.** This action does not seek to appeal or overturn the substance of any state court custody determination. Plaintiff challenges the *complete absence of constitutionally required process* and the *undisclosed structural conflict of interest* by which Defendants deprived him of fundamental liberty interests. 11\. **Domestic Relations Exception.** This is not a domestic relations case. Plaintiff does not seek a custody determination. The Sixth Circuit has squarely held that § 1983 claims challenging the constitutionality of procedures in family court proceedings are cognizable in federal court. *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279, 291 (6th Cir. 1998); *Pittman v. Cuyahoga Cnty. Dep't of Children & Family Servs.*, 640 F.3d 716, 724 (6th Cir. 2011); *Ankenbrandt v. Richards*, 504 U.S. 689, 704 (1992).

Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971); *Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n*, 457 U.S. 423, 432 (1982); *Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124 (1975).

13\. **Exhaustion.** Exhaustion of state remedies is not required for actions under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. *Patsy v. Board of Regents*, 457 U.S. 496, 516 (1982). Notwithstanding, Plaintiff has pursued — and been denied — every available state remedy, as detailed in Section IV below.

14\. This Court is authorized to award compensatory damages, punitive damages, declaratory relief, injunctive relief, and reasonable costs and fees pursuant to **42 U.S.C. § 1988**.
