# DEFENDANT'S BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF SELF-DEFENSE

## I. INTRODUCTION

1. The Defendant, Andrew James Pigors, submits this brief in support of his claim of self-defense. The Defendant was informed by a corrections officer that five (5) inmates had planned to kill him. This statement was captured on the officer's body-worn camera. Any force used by the Defendant was a reasonable response to an imminent and deadly threat to his life.

2. Under Michigan law, the prosecution bears the burden of disproving self-defense beyond a reasonable doubt once the defense is raised. The body camera evidence — in which law enforcement itself acknowledged the threat — makes that burden insurmountable.

## II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

3. The Defendant was detained at the Muskegon County Jail during the period relevant to this case.

4. While in custody, a corrections officer informed the Defendant that **five (5) inmates had formulated a plan to kill him**. This warning was captured on the officer's body-worn camera.

5. The Defendant, faced with an imminent threat to his life and confined in an environment where retreat was not possible, took action to defend himself.

6. The Defendant submits that any force used was proportionate and reasonable under the totality of the circumstances, including: the number of aggressors (five), the nature of the threat (death), the confined custodial environment (no safe retreat), and the corroboration of the threat by a corrections officer.

## III. MICHIGAN SELF-DEFENSE LAW

### A. The Self-Defense Act — MCL 780.972

7. Michigan's Self-Defense Act, MCL 780.972, provides in pertinent part:

> **(1) An individual who has not or is not engaged in the commission of a crime at the time he or she uses deadly force or force other than deadly force may use deadly force or force other than deadly force against another individual anywhere he or she has the legal right to be with no duty to retreat if either of the following applies:**
>
> **(a) The individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force or force other than deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent death of or imminent great bodily harm to himself or herself or to another individual.**
>
> **(b) The individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force or force other than deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent sexual assault of himself or herself or of another individual.**

8. The statute contains **no duty to retreat** when the individual is in a place where he has a legal right to be. MCL 780.972(1). While the Defendant's presence in the jail was involuntary rather than voluntary, this reinforces — rather than diminishes — his right to defend himself, as retreat was physically impossible.

### B. Presumption of Reasonable Belief — MCL 780.951

9. MCL 780.951 provides definitions applicable to the Self-Defense Act:

> **"Dwelling"** means a structure or shelter that is used permanently or temporarily as a place of abode, including an appurtenant structure attached to that structure or shelter.

10. While the custodial setting may not fall neatly within the statutory definition of "dwelling," the policy rationale underlying the presumption applies with even greater force: a person confined against his will in a small space, facing a credible lethal threat from multiple aggressors, has *even less* ability to avoid confrontation than a homeowner in a dwelling.

### C. Use of Deadly Force — MCL 780.972(1)(a)

11. The statute authorizes the use of deadly force when an individual "honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent death of or imminent great bodily harm to himself." MCL 780.972(1)(a).

12. The test has two components:

   - **Subjective (honest belief):** The Defendant must have actually believed his life was in danger. A corrections officer's explicit warning that five inmates planned to kill him establishes this element conclusively.

   - **Objective (reasonable belief):** A reasonable person in the Defendant's position would have believed the same. The objective circumstances — a confined custodial environment, five identified aggressors, a death threat corroborated by law enforcement, and no possibility of escape — overwhelmingly satisfy this element.

## IV. BURDEN OF PROOF — PROSECUTION MUST DISPROVE SELF-DEFENSE

13. In Michigan, once a defendant raises self-defense, **the prosecution bears the burden of disproving self-defense beyond a reasonable doubt**. The defendant has no burden to prove self-defense.

14. The Michigan Supreme Court has repeatedly confirmed this allocation of the burden. The prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the defendant did not act in lawful self-defense. This is not an affirmative defense that the defendant must prove — it negates an element of the crime.

15. Michigan Criminal Jury Instructions confirm this framework:

### M Crim JI 7.15 — Self-Defense

16. M Crim JI 7.15 (Use of Nondeadly Force in Self-Defense) instructs the jury:

> The defendant claims that [he/she] acted in lawful self-defense. If a person acts in lawful self-defense, that person's actions are justified and [he/she] is not guilty of [the charged crime].
>
> ... [T]he prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the defendant did not act in self-defense.

17. The instruction requires the jury to consider whether the defendant "honestly and reasonably believed that [he/she] had to use force to protect [himself/herself] from the imminent unlawful use of force by another person."

### M Crim JI 7.16 — Deadly Force in Self-Defense

18. M Crim JI 7.16 (Use of Deadly Force in Self-Defense) applies when deadly force is at issue. The instruction provides:

> A person has the right to use deadly force to defend [himself/herself] if [he/she] honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force is necessary to prevent imminent death or great bodily harm to [himself/herself].

19. Key elements the jury must evaluate:
   - Whether the Defendant honestly believed he was in danger of death or great bodily harm;
   - Whether a reasonable person in the same situation would have the same belief;
   - Whether the Defendant believed that using deadly force was necessary to prevent imminent death or great bodily harm;
   - Whether a reasonable person in the same situation would have believed the same.

### M Crim JI 7.22 — Aggressor Defense

20. M Crim JI 7.22 addresses situations where the defendant may be characterized as the initial aggressor. However, even an initial aggressor may regain the right to self-defense if:

> (a) [He/She] withdrew from the encounter or clearly indicated to the other person by words or actions that [he/she] wanted to end the physical confrontation.

21. The Defendant submits that he was **not the aggressor** in this case — he was the intended victim of a planned attack by five inmates. However, even under an aggressor analysis, the disparity of force (five against one in a confined space) and the Defendant's inability to retreat support the self-defense claim.

## V. APPLICATION TO THE FACTS

### A. The Defendant Honestly Believed His Life Was in Danger

22. A corrections officer — a person with authority over and knowledge of the facility and its inmates — told the Defendant directly that five inmates planned to kill him. This is not speculation, rumor, or paranoia. It is a warning from law enforcement, captured on body camera.

23. No reasonable juror could conclude that the Defendant did not honestly believe his life was in danger after receiving such a warning.

### B. The Defendant's Belief Was Objectively Reasonable

24. The objective reasonableness of the Defendant's belief is established by the following factors:

   a. **The source of the threat information was a corrections officer.** This is not an anonymous rumor — it came from the very authority responsible for the Defendant's safety.

   b. **Five inmates planned the attack.** A five-on-one assault in a confined space is inherently life-threatening.

   c. **The stated plan was to kill the Defendant.** This was not a threat of minor harm — it was a death threat.

   d. **The environment was a jail.** The Defendant could not leave, could not call police (he was already in custody), and had extremely limited options for self-protection.

   e. **No duty to retreat.** MCL 780.972 eliminates the duty to retreat. In any event, retreat was physically impossible in a custodial setting.

25. Under these circumstances, any reasonable person would have believed that the use of force was necessary to prevent imminent death or great bodily harm.

### C. The Force Used Was Proportionate

26. The Defendant asserts that any force used was proportionate to the threat faced. When confronted with a planned lethal attack by five individuals in a confined space with no possibility of escape, the use of significant force to defend oneself is not merely reasonable — it is necessary.

27. The reasonableness of force must be evaluated from the perspective of a reasonable person in the Defendant's position, considering:
   - The number of aggressors (five);
   - The nature of the threat (death);
   - The inability to retreat (confined in jail);
   - The corroboration by law enforcement;
   - The immediacy and specificity of the threat.

### D. The Body Camera Footage Is Dispositive

28. The body camera footage is not merely corroborative — it is potentially dispositive. When the State's own officer, speaking in an official capacity and captured on an official recording device, confirms that five inmates planned to kill the Defendant, the prosecution faces an extraordinary challenge in disproving self-defense beyond a reasonable doubt.

29. The footage constitutes a contemporaneous, objective record that:
   - Confirms the existence of the threat;
   - Confirms law enforcement's awareness of the threat;
   - Corroborates the Defendant's subjective fear;
   - Establishes the objective reasonableness of that fear;
   - Raises serious questions about whether jail authorities took adequate steps to protect the Defendant.

---

## VI. THE PROSECUTION CANNOT MEET ITS BURDEN

30. To secure a conviction, the prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the Defendant did **not** act in lawful self-defense. Given the evidence:

   a. The prosecution would need to disprove that five inmates planned to kill the Defendant — directly contradicting their own officer's recorded statement;

   b. The prosecution would need to show that a reasonable person in the Defendant's position would not have feared for his life — an untenable position given the circumstances;

   c. The prosecution would need to demonstrate that the Defendant had a viable alternative to using force — in a confined jail setting with five aggressors, no such alternative existed.

31. The prosecution cannot reasonably meet this burden.

## VII. ADDITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS

### A. Duty of Care — Jail Authorities

32. Jail authorities have a constitutional obligation to protect inmates from violence by other inmates. *Farmer v. Brennan*, 511 U.S. 825 (1994). The officer's body camera statement indicates that authorities were aware of the threat. If authorities failed to take adequate protective measures (such as separating the Defendant from the threatening inmates), this failure underscores the reasonableness of the Defendant's actions in protecting himself.

### B. Brady Implications

33. The body camera footage constitutes *Brady* material under *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963). A separate Motion for Disclosure of Favorable Evidence has been filed concurrently with this brief. The Defendant respectfully urges the Court to ensure this critical evidence is produced before trial.

## VIII. CONCLUSION

34. The Defendant, Andrew James Pigors, acted in lawful self-defense. A corrections officer warned him that five inmates planned to kill him. This warning was captured on body camera. The Defendant was confined in a jail with no ability to retreat. Any force he used was a reasonable response to an imminent, lethal threat.

35. The prosecution cannot disprove self-defense beyond a reasonable doubt.
