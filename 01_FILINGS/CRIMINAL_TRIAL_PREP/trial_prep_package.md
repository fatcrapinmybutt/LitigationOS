## 3. SELF-DEFENSE ELEMENTS

### Michigan Self-Defense Act — MCL 780.972

**MCL 780.972 — Use of deadly force or force other than deadly force:**

> § 780.972(1): An individual who has not or is not engaged in the commission of a
> crime at the time he or she uses deadly force or force other than deadly force may
> use deadly force or force other than deadly force against another individual anywhere
> he or she has the legal right to be with no duty to retreat if either of the
> following applies:
>
> (a) The individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force or
> force other than deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent death of or
> imminent great bodily harm to himself or herself or to another individual.
>
> (b) The individual honestly and reasonably believes that the use of deadly force or
> force other than deadly force is necessary to prevent the imminent sexual assault of
> himself or herself or of another individual.

### Application to This Case

| Element | Facts | Status |
|---------|-------|--------|
| **Not committing a crime** | Andrew was an inmate — not committing a crime at time of incident | [VERIFY — confirm Andrew was not the aggressor] |
| **Legal right to be present** | Andrew was lawfully housed in the facility (involuntary — incarcerated) | ✓ Present by legal compulsion |
| **No duty to retreat** | Michigan's Stand Your Ground law — no duty to retreat applies anywhere defendant has legal right to be | ✓ No retreat obligation |
| **Honest belief** | Officer told Andrew on camera that 5 inmates planned to kill him — subjective belief clearly honest | ✓ Body cam evidence |
| **Reasonable belief** | A reasonable person told by a corrections officer that 5 people plan to kill them would believe deadly/serious harm is imminent | ✓ Officer's statement is objectively alarming |
| **Imminent threat** | Threat of 5 inmates conspiring to kill — ongoing danger in enclosed facility | ✓ Cannot escape / retreat from prison |
| **Proportional force** | Force used must be proportional to threat | [VERIFY — document what force Andrew actually used] |

### MCL 780.971 — Definitions

> § 780.971(1): As used in MCL 780.972:
>
> (a) "Dwelling" means a structure or shelter that is used permanently or temporarily
> as a place of abode, including an appurtenant structure attached to that structure
> or shelter.

**Note:** An inmate's cell or housing unit may constitute a "dwelling" for Castle
Doctrine purposes under MCL 780.951. See *People v. Wafer*, 504 Mich. 127 (2019)
for the Court's analysis of dwelling and self-defense intersections.

### MCL 780.951 — Self-Defense Act; Definitions (Supplemental)

> § 780.951(1): "It is a rebuttable presumption in a criminal case that an individual
> who uses deadly force or force other than deadly force under section 2 of the
> self-defense act has an honest and reasonable belief that imminent death of, sexual
> assault of, or great bodily harm to himself or herself or another individual will
> occur if both of the following apply:
>
> (a) The individual against whom deadly force or force other than deadly force is
> used is in the process of breaking and entering a dwelling or business premises, or
> has broken and entered a dwelling or business premises, or is removing or trying to
> remove another individual against that individual's will from a dwelling, business
> premises, or vehicle.

### Key Michigan Self-Defense Case Law

| Case | Holding | Relevance |
|------|---------|-----------|
| *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich. 116 (2002) | Defendant has initial burden to produce SOME evidence of self-defense; then burden shifts to prosecution to disprove beyond reasonable doubt | Framework for self-defense burden |
| *People v. Dupree*, 486 Mich. 693 (2010) | Jury must be instructed on self-defense if ANY evidence supports it, even if contradicted | Ensures jury instruction regardless of prosecution's view |
| *People v. Guajardo*, 300 Mich. App. 26 (2013) | Self-defense applies even if defendant was initially the aggressor, if defendant withdrew | Relevant if prosecution claims Andrew started it |
| *People v. Conyer*, 281 Mich. App. 526 (2008) | Self-defense is available even when defendant is a felon | Incarceration/prior record does not bar self-defense |
| *People v. Wafer*, 504 Mich. 127 (2019) | Castle Doctrine requires occupant's reasonable belief | Standard for "honest and reasonable belief" |

### Burden of Proof

**CRITICAL:** Once Andrew produces SOME evidence of self-defense (the body camera
footage of the officer's warning), the **burden shifts to the prosecution** to
disprove self-defense **beyond a reasonable doubt**. *People v. Riddle*, 467 Mich.
116, 120 (2002). The defense does NOT need to prove self-defense — the prosecution
must disprove it.

### I. EXCLUDE PRIOR BAD ACTS — MRE 404(b)

**MRE 404(b)** provides:

> Evidence of other crimes, wrongs, or acts is not admissible to prove the character
> of a person in order to show action in conformity therewith. It may, however, be
> admissible for other purposes, such as proof of motive, opportunity, intent,
> preparation, scheme, plan, or system in doing an act, knowledge, identity, or
> absence of mistake or accident when the same is material.

**Defendant moves to exclude:**

1. **Any reference to Andrew's other legal cases** — The custody case (2024-001507-DC),
   housing case (2025-002760-CZ), and PPO proceedings (2023-5907-PP) are irrelevant to
   whether Andrew acted in self-defense in this incident. Admission would be highly
   prejudicial under MRE 403.

2. **Any reference to prior incarceration history** — Why Andrew was incarcerated is
   irrelevant to whether he reasonably feared for his life when told 5 inmates planned
   to kill him. See *People v. Crawford*, 458 Mich. 376 (1998) (limiting 404(b) evidence).

3. **Any PPO or domestic allegations** — These are separate civil matters and are
   inadmissible character evidence. The jury should not hear about unrelated family
   court proceedings.

4. **Any reference to "pattern of violence"** — The prosecution may attempt to
   characterize Andrew as violent. This is precisely the prohibited propensity
   inference under MRE 404(b).

### II. EXCLUDE UNDULY PREJUDICIAL EVIDENCE — MRE 403

**MRE 403** provides:

> Although relevant, evidence may be excluded if its probative value is substantially
> outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues, or
> misleading the jury.

**Defendant moves to exclude:**

5. **Inflammatory photographs** not directly related to the incident
6. **Hearsay statements** from other inmates not subject to cross-examination
7. **Jail classification records** suggesting dangerousness (these reflect
   administrative housing decisions, not actual conduct)

### III. REQUIRE PROSECUTION TO PRODUCE BODY CAMERA EVIDENCE

8. If the prosecution intends to introduce **any** body camera footage, Defendant
   demands the **complete, unedited footage** — not clips or excerpts. Selective
   editing would be misleading under MRE 106 (rule of completeness).

### IV. EXCLUDE EXPERT TESTIMONY WITHOUT PROPER FOUNDATION — MRE 702

9. Any expert testimony must satisfy *Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals*,
   509 U.S. 579 (1993), as adopted by Michigan in MRE 702.

### V. PRESERVE RIGHT TO INTRODUCE SELF-DEFENSE EVIDENCE

10. Defendant reserves the right to introduce evidence of the **victim's/aggressor's
    character for violence** under MRE 404(a)(2) — this is permitted when
    self-defense is raised.

11. Defendant reserves the right to introduce evidence of **specific instances of
    conduct** of the aggressors under MRE 405(b) when character is an essential

### VI. STANDARD — People v. VanderVliet

Under *People v. VanderVliet*, 444 Mich. 52 (1993), before admitting MRE 404(b)
evidence, the court must find:

1. The evidence is offered for a proper purpose (not character/propensity)
2. The evidence is relevant under MRE 402
3. The probative value is not substantially outweighed by prejudice under MRE 403
4. The court may, upon request, give a limiting instruction under MRE 105

## 9. RIGHT TO SELF-REPRESENTATION

### Faretta v. California, 422 U.S. 806 (1975)

If Amy Campanelli continues to not communicate, Andrew has a constitutional right to
represent himself under *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806 (1975).

### Requirements for Faretta Waiver

The court must conduct a colloquy to ensure:

1. **Knowing waiver:** Defendant understands the charges, possible penalties, and
   nature of the proceedings
2. **Intelligent waiver:** Defendant is competent to understand the proceedings
3. **Voluntary waiver:** Defendant is choosing self-representation freely, not due
   to frustration with counsel's non-communication (though this IS a valid reason)
4. **Unequivocal request:** Defendant clearly and unambiguously requests to proceed
   pro se

### Michigan Standard — People v. Anderson, 398 Mich. 361 (1976)

Michigan adopted *Faretta* in *People v. Anderson*, requiring the trial court to
determine that the defendant's waiver of counsel is:
- Made knowingly, intelligently, and voluntarily
- Not the result of coercion or manipulation
- Made with understanding of the disadvantages of self-representation

### MCR 6.005 — Right to Counsel

MCR 6.005(D) provides for waiver of counsel:

> If the defendant requests to proceed without a lawyer, the court must advise the
> defendant of the charge, the maximum possible prison sentence, any mandatory
> minimum sentence, and the risk involved in self-representation, and must ascertain
> that the waiver is voluntarily, understandingly, and knowingly made.
