## I. STATEMENT OF FACTS

1. The Defendant, Andrew James Pigors, is charged in the above-captioned matter, with trial scheduled for **April 7, 2026**.

2. The Defendant asserts a claim of **self-defense** in this matter. It is the Defendant's position that any actions taken were in reasonable response to an imminent threat to his life.

5. Despite the approaching trial date, the prosecution has not disclosed all evidence favorable to the defense, including but not limited to the body camera footage referenced above.

---

## II. LEGAL AUTHORITY

### A. Brady v. Maryland and Its Progeny

6. In *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83, 87 (1963), the United States Supreme Court held that "the suppression by the prosecution of evidence favorable to an accused upon request violates due process where the evidence is material either to guilt or to punishment, irrespective of the good faith or bad faith of the prosecution."

7. The *Brady* obligation extends to impeachment evidence. *Giglio v. United States*, 405 U.S. 150, 154 (1972) (holding that evidence affecting the credibility of a key prosecution witness falls within the *Brady* rule).

8. Evidence is "material" under *Brady* when "there is a reasonable probability that, had the evidence been disclosed to the defense, the result of the proceeding would have been different." *United States v. Bagley*, 473 U.S. 667, 682 (1985).

9. The *Brady* obligation encompasses evidence known to police investigators, not merely evidence in the actual possession of the prosecutor. *Kyles v. Whitley*, 514 U.S. 419, 437 (1995).

### B. Michigan Court Rules

10. MCR 6.201 governs discovery in criminal cases in Michigan courts. MCR 6.201(A) requires the prosecution, upon request, to provide the defendant with information and items in its possession or control that are material to the preparation of the defense.

11. MCR 6.201(B) provides that, upon request, the prosecution shall provide the defendant with, among other things:

- (a) Any written or recorded statements by the defendant or a co-defendant;
- (b) A description of the substance of any oral statements by the defendant or a co-defendant;
- (c) The criminal record of the defendant and any accomplice, witness, or co-defendant;
- (e) Any exculpatory information or evidence;
- (f) Any police report and investigative report relating to the case.

### C. Michigan Constitutional Protections

12. Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963 guarantees the right to due process of law. Article 1, § 20 guarantees the right to the assistance of counsel and to compulsory process for obtaining witnesses. The suppression of material exculpatory evidence violates both provisions.

13. The Defendant respectfully requests that the prosecution be ordered to disclose the following categories of evidence, to the extent such evidence exists and is in the possession, custody, or control of the prosecution or any law enforcement agency involved in this case:

### A. Body-Worn Camera Footage

14. All body-worn camera footage from **all officers** present at the scene of the incident giving rise to the charges in this case, including but not limited to footage capturing any officer's statement to the Defendant regarding threats made by inmates.

### B. Jail Surveillance Video

15. All jail and/or facility surveillance video recordings from the date of the incident, including footage from the location where the alleged offense occurred and all adjacent areas.

### C. Jail Medical Records

16. All medical records for the Defendant, Andrew James Pigors, generated during his detention, including intake assessments, injury reports, treatment records, and mental health evaluations.

### D. Inmate Threat Reports

17. All reports, assessments, records, or documentation relating to threats against the Defendant by other inmates, including but not limited to inmate separation orders, threat assessments, protective custody requests, and any documentation of the planned attack by five (5) inmates referenced above.

### E. Officer Incident Reports

18. All incident reports, use-of-force reports, supplemental reports, and memoranda prepared by any officer or jail staff member relating to the incident at issue.

### F. 911 Call Recordings and Dispatch Records

19. All 911 call recordings, dispatch logs, CAD (Computer-Aided Dispatch) records, and communications between dispatch and responding officers related to the incident.

### G. Other Exculpatory Evidence

20. Any and all other evidence favorable to the Defendant, whether exculpatory or impeaching, including but not limited to:
- Witness statements from any inmate, officer, or staff member;
- Disciplinary records of the inmates alleged to have planned the attack;
- Any prior incidents involving threats against the Defendant while in custody;
- Any communications between officers regarding the threat to the Defendant;
- Personnel records of involved officers relevant to credibility.

## IV. MATERIALITY

21. The evidence requested above is material to the defense for the following reasons:

22. **Body camera footage** is directly material because it captures a corrections officer's contemporaneous acknowledgment that five inmates planned to kill the Defendant. This evidence goes to the heart of the Defendant's self-defense claim, establishing both the existence and immediacy of the threat.

23. **Jail surveillance video** is material because it will show the events as they occurred, providing an objective record of the Defendant's conduct and the conduct of others involved.

24. **Medical records** are material because they may document injuries sustained by the Defendant, corroborating the existence of an attack and the need for self-defense.

25. **Inmate threat reports** are material because they may establish that jail authorities were aware of threats against the Defendant, further corroborating the reasonableness of the Defendant's belief that he was in imminent danger.

26. **Officer incident reports** are material because they contain the officers' contemporaneous observations and may include exculpatory statements or information favorable to the defense.

27. **911 call recordings** are material because they may capture information about the nature and circumstances of the incident as reported in real time.

---

## V. TIMELINESS AND URGENCY

28. Trial is scheduled for **April 7, 2026 — fourteen (14) days** from the date of this motion. The Defendant requires immediate disclosure to have adequate time to review the evidence, prepare his defense, and consult with any witnesses.

29. The failure to disclose this evidence at this stage would effectively deny the Defendant his constitutional right to present a defense, as there would be insufficient time to incorporate newly disclosed evidence into trial preparation.

30. The Defendant respectfully requests that the Court order disclosure within **five (5) days** of the date of this Order, or alternatively, that the Court grant a continuance to allow adequate preparation time following disclosure.

# ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT'S MOTION FOR DISCLOSURE OF FAVORABLE EVIDENCE

---

This matter having come before the Court on Defendant's Motion for Disclosure of Favorable Evidence Pursuant to *Brady v. Maryland*, and the Court having reviewed the motion, any response, and being otherwise fully advised in the premises;

**IT IS HEREBY ORDERED:**

1. Defendant's Motion is **GRANTED**.

2. The prosecution shall, within **five (5) days** of the date of this Order, disclose to the Defendant all evidence favorable to the defense that is in the possession, custody, or control of the prosecution or any law enforcement agency involved in this case, including but not limited to:

   a. All body-worn camera footage from officers present at the incident;
   b. All jail and/or facility surveillance video from the date of the incident;
   c. All medical records for the Defendant generated during detention;
   d. All inmate threat reports, assessments, and separation orders;
   e. All officer incident reports, use-of-force reports, and supplemental reports;
   f. All 911 call recordings and dispatch records;
   g. All other exculpatory and impeachment evidence.

3. The prosecution shall file a **certification** with this Court, within the same five-day period, confirming that a diligent search has been conducted and all *Brady* material has been disclosed.

4. The prosecution's **obligation to disclose is ongoing**. Any additional favorable evidence discovered after the initial disclosure shall be immediately provided to the Defendant.

5. Failure to comply with this Order may result in sanctions, including but not limited to suppression of evidence, adverse inference instructions, dismissal, or contempt of court.
