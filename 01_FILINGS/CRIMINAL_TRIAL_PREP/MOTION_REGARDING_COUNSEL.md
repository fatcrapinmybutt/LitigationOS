## II. LEGAL AUTHORITY

### A. Right to Effective Assistance of Counsel

7. The Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution guarantees that "[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right ... to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence." U.S. Const. amend. VI.

8. Article 1, § 20 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963 similarly provides: "In every criminal prosecution, the accused shall have the right ... to have the assistance of counsel for his or her defense."

9. The right to counsel means the right to *effective* assistance of counsel. *Strickland v. Washington*, 466 U.S. 668, 686 (1984). A complete failure to communicate with one's client in the weeks preceding trial renders the assistance of counsel constitutionally deficient.

10. MCR 6.005 governs the right to counsel in Michigan criminal cases. MCR 6.005(A) provides that the court must advise the defendant of the right to counsel at the first opportunity.

### B. Substitution of Counsel

11. MCR 6.005(E) provides: "The court may, in the interest of justice, and upon a proper showing of good cause, permit the defendant to change assigned lawyers."

12. A complete breakdown in the attorney-client relationship constitutes good cause for substitution. When counsel fails to communicate, the relationship necessary for effective representation cannot exist.

### C. Right to Self-Representation

13. In *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806, 819 (1975), the United States Supreme Court held that the Sixth Amendment guarantees a defendant the right to proceed without counsel when he voluntarily and intelligently elects to do so.

14. Under Michigan law, the right to self-representation is protected by both the federal and state constitutions. The trial court must conduct an inquiry to ensure the waiver of counsel is knowing, intelligent, and voluntary. MCR 6.005(D).

### D. Right to Adequate Time to Prepare

15. Both the federal and Michigan constitutions guarantee the right to adequate time and opportunity to prepare a defense. *Powell v. Alabama*, 287 U.S. 45, 59 (1932).

16. When a defendant's preparation has been compromised by counsel's failure to communicate, the court should grant additional time to ensure a fair trial.
