## II. TIMELINE OF EX PARTE ORDERS RECEIVED WITHOUT NOTICE

### A. The PPO and Early Proceedings (2023-2024)

4. On or about November 1, 2023, a Personal Protection Order (domestic) was issued against me under Case No. 2023-5907-PP without a full evidentiary hearing. I was not given adequate notice or opportunity to present evidence before the PPO was issued.

5. On or about April 1, 2024, I filed a Complaint for Custody, Parenting Time, and Child Support under Case No. 2024-001507-DC because I wanted to formalize my parental rights and ensure my daughter's well-being.

6. On April 11, 2024, the PPO was amended through an Ex Parte Order. I was not provided notice of this amendment before it was entered. I learned of the amended PPO after it was already in effect.

7. On or about April 29, 2024, an Ex Parte Order for Custody, Parenting Time, and Child Support was entered. While this followed a Facilitative Information Gathering Conference, the order was styled as "ex parte" and I was not given a meaningful opportunity to contest its terms before entry.

8. On or about July 1, 2024, an Ex Parte Custody Order was entered in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. Again, I received no prior notice that an ex parte order would be sought or entered.

### B. The August 2025 Total Suspension

9. On or about August 6-8, 2025, Defendant filed an ex parte motion to suspend my parenting time. **I was not served with this motion or the resulting order before the scheduled exchange on August 8, 2025.**

10. On August 8, 2025, I arrived at the designated exchange location at the scheduled time, expecting to exercise my regular parenting time with Chloe. Upon arrival, I discovered — only then — that an ex parte order had been entered suspending all of my parenting time. I was given no advance warning.

11. The ex parte motion, on information and belief, did not allege specific facts showing that Chloe faced **immediate physical danger** from continued parenting time with me. There were no allegations of abuse, neglect, or any conduct that would constitute an emergency under MCR 3.207(B).

12. No post-deprivation hearing was held within 14 days of the August 2025 ex parte order, as required by MCR 3.207(B).

    - [NEEDS VERIFICATION — specific dates of continuation orders]

14. A hearing was eventually held on or about October 29, 2025 — approximately **82 days** after the ex parte order, far exceeding the 14-day requirement of MCR 3.207(B).

15. A further hearing was held on November 26, 2025, at which the Court addressed the ex parte suspension. The court record from that hearing confirms that "Defendant requested, and the Court signed an ex parte order suspending the Plaintiff's parenting time."

16. As of the date of this Affidavit, I have been completely separated from my daughter for approximately **329+ consecutive days** — since on or about August 8, 2025.

## III. IMPACT ON PARENT-CHILD RELATIONSHIP

17. Before the August 2025 ex parte suspension, I was an active and involved father. I regularly exercised my parenting time and was deeply engaged in Chloe's life, including her education, health care, and daily activities.

18. Since August 8, 2025, I have had **zero** in-person contact with my daughter. I have been unable to:
    - Attend any of her school events or parent-teacher conferences;
    - Participate in any medical or dental appointments;
    - Celebrate any holidays, birthdays, or milestones with her;
    - Maintain the daily routines and bonds that are essential to the parent-child relationship.

19. The emotional impact on both Chloe and myself has been devastating. A child Chloe's age (approximately 5 years old) is at a critical developmental stage where consistent, reliable contact with both parents is essential for healthy emotional and psychological development.

20. I have maintained stable housing throughout this period specifically so that Chloe would have a safe, comfortable home to come to when parenting time is restored.

21. I have maintained full-time employment throughout this period to demonstrate my ability to provide for Chloe's needs and to maintain the stability that the Court should expect of a custodial parent.

22. At no time have I engaged in any conduct that would justify the complete suspension of my parenting time. I have no criminal convictions for child abuse or neglect. I have not been the subject of any CPS investigation finding abuse or neglect. There is no factual basis for an emergency suspension under MCR 3.207(B).

## IV. PATTERN OF EX PARTE ADJUDICATION

23. Through my review of the docket records in both Case No. 2024-001507-DC and Case No. 2023-5907-PP, I have identified **42 documented ex parte events** — instances where orders were entered or actions were taken without proper notice and adversarial process.

24. This constitutes an **ex parte order rate of approximately 44%** — meaning that nearly half of all significant court actions in these consolidated proceedings were taken ex parte.

25. I have been offered supervised parenting time as a substitute, which I believe is inappropriate given that no finding of abuse, neglect, or danger has ever been made. Accepting supervised visitation under these circumstances would effectively concede that I am a danger to my child — a characterization that has no basis in fact and that would prejudice my position in these proceedings.

26. The Court has noted on the record that supervised parenting time was offered and rejected. However, this characterization fails to account for the fact that supervised parenting time is an extraordinary measure reserved for parents who have been found to pose a risk to their children — a finding that has never been made in my case.
