## III. LEGAL AUTHORITY

### A. MCR 2.612(C)(1) — Relief from Judgment or Order

MCR 2.612(C)(1) provides that a court may relieve a party from a final judgment, order, or proceeding for the following reasons:

- **(b)** Newly discovered evidence which by due diligence could not have been discovered in time to move for a new hearing;
- **(c)** Fraud, misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party;
- **(d)** The judgment or order is void;
- **(f)** Any other reason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment or order.

The ex parte orders at issue are **void** under subsection (d) because they were entered in violation of fundamental due process requirements. Alternatively, relief is warranted under subsection (f) because the cumulative pattern of ex parte adjudication constitutes extraordinary circumstances justifying relief.

### B. MCR 2.119(B) — Ex Parte Orders

MCR 2.119(B) strictly limits ex parte relief:

> "A motion may be heard ex parte if it appears from specific facts shown by affidavit or other sworn statement that **immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result** from the delay required to effect notice…"

None of the ex parte orders challenged herein were supported by verified allegations of immediate and irreparable injury sufficient to justify dispensing with notice.

### C. MCR 3.207(B) — Emergency Parenting Time Orders

MCR 3.207(B) governs emergency ex parte custody and parenting-time orders and requires:

- **Specific facts** in a verified pleading or affidavit showing immediate danger to the child;
- **Factual findings** of immediate harm or risk documented in the order;
- An expedited hearing within 14 days of the ex parte order.

The August 2025 ex parte suspension order failed to satisfy these requirements.

### D. Constitutional Due Process

The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution guarantee that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.

A parent's right to the care, custody, and control of their child is a **fundamental liberty interest** protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982).

The systematic denial of parenting time through ex parte orders — without notice, without hearing, and without findings of imminent danger — constitutes a deprivation of this fundamental right without due process.

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. The Ex Parte Orders Are Void for Lack of Due Process (MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d))

**Issue:** Whether ex parte orders entered without constitutionally adequate notice and opportunity to be heard are void.

**Rule:** Due process requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a party is deprived of a protected liberty interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). A parent's right to custody and parenting time is a fundamental liberty interest. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000).

**Application:** The record demonstrates that multiple orders affecting Plaintiff's parenting time and custody were entered ex parte — without notice to Plaintiff and without any opportunity to be heard. Specifically:

- **August 6-8, 2025**: Ex parte order suspending ALL parenting time
  - Plaintiff was not served with the ex parte motion or order before the August 8, 2025 exchange. He arrived expecting regular parenting time and discovered the suspension only after the fact. No verified allegations of imminent physical danger were presented. The suspension has continued for 329+ days.

- **April 29, 2024**: Ex Parte Order for Custody, Parenting Time and Child Support
  - Entered following a Facilitative Information Gathering Conference. The temporary order was styled as an 'Ex Parte Order' despite the availability of both parties, undermining the procedural protections intended by the ex parte designation.

- **April 11, 2024**: PPO Amended — Ex Parte Order
  - The personal protection order was amended ex parte without notice to Plaintiff, expanding restrictions without opportunity to contest.

- **July 1, 2024**: Ex Parte Custody Order
  - Custody order entered ex parte, further restricting Plaintiff's rights without a hearing on the merits.

**Conclusion:** Each of these orders was entered in violation of Plaintiff's fundamental due process rights. Orders entered without jurisdiction over the person or in violation of due process are void and subject to relief under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d).

### B. The Ex Parte Orders Failed to Meet the Requirements of MCR 3.207(B)

**Issue:** Whether the ex parte orders complied with Michigan Court Rule requirements for emergency parenting-time orders.

**Rule:** MCR 3.207(B) requires that emergency ex parte parenting-time orders be supported by: (1) specific facts in a verified pleading or affidavit showing immediate danger to the child; (2) factual findings of immediate harm or risk documented in the order; and (3) an expedited hearing within 14 days.

**Application:** The August 2025 ex parte order suspending all parenting time:
- Was not supported by specific facts showing **immediate physical danger** to the child;
- Did not contain adequate **factual findings** justifying the emergency suspension;
- Did not result in a timely post-deprivation hearing within 14 days as required;
- Has continued for months without adequate review.

**Conclusion:** The failure to comply with MCR 3.207(B) renders the ex parte orders procedurally defective and subject to vacation.

### C. The Pattern of Ex Parte Adjudication Constitutes Extraordinary Circumstances (MCR 2.612(C)(1)(f))

**Issue:** Whether the cumulative pattern of 42 documented ex parte events constitutes extraordinary circumstances warranting relief.

**Rule:** MCR 2.612(C)(1)(f) permits relief from an order for "any other reason justifying relief." Michigan courts have held that this subsection should be applied to achieve justice in extraordinary circumstances. *Heugel v. Heugel*, 237 Mich App 471 (1999).

**Application:** This is not a case of a single emergency order in exigent circumstances. The record demonstrates:

1. **42 documented ex parte events** across both case files;
2. A **44% ex parte order rate** — nearly half of all significant court actions were taken without adversarial process;
3. **329+ days** of complete parenting-time deprivation flowing from ex parte orders;
4. A pattern where ex parte process was used not for genuine emergencies but as the **default mode of adjudication**;
5. **Benchbook violations** including failure to comply with MCR 3.207(B) emergency requirements, MCR 3.210(C) hearing notice requirements, and MCL 552.511b parenting time enforcement requirements.

**Conclusion:** The extraordinary pattern of ex parte adjudication — resulting in the functional termination of a parent-child relationship without adversarial process — constitutes the type of exceptional circumstance contemplated by MCR 2.612(C)(1)(f).
