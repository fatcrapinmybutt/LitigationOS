## ARGUMENT

### I. The Ex Parte Orders Violate Constitutional Due Process

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed that the right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of their child is among the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized under the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972).

The Due Process Clause mandates that before a state may sever the parent-child bond — even temporarily — it must provide fundamentally fair procedures, including, at minimum, **notice** and an **opportunity to be heard** at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976).

The *Mathews* balancing test weighs:
1. The **private interest** that will be affected by the official action;
2. The **risk of erroneous deprivation** through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional safeguards; and
3. The **government's interest**, including the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional process.

Applied here:

**Factor 1 — Private Interest:** The parental interest at stake is of the highest constitutional magnitude. Plaintiff has been deprived of **all contact** with his son for over 329 days. This is not a minor adjustment of parenting time; it is a functional termination of the parent-child relationship.

**Factor 2 — Risk of Erroneous Deprivation:** The risk is not theoretical — it has been realized. The ex parte process, by definition, deprived Plaintiff of the opportunity to present his side before the deprivation occurred. The 44% ex parte order rate demonstrates that this was not an isolated emergency measure but a systematic bypassing of adversarial process. Each ex parte order compounded the risk of error because the court heard only one side.

**Factor 3 — Government Interest:** The state has a legitimate interest in protecting children from imminent harm. But that interest does not justify 42 ex parte events, months of suspension without hearing, and functional termination of parental rights. The state's interest is adequately served by requiring verified allegations of imminent danger before dispensing with notice — exactly what MCR 3.207(B) requires.

The balance overwhelmingly favors Plaintiff. The ex parte orders violated the Due Process Clause.

### II. The Ex Parte Orders Fail MCR 3.207(B) Requirements

MCR 3.207(B) establishes mandatory prerequisites for emergency ex parte custody and parenting-time orders. These are not discretionary guidelines — they are **binding procedural requirements**.

The rule requires:
1. **Specific facts** in a verified pleading or affidavit showing that the child faces **immediate danger** of physical harm;
2. The order must contain **factual findings** documenting the basis for the emergency action;
3. An **expedited hearing** must be held within **14 days** of the ex parte order.

The August 2025 ex parte suspension fails on all three prongs:

**Prong 1 — Specific Facts:** The ex parte motion did not allege specific facts showing that Chloe faced immediate physical danger from continued contact with Plaintiff. General allegations of conflict or disagreement between parents do not satisfy this standard. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003) (requiring "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" based on specific factual evidence).

**Prong 2 — Factual Findings:** The ex parte order failed to articulate adequate factual findings justifying the emergency suspension. An ex parte order affecting fundamental parental rights must do more than recite conclusions; it must specify what imminent danger the child faces and why notice cannot be given. *Brown v. Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576 (2004).

**Prong 3 — Expedited Hearing:** No post-deprivation hearing was conducted within 14 days of the August 2025 ex parte order. The suspension has now persisted for months — converting what should have been a brief emergency measure into a prolonged deprivation without due process.

### III. MCR 2.612(C)(1) Mandates Relief

#### A. The Orders Are Void — MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d)

An order entered without jurisdiction or in fundamental violation of due process is **void** and subject to relief under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d). *Heugel v. Heugel*, 237 Mich App 471 (1999).

The ex parte orders challenged herein were entered in violation of:
- The Due Process Clauses of the U.S. and Michigan Constitutions;
- MCR 3.207(B) mandatory emergency order requirements;
- MCR 2.119(B) ex parte motion standards;
- MCR 3.210(C) post-judgment hearing notice requirements.

Orders that violate these fundamental procedural protections are void *ab initio*.

### V. The Child's Best Interest Requires Restoration of Parenting Time

Michigan law establishes a strong public policy favoring the maintenance of a close relationship between the child and **both** parents. MCL 722.27a(1); *Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186 (2004).

The best interest factors under MCL 722.23 weigh heavily in favor of restoring parenting time:

- **Factor (a)** — Love, affection, emotional ties: Plaintiff has demonstrated deep love for his son and an unwavering commitment to their relationship despite 329+ days of enforced separation.
- **Factor (c)** — Capacity to provide: Plaintiff has maintained stable housing and employment throughout the proceedings.
- **Factor (j)** — Willingness to facilitate close relationship: Plaintiff has consistently sought parenting time and demonstrated willingness to facilitate the child's relationship with both parents.
- **Factor (l)** — Domestic violence: Any domestic violence allegations must be weighed against the disproportionate response of complete parenting-time termination via ex parte process.

The prolonged separation itself causes harm. Child development research consistently demonstrates that abrupt, prolonged separation from a parent causes emotional and psychological injury to the child. Every additional day of separation compounds this harm.

## CONCLUSION AND RELIEF REQUESTED

The record in this case demonstrates a deeply troubling pattern: 42 ex parte events, a 44% ex parte order rate, 329+ days of complete parenting-time deprivation, and systematic violations of MCR 3.207(B), MCR 2.119(B), and constitutional due process requirements.

This is not how family courts are designed to operate. Ex parte orders are an extraordinary remedy for extraordinary emergencies — not the default mode of adjudication. When ex parte process is used to achieve a functional termination of the parent-child relationship, the procedural protections of the Due Process Clause and the Michigan Court Rules demand relief.

Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court:

1. Vacate the ex parte orders identified herein;
2. Restore parenting time to the status quo ante;
3. Prohibit future ex parte orders absent verified allegations of imminent physical danger;
4. Schedule an expedited evidentiary hearing; and
5. Grant such other relief as justice requires.
