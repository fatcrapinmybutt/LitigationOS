## II. LEGAL STANDARD

**A. Statutory Authority.** MCL 600.2950(12) provides that a PPO "shall remain in effect until modified or rescinded by the court." Either party may petition for modification or rescission. MCR 3.707(A) establishes the procedural mechanism.

**B. Termination Standard.** A court may terminate a PPO when the movant demonstrates that the circumstances justifying the PPO have materially changed, such that the PPO is no longer necessary for the protection of the petitioner. *Hayford v. Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 (2008).

**C. Credibility of Basis.** A PPO must be supported by credible evidence establishing a reasonable apprehension of violence or threatening behavior. *Pickering v. Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694 (2002); *Kampf v. Kampf*, 237 Mich App 377 (1999). Where the factual predicate for the PPO has been disproven, termination is warranted.

**D. PPO Cannot Serve as Custody Tool.** A PPO cannot operate as a substitute for custody modification proceedings under the Child Custody Act. *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247 (2009). Custody may only be modified through MCL 722.27 proceedings with best-interest analysis under MCL 722.23.

**E. Constitutional Framework.** The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982). A PPO restricting parent-child communication implicates First Amendment protections and must employ the least restrictive means. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656 (2009); *Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378 (6th Cir. 1999).

### D. Constitutional Violations — First and Fourteenth Amendments

#### Issue

Whether the PPO, as applied, unconstitutionally restricts Respondent's fundamental parenting rights and right to communicate with his child.

#### Rule

The parent-child relationship is among the oldest of fundamental liberty interests. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982). A State may not deprive a parent of this interest without due process, requiring adequate notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976).

A PPO prohibiting all communication between a parent and child operates as a prior restraint on protected expression. While PPOs may restrict threatening or harassing speech, a blanket prohibition on all parent-child communication exceeds what the First Amendment permits. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656 (2009); *Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378, 394 (6th Cir. 1999) (retaliation for exercise of protected conduct violates the First Amendment).

Michigan Constitution, art 1, §§ 2 (equal protection), 5 (free speech), 17 (due process) provide independent protections. MCL 722.27a establishes that a child has a right to parenting time with both parents absent clear and convincing evidence of endangerment.

#### Application

**1. Inadequate due process in issuance.** The PPO was issued ex parte on December 3, 2023, without notice to Respondent and without an evidentiary hearing. Under the *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test: (a) the private interest affected — a fundamental parenting right — is of the highest order; (b) the risk of erroneous deprivation is extremely high given the fabricated factual basis; and (c) no legitimate government interest supports maintaining a PPO based on disproven allegations.

**2. Over 223 days of total separation without adequate process.** Respondent has been completely separated from his child for over 223 consecutive days. No evidentiary hearing has produced findings, based on competent evidence, that Respondent's parenting poses any risk to L.D.W. The continuation of this separation without such findings violates substantive due process. *Santosky*, 455 US at 753.

**3. Birthday messages are protected speech.** The conduct underlying Respondent's incarceration — sending birthday messages to his child through a court-approved application — constitutes protected parent-child communication. A PPO that criminalizes birthday greetings to one's own child operates as an unconstitutional prior restraint that is not narrowly tailored to any legitimate protective purpose.

**4. Least restrictive means not employed.** Even assuming *arguendo* that some restriction on contact between Respondent and Petitioner were justified, the PPO's blanket prohibition on all communication — including parent-child communication through court-approved channels — is not the least restrictive means available. Modification to permit parenting communication while maintaining a no-contact provision between the parties would adequately serve any legitimate protective interest.

**5. MCL 722.27a — the child's right to parenting time.** L.D.W. has a statutory right to a relationship with both parents. The PPO as enforced deprives L.D.W. of this right without the clear and convincing evidence of endangerment required by Michigan law.

**6. Federal firearms prohibition.** The PPO triggers automatic application of 18 USC § 922(g)(8)(C), imposing a federal firearms disability. This collateral consequence — imposed without any adversarial finding of violence or credible threat — further demonstrates the constitutional magnitude of the deprivation. A PPO obtained ex parte on fabricated grounds should not carry the power to extinguish fundamental constitutional rights under both state and federal law.

#### Conclusion

The PPO as currently enforced violates the First and Fourteenth Amendments to the United States Constitution and the corresponding provisions of the Michigan Constitution. At minimum, the PPO must be modified to permit parent-child communication. Termination is the constitutionally required remedy given the complete absence of credible evidence supporting continued restriction.
