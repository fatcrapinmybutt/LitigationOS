## IV. COUNT I: FOURTEENTH AMENDMENT — PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS
### (42 U.S.C. § 1983)
### Against All Defendants

42. Plaintiff repeats and incorporates by reference all preceding allegations.

43. Procedural due process requires, at minimum, (1) adequate notice, (2) a meaningful opportunity to be heard, and (3) a neutral decision-maker, before the government deprives a person of a protected liberty or property interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976).

44. The *Mathews* balancing test weighs: (a) the private interest affected; (b) the risk of erroneous deprivation through current procedures; and (c) the government's interest. *Id.* at 335.

45. Here:
    - **(a) Private interest:** FUNDAMENTAL — the parent-child relationship is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000);
    - **(b) Risk of erroneous deprivation:** EXTREME — 24 ex parte orders entered without any notice to Plaintiff; 5 ex parte orders on a single day; court refused to view exculpatory evidence; no MCR 3.207(B) affidavit; no MCR 3.207(C)(2) compliance;
    - **(c) Government interest in summary adjudication:** MINIMAL — no imminent danger was shown; no emergency justified bypassing notice and hearing; the deprivation has continued for more than 200 consecutive days, long past any conceivable emergency.

46. On August 8, 2025, Plaintiff was not served with the ex parte motion or order before the scheduled exchange. He had no notice, no opportunity to be heard, and no ability to contest the allegations before his parenting time was suspended. The suspension has continued for more than 200 consecutive days.

47. The ex parte order of August 8, 2025 was entered **without the affidavit required by MCR 3.207(B)**, which mandates that an ex parte order regarding custody or parenting time may only be entered upon verified motion supported by specific sworn facts demonstrating imminent danger. The absence of this jurisdictional prerequisite renders the order void ab initio.

48. At multiple hearings, Plaintiff was muted, silenced, and prevented from presenting evidence — including exculpatory photographs, police reports, and recordings. This constitutes a denial of meaningful opportunity to be heard even at post-deprivation proceedings.

49. Judge McNeill's self-ruling on her own disqualification motion (in violation of MCR 2.003(D)) deprived Plaintiff of a neutral decision-maker — one of the three irreducible requirements of procedural due process.

50. Defendant Rusco's biased recommendations, adopted without independent review, further deprived Plaintiff of a neutral decision-making process.

51. The child support obligation of $1,056.60 per month was imposed without an evidentiary hearing, further demonstrating the systematic denial of procedural due process.

52. The systematic denial of notice and hearing before depriving Plaintiff of his fundamental liberty interest constitutes a per se violation of procedural due process.

## XI. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY ANALYSIS

### A. The Two Exceptions to Judicial Immunity

93. Judges are generally immune from § 1983 damages for acts performed in their judicial capacity. *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9 (1991). However, two well-established exceptions apply:

94. **Exception 1 — Non-Judicial Acts:** Judicial immunity does not apply to acts that are "not taken in the judge's judicial capacity." *Id.* at 11-12; *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219 (1988). McNeill's non-judicial acts include:
    - Ex parte communications with Watson and/or Berry outside the formal record;
    - Self-ruling on her own disqualification motion — a function assigned by MCR 2.003(D) to the Chief Judge;
    - Administrative imposition of the $250 bond without statutory authority;
    - Cross-case coordination between the custody and PPO proceedings.

95. **Exception 2 — Complete Absence of All Jurisdiction:** Judicial immunity does not apply to acts "taken in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356-57 (1978). The jurisdiction to act ex parte under MCR 3.207(C)(2) is conditional — it requires specific sworn facts showing imminent danger. The absence of the required MCR 3.207(B) affidavit is a jurisdictional defect. McNeill's five ex parte orders of August 8, 2025, entered without this jurisdictional prerequisite, were entered in the complete absence of jurisdiction.

### B. The Dennis v. Sparks Conspiracy Exception

96. The conspiracy with Watson, Berry, and Rusco pierces judicial immunity. Under *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980), when a judge acts as a co-conspirator, the judge may be liable. The private co-conspirators — Watson, Berry, and Rusco — have **no judicial immunity whatsoever**. *Id.* at 28-29.

### C. Official-Capacity Claims

97. Regardless of individual immunity, Plaintiff seeks prospective injunctive and declaratory relief against McNeill and Rusco in their official capacities. *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984); *Ex parte Young*, 209 U.S. 123 (1908). Judicial immunity is no defense to claims for declaratory and injunctive relief.
