## I. INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors brings this civil rights action under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 against Judge Jenny L. McNeill, Emily A. Watson, Ronald Berry (non-attorney), Pamela Rusco, and Muskegon County, Michigan. This Memorandum addresses the principal legal issues this Court will confront at the threshold: federal abstention doctrines, judicial immunity, and the sufficiency of each of the seven counts.

This is not a case about a disputed custody ruling or a dissatisfied litigant seeking federal appellate review of a state judgment. This is an original federal civil rights action challenging the **process** — or total absence of process — by which Defendants systematically deprived a father of his fundamental constitutional rights over more than 200 consecutive days through 24 ex parte orders, 59 days of wrongful incarceration, and the imposition of a $250 financial barrier that now blocks all access to the state courts.

The scale of documented misconduct is staggering: 1,127 discrete judicial conduct violations across 19 categories, a documented pattern of parental alienation under MCL 722.23(j), 305 Watson family interference incidents, and three jobs lost to wrongful incarceration. These are not the hallmarks of judicial discretion — they are the hallmarks of systematic constitutional deprivation.

## II. STANDARD OF REVIEW

At the pleading stage, Plaintiff must state a plausible claim for relief under *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007), and *Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009). The Court accepts all well-pleaded factual allegations as true and draws all reasonable inferences in favor of the plaintiff. *Id.* Pro se complaints are held to "less stringent standards" than formal pleadings. *Haines v. Kerner*, 404 U.S. 519, 520 (1972); *Erickson v. Pardus*, 551 U.S. 89, 94 (2007).

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. Rooker-Feldman Does Not Bar This Action

#### 1. Rule

The Rooker-Feldman doctrine divests lower federal courts of jurisdiction only over cases that are, in substance, appeals of state court judgments. *Rooker v. Fidelity Trust Co.*, 263 U.S. 413 (1923); *District of Columbia Court of Appeals v. Feldman*, 460 U.S. 462 (1983). The doctrine is "narrow" and "confined to cases brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments rendered before the district court proceedings commenced and inviting district court review and rejection of those judgments." *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp.*, 544 U.S. 280, 284 (2005).

#### 2. Explanation

The Sixth Circuit applies Rooker-Feldman narrowly. The doctrine does not bar a federal § 1983 suit where the plaintiff's injury is caused by the defendant's *conduct* rather than by the state court *judgment* itself. *McCormick v. Braverman*, 451 F.3d 382, 393 (6th Cir. 2006). A plaintiff may challenge the constitutionality of the *process* used to reach a judgment without running afoul of Rooker-Feldman. *Id.*

#### 3. Application

Plaintiff does not ask this Court to reverse any custody order or substitute its judgment for the state court's. Plaintiff challenges:
- The **absence of notice** before entry of 24 ex parte orders;
- The **absence of a hearing** before suspension of fundamental rights;
- The **imposition of a $250 bond** as a retaliatory financial barrier;
- The **conspiracy** among Defendants to coordinate ex parte deprivations;
- The **59 days of wrongful incarceration** on contempt without adequate process.

These are independent federal injuries caused by Defendants' conduct — not complaints about the substance of state court judgments. Rooker-Feldman does not apply.

#### 4. Conclusion

This Court has jurisdiction. Plaintiff's claims are original, not appellate.

### B. Younger Abstention Does Not Apply

#### 1. Rule

Under *Younger v. Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971), federal courts generally should not interfere with ongoing state judicial proceedings. However, three well-established exceptions exist: (1) bad faith or harassment by state officials; (2) the state statute or procedure is flagrantly unconstitutional; or (3) there is no adequate opportunity to raise federal claims in the state forum. *Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n*, 457 U.S. 423, 432 (1982); *Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124 (1975).

#### 2. Application — All Three Exceptions Apply

**(a) Bad Faith and Harassment.** Twenty-four ex parte orders — entered without notice, without hearings, and without the mandatory MCR 3.207(B) affidavit — constitute bad faith. The $250 bond imposed in retaliation for protected filing activity is harassment. The 59 days of wrongful incarceration is coercion. The Sixth Circuit has recognized bad faith where "the state proceedings were brought to harass the federal plaintiff." *Fieger v. Cox*, 524 F.3d 770, 776 (6th Cir. 2008).

**(b) Flagrantly Unconstitutional Procedure.** The $250 bond requirement, which conditions a pro se father's access to the courts on the ability to pay for each motion, is flagrantly unconstitutional under *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971), particularly when fundamental parental rights are at stake.

**(c) No Adequate Opportunity to Raise Federal Claims.** The $250 bond blocks all new motions. The COA appeal (Case No. 366810) has been pending for months with no meaningful action. The Friend of Court is compromised by Rusco's documented bias. There is no adequate state forum in which Plaintiff can raise federal constitutional claims.

#### 3. Conclusion

Younger abstention is inappropriate. Federal intervention is warranted.

### C. The Domestic Relations Exception Is Inapplicable

The domestic relations exception is narrow: it divests federal courts of jurisdiction only over suits seeking to issue a divorce, alimony, or child custody decree. *Ankenbrandt v. Richards*, 504 U.S. 689, 703-04 (1992). The exception does not bar "federal-question jurisdiction to determine federal constitutional issues arising in the context of the domestic relations." *Id.* at 704.

Plaintiff does not seek a custody determination. Plaintiff seeks monetary damages for constitutional violations and injunctive relief to restore due process. This is squarely within federal jurisdiction under *Ankenbrandt*.

### D. Judicial Immunity Does Not Shield Defendant McNeill

#### 1. The Framework

Judicial immunity protects "truly judicial acts." *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11-12 (1991). Two exceptions strip immunity: (1) acts "not taken in the judge's judicial capacity" and (2) acts "taken in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Id.* Both exceptions are met here.

#### 2. Non-Judicial Acts (Exception One)

Under *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 227 (1988), the Supreme Court distinguished between judicial acts (adjudicating cases) and administrative or executive acts. Four categories of McNeill's conduct are non-judicial:

**(a) Self-Ruling on Disqualification.** MCR 2.003(D) assigns disqualification decisions to the Chief Judge when the challenged judge does not grant the motion. McNeill's decision to rule on her own disqualification was an administrative act assigned by rule to a different judicial officer. Under *Forrester*, administrative acts receive no immunity.

**(b) Ex Parte Communications.** Canon 3A(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct and MCR 2.003 prohibit ex parte communications. When McNeill engaged in ex parte coordination with Watson or Berry — evidenced by Berry's voicemail and the temporal coordination pattern — she was not performing a judicial function. *See Bright v. Gallia County*, 753 F.3d 639, 650 (6th Cir. 2014).

**(c) Cross-Case Coordination.** Using the PPO proceedings to generate orders for the custody case — with 30+ same-day events scoring 10/10 on weaponization analysis — is strategic case management, not adjudication.

**(d) Retaliatory Bond Imposition.** Imposing the $250 bond in retaliation for protected filing activity is punitive, not judicial.

#### 3. Complete Absence of Jurisdiction (Exception Two)

The jurisdiction to act ex parte under MCR 3.207(C)(2) is conditional — it requires a verified motion supported by an affidavit setting forth specific sworn facts showing imminent danger. *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356-57 (1978), holds that judicial immunity does not apply when a judge acts "in the clear absence of all jurisdiction."

On August 8, 2025, McNeill entered five ex parte orders without: (a) the mandatory MCR 3.207(B) affidavit; (b) sworn facts of imminent danger per MCR 3.207(C)(2); (c) notice to Plaintiff; or (d) any evidentiary hearing. These jurisdictional prerequisites are not procedural niceties — they are conditions that confer the authority to act ex parte. Without them, McNeill lacked jurisdiction. *See King v. Love*, 766 F.2d 962, 967 (6th Cir. 1985).

#### 4. The Scale Factor

Even if any single act might arguably be judicial, the aggregate pattern of **1,127 violations** across 19 categories — including 24 ex parte orders, 59 days of wrongful incarceration, and a $250 retaliatory bond — negates any characterization as ordinary judicial discretion. *See Barnes v. Winchell*, 105 F.3d 1111, 1116 (6th Cir. 1997) (pattern relevant to immunity analysis). One thousand violations is not discretion; it is deliberate indifference.

#### 5. Official-Capacity Claims Survive Regardless

Prospective injunctive and declaratory relief against McNeill in her official capacity is available regardless of individual immunity. *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984); *Ex parte Young*, 209 U.S. 123 (1908). The requested injunctions — restraining future ex parte orders, vacating the $250 bond, ordering case reassignment — are classic forms of prospective relief.

#### 6. Conclusion on Immunity

Judicial immunity does not bar any of Plaintiff's claims. The non-judicial act exception, the absence-of-jurisdiction exception, the conspiracy exception, and the availability of prospective relief collectively ensure that Plaintiff's claims survive immunity screening.

### E. The Conspiracy Claim Is Sufficient Under Dennis v. Sparks

Under *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980), private parties who corrupt a judicial officer act under color of state law for § 1983 purposes, and the judge's immunity does not extend to co-conspirators. *Id.* at 27-29.

**Agreement:** Circumstantial evidence of conspiracy is sufficient. *Halberstam v. Welch*, 705 F.2d 472 (D.C. Cir. 1983). The temporal coordination pattern — Watson files, McNeill rules same-day, Berry files within 48 hours — repeated across months with one-directional benefit, is powerful circumstantial evidence. Berry's voicemail is direct evidence. Rusco's biased recommendations adopted wholesale by McNeill establish her participation.

**Under Color of State Law:** Watson, Berry, and Rusco, by conspiring with Judge McNeill, acted under color of state law. *Dennis*, 449 U.S. at 27-28; *Adickes v. S.H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144, 152 (1970).

**Overt Acts:** Each of the 24 ex parte orders, the $250 bond, the biased FOC recommendations, the 305 Watson interference incidents, and Berry's ex parte communications are overt acts in furtherance of the conspiracy.

**Injury:** More than 200 consecutive days of parent-child separation, 59 days of incarceration, three jobs lost, severe emotional distress.

### F. The Monell Claim Is Viable

Municipal liability under *Monell v. Department of Social Services*, 436 U.S. 658 (1978), does not require respondeat superior. It requires: (1) an official policy or established custom; (2) attributable to the municipality; (3) that directly caused the constitutional deprivation. *Id.* at 694.

Plaintiff identifies multiple policies and customs:

1. **Rubber-stamping ex parte orders** without MCR 3.207 compliance verification — enabling 24 unchecked ex parte orders.
2. **Rubber-stamping FOC recommendations** without independent judicial review — making Rusco a de facto decision-maker.
3. **Failure to supervise** when McNeill self-ruled on disqualification in violation of MCR 2.003(D).
4. **Retaliatory bond imposition** without findings of vexatious litigation.
5. **Deliberate indifference** to 1,127 documented violations without corrective action. *City of Canton v. Harris*, 489 U.S. 378, 388 (1989).

A single decision by a final policymaker can establish Monell liability. *Pembaur v. City of Cincinnati*, 475 U.S. 469, 483 (1986). Municipalities cannot assert qualified immunity. *Owen v. City of Independence*, 445 U.S. 622 (1980).

### G. Each Constitutional Claim Satisfies Twombly/Iqbal

#### 1. Count I — Procedural Due Process (Fourteenth Amendment)

Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), Plaintiff must show: (a) a protected interest; (b) inadequate process. The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest. *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65. Twenty-four ex parte orders without notice, without hearing, and without the mandatory MCR 3.207(B) affidavit facially satisfy the deprivation element. Plaintiff pleads specific dates, specific orders, and specific procedural failures — well beyond the plausibility threshold.

#### 2. Count II — Substantive Due Process / Parental Liberty (Fourteenth Amendment)

The "shocks the conscience" test applies. *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833, 846 (1998). A total separation exceeding 200 consecutive days without a hearing, maintained through ex parte orders, enforced by wrongful incarceration and a financial barrier, producing a documented pattern of parental alienation under MCL 722.23(j) — this shocks the conscience of any reasonable observer. The Sixth Circuit recognizes this claim. *Doe v. Claiborne County*, 103 F.3d 495, 506 (6th Cir. 1996).

#### 3. Count III — Conspiracy (§ 1983 / § 1985(3))

As demonstrated in Section IV.E above, the conspiracy claim is supported by both direct evidence (Berry's voicemail) and powerful circumstantial evidence (temporal coordination, one-directional benefit pattern). This satisfies *Twombly*'s requirement of "enough facts to state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face." 550 U.S. at 570.

#### 4. Count IV — Monell (Municipal Liability)

As demonstrated in Section IV.F above, Plaintiff identifies specific policies, customs, and failures that directly caused the constitutional deprivation.

#### 5. Count V — Equal Protection (Fourteenth Amendment)

The disparate treatment table in the Complaint demonstrates systematic one-directional treatment: 100% of adverse actions against the father, 0% of sanctions against the mother. This pleads both sex-based discrimination (intermediate scrutiny under *Craig v. Boren*, 429 U.S. 190 (1976)) and a "class of one" claim under *Village of Willowbrook v. Olech*, 528 U.S. 562 (2000).

#### 6. Count VI — First Amendment Retaliation

Plaintiff pleads: (1) protected conduct (filing motions, disqualification motion, JTC complaint); (2) adverse action ($250 bond, escalating contempt, incarceration); (3) causal connection (temporal proximity — bond imposed immediately after challenging ex parte orders). *Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378, 394 (6th Cir. 1999) (en banc).

#### 7. Count VII — State Law Claims (Supplemental Jurisdiction)

IIED and abuse of process arise from the same operative facts. Supplemental jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1367 is proper. The "extreme and outrageous" element of IIED is met by the aggregate conduct — more than 200 consecutive days of separation, 59 days incarceration, $250 bond, 305 interference incidents. The "ulterior purpose" element of abuse of process is met by the one-directional pattern of harm.

## V. CONCLUSION

Plaintiff has stated plausible claims across all seven counts. The jurisdictional defenses — Rooker-Feldman, Younger, domestic relations exception — do not apply. Judicial immunity is pierced by non-judicial acts, absence of jurisdiction, and conspiracy. The Monell claim identifies specific policies and customs. The First Amendment retaliation claim is supported by direct temporal evidence. The state-law claims are within supplemental jurisdiction.

This Court should deny any motion to dismiss and permit this case to proceed to discovery, where the full scope of Defendants' coordinated deprivation — documented across 1,127 judicial violations and more than 200 consecutive days of complete parent-child separation — can be established.
