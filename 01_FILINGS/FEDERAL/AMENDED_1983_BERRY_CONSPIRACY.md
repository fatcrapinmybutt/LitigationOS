## V. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY DOES NOT BAR THESE CLAIMS

55\. Judicial immunity is not absolute. The Supreme Court has recognized two exceptions: (1) actions taken in the **clear absence of all jurisdiction**, and (2) actions that are **not judicial in nature**. *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11–12 (1991); *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356–57 (1978).

56\. A third exception applies with equal force: **when a judge conspires with private parties**, the conspiratorial acts fall outside the scope of judicial immunity. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 28–29 (1980) ("Immunity . . . does not change the character of the judge's action or that of his co-conspirators."). The private co-conspirators — Watson, Ronald Berry, and Cavan Berry — have **no judicial immunity whatsoever**. *Id.*

57\. Judge McNeill's actions fall within all three exceptions:

&emsp;(a) **Clear absence of jurisdiction**: A judge who presides over a case involving the domestic partner of her husband's relative, without disclosure, acts in the clear absence of all jurisdiction. The proceedings were void ab initio due to the structural conflict of interest;

&emsp;(b) **Non-judicial acts**: Ruling on her own disqualification motion is an administrative act, not a judicial one, as MCR 2.003(D) assigns that function to the Chief Judge. *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 229 (1988) (administrative acts are not immune);

&emsp;(c) **Conspiracy exception**: McNeill's participation in a conspiracy with Watson and the Berry family to deprive Plaintiff of his rights strips her of judicial immunity for all acts taken in furtherance of that conspiracy. *Dennis*, 449 U.S. at 28–29.

58\. As to Chief Judge Hoopes, failing to provide impartial review of a disqualification motion against his former law partner is an administrative act, not a judicial one. Hoopes is not immune for administrative failures that perpetuate constitutional violations. *Forrester*, 484 U.S. at 229.
