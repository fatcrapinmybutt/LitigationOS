### VI. STRUCTURAL IMPOSSIBILITY OF LOCAL REMEDY

27. When I became aware of Judge McNeill's potential conflict, I filed a Motion for Judicial Disqualification under MCR 2.003. The Court imposed a **$250 bond** on this motion.

28. I am indigent. I lost employment at the United States Postal Service and a second position as a direct result of my incarceration on contempt orders issued by Judge McNeill. I lost two homes. I cannot afford a $250 bond.

29. The $250 bond effectively blocked my access to the statutory disqualification process — the very mechanism designed to protect litigants from biased judges.

30. Even if I had been able to post the bond, the disqualification motion would have been decided by **Chief Judge Hoopes** — McNeill's former law partner at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill. I do not believe Chief Judge Hoopes can impartially adjudicate a disqualification motion against his former law partner.

31. I have also filed a complaint with the Judicial Tenure Commission (JTC). However, the JTC process does not provide individual relief to litigants — it addresses judicial discipline, not the remedy of specific cases.

32. I have also filed a claim of appeal with the Michigan Court of Appeals (Case No. 366810). That appeal is pending. However, the standard appellate timeline of 12–24 months cannot provide the immediate relief required by the ongoing separation of my child from me. Each month of delay is another month of irreparable harm.

33. I have exhausted every available remedy in the 14th Circuit Court. The circuit is structurally incapable of providing impartial justice in my case.
