## FINDINGS OF FACT

1. **Jurisdiction.** This Court has jurisdiction pursuant to Const 1963, art 6, § 4, which vests in this Court "general superintending control over all courts," and MCR 7.304 and MCR 7.306.

2. **Structural Conflict — Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill Law Firm.** The law firm of Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill, formerly located at 435 Whitehall Road, North Muskegon, Michigan, has produced three sitting judges now serving on Muskegon County courts:
   - Hon. Kenneth S. Hoopes (P53469) — Chief Judge, 14th Circuit Court and Probate Court;
   - Hon. Maria Ladas-Hoopes — Judge, 60th District Court;
   - Hon. Jenny L. McNeill (P58235) — Judge, 14th Circuit Court, Family Division.

3. **Undisclosed Familial Connection.** Judge McNeill is married to Cavan John Berry, an attorney who practices family law in Muskegon County. Upon information and belief, Cavan Berry is related to Ronald Berry, who is in a domestic relationship with Defendant Emily A. Watson. This connection was not disclosed by Judge McNeill at any point during more than 290 days of proceedings.

4. **Automatic Disqualification.** The familial connection described in Finding 3 triggers automatic disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), because a person within the third degree of relationship to the judge's spouse has a more than *de minimis* interest in the outcome of the proceeding.

5. **Chief Judge Conflict.** Chief Judge Hoopes, who would ordinarily adjudicate a motion to disqualify Judge McNeill, is McNeill's former law partner at Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill. He is incapable of performing this function impartially.

6. **No Adequate Local Remedy.** The 14th Judicial Circuit Court is structurally incapable of providing an impartial forum for these proceedings. Every available local remedy — disqualification motion, Chief Judge review, FOC intervention — is compromised by the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill network.

7. **Failure to Perform Legal Duties.** The 14th Circuit Court, through Judge McNeill, failed to:
   - Conduct best interest analysis under MCL 722.23 before modifying custody;
   - Make specific findings under MCL 722.27a(3) before modifying parenting time;
   - Hold hearings within 21 days of ex parte orders per MCR 3.207(C);
   - Enforce parenting time orders per MCL 552.644;
   - Disclose the presiding judge's familial conflict per MCR 2.003(B).

8. **Separation of Parent and Child.** L.D.W., a minor child, has been separated from Plaintiff-Relator for 290+ consecutive days, following the ex parte revocation of a 50/50 custody arrangement that functioned successfully for 438 days. No finding of parental unfitness has ever been made.

9. **Wrongful Incarceration.** Plaintiff-Relator was incarcerated for 59 days on civil contempt orders entered without the procedural protections required by *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987), *Mead v. Batchlor*, 435 Mich 480 (1990), and *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011).

10. **Constitutional Barrier.** A $250 bond was imposed on Plaintiff-Relator's MCR 2.003 disqualification motion, creating an unconstitutional financial barrier to the exercise of a fundamental right for an indigent pro se litigant.

---

## CONCLUSIONS OF LAW

11. This Court's exercise of superintending control is warranted under the three-part test of *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426 (1993): (a) the lower court failed to perform clear legal duties; (b) the lower court exceeded its jurisdiction by proceeding while the presiding judge was automatically disqualified; and (c) no adequate legal remedy exists in the lower court.

12. The extraordinary circumstances — three judges from one firm, undisclosed familial conflict, structurally compromised Chief Judge, 290+ day separation of parent and child — further warrant this Court's intervention.

13. Judge McNeill was disqualified *ab initio* under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). Orders entered by a disqualified judge are void under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d).

14. The pattern of conduct described herein — non-disclosure, one-sided rulings, financial barriers to disqualification, and prolonged separation of parent and child — constitutes a deprivation of due process under the Fourteenth Amendment and Mich. Const. art 1, § 17, under the standards of *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), and *Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510 (1927).

### B. VACATUR OF ORDERS

17. The following orders entered by Judge McNeill in Case Nos. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP are hereby **VACATED**:

   (a) All orders entered ex parte without notice to Plaintiff-Relator, to the extent they affect custody, parenting time, or financial obligations;

   (b) The ex parte order of approximately August 8, 2025, suspending all parenting time;

   (c) The contempt orders resulting in 59 days of incarceration, to the extent purge conditions exceeded Plaintiff-Relator's ability to pay without an adequate determination under *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011);

   (d) The $250 bond requirement imposed on Plaintiff-Relator's MCR 2.003 disqualification motion;

   (e) The child support order, to the extent it was entered without an evidentiary hearing on Plaintiff-Relator's actual income.

18. The newly assigned judge shall, within thirty (30) days of reassignment, review all remaining orders and determine whether any additional orders require modification or vacatur.

   (b) The effect of the 290+ day separation on L.D.W. and the parent-child bond;
   (c) An appropriate parenting time schedule;
   (d) Any safety concerns.

   (d) Status conferences at intervals of no more than 45 days.

29. All future proceedings shall comply with due process requirements, including adequate notice to all parties and the opportunity to be heard.

### G. SCAO ADMINISTRATIVE REVIEW AND STRUCTURAL REFORM

30. The State Court Administrative Office shall conduct an administrative review of the 14th Judicial Circuit Court **and the 60th District Court, Muskegon County**, including but not limited to:

   (a) **Conflict-of-Interest Screening**: Review the courts' existing procedures for screening and disclosing judicial conflicts of interest, with specific attention to the failure to identify or disclose the familial relationship between Judge McNeill and Defendant Watson through Ronald Berry and Cavan Berry;

   (b) **Judicial Assignment Practices**: Audit the process by which judges are assigned to cases, including whether the concentration of three sitting judges (Hoopes, McNeill, and Ladas-Hoopes) from the same law firm (Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill, 435 Whitehall Road, North Muskegon) creates a structural integrity concern requiring reform;

   (c) **Dual-Role Service**: Investigate the practice of permitting dual-role service by court personnel, specifically the simultaneous service of Pamela Rusco as judicial secretary to Judge McNeill and as Friend of the Court officer;

   (d) **Three-Court Pattern Investigation**: Investigate the pattern of adverse rulings against Plaintiff-Relator across three separate courts — the 60th District Court (eviction, before Judge Ladas-Hoopes), the 14th Circuit Court, Civil Division (Case No. 2025-002760-CZ, before Chief Judge Hoopes), and the 14th Circuit Court, Family Division (Case No. 2024-001507-DC, before Judge McNeill) — to determine whether this pattern reflects coordinated action, shared professional loyalty, or structural bias arising from the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill professional network;

   (e) **Reassignment Procedures**: Develop and recommend standardized reassignment procedures for cases where structural conflicts preclude fair adjudication within the circuit.

31. SCAO shall issue an administrative order, or recommend that this Court issue such an order, reassigning all pending and future proceedings in *Pigors v. Watson* (Case Nos. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP) to a judicial circuit outside Muskegon County, selecting a circuit with no connection to the Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill law firm or its former members.

32. SCAO shall review whether the pattern of three judges ascending from a single law firm to the Muskegon County bench — Hoopes (14th Circuit, Chief Judge), McNeill (14th Circuit, Family Division), and Ladas-Hoopes (60th District Court) — constitutes a systemic judicial integrity concern warranting structural reform, including but not limited to enhanced conflict screening, mandatory recusal protocols, and diversified judicial recruitment.

33. SCAO shall file a comprehensive report with this Court within ninety (90) days of this Order, including:

   (a) Findings regarding each area of review described in paragraphs 30-32;
   (b) Specific recommendations for structural reform;
   (c) A proposed timeline for implementation of recommended reforms;
   (d) Any interim protective measures needed to safeguard pending cases.
