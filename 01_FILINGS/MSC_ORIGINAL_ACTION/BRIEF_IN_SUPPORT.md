## I. THE STANDARD FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL

### A. The Three-Part *Hatcher* Test

This Court has established a three-part test for the exercise of superintending control in *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–42 (1993). The petitioner must demonstrate:

> **(1)** The lower court has failed to perform a clear legal duty;
> **(2)** The lower court has exceeded its jurisdiction or authority; and
> **(3)** The petitioner has no adequate legal remedy.

*See also Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347 (1975); *Judges of 74th Judicial Dist v. Bay County*, 385 Mich 710 (1971).

All three elements are satisfied here — and the extraordinary circumstances of structural judicial capture warrant the most vigorous exercise of this Court's superintending authority.

### B. The First Element: Failure to Perform Clear Legal Duties

The 14th Circuit Court, through Judge McNeill, failed to perform at least **five distinct clear legal duties**:

**Duty 1: Best Interest Analysis (MCL 722.23).** Michigan law requires that custody determinations be based on an evaluation of the twelve best interest factors codified at MCL 722.23(a)–(l). *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010). Judge McNeill modified custody without conducting this analysis. This is not a discretionary call — it is a mandatory statutory duty.

**Duty 2: Specific Findings Before Modifying Parenting Time (MCL 722.27a(3)).** Before modifying an existing parenting time order, the court must make specific findings. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509 (2003). No such findings were made.

**Duty 3: Hearing Within 21 Days of Ex Parte Order (MCR 3.207(C)).** When an ex parte order is entered, MCR 3.207(C) requires a hearing within 21 days. The ex parte order of August 8, 2025 suspending all parenting time was not followed by the required hearing.

**Duty 4: Enforcement of Parenting Time (MCL 552.644).** MCL 552.644 imposes a mandatory duty on the court to enforce parenting time orders. Despite documented violations by Watson, the Court took no enforcement action.

**Duty 5: Disclosure of Disqualifying Conflict (MCR 2.003(B)).** MCR 2.003(B) requires a judge to disqualify herself when disqualifying grounds exist. The duty to disclose potential grounds for disqualification is inherent in this obligation. McNeill failed to disclose the Berry family connection for 290+ days.

### C. The Second Element: Excess of Jurisdiction

A judge who is automatically disqualified under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) and who fails to recuse acts **without jurisdiction**. Orders entered by a judge acting without jurisdiction are **void** under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d). *See Lapeer County Clerk v. Lapeer Circuit Court*, 469 Mich 146 (2003).

Judge McNeill was disqualified because her husband's family member (Ronald Berry, upon information and belief) is in a domestic relationship with a party (Watson). This satisfies MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(iii) — a person within the third degree of relationship to the judge's spouse has a more than *de minimis* interest in the outcome of the proceeding.

Every order McNeill entered after the point at which she knew or should have known of the Berry-Watson connection was entered in excess of her jurisdiction.

### D. The Third Element: No Adequate Legal Remedy

As set forth in the Complaint at Section IV.G, Plaintiff-Relator has exhausted every available remedy:

| Remedy Attempted | Result |
|---|---|
| Motion for Disqualification (MCR 2.003) | Blocked by $250 bond |
| Chief Judge Review | Hoopes = McNeill's former law partner |
| Other 14th Circuit Judges | All within firm's influence network |
| FOC | Rusco = McNeill's judicial secretary |
| COA Appeal (No. 366810) | Pending — 1-2 year timeline; no emergency relief |
| JTC Complaint | Filed — no individual relief available |

The 14th Circuit Court is structurally incapable of self-correction. Only this Court can provide an independent, impartial forum.

### E. Extraordinary Circumstances

Even where the three elements might be debatable, this Court retains authority to exercise superintending control in "extraordinary circumstances." *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 443.

The circumstances here are extraordinary by any measure:

1. **Three judges from one law firm** control two Muskegon County courts;
2. **The presiding judge has an undisclosed familial connection** to the opposing party;
3. **The Chief Judge is the presiding judge's former law partner** and cannot independently adjudicate the conflict;
4. **A young child has been separated from his father** for 290+ days without a finding of unfitness;
5. **The father was jailed for 59 days** under contempt orders issued by the conflicted judge;
6. **Every local remedy is structurally compromised**.

This is not merely an "extraordinary circumstance." It is a systemic failure of judicial independence in a Michigan county that only this Court's plenary superintending authority can correct.

## II. THE 14TH CIRCUIT COURT FAILED TO PERFORM CLEAR LEGAL DUTIES

### A. Failure to Conduct Best Interest Analysis

Under MCL 722.23, the court must evaluate twelve factors in determining the best interests of the child:

> (a) The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.
> (b) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.
> (c) The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care recognized and permitted under the laws of this state in place of medical care, and other material needs.
> (d) The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.
> (e) The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.
> (f) The moral fitness of the parties involved.
> (g) The mental and physical health of the parties involved.
> (h) The home, school, and community record of the child.
> (i) The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.
> (j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents.
> (k) Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.
> (l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

Judge McNeill eliminated Plaintiff-Relator's parenting time without making findings on any of these factors. The 438-day successful custody arrangement — the strongest possible evidence that L.D.W.'s best interests were served by shared custody — was destroyed by judicial fiat without the analysis that MCL 722.23 makes mandatory.

This is not a case where the court conducted the analysis but weighed the factors differently than Plaintiff-Relator would prefer. The analysis was **never conducted**. That is a failure to perform a clear legal duty.

### B. Failure to Hold Required Hearings

MCR 3.207(B) requires that ex parte orders affecting custody or parenting time be entered only upon a showing of "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage." MCR 3.207(C) requires that a hearing be held within 21 days of any such order.

The ex parte order of approximately August 8, 2025, suspending all parenting time, was entered without the required showing and without the required follow-up hearing. The order was entered **without notice** to Plaintiff-Relator — he had no opportunity to be heard before his parenting rights were eliminated.

This violated not only the court rule but the most basic requirements of procedural due process. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) (due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before the government deprives an individual of a protected interest).

### C. Failure to Enforce Parenting Time

MCL 552.644 provides that the court "shall" take enforcement action when parenting time is denied. The word "shall" imposes a mandatory duty. Despite documented parenting time violations by Watson, the Court failed to enforce its own orders.

This failure is not merely prejudicial to Plaintiff-Relator — it is directly harmful to L.D.W. Michigan law recognizes that children benefit from a relationship with both parents, and the court has a duty to facilitate that relationship. *Ireland v. Smith*, 451 Mich 457, 463 (1996).

### D. Failure to Disclose Disqualifying Conflict

MCR 2.003(B) provides that a judge "shall disqualify himself or herself" in any proceeding where disqualification is required. The duty is **mandatory and self-executing** — the judge must act sua sponte when she knows or should know of disqualifying grounds.

Judge McNeill knew she was married to Cavan Berry. She knew (or should have known) of Cavan Berry's relationship to Ronald Berry. She knew (or should have known) that Ronald Berry was in a relationship with Defendant Watson. The duty to investigate potential conflicts and to disclose them is part of the judicial function.

Her silence for 290+ days is not mere oversight. It is a **sustained failure** to perform one of the most fundamental duties of a judge — the duty to ensure that the parties receive a fair trial before an impartial tribunal.

## III. DUE PROCESS REQUIRES VACATUR OF ALL McNEILL ORDERS

### A. The *Caperton*–*Tumey* Framework

The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause requires "a fair trial in a fair tribunal." *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 876 (2009). This principle is "implemented by the requirement that a judge be disqualified where there is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions." *Id.* at 886.

The *Caperton* standard does not require proof of actual bias. It requires only that "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 872.

This standard derives from *Tumey v. Ohio*, 273 U.S. 510 (1927), where the Court held that due process is violated when a judge has "a direct, personal, substantial, pecuniary interest in reaching a conclusion against" a party — or, more broadly, when the situation creates "a possible temptation to the average man as a judge . . . not to hold the balance nice, clear, and true." *Id.* at 532.

### B. Application to This Case

The facts here satisfy the *Caperton*–*Tumey* standard with room to spare:

1. **Judge McNeill is married to Cavan John Berry.** This is not disputed — it is confirmed by public records.

2. **Cavan Berry is, upon information and belief, related to Ronald Berry.** The evidence strongly supports a familial connection.

3. **Ronald Berry is, upon information and belief, in a domestic relationship with Defendant Watson.** Watson and Ronald Berry share a household with L.D.W.

4. **This means Judge McNeill was presiding over a custody case involving her husband's family member's domestic partner.** Every ruling she made directly affected the household of a person within the third degree of relationship to her spouse.

The "probability of actual bias" in this scenario is not merely "too high to be constitutionally tolerable." It approaches certainty. No reasonable, objective observer could conclude that a judge in this position would — or could — "hold the balance nice, clear, and true." *Tumey*, 273 U.S. at 532.

The pattern of one-sided rulings confirms what the structural analysis predicts:

| Watson's Treatment | Pigors' Treatment |
|---|---|
| Ex parte requests consistently granted | Motions denied or left without ruling |
| Custody expanded to sole | Parenting time eliminated entirely |
| Permitted to take child out of country | Jailed for 59 days |
| No enforcement action despite violations | $250 bond on disqualification motion |

### C. The Non-Disclosure Amplifies the Violation

Under *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (2014), the failure to disclose disqualifying information is itself an independent ground for disqualification. McNeill's 290+ days of silence compounded the violation with each successive hearing and order.

The non-disclosure was not passive — it was active concealment. Each time McNeill took the bench in this case, she made an implicit representation that she was qualified to preside. Each time was false.

### D. The $250 Bond as Constitutional Barrier

When Plaintiff-Relator attempted to exercise his statutory right to seek disqualification under MCR 2.003, Judge McNeill imposed a $250 bond. For an indigent, pro se litigant who had lost his job and his home (in part due to McNeill's own contempt orders), this bond was an insurmountable barrier.

The United States Supreme Court has consistently held that the government may not condition access to fundamental judicial processes on the ability to pay. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371, 382 (1971) (state may not deny access to court due to inability to pay fees in divorce proceedings); *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102, 116–17 (1996) (state may not condition appellate review of termination of parental rights on ability to pay for transcript).

The right to a fair trial before an impartial judge is at least as fundamental as the right to file for divorce or to appeal a termination of parental rights. A bond requirement that effectively denies an indigent litigant access to the disqualification process is unconstitutional.

## IV. THE CONSPIRACY CHAIN UNDER 42 USC § 1983

### A. *Dennis v. Sparks* — Private Conspirators Lose Immunity

In *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980), the United States Supreme Court addressed whether private parties who conspire with a judge to fix cases are liable under 42 USC § 1983. The Court held unequivocally that they are:

> "The conduct of private parties acting in concert with state officials may be attributable to the State. . . . [P]rivate persons, jointly engaged with state officials in the challenged action, are acting 'under color' of law for purposes of § 1983 actions." *Id.* at 27–28.

The Court further held that while judges may enjoy absolute immunity for their judicial acts, "a judicial officer acting in the clear absence of all jurisdiction is not immune." And critically, "a private party who corrupts a judicial officer" is not entitled to share in that immunity. *Id.* at 29.

### B. The McNeill–Berry–Watson Chain

The conspiracy chain is not speculative. Each link is documented:

| Link | Connection | Source |
|---|---|---|
| McNeill → Cavan Berry | Marriage | Public records: address, attorney profiles, ICLE |
| Cavan Berry → Ronald Berry | Familial (on info & belief) | Surname, locality, circumstantial evidence |
| Ronald Berry → Watson | Domestic relationship (on info & belief) | Personal knowledge, observation |
| Watson → Favorable Rulings | Direct beneficiary of McNeill's orders | Court docket, orders, pattern evidence |

Plaintiff-Relator does not allege an express agreement to fix the case. *Dennis* does not require one. What is required is that private parties "jointly engaged" with a state official in conduct that deprives a citizen of constitutional rights. *Id.* at 27–28.

The chain of relationships, combined with the pattern of one-sided rulings and the sustained non-disclosure, creates a strong circumstantial case that Watson — whether through active conspiracy or willful blindness — participated in the deprivation of Plaintiff-Relator's constitutional rights.

### C. Pattern Evidence as Circumstantial Proof

The pattern of rulings is relevant not as independent proof of bias (which can be debated) but as **corroboration of the structural conflict**. When a judge has an undisclosed familial connection to one party and the rulings consistently favor that party, the pattern is evidence of the very bias that the structural analysis predicts.

Key pattern indicators:

- **Ex parte orders**: Multiple ex parte orders suspending Pigors' parenting time — none suspending Watson's;
- **Contempt**: Pigors incarcerated for 59 days — Watson never sanctioned despite documented parenting time violations;
- **International travel**: Watson permitted to remove L.D.W. from the country — Pigors denied all contact;
- **Financial barriers**: $250 bond on Pigors' disqualification motion — no financial barriers imposed on Watson;
- **Motion disposition**: Watson's motions granted; Pigors' motions denied, ignored, or blocked.

### D. Judicial Immunity Does Not Bar Relief

Judge McNeill may assert judicial immunity. However:

1. **Judicial immunity does not apply when a judge acts "in the clear absence of all jurisdiction."** *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 12 (1991). A judge who is automatically disqualified under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) and who continues to preside acts without jurisdiction.

2. **Judicial immunity does not bar injunctive relief.** *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984). The primary relief sought here — reassignment, transfer, and vacatur — is injunctive in nature.

3. **Private co-conspirators have no immunity.** *Dennis*, 449 U.S. at 29. Watson cannot claim any derivative judicial immunity.

## V. MONELL LIABILITY OF THE COUNTY OF MUSKEGON

### A. The Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill Pipeline as Policy/Custom

Under *Monell v. Dep't of Social Services*, 436 U.S. 658 (1978), a municipality is liable under § 1983 when unconstitutional conduct is caused by an official "policy" or by a governmental "custom" — even one not formally adopted. *Id.* at 690–91.

The County of Muskegon has permitted — and, through its appointment and election processes, actively facilitated — the concentration of judicial power from a single law firm into three judicial seats:

| Judge | Firm Origin | Court | How Appointed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kenneth S. Hoopes | Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill | 14th Circuit (Chief Judge) | Governor Whitmer, 2022 |
| Maria Ladas-Hoopes | Ladas (daughter of founder) | 60th District | Governor Granholm, 2006 |
| Jenny L. McNeill | Ladas, Hoopes & McNeill | 14th Circuit, Family Division | Elected, 2022 |

This is not coincidence. It is a **pipeline** — a systematic pattern of judicial appointments and elections from a politically connected firm that spans two decades and two governors. The result is a county judiciary dominated by a single professional and familial network.

Under *Pembaur v. City of Cincinnati*, 475 U.S. 469 (1986), municipal liability attaches when "a deliberate choice to follow a course of action is made from among various alternatives." The County's acquiescence in the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill judicial monopoly — its failure to implement conflict screening, its failure to ensure judicial independence — constitutes such a choice.

### B. Failure to Screen for Conflicts

The County has no policy, procedure, or mechanism to:

1. Identify judicial conflicts arising from shared law firm backgrounds;
2. Screen for familial connections between judges' families and litigants;
3. Prevent dual roles (such as Rusco's combined service as judicial secretary and FOC);
4. Ensure that the Chief Judge can independently adjudicate disqualification motions against judges who were his own law partners.

This failure is not merely negligent — it is "deliberately indifferent" to the obvious risk that the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill pipeline would produce exactly the kind of structural conflict presented here. Under *Cady v. Arenac County*, 574 F.3d 334 (6th Cir. 2009), and *Gregory v.
