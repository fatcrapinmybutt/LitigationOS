## SPECIFIC SCAO CONCERNS

I respectfully direct SCAO's attention to the following matters within its administrative oversight authority:

### 1. Failure of Conflict-of-Interest Screening

Judge McNeill presided over *Pigors v. Watson* for more than **290 consecutive days** without disclosing her familial connection to the opposing party through the Berry family. Michigan's conflict screening procedures failed to identify or flag this relationship at the outset. SCAO should review whether the 14th Circuit's screening procedures are adequate and, if not, what reforms are needed.

### 2. The Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill Judicial Pipeline

Three judges from a single law firm now occupy seats on two Muskegon County courts. This concentration raises systemic concerns about judicial independence, recusal capacity, and the ability of the circuit to self-correct when one of its own members has a conflict. SCAO should evaluate whether this pattern — three judges from one firm — constitutes a structural integrity concern warranting diversified judicial recruitment or enhanced conflict protocols.

### 3. Dual-Role Court Personnel

**Pamela Rusco** serves simultaneously as Judge McNeill's **judicial secretary** and as a **Friend of the Court (FOC) officer** in my case. The FOC is supposed to function as a neutral officer of the court. Her dual role creates an inherent conflict and compromises the independence of FOC recommendations. SCAO should investigate whether this dual-role practice is occurring elsewhere and whether it violates SCAO administrative standards.

### 4. The $250 Bond Barrier

When I attempted to file a motion for disqualification under MCR 2.003, the court required a **$250 bond** — a financial barrier that, for a litigant who has lost two jobs and two homes as a direct result of wrongful incarceration in this very case, effectively blocks the only local remedy available. SCAO should consider whether this bond requirement, as applied, creates an unconstitutional barrier to access to justice.
