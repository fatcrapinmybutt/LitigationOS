## PART I: THE EX PARTE REVIEW VIOLATION

### A. The Violation

Judge McNeill and her staff reviewed the video recording of the phone call **before** Father was served with the evidence and **before** Father was given notice of the ex parte hearing.

**Timeline of Events:**
- **August 5, 2025 (Friday):** Father's scheduled parenting time
- **Same day (August 5, 2025):** Emily Watson submits USB recording to clerk's office
- **Same day (August 5, 2025):** Judge McNeill's staff listens to recording
- **Same day (August 5, 2025):** Judge McNeill issues suspension order
- **After August 5, 2025:** Father receives notice of suspension

### B. Legal Standard Violated

**MCR 3.207(B)** provides: "A court may issue an ex parte order only when it appears on the basis of specific facts shown by affidavit or by the verified complaint that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result to a party before that party can be heard in response."

The rule requires:
1. **Affidavit or verified complaint** - Not satisfied (USB recording is neither)
2. **Specific facts** - Not satisfied (unverified allegations)
3. **Immediate and irreparable injury** - Not satisfied (no emergency)
4. **Before party can be heard** - **VIOLATED** (Father not heard before order issued)

**MCR 2.305** governs discovery and service of evidence. Evidence must be served on the opposing party before it can be used in court proceedings.

**MRE 901** requires authentication of evidence. A recording must be authenticated by:
- Testimony from someone with personal knowledge
- Proper foundation
- Verification of accuracy

**14th Amendment Due Process** requires that a party have notice and an opportunity to be heard before deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest (parental rights).

### C. Judge McNeill's Specific Violations

**Violation 1: Ex Parte Review Before Service**

Judge McNeill reviewed the USB recording before Father was served with it. This is a clear violation of:
- MCR 2.305 (service of evidence)
- MCR 3.207 (ex parte procedures)
- Due Process (notice and opportunity to be heard)

**Evidence:**
- USB recording submitted directly to clerk's office (not served on Father)
- Judge's staff listened to recording same day
- Father not served until 4 days later (Friday before Monday hearing)
- Judge issued order same day without hearing from Father

**Violation 2: Prejudicial Pre-Judgment**

By listening to the recording before Father could respond, Judge McNeill prejudged the case. She formed opinions based on:
- One-sided evidence
- Unverified allegations
- Father's inability to provide context or explanation

This violates:
- MCR 2.003 (impartiality requirement)
- Due Process (fair hearing requirement)
- Judicial Code of Conduct (impartiality)

**Evidence:**
- Judge admitted on record that she had her staff listen to recording
- Father asked at first objection hearing if judge had already listened
- Judge confirmed she had her staff listen
- No hearing before order issued

**Violation 3: Improper Evidence Submission**

Emily Watson submitted the USB recording directly to the clerk's office instead of serving it on Father. This is improper because:
- Evidence must be served on opposing party (MCR 2.305)
- Clerk's office is not proper recipient of evidence
- Judge should not accept evidence not properly served
- Judge should have rejected evidence and ordered proper service

**Evidence:**
- USB submitted directly to clerk's office
- Not served on Father at time of submission
- Judge accepted evidence without objection
- Judge's staff reviewed evidence before Father received it

### D. Prejudicial Effect

The ex parte review resulted in:
- Deprivation of Father's right to be heard before deprivation of liberty interest
- Prejudicial review of evidence by judge before Father could respond
- Formation of judicial bias based on one-sided evidence
- Erroneous suspension order based on unverified evidence
- Violation of Father's fundamental due process rights

## PART II: THE INADEQUATE SERVICE VIOLATION

### A. The Violation

Emily Watson served Father with the video recording only **4 days before the hearing**, on a **Friday**, with the hearing scheduled for **Monday**. This violates MCR 2.305 and due process.

**Timeline:**
- **Monday, August 11, 2025:** Second hearing on objections
- **Friday, August 8, 2025:** Emily Watson serves Father via email (3 days before hearing)
- **Father's response time:** 3 business days to review evidence, consult attorney, prepare response

### B. Legal Standard Violated

**MCR 2.305** governs discovery and service. The rule requires:
- Service of evidence at least **5 business days** before hearing (or as ordered by court)
- Service by proper means (mail, hand delivery, email with confirmation)
- Adequate time for opposing party to review and respond

**MCR 2.119** governs motions. The rule requires:
- Service of motion at least **5 business days** before hearing
- Adequate time for opposing party to respond

**Due Process** requires:
- Adequate notice of charges/allegations
- Adequate time to prepare response
- Opportunity to present evidence and arguments

### C. Judge McNeill's Violation

Judge McNeill allowed Emily Watson to serve evidence only 3 days before hearing, violating MCR 2.305 and due process. She:
- Did not require proper service
- Did not enforce 5-day rule
- Did not grant continuance for Father to prepare
- Proceeded with hearing despite inadequate service

**Evidence:**
- Email service on Friday, August 8, 2025
- Hearing on Monday, August 11, 2025
- Only 3 business days for Father to review and respond
- No continuance granted
- Hearing proceeded as scheduled

## PART III: THE UNDISCLOSED SECOND EVALUATION VIOLATION

### A. The Violation

Judge McNeill ordered Father to obtain a mental health evaluation from HealthWest. Father obtained the evaluation honestly and was instructed by Judge McNeill to send it directly to her secretary via email instead of filing it with the clerk's office.

Judge McNeill then obtained a **second mental health evaluation** that Father never received, never saw, and never had opportunity to challenge or respond to.

### B. Legal Standard Violated

**MCR 3.210** governs custody procedures. The rule requires:
- All evidence be available to both parties
- All evidence be properly authenticated
- Both parties have opportunity to challenge evidence

**MRE 901** requires authentication of evidence. Expert evaluations must be:
- Properly obtained
- Properly authenticated
- Available to opposing party
- Subject to cross-examination

**MRE 702-703** govern expert testimony. Expert evaluations must:
- Be based on reliable methodology
- Be properly authenticated
- Be subject to challenge

**Due Process** requires:
- Access to all evidence against you
- Opportunity to challenge evidence
- Right to confront witnesses/evidence

### C. Judge McNeill's Specific Violations

**Violation 1: Directing Non-Compliance with Filing Procedures**

Judge McNeill specifically instructed Father to send the HealthWest evaluation to her secretary via email instead of filing it with the clerk's office. This is improper because:
- Court documents must be filed with clerk's office (MCR 3.101)
- Judge should not receive documents outside normal filing procedures
- This creates appearance of impropriety
- This prevents other parties from accessing documents
- This violates open court principle

**Evidence:**
- Judge McNeill's instruction to email evaluation to secretary
- Evaluation sent to secretary, not filed with clerk
- Emily Watson may not have received copy
- Evaluation not in public record

**Violation 2: Obtaining Undisclosed Second Evaluation**

Judge McNeill obtained a second mental health evaluation that:
- Father did not request
- Father did not authorize
- Father never received
- Father never saw
- Father never had opportunity to challenge

**Evidence:**
- Judge McNeill referenced "second" evaluation in court orders
- Father never received copy of second evaluation
- Second evaluation not in public record
- Second evaluation used to justify continued suspension

**Violation 3: Using Undisclosed Evidence Against Father**

Judge McNeill used the second evaluation to support her orders without:
- Providing Father with copy
- Allowing Father to challenge evaluation
- Allowing Father to cross-examine evaluator
- Allowing Father to obtain counter-evaluation
- Issuing findings about evaluation

**Evidence:**
- Court orders reference "second" evaluation
- Orders cite second evaluation as basis for decisions
- Father had no opportunity to respond
- Father had no opportunity to challenge

### D. Prejudicial Effect

The undisclosed second evaluation resulted in:
- Deprivation of Father's right to access evidence
- Deprivation of Father's right to challenge evidence
- Deprivation of Father's right to confront evidence
- Use of unauthenticated evidence against Father
- Erroneous orders based on undisclosed evidence
- Violation of due process

## PART VI: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

### A. Proper Ex Parte Procedure (What Should Have Happened)

1. **Emily Watson files affidavit** with specific facts showing emergency
2. **Emily Watson serves Father** with affidavit and notice of ex parte hearing
3. **Father receives notice** of hearing (at least 24 hours in advance)
4. **Father has opportunity to respond** (file objections, present evidence)
5. **Judge reviews both sides** before making decision
6. **Judge issues findings** explaining emergency circumstances

### B. What Actually Happened

1. **Emily Watson submits USB** directly to clerk's office (no affidavit)
2. **Judge's staff reviews recording** before Father is served
3. **Judge prejudges case** based on one-sided evidence
4. **Father receives notice** after order is already issued
5. **Father has no opportunity to respond** before deprivation
6. **Judge issues order** without findings or explanation
7. **Order issued** with no opportunity for Father to seek reconsideration

### C. Violations Demonstrated

The actual procedure violated:
- MCR 3.207 (ex parte procedures)

- MRE 901 (authentication)
