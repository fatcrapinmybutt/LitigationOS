PLAINTIFF'S MOTION FOR DISQUALIFICATION OF JUDGE

PURSUANT TO MCR 2.003(C)(1)(A) AND (C)(1)(B)



NOW COMES Plaintiff Andrew James Pigors, appearing in propria persona, and respectfully moves this Honorable Court for the disqualification of the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill from all further proceedings in this matter. In support of this Motion, Plaintiff states as follows:



I. INTRODUCTION

1. Plaintiff is the father of the minor child, L.D.W. (DOB: November 9, 2022), and has appeared in propria persona throughout these proceedings. This case was initiated on or about April 1, 2024, as an original action for custody, parenting time, and child support under MCL 722.21 et seq.

2. On August 23, 2024, this Court entered a Final Judgment granting sole legal and sole physical custody of the minor child to Defendant Emily A. Watson, without conducting an evidentiary hearing on the twelve best-interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23(a)–(l).

3. On or about August 6–8, 2025, this Court entered an ex parte order suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time with his three-year-old son, without prior notice to Plaintiff and without the verified pleading or specific findings of immediate and irreparable injury required by MCR 3.207(B)(1) and (B)(5)–(6).

4. As of the date of this filing, Plaintiff has been separated from his child for over 329 consecutive days. This extraordinary deprivation of the parent-child relationship, imposed without adequate procedural safeguards, forms the backdrop against which this Motion must be evaluated.

5. Plaintiff brings this Motion pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b), on the grounds that Judge McNeill is biased and prejudiced against Plaintiff, and that objective and reasonable perceptions establish both a serious risk of actual bias impacting Plaintiff's due process rights and a failure to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

6. This Motion is timely filed pursuant to MCR 2.003(D)(1)(a), within 14 days of the most recent discovery of grounds for disqualification, and before the next scheduled hearing date of March 15, 2026.



II. LEGAL STANDARD — MCR 2.003(C)(1)

7. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that "[d]isqualification of a judge is warranted for reasons that include, but are not limited to, the following:

    (a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.

    (b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in Caperton v Massey, 556 US 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct."

8. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that the test for disqualification is an objective one: "whether a reasonable person, informed of the facts, would have a reasonable basis for doubting the judge's impartiality." Cain v Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996).

9. In Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp, 248 Mich App 573, 597; 640 NW2d 321 (2001), the Court of Appeals emphasized that even the appearance of bias or prejudice is sufficient to warrant disqualification. The standard protects not only against actual bias, but against the reasonable perception of bias.

10. The United States Supreme Court has confirmed that "[t]here is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective and reasonable perceptions — when a person with a personal stake in a particular case had a significant and disproportionate influence" on the proceeding. Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 US 868, 884 (2009). Due process under the Fourteenth Amendment requires recusal in such circumstances.

11. MCR 2.003(D)(2) requires that "the moving party must include all grounds for disqualification that are known at the time the motion is filed." This Motion comprehensively catalogs all known grounds. An affidavit is attached hereto as Exhibit A, as required by MCR 2.003(D)(2).



III. FACTUAL BASIS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

A. PATTERN OF EX PARTE ORDERS WITHOUT NOTICE OR HEARING

12. Across the duration of this case, the record documents 267 instances of ex parte communications, orders, and procedural irregularities attributable to Judge McNeill's conduct. Of these, 178 are rated critical severity and 89 are rated high severity based on their impact on Plaintiff's rights and the integrity of the proceedings.

13. The following specific ex parte orders were entered without proper notice to Plaintiff and without compliance with MCR 3.207(B)(1) and (B)(5)–(6):

    a. April 29, 2024 — Ex parte custody order entered. Plaintiff received no prior notice and no opportunity to be heard. MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that ex parte custody orders be supported by "specific facts in a verified pleading or affidavit" demonstrating "immediate and irreparable injury" absent the order. No such findings appear on the record.

    b. July 1, 2024 — Ex parte custody order entered, further restricting Plaintiff's rights without hearing.

    c. August 6–8, 2025 — Ex parte order suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time with his three-year-old son. Defendant's ex parte motion was filed on August 8, 2025. Plaintiff arrived at the scheduled parenting-time exchange expecting his regular parenting time, only to discover at the exchange location that his parenting time had been terminated by ex parte order. Plaintiff received no advance notice whatsoever.

    d. August 22, 2025 — Interim hearing on the ex parte order, but the suspension was continued rather than vacated. The Court did not make the findings required by MCR 3.207(B)(5)–(6) to justify continuation.

    e. November 26, 2025 — Hearing on Plaintiff's objection to the ex parte order. The Court again continued the ex parte suspension, extending the total separation to months beyond the 21-day hearing expectation reflected in MCR 3.207(B)(6).

14. MCR 2.119(H) restricts ex parte orders to situations involving "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage." MCR 3.207(B)(1) further requires that ex parte custody and parenting-time orders be supported by verified specific facts. The pattern of entering ex parte orders without these prerequisites — and then continuing them for months without required findings — demonstrates a systematic disregard for Plaintiff's procedural rights. This pattern constitutes bias against Plaintiff under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a).

B. $250 FILING BOND AS AN ACCESS-TO-COURT BARRIER

15. On May 16, 2025, this Court entered an order imposing a $250.00 filing bond requirement on Plaintiff, requiring Plaintiff to pay $250.00 for each motion before he may file any further motion in this matter.

16. This filing bond was imposed without any finding that Plaintiff's prior filings were frivolous, groundless, or filed for an improper purpose, as required by MCR 2.114(D)–(E) before sanctions may be imposed. MCR 2.114(D) permits sanctions only upon a specific finding that a party's claim or filing was "not well grounded in fact" or "not warranted by existing law or a good faith argument."

17. The practical effect of this filing bond has been devastating. Following the imposition of the PPO proceedings and custody litigation, Plaintiff lost his employment. The $250.00 per-motion barrier has functioned as a complete financial gate preventing Plaintiff from bringing necessary motions regarding his child — including motions to restore parenting time, motions to compel compliance with court orders, and motions to modify custody.

18. The United States Supreme Court has held that the right of access to the courts is a fundamental right. Boddie v Connecticut, 401 US 371, 379 (1971) (holding that due process prohibits states from denying access to courts based on inability to pay). A filing bond that effectively prevents a parent from seeking relief regarding his own child, imposed without a finding of frivolousness, constitutes a punitive barrier evidencing bias against Plaintiff.

19. The disparity is stark: Defendant has filed freely throughout these proceedings, while Plaintiff is uniquely burdened with a per-motion financial toll. This asymmetric access to the Court — imposed only on the pro se father — creates both the reality and the appearance of bias. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a); MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii).

C. FAILURE TO CONDUCT REQUIRED BEST-INTEREST ANALYSIS

20. MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve factors, (a) through (l), that the court "shall consider, evaluate, and determine" in resolving child custody disputes. MCL 722.27(1)(c) further provides that "[t]he court shall not modify or amend its previous judgments or orders or issue a new order so as to change the established custodial environment of a child unless there is presented clear and convincing evidence that it is in the best interest of the child."

21. Despite these mandatory requirements, this Court entered the August 23, 2024 Final Judgment granting sole legal and sole physical custody to Defendant without:

    a. Conducting an evidentiary hearing on the MCL 722.23(a)–(l) factors;

    b. Making findings of fact on the record as to each statutory factor;

    c. Applying the clear-and-convincing-evidence standard required by MCL 722.27(1)(c) when modifying an established custodial environment.

22. Similarly, the August 6–8, 2025 ex parte suspension of all parenting time was imposed without any analysis of the best-interest factors and without the required findings under MCR 3.207(B)(5)–(6) that "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result" absent the ex parte order.

23. The Michigan Court of Appeals has held that a trial court's failure to make specific findings on each best-interest factor constitutes reversible error. Vodvarka v Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 1, 8; 672 NW2d 847 (2003) (requiring proper cause or change of circumstances and best-interest analysis before modifying custody). A pattern of entering custody-altering orders without the required statutory analysis evidences a predetermined outcome — a hallmark of judicial bias.

D. PRO SE ACCOMMODATION FAILURES

24. Canon 3(B)(5) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires that a judge "shall perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice" and Canon 3(A)(4) requires a judge to "be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants."

25. MCR 3.206(A) provides that when a party appears "without the assistance of counsel, the friend of the court must provide [the party] with the form pleadings and written instructions and must schedule a hearing." This Court has failed to ensure compliance with this rule.

26. Throughout these proceedings, Judge McNeill has exhibited a pattern of hostility and dismissiveness toward Plaintiff's pro se filings and oral presentations, including:

    a. Muting Plaintiff's microphone during hearings, cutting off Plaintiff mid-objection;

    b. Instructing Plaintiff not to file further materials and stating that the Court would not review them;

    c. Holding Plaintiff's pro se filings to a standard more exacting than that applied to Defendant's counsel, while simultaneously refusing to consider the substance of Plaintiff's submissions;

    d. Failing to make reasonable accommodations for Plaintiff's self-represented status, as required by Canon 3(A)(4) and Canon 3(B)(8) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

27. The combination of muting a pro se litigant, instructing him not to file, and then imposing a $250 filing bond creates a complete silencing — a procedural trap from which Plaintiff cannot escape. A reasonable person would question the impartiality of a judge who so systematically restricts one party's ability to be heard. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii).

E. OFF-RECORD EVIDENCE HANDLING

28. During the course of these proceedings, the Court received and considered evidence off the record and ex parte, in violation of Canon 3(A)(5) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which bars ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters.

29. Specifically, a USB recording and mental-health evaluation materials were submitted and handled outside the normal evidentiary process. Plaintiff attempted to present exculpatory photographs from the July 29, 2025 parenting-time exchange showing the minor child's well-being, but the Court refused to review them while simultaneously relying on unsworn, hearsay materials submitted ex parte by Defendant.

30. This asymmetric evidence handling — accepting Defendant's ex parte submissions while refusing Plaintiff's exculpatory evidence — is inconsistent with Canon 3 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct and with the fundamental requirement that judicial decisions be based on the record. The combination of ex parte submissions, refusal to review exculpatory evidence, and willingness to rely on hearsay demonstrates actual bias. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a).

F. UNEQUAL TREATMENT AND PATTERN OF FAVORING DEFENDANT

31. The cumulative pattern of this Court's rulings reveals systematic favoritism toward Defendant and hostility toward Plaintiff:

    a. Every contested custody determination has been resolved in Defendant's favor, culminating in sole legal and sole physical custody without an evidentiary hearing;

    b. Every ex parte request by Defendant has been granted, while Plaintiff's filings have been met with hostility, sanctions, and a filing bond;

    c. Parenting time has been progressively restricted from the initial arrangement to complete suspension, without the statutory analysis required by MCL 722.23 and MCL 722.27a(3);

    d. Plaintiff's parenting time was withheld for 37 consecutive days (March 26–May 2, 2024) without court intervention; additional withholding periods followed, including from October 20 to November 12, 2024, with no enforcement by the Court;

    e. Plaintiff was ordered to undergo a mental health assessment at Health West on September 22, 2025, as a condition of regaining parenting time, while no parallel requirement was imposed on Defendant — despite Defendant's documented pattern of alienating behavior under MCL 722.23(j);

    f. The Court failed to enforce its own orders when Defendant refused to facilitate parenting time (including Easter 2025 and make-up days), declined to facilitate video chats, and obstructed medical information sharing — all in violation of parenting-time provisions. See MCL 722.27a(3) (court "shall not modify or amend" parenting time absent proper cause).

32. Michigan courts have recognized that a pattern of one-sided rulings, combined with procedural irregularities, can establish the appearance of bias sufficient to warrant disqualification. Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001). The pattern here is not one of isolated error but of sustained, directional prejudice against a single party.

V. CONSTITUTIONAL DIMENSIONS

37. The fundamental right of parents to the care, custody, and control of their children is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This right is protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

38. The deprivation of Plaintiff's parenting time for over 329 days — imposed initially by ex parte order, continued without required findings, and effectively made permanent by the $250 filing bond that prevents Plaintiff from seeking relief — implicates Plaintiff's fundamental constitutional rights. A biased judge who presides over the termination of these rights denies Plaintiff the "fair trial in a fair tribunal" guaranteed by the Due Process Clause. Caperton, 556 US at 876.

39. Where a judge's pattern of conduct creates an "unconstitutional potential for bias," disqualification is constitutionally required regardless of whether actual bias is proven. Caperton, 556 US at 881. The record in this case — 1,127 violations, 267 ex parte irregularities, complete suppression of parenting time without required findings, asymmetric access to the court — far surpasses the threshold for constitutionally compelled disqualification.
