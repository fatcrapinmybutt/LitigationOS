## III. ARGUMENT

### A. The Suspension Violates MCL 722.27a

**Issue:** Whether parenting time may be suspended without a finding under MCL 722.27a(3).

**Rule:** MCL 722.27a(1) establishes Michigan's public policy "to assure that each minor child ... has close contact with both parents." MCL 722.27a(3) provides that parenting time may be restricted only where the court determines "on the basis of clear and convincing evidence" that "the child's physical, mental, or emotional health would be seriously endangered by an unsupervised parenting time order." This is a high evidentiary standard — clear and convincing evidence, not a mere preponderance.

**Application:** No evidentiary hearing produced a finding of serious endangerment. The suspension was based on an ex parte motion filed by a relative of Mother — not on evidence presented at a hearing where Father could cross-examine witnesses and present his own evidence. As the Michigan Supreme Court held in *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186; 680 NW2d 835 (2004), custody determinations must be based on admissible evidence and proper application of best-interest factors — not on unsworn, untested ex parte allegations. The 204-day suspension (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026), without any endangerment finding, violates the express terms of MCL 722.27a(3) and contravenes the state's public policy of close contact with both parents.

Moreover, **MCL 722.27a(7)** provides an affirmative prohibition: "A parenting time order shall not be considered a change of the established custodial environment and **the court shall not deny a parent parenting time with the child unless the court determines by clear and convincing evidence that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health.**" (Emphasis added.) This statutory command is mandatory — the court *shall not* deny parenting time absent clear and convincing evidence of endangerment. No such evidence exists in this record. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders were entered without any evidentiary hearing, without sworn testimony, and without a clear-and-convincing-evidence finding. The suspension therefore violates MCL 722.27a(7) as a matter of law.

**Conclusion:** The suspension must be lifted because it lacks the required statutory finding under both MCL 722.27a(3) and the mandatory prohibition of MCL 722.27a(7).

### B. The Ex Parte Procedure Violated MCR 3.207(B)

**Issue:** Whether the ex parte orders comply with MCR 3.207(B).

**Rule:** MCR 3.207(B) permits ex parte orders in domestic relations matters only where "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result" and requires the movant to "certify to the court in writing the efforts, if any, which have been made to give notice." MCR 3.207(B)(3) requires a hearing "within 14 days."

**Application:** The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders did not comply:
- No certification of efforts to give notice appears in the record.
- No finding of "immediate and irreparable injury" was made.
- No hearing was held within 14 days as required.
- The suspension has now lasted **204 days** (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026) — over 14 times the maximum permitted duration under MCR 3.207(B)(3).

**Conclusion:** The ex parte orders are procedurally defective and should be vacated.

### C. The 204-Day Separation Constitutes Irreparable Harm

**Issue:** Whether the ongoing separation constitutes irreparable harm warranting emergency relief.

**Rule:** The parent-child relationship is a "fundamental liberty interest" protected by the Due Process Clause. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Michigan law recognizes that prolonged separation causes irreparable harm to both parent and child. See *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 55 (1992) (recognizing significance of stability and continuity in parent-child relationship).

**Application:** Father has been separated from his children for **204 consecutive days** (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026). This separation:
- Exceeds nearly seven months of zero contact
- Was imposed without Father's participation in any proceeding
- Has caused developmental milestones to be missed
- Has damaged the parent-child bond in ways that compound daily
- Violates MCL 722.23(j) — the willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship

Every day that passes without restoration increases the harm exponentially. This is the definition of irreparable injury.

**Conclusion:** Emergency restoration of parenting time is warranted.

---

### D. The Best Interest Factors Support Restoration

**Issue:** Whether the MCL 722.23 best interest factors support restoring Father's parenting time.

**Rule:** MCL 722.23 lists 12 factors (a)-(l). *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 1 (2003). Critically, the Michigan Supreme Court requires that the trial court conduct a factor-by-factor analysis of each best-interest factor and make specific findings on the record. *Sparks v Sparks*, 440 Mich 141 (1992). No such factor-by-factor analysis was conducted here.

**Application:**

| Factor | Analysis |
|--------|----------|
| **(a)** Love/affection | Father actively exercised parenting time before suspension — strong bond |
| **(b)** Capacity for guidance | Father provided care, guidance, and nurturing prior to suspension |
| **(c)** Provide necessities | Father maintained housing, provided for children's needs |
| **(d)** Stable environment | Children had established custodial environment with Father |
| **(e)** Permanence | Father offers permanent, stable family unit |
| **(f)** Moral fitness | No criminal convictions; no findings of unfitness |
| **(g)** Health | No mental or physical health issues precluding parenting |
| **(h)** School/community | Children's school and community ties include Father |
| **(i)** Child preference | No evidence children expressed preference against Father |
| **(j)** Facilitate relationship | **Mother is actively PREVENTING the relationship** — Factor J strongly favors Father |
| **(k)** Domestic violence | No finding of domestic violence by Father |
| **(l)** Other factors | 204-day separation (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026) is the single most relevant "other" factor |

The vast majority of factors favor restoration. Factor (j) — willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship — is particularly significant: **the party who obtained the ex parte suspension is the party who is failing Factor J.**

Critically, the Court's own best-interest-factor analysis in its earlier order found that "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and that "[t]his Factor is equal between the parties" under MCL 722.23(a). *See* Record, Final Judgment dated August 23, 2024, Best Interest Factor Analysis. This Court's own finding of equal parental bond directly contradicts the total suspension of Father's parenting time — a measure that presupposes a deficiency the Court itself found does not exist.

**Conclusion:** The best interest factors overwhelmingly support restoring Father's parenting time.

### E. MCR 3.303 Authorizes Habeas Corpus to Compel Production of the Child

**Issue:** Whether Father may invoke habeas corpus to compel production of a child being unlawfully withheld from a parent.

**Rule:** **MCR 3.303** governs habeas corpus proceedings and authorizes the issuance of a writ of habeas corpus to compel the production of a person who is unlawfully restrained or confined. In the family law context, habeas corpus lies to compel the production of a child who is being withheld from a parent in violation of a court order or in the absence of lawful authority for the detention. *See Haton v Haton*, 116 Mich App 679, 684 (1982) (habeas corpus available in custody disputes where child is unlawfully detained).

**Application:** Father's parenting time has been completely suspended for **204 consecutive days** (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026) based on ex parte orders that failed to comply with MCR 3.207(B) and MCL 722.27a(3). The procedural defects in these orders — no notice, no hearing within 14 days, no finding of serious endangerment — render them legally insufficient to authorize the continued withholding of the children from Father. Where the legal basis for detention is defective, habeas corpus is an available remedy. Father expressly reserves the right to petition for a writ of habeas corpus under MCR 3.303 if this Court does not immediately restore parenting time.

**Conclusion:** MCR 3.303 provides Father with an independent procedural vehicle to compel production of the children, and Father reserves this right if parenting time is not restored forthwith.

### F. Father's PPO History Does Not Justify Indefinite Suspension Without a Hearing

**Issue:** Whether Father's PPO violation and resulting incarceration constitute legal grounds for the indefinite suspension of all parenting time without a best-interest hearing.

**Rule:** A PPO violation, even one resulting in incarceration, does not authorize the permanent elimination of a parent's fundamental right to a relationship with his children without due process. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014); *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). MCL 722.27a(7) requires clear and convincing evidence of endangerment — not the mere existence of a prior PPO violation.

**Application:** Father acknowledges that he was found in violation of the personal protection order and served a period of incarceration. Father does not minimize these events. However, the appropriate remedy for a PPO violation is the criminal sanction already imposed — not the indefinite suspension of all parenting time without any hearing on the best-interest factors. No court has found, after an evidentiary hearing, that Father's contact with the children would endanger their physical, mental, or emotional health. The 204-day separation (from August 8, 2025 to February 28, 2026) exceeds any reasonable remedial response and has itself become the primary source of harm to the children.

**Conclusion:** Father's PPO history does not excuse the absence of a best-interest hearing or the lack of an endangerment finding under MCL 722.27a(3) and (7).
