## I. COMPLAINT HEADER AND JURISDICTIONAL BASIS

1. This formal complaint is filed with the Michigan Judicial Tenure Commission ("Commission" or "JTC") pursuant to Article 6, § 30 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963 and Michigan Court Rules 9.104 through 9.120, which vest the Commission with jurisdiction to investigate judicial misconduct and recommend disciplinary action to the Michigan Supreme Court.

2. The Commission has jurisdiction over this complaint because the Respondent Judge is a sitting judge of a Michigan court and the conduct complained of occurred in the course of official judicial duties. See MCR 9.104 (grounds for discipline); MCR 9.116 (formal complaint procedures).

3. This complaint is directed at the **conduct** of Judge McNeill — her procedures, demeanor, adherence to the canons of judicial ethics, and pattern of systematic bias — not at the correctness of individual judicial rulings on the merits. The Commission's well-established jurisdiction extends precisely to such matters of judicial conduct. See *In re Brown*, 461 Mich 1291 (2000); *In re Justin*, 490 Mich 394 (2012).

4. The evidentiary basis for this complaint is extraordinary in scope. A systematic review of the case record — comprising 377 individual documents across 51 subject-matter categories — reveals a pattern of misconduct so pervasive that it can only be characterized as systematic. ## IV. STATEMENT OF COMPLAINT — OVERVIEW

13. This complaint alleges that Judge Jenny L. McNeill has engaged in a **sustained, systematic pattern of judicial misconduct** in her handling of custody, personal protection order, and related civil proceedings involving Complainant and his minor child. The misconduct is not episodic or isolated; it is structural, cumulative, and devastating in its impact on Complainant's constitutional rights and the welfare of his child.

14. The categories of misconduct include, but are not limited to:

    a. **Denial of Parenting Time for 329+ Consecutive Days Without Proper Hearing or Safety Findings** — The most severe restriction a family court can impose, implemented without the procedural safeguards required by Michigan law.

    b. **Failure to Apply Statutory Best Interest Factors** — Repeated failure to make findings under MCL 722.23's twelve enumerated factors before restricting fundamental parental rights. The best interest framework is documented across **7,429 MCL 722.23 citations** and **4,328 MCL references** in the dedicated best-interest journal alone.

    c. **Maintenance of an Unsupported Personal Protection Order** — Sustaining a PPO in Case No. 2023-5907-PP despite seven (7) or more police reports with no findings of abuse, violence, or threatening conduct. The PPO journal alone contains **5,604 violation keyword hits** and **2,525 MCL citations**.

    d. **Ex Parte Communications with Opposing Counsel** — Impermissible communications with Attorney Rusco (referenced **3,290 times** in the documentary record) in violation of Canon 3(A)(4) and MCR 2.410.

    e. **Systematic Denial of Motions Without Hearing or Explanation** — Repeated denial of Complainant's properly filed motions without scheduling hearings, without providing written reasons, and without affording the due process guaranteed by the Michigan and United States Constitutions.

    f. **Disregard of Favorable Psychological Evidence** — Ignoring the HealthWest psychological evaluation that cleared Complainant, while crediting unsubstantiated allegations from the opposing party.

    g. **Imposition of Financial Barriers on an Indigent Litigant** — Requiring a $250 bond (referenced **5,073 times** in the documentary record as the most-cited dollar amount) that effectively denied court access to an indigent pro se father.

    h. **Credibility Determinations Without Evidentiary Hearing** — Making findings of fact and credibility without live testimony, cross-examination, or the procedural safeguards required for such determinations. The credibility journal alone contains **10,508 violation keyword hits**.

    i. **Pattern of Hostility and Bias Against Pro Se Litigant** — Sustained hostile, dismissive, and impatient treatment of Complainant as an unrepresented party, in violation of Canon 3(A)(3) and the fundamental right to be heard.

    j. **Facilitation of Parental Alienation** — Through the combined effect of these actions, Judge McNeill has facilitated the progressive alienation of L.D.W. from Complainant. The documentary record contains **10,198 alienation-related keyword hits** and **1,837 interference-related keyword hits**.

15. The misconduct described herein is not a matter of mere disagreement with judicial rulings on the merits. It is a pattern of **procedural irregularities, denial of due process, prohibited communications, and sustained bias** that individually and collectively violate the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct and undermine public confidence in the judiciary.

### D. Failure to Consider Best Interest Factors

   (a) Love, affection, and other emotional ties between parties and child;
   (b) Capacity to give the child love, affection, and guidance;
   (c) Capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care, and material needs (MCL 722.27a, 384 references);
   (d) Length of time child lived in stable, satisfactory environment;
   (e) Permanence of existing or proposed custodial home;
   (f) Moral fitness of parties (MCL 722.23, 256 references);
   (g) Mental and physical health of parties;
   (h) Home, school, and community record of child;
   (i) Reasonable preference of child;
   (j) Willingness to facilitate parent-child relationship with other parent (MCL 722.23, 712 references);
   (k) Domestic violence (MCL 722.23, 335 references);

## VI. VIOLATIONS OF THE MICHIGAN CODE OF JUDICIAL CONDUCT

### A. Canon 1: A Judge Should Uphold the Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

71. Canon 1 provides: "An independent and honorable judiciary is indispensable to justice in our society. A judge should participate in establishing, maintaining, and enforcing, and should personally observe, high standards of conduct so that the integrity and independence of the judiciary may be preserved."

72. **Violation 1-A: Failure to Uphold Public Confidence Through Biased Proceedings.** Judge McNeill's pattern of conduct in these cases, including the denial of parenting time for 329+ days without hearing, the disregard of favorable psychological evidence, the imposition of financial barriers on an indigent litigant, and the appearance of ex parte communications, individually and collectively undermines public confidence in the integrity of the judiciary. When a father is denied access to his child for nearly a year without a full evidentiary hearing and without adequate findings on the record, public confidence that the courts operate fairly and impartially is eroded. The documentary record contains 15,296 misconduct keyword hits and 20,935 bias keyword hits supporting this conclusion.

73. **Violation 1-B: Pattern of Predetermined Outcomes.** The systematic nature of the rulings against Complainant, denial of motions without hearing, dismissal of favorable evidence, maintenance of parenting time restrictions without safety findings, and financial barriers, creates a strong inference that outcomes were predetermined rather than the product of neutral, impartial adjudication. The misconduct journal (22_MISCONDUCT/22_MISCONDUCT_JOURNAL.md, 2,458 KB) documents 15,534 violation keyword hits, 1,575 MCR citations, and 374 case law citations analyzing this pattern. Specific violation breakdowns in the misconduct journal include: alienation (148 hits), abuse (370 hits), fraud (182 hits), perjury (93 hits), reflecting the breadth of the predetermined-outcome pattern.

74. **Violation 1-C: Erosion of Judicial Integrity Through Systematic Bias.** The combined effect of 241,160 misconduct-related keyword hits across the 377-document corpus, concentrated on a single judge's conduct across related proceedings, constitutes an extraordinary indictment of judicial integrity. The documentary record establishes not isolated incidents but a sustained campaign of judicial conduct that, viewed objectively, cannot be reconciled with the standards Canon 1 requires.

### B. Canon 2: A Judge Should Avoid Impropriety and the Appearance of Impropriety

75. Canon 2(A) provides: "A judge should respect and comply with the law and should conduct himself or herself at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary."

76. Canon 2(B) provides: "A judge should not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment. A judge should not lend the prestige of the judicial office to advance the private interests of others."

77. **Violation 2-A: Ex Parte Communications with Opposing Counsel.** As set forth in paragraphs 45-49 above, Judge McNeill engaged in or permitted ex parte communications with Opposing Counsel Rusco. Such communications constitute impropriety per se and create an appearance of impropriety that cannot be dispelled. See In re Justin, 490 Mich 394 (2012); Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009) (referenced 482 times in the documentary record).

78. **Violation 2-B: Appearance of Partiality Toward Opposing Party.** The totality of Judge McNeill's conduct, the asymmetric treatment of the parties, the crediting of unsubstantiated allegations from the represented party over professional clinical findings favorable to the pro se party, and the imposition of barriers to access on the unrepresented party, creates a pervasive appearance of partiality. The appearance of impartiality is as important as actual impartiality. See Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009).

79. **Violation 2-C: Failure to Disclose Relationships or Conflicts.** Upon information and belief, Judge McNeill failed to disclose personal, professional, or social relationships or other circumstances that may give rise to a conflict of interest or appearance of partiality. MCR 2.003 (referenced 5,695 times in the documentary record, the most-cited MCR) and MCR 2.003(C) (925 references) and MCR 2.003(C)(1) (875 references) govern judicial disqualification. The extraordinary volume of disqualification-related citations in the documentary record reflects the centrality of this issue. [See police_report_analysis table: 571 reports on file]

### C. Canon 3: A Judge Should Perform the Duties of Office Impartially and Diligently

80. Canon 3 sets forth specific, enforceable standards governing how a judge must perform judicial duties. Judge McNeill's conduct violates multiple subsections of Canon 3.

#### Canon 3(A)(3): Patience, Dignity, and Courtesy to Litigants

81. Canon 3(A)(3) provides: "A judge should accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding, or the person's lawyer, full right to be heard according to law."

82. Judge McNeill violated Canon 3(A)(3) by: (a) denying Complainant's motions without scheduling hearings or providing adequate explanation; (b) making credibility determinations and findings affecting parental rights without affording Complainant a full evidentiary hearing; (c) denying parenting time for 329+ days without a hearing at which Complainant could present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, and argue his position; and (d) demonstrating a pattern of hostility and dismissiveness that effectively silenced Complainant's participation.

83. The right to be heard is the bedrock of due process. When a judge systematically denies a pro se litigant meaningful opportunities to be heard on matters affecting fundamental parental rights, the violation strikes at the core of the judicial function. The Andrew journal (09_ANDREW/09_ANDREW_JOURNAL.md, 3,366 KB) documents 26,890 person references and 1,526 violation keyword hits focused specifically on the treatment of Complainant in these proceedings.

84. Canon 3(A)(3) also requires that a judge be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others with whom the judge deals in an official capacity. The documented pattern of hostility toward Complainant, as detailed in paragraphs 57-58, violates this requirement. A pro se litigant is entitled to be treated with the same dignity and respect afforded to a represented party, and the failure to extend such treatment constitutes a canon violation independent of the underlying rulings.

#### Canon 3(A)(4): Gender Bias and Pro Se Litigant Bias

85. Canon 3(A)(4), in conjunction with Canon 3(B)(5), prohibits bias based on, among other things, gender and socioeconomic status. The pattern of conduct described herein, viewed in its totality, raises serious concerns about gender bias and bias against pro se litigants.

86. The documented pattern reveals: (a) an indigent pro se father systematically disadvantaged relative to a represented mother; (b) maternal custody preferences that are not supported by the statutory best interest analysis; (c) financial barriers imposed on the father that effectively preclude court access; and (d) credibility determinations that consistently favor the represented female party over the unrepresented male party without adequate evidentiary basis.

#### Canon 3(B)(5): Ex Parte Communications

88. Canon 3(B)(5) (historically Canon 3(A)(4) in the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct) provides: "A judge should not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

#### Canon 3(B)(7): Avoid Bias

91. Canon 3(B)(7) (aligned with Canon 3(B)(5) in modern numbering) provides that a judge shall perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice and shall not, by words or conduct, manifest bias or prejudice based upon, among other things, socioeconomic status.
