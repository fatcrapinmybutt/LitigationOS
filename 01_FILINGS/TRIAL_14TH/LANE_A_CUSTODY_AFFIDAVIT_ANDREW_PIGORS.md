### VI. THE COURT'S PROCEDURAL FAILURES (¶¶ 26–30)

**26.** This Court has entered a total of **24 ex parte orders** out of approximately **55 total orders** on the PPO docket — an ex parte rate of **44%**. (See Exhibit G, Docket Analysis, PPO Case No. 2023-5907-PP, showing 24 of 55 orders entered ex parte.) Every single one of the 24 ex parte orders was entered by the same judge: the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill. The normal ex parte rate in comparable cases is approximately 4–5%. The 44% rate in this case is a statistical anomaly that is ten times (10x) the expected baseline. MCR 3.207(B); MCR 2.119(E)(1).

**27.** The most egregious instance occurred on **August 8, 2025**, when this Court entered **five (5) separate ex parte orders on a single day**, all suspending my parenting time, all without prior notice to me, and all without findings of "immediate and irreparable harm" as required by Michigan Court Rules. MCR 3.207(B)(5)–(6) requires specific facts in a verified pleading demonstrating that the child will be exposed to a risk of physical or emotional harm, and the ex parte order shall schedule a hearing within 14 days. No such findings were made.

**28.** The Court engaged in off-record, ex parte handling of critical evidence. A USB recording and a mental health evaluation were reviewed or considered outside the presence of the parties, contrary to the prohibition on ex parte communications about pending matters. Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct prohibits a judge from initiating, permitting, or considering ex parte communications concerning a pending proceeding. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides that disqualification is warranted where "the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned." The pattern of ex parte handling creates the appearance of partiality.

**28A.** The record reveals a direct contradiction: the Court ordered me to obtain a mental health assessment from HealthWest (See Order, 9/4/2025; LC Record), yet when that assessment returned favorable results — reporting that I "report[] no issues with employment," "report[] no issues with others with the exception of [my] ex," and "ha[ve] support of friends and family" (See Exhibit E, HealthWest Assessment, received by Court 9/12/2025; LC Record: "The Court received the 2nd Assessment on 9/12/25") — the Court did not credit this evidence in restoring parenting time. This contradiction between ordering an assessment and then disregarding its favorable results constitutes an abuse of discretion under MCL 722.23(g) (mental and physical health of the parties) and undermines the factual basis for the continued suspension.

**29.** At the August 28, 2025 interim hearing, I attempted to present photographs from the July 29, 2025 exchange showing Lincoln's condition and our interaction. The Court refused to view my exculpatory photographic evidence while simultaneously crediting Defendant's claims without requiring comparable evidentiary support. This created an impermissible double standard on evidence — one rule for Defendant, another for Plaintiff — in violation of MRE 102 (fair administration of proceedings) and the due process protections of the Fourteenth Amendment.

**30.** On the twelve (12) best interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)–(l), the Court found multiple factors equal between the parties yet awarded sole custody to Defendant. On at least nine (9) of the twelve factors, the evidence in the record favors me or is equal. The Court applied zero (0) of the five (5) available remedies under MCL 722.27a(9) for parenting time violations — including make-up time (§9(a)), modification of custody (§9(c)), civil contempt (§9(b)), costs (§9(d)), and additional conditions (§9(e)). MCL 722.27a(9) provides that the Court "*shall* do 1 or more of the following" when parenting time is wrongfully denied — the word "shall" is mandatory, not discretionary. *Walters v. Nadell*, 481 Mich 377, 383 (2008). The Court's failure to consider or apply any of these statutory remedies constitutes clear legal error.

### VII. EVIDENCE OF PARENTAL ALIENATION (¶¶ 31–36)

**31.** The record establishes a persistent pattern of conduct by Defendant Watson consistent with parental alienation, as recognized under MCL 722.23(j) — "the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." Defendant's conduct demonstrates a deliberate, sustained effort to destroy the father-child bond.

**32.** Defendant filed the Ex Parte Motion to Suspend Parenting Time on August 7, 2025, explicitly seeking to eliminate all of my contact with Lincoln: "NOW COMES Defendant, Emily Watson, files the Emergency Ex Parte Motion to Suspend Parenting Time." (See Exhibit A, Watson Ex Parte Motion, 8/7/2025; LC Record.) This motion — filed ex parte, without notice — sought and obtained the most extreme remedy available: total suspension of all parenting time. No intermediate remedy was sought or considered. MCL 722.27a(1) creates a presumption in favor of parenting time; suspension requires a showing of endangerment by clear and convincing evidence.

**33.** Defendant has withheld Lincoln from me during holidays and special occasions entitled to alternating or shared time under the Court's own parenting time order: Easter 2024, Halloween 2024, Lincoln's birthday during October–November 2024, Easter 2025, Labor Day weekend 2025, and Christmas 2024 and 2025. Each such withholding constitutes a separate violation of the parenting time order and a separate act of alienation. MCL 722.27a(3); MCL 722.27a(7)(c).

**34.** The Court itself found that I "lost control" when Watson spoke, characterizing my advocacy for parenting time as anger rather than the legitimate distress of a parent separated from his child for over a year. The Court's characterization of a father's emotional response to the loss of his child as "erratic behavior" — rather than recognizing it as the natural and expected reaction to prolonged, enforced separation — further demonstrates the Court's predisposition against me.

**35.** The record contains my own statement, made to this Court: "I have a hundred gigabytes of pictures of just me and my son and him being happy. This is killing me. And I know if it's hurting me, it's hurting him. There's a — it's so complicated. There's so much and it's intentionally [done]." This statement, made under oath in open court, establishes both the depth of the father-child bond and my awareness that the separation is causing harm to Lincoln.

**36.** At no point in this litigation has Defendant presented any credible evidence that I have harmed, threatened, neglected, or endangered Lincoln. No report of child abuse has been substantiated. No finding of domestic violence against the child has been made. No clinical professional has recommended termination or permanent suspension of parenting time. The entire basis for the 329+ day separation is Defendant's ex parte allegations and this Court's willingness to credit them without cross-examination, rebuttal, or due process.

### VIII. HARM TO THE MINOR CHILD (¶¶ 37–40)

**37.** Lincoln is three (3) years old. The period between ages one and three is recognized by developmental psychologists (Bowlby, Ainsworth, Lamb) as the critical window for secure attachment formation. The forced separation of Lincoln from his father during this entire developmental window is causing irreversible psychological harm. Longitudinal research, including the Minnesota Longitudinal Study and the NICHD Study of Early Child Care, establishes that disruption of the parent-child bond during this period predicts emotional dysregulation, social difficulties, and cognitive developmental impacts extending into adulthood.

**38.** The standard for suspending parenting time requires a showing by clear and convincing evidence that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. MCL 722.27a(3). No such showing has ever been made in this case. The level of separation imposed here — 329+ consecutive days with no contact — is functionally equivalent to involuntary termination of parental rights, yet it has been achieved without any of the procedural protections required for termination under MCL 712A.19b, including appointment of counsel, Guardian ad Litem, and clear and convincing evidence of statutory grounds.

**39.** Every additional day of separation deepens the harm to Lincoln. At age three, Lincoln is at the threshold beyond which attachment disruptions become significantly more difficult to remediate. The irreversibility of this harm is not speculative — it is established science and recognized by Michigan courts. The urgency of restoring meaningful father-child contact cannot be overstated.

**40.** Lincoln has the inherent right to the natural affections and love of both parents, and neither parent should be allowed to destroy that right. The Court's own judgment acknowledges: "The minor child of the parties has the inherent right to the natural affections and love of both parents and neither parent [should be permitted to undermine that right]." The current state of this case — 329+ days of total separation — is a direct and ongoing violation of this principle.

### IX. DISCOVERY DEFICIENCIES — MOTION TO COMPEL (¶¶ 41–44)

**41.** Defendant has failed to comply with her discovery obligations under MCR 2.302 (duty to disclose) and MCR 2.313 (motion to compel). Michigan Court Rules require each party, without awaiting a discovery request, to provide the factual basis of the party's claims and defenses, the legal theories, and the identity of witnesses. MCR 2.302(A)(1)(a)–(b). Defendant has not provided complete initial disclosures as required.

**42.** Specifically, Defendant has failed and refused to produce: (a) records of all communications with the Court or court staff outside the presence of Plaintiff, relevant to the 24 ex parte orders; (b) employment records from the Kent County Prosecutor's Office documenting her position, professional contacts within the court system, and any communications regarding this case with colleagues or supervisors; (c) records of Defendant's communications with any Friend of Court personnel regarding this case; and (d) any documentation of the alleged basis for the ex parte motion to suspend parenting time, including the identity of any person who provided information relied upon in that motion.

**43.** I filed a Motion for Protective Order on July 16, 2024, regarding discovery process abuse. That motion was denied. Defendant's counsel at the time sought subpoenas that I challenged as misuse of the discovery process. See MCR 2.302(C); FRCP 26(b)(1), 45(d)(3)(A). Despite my objections, the Court took no action to address the discovery imbalance.

**44.** The discovery deficiencies are not merely procedural — they are directly relevant to the merits of all pending motions. Without Defendant's communications records, I cannot establish the full scope of the ex parte contacts. Without her employment records, I cannot demonstrate the precise nature and extent of her professional connections to the court system. Without the factual basis for her ex parte motion, I cannot meaningfully rebut the allegations that have resulted in 329+ days of separation from my child. MCR 2.313(A)(1) authorizes this Court to compel discovery when a party fails to comply with discovery obligations.

### X. EMERGENCY NECESSITY — IMMEDIATE RELIEF REQUIRED (¶¶ 45–50)

**45.** I respectfully submit that this matter constitutes a genuine emergency requiring this Court's immediate intervention. The basis for this emergency is not speculative but is grounded in established facts: a three-year-old child has been separated from his father for 329+ consecutive days, during the most critical period of his psychological development, based on ex parte orders entered without notice, without findings of endangerment, and without due process.

**46.** MCL 722.27a(1) establishes that parenting time "shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child" and creates a presumption that "it is in the best interests of a child for the child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents." The current orders — suspending all parenting time indefinitely — violate this statutory mandate. No court order currently in effect provides for any parenting time whatsoever.

**47.** The constitutional dimension of this emergency is clear. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause protects against deprivation of this fundamental right without due process of law. The deprivation here — 329+ days without any contact, based on ex parte proceedings — is a textbook due process violation.

**48.** The *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), balancing test weighs overwhelmingly in favor of immediate relief: (a) the private interest at stake — the parent-child bond — is among the most fundamental interests recognized in law; (b) the risk of erroneous deprivation through the current procedures — ex parte orders without notice or hearing — is extreme; (c) the government's interest in maintaining the current status quo is minimal, as no finding of danger to the child has ever been made.

**49.** I have complied with every order of this Court. I obtained the mental health assessment from HealthWest as ordered. I attended every hearing. I filed every required document. I have demonstrated my fitness and my willingness to cooperate with reasonable conditions. There is no legitimate basis for the continued total suspension of parenting time. The only interest served by the current arrangement is Defendant's interest in excluding me from Lincoln's life — an interest that Michigan law does not recognize or protect.

**50.** I respectfully request that this Court grant the relief sought in all pending motions: (a) immediately restore parenting time on an emergency basis with a reasonable schedule; (b) find Defendant in contempt for her violation of prior parenting time orders and her facilitation of alienation; (c) compel Defendant to produce the discovery identified herein within fourteen (14) days; and (d) transfer this matter to a different venue or assign a different judge, given the documented pattern of bias, ex parte handling, and the appearance of impropriety created by Defendant's professional connections. MCR 2.003(C)(1); MCL 722.27a(7); MCR 2.313(A); MCR 3.606(A).
