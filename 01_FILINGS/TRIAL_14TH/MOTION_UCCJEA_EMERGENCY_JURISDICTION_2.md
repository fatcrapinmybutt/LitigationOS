## I. STATUTORY BASIS — MCL 722.1203 (UCCJEA EMERGENCY JURISDICTION)

1. MCL 722.1203(1) provides:

> *"A court of this state has temporary emergency jurisdiction if the child is present in this state and **the child has been subjected to or threatened with mistreatment or abuse**, or it is necessary in an emergency to protect the child because the child, or a sibling or parent of the child, is subjected to or threatened with mistreatment or abuse."*

2. This emergency jurisdiction is **independent of and in addition to** the Court's existing home-state jurisdiction under MCL 722.1201. Emergency jurisdiction under MCL 722.1203 authorizes immediate temporary orders to protect a child even where home-state jurisdiction already exists.

3. The minor child L.D.W. satisfies both statutory prongs of MCL 722.1203(1):

   a. **L.D.W. is present in Michigan.** L.D.W. has been a Michigan resident since birth and currently resides at 2160 Garland Drive, Norton Shores, Muskegon County, Michigan.

   b. **L.D.W. has been subjected to mistreatment or abuse, and emergency protection is necessary.** The evidence supporting this finding is set forth in Section II below.

## IV. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. MCL 722.1203 Emergency Jurisdiction Applies

21. The UCCJEA emergency jurisdiction standard requires a showing that: (1) the child is present in Michigan, and (2) the child has been subjected to or threatened with mistreatment or abuse. Both elements are satisfied.

22. **"Mistreatment or abuse" under MCL 722.1203 includes psychological abuse.** The systematic destruction of the father-child bond through 540 documented alienation incidents — including complete denial of all contact for 329+ consecutive days — constitutes psychological mistreatment of L.D.W. *See In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (recognizing alienation as contrary to child's best interests).

23. The **physical abuse risk** from Cody Watson's self-admitted physical discipline of L.D.W., combined with his NIBRS criminal record, independently satisfies the mistreatment prong.

### B. Due Process Requires Immediate Action

24. "The interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

25. When a fundamental liberty interest is at stake, due process requires: (1) notice, (2) an opportunity to be heard, and (3) a decision based on evidence. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). **None of these requirements have been satisfied during 329+ days of total parenting time deprivation.**

26. *Fuentes v Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67, 82 (1972): *"The right to be heard before being condemned to suffer grievous loss of any kind"* is a fundamental requirement of due process. Father was denied this right.

### C. The Court Cannot Rely on Discredited Evidence

27. The entire basis for the current custody arrangement rests on the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders, which were predicated on allegations that HealthWest's clinical evaluation has **comprehensively contradicted**: Psychosis = 0, Substance Use = 0, Danger = 0, "Neither SMI nor SED," Level of Urgency = "Routine."

28. When the factual predicate for a court order is shown to be false, the order cannot stand. *MCR 2.612(C)(1)(b)* (relief from judgment based on newly discovered evidence).

### D. Shade v Wright — Total Denial Is Extraordinary

29. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31 (2010): *"Total denial of parenting time is an extraordinary remedy"* requiring compelling justification. No compelling justification exists when the court's own ordered clinical evaluation found the opposite of what was alleged.
