## III. ARGUMENT

### A. Father Has a Fundamental Constitutional Right to the Care, Custody, and Companionship of His Child

39. The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

40. In *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982), the Supreme Court held that a parent's right to "the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children" is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court held that this right cannot be terminated absent proof by at least **clear and convincing evidence** and with full procedural protections.

41. What has occurred in this case is functionally equivalent to a termination of Father's parental rights — a complete, indefinite suspension of all parenting time for 329+ days — yet it was accomplished through an **ex parte order**, without any notice, without any evidentiary hearing, without any standard of proof, and without any written findings.

42. If the State cannot terminate parental rights without clear and convincing evidence and full procedural protections (*Santosky*), it certainly cannot achieve the same practical result — the complete elimination of all parent-child contact for nearly a year — through an ex parte order entered without notice or hearing. The form of the deprivation (ex parte order vs. formal termination) does not change the substance of the constitutional violation.

42A. The pattern of judicial conduct in this case independently violates due process under *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 884 (2009), which holds that "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable" when objective indicators of bias are present. Here, the 44% ex parte rate, admitted ex parte evidence review, muting of Father during hearings, "do not file" directive, $250 filing barrier, and 329+ days without evidentiary hearing collectively establish structural bias that renders the proceedings fundamentally unfair.

### B. The Ex Parte Order Violates Michigan Court Rules and Must Be Vacated

43. Under MCR 3.207(B), interim orders regarding custody and parenting time may only be entered upon a showing of **proper cause or change of circumstances**. No such showing was made on the record, and no findings to that effect were entered.

44. MCR 3.207(C)(5) **requires** that when an ex parte order is entered affecting custody or parenting time, a hearing must be scheduled within **14 days**. This is a mandatory procedural protection, not a discretionary guideline.

45. No hearing was scheduled within 14 days. No hearing was held within 14 days. No hearing has **ever** been held — not within 14 days, not within 30 days, not within 90 days, not within 180 days, and not within 329+ days. This is a fundamental failure of due process.

46. The entry of **five (5) separate ex parte orders on a single date** — all without notice — compounds this violation. The cumulative effect of these orders stripped Father of all parenting time, imposed financial barriers to filing, and effectively locked Father out of both his child's life and the courts.

47. An ex parte order that is not followed by a timely hearing as required by MCR 3.207(C)(5) is procedurally defective and must be **vacated**. The order cannot stand as a valid exercise of judicial authority when the mandatory procedural requirements were not followed.

### C. The Complete Suspension of Parenting Time Without Best-Interest Findings Violates MCL 722.27a

48. Michigan law requires that all custody and parenting time decisions be made in accordance with the **best interests of the child** as defined by the twelve factors set forth in MCL 722.23. These factors are:

    (a) **The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.** Father has a deep, loving bond with L.D.W., formed during the first two years of 50/50 custody. The HealthWest evaluation confirmed Father's appropriate emotional relationship with the child.

    (b) **The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.** Father has consistently demonstrated the capacity and willingness to provide love, affection, and guidance. No evaluation has ever found otherwise.

    (c) **The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care recognized and permitted under the laws of this state in place of medical care, and other material needs.** Father has always provided for L.D.W.'s material needs during his parenting time.

    (d) **The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.** L.D.W. lived in a stable, 50/50 environment with both parents for the first two years of his life. The August 8, 2025 order **destroyed** that continuity.

    (e) **The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.** Father's home is permanent and stable.

    (f) **The moral fitness of the parties involved.** Father has been cleared by HealthWest evaluation. Seven police reports found no violations. Mother has withheld parenting time 27+ times.

    (g) **The mental and physical health of the parties involved.** The HealthWest evaluation cleared Father. No contrary finding exists.

    (h) **The home, school, and community record of the child.** L.D.W. thrived during the period of 50/50 custody.

    (i) **The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.** L.D.W. is approximately three years old and too young to express a preference.

    (j) **The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents.** This factor weighs **heavily** against Mother. The HealthWest evaluation documented alienation patterns. Mother has denied parenting time 27+ times. Mother's family members have actively participated in alienation. Father, by contrast, has consistently sought to maintain his relationship with L.D.W. and has never interfered with Mother's parenting time.

    (k) **Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.** No finding of domestic violence by Father has ever been made.

    (l) **Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.** The 329+ days of complete separation, the ex parte nature of the order, the documented alienation, and the procedural irregularities are all highly relevant.
