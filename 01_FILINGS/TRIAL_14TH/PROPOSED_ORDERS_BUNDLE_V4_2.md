# PROPOSED ORDER NO. 1: RESTORATION OF PARENTING TIME

## ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF'S EMERGENCY MOTION TO RESTORE PARENTING TIME

At a session of said Court held in the Courthouse in the County of Muskegon, State of Michigan, on the _____ day of _________________, 2026.

**PRESENT: Honorable ________________________, Circuit Court Judge**

The Court, having considered Plaintiff's Emergency Motion to Restore Parenting Time, the file and record herein, and being otherwise fully advised in the premises:

**THE COURT FINDS:**

1. The ex parte orders entered August 8, 2025 suspended all of Plaintiff-Father's parenting time without the procedural safeguards required by MCR 3.206(B)(5)-(6), MCR 3.207(B)(1), and MCR 2.517(A)(1);

2. No findings were made under MCL 722.23 or MCL 722.27a(3);

3. The 329+ day separation constitutes irreparable harm to the parent-child bond; (R. at PIGORS-0551; Exhibit A, Chronological Separation Timeline)

4. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a presumption that parenting time with both parents is in the child's best interests;

5. Defendant's conduct demonstrates a pattern of parental alienation in violation of MCL 722.23(j) — the willingness and ability to facilitate the parent-child relationship — which *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004), recognized as "an important consideration" in custody disputes; (R. at PIGORS-0554; Exhibit B, Parental Alienation Evidence Compilation)

6. Defendant, employed by the **Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division** for nine (9) years, used her institutional familiarity with court procedures to orchestrate the suspension of parenting time, warranting mandatory remedies under MCL 722.27a(9). (R. at PIGORS-0557; Exhibit C, KCPO Employment Confirmation, 9 Years)

**SUPPORTING RATIONALE:** The statutory presumption under MCL 722.27a(1) — that parenting time with both parents is in the child's best interests — was overcome without the required showing under MCL 722.27a(3) that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. The ex parte motion was unsworn in violation of MCR 3.207(B)(1), relied on inadmissible hearsay, and resulted in no MCL 722.23 best interest findings. Father's NEGATIVE narcotics screen and favorable HealthWest assessment affirmatively contradict the basis for the suspension. (R. at PIGORS-0561; Exhibit D, HealthWest Assessment Report, received 9/12/2025; Exhibit D-1, Negative Drug Screen Results) The 329+ day separation during L.D.W.'s critical developmental window (ages 2-4) constitutes ongoing irreparable harm. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31-32 (2010) (most restrictive remedy only as last resort).

**IT IS HEREBY ORDERED:**

A. The ex parte orders of August 8, 2025 suspending Plaintiff's parenting time are **VACATED**;

B. Plaintiff's parenting time is **RESTORED** effective immediately, with the following schedule:
   - Supervised parenting time: twice weekly, two hours minimum, at a neutral location, for the first 30 days;
   - Transition to unsupervised parenting time per the pre-August 2025 schedule upon completion of the supervised period, absent specific findings of danger;

C. A full evidentiary hearing on custody and parenting time shall be scheduled within **14 days** of this Order;

D. Make-up parenting time shall be awarded pursuant to MCL 722.27a(9);

E. This Order supersedes and replaces the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders to the extent they are inconsistent.

# ANTICIPATED DEFENSE & REBUTTAL — PROPOSED ORDERS (CHESS-MODE)

> **Strategic Framework:** Barnes (P55406) may object to the proposed orders themselves — arguing they are premature, presumptuous, or improperly drafted. Watson's familiarity with family court procedure means she knows the technical objections. Pre-rebutting here ensures the orders withstand scrutiny and are ready for judicial signature.

---

### Defense 1: "Proposed Orders Are Premature — No Hearing Has Been Held"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** Submitting proposed orders before a hearing has been held on the underlying motions is premature and presumptuous. The court has not yet made findings of fact or conclusions of law to support these orders.

**This argument fails because:** MCR 2.602(B)(3) specifically requires that proposed orders be submitted **with the motion** — not after the hearing. The rule anticipates that the moving party will provide a proposed order for the court's convenience. Submission of proposed orders is not a presumption of victory — it is compliance with MCR 2.602(B)(3). (R. at PIGORS-0582; Docket Entry No. 22, Companion Motions Filing) The orders contain blank lines for dates and judicial signatures, demonstrating they are proposed, not self-executing. The court retains full discretion to modify, adopt, or reject each proposed order after hearing.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** MCR 2.602(B)(3) (requiring proposed orders with motions); the blank date lines and signature blocks confirming these are proposals; each order cross-references the companion motion it supports.

---

### Defense 2: "The Parenting Time Schedule is Inappropriate / Too Aggressive"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** The proposed graduated schedule (supervised → unsupervised in 30 days) is too aggressive given the 329+ day separation. Barnes will argue the child needs months of therapeutic reunification before any unsupervised contact.

**This argument fails because:** The 329+ day separation was caused by Watson's alienation campaign and the court's failure to hold a timely hearing under MCR 3.206(B)(6). MCL 722.27a(9) mandates make-up parenting time for wrongful denial — the statute contemplates prompt restoration. The proposed schedule already includes a 30-day supervised phase — a reasonable accommodation that addresses any legitimate reintegration concern while preventing the alienation from becoming permanent. Indefinite supervision would reward the alienating parent and violate the MCL 722.27a(1) presumption that a "strong relationship with both parents" is in the child's best interests. *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004), recognizes Factor (j) as critical — a schedule designed to indefinitely limit Father's contact serves Watson's alienation goals, not the child's best interests.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** MCL 722.27a(9) mandating make-up parenting time; MCL 722.27a(1) presumption favoring relationship with both parents; the 30-day supervised transition as a reasonable graduated approach; Father's prior positive parenting record.

---

### Defense 3: "Disqualification Order Should Not Include PPO Case"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** The Motion to Disqualify was filed in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, and the proposed disqualification order improperly extends to Case No. 2023-5907-PP (the PPO case). Barnes will argue the PPO is a separate proceeding requiring a separate disqualification motion.

**This argument fails because:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) requires disqualification whenever a judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party." The bias documented in the disqualification motion is bias against Father as a party — not bias limited to a single case number. Where the same judge presides over related cases involving the same parties, and the demonstrated bias pervades the judge's treatment of one party across proceedings, disqualification from all related cases is appropriate. The PPO in Case No. 2023-5907-PP is directly intertwined with the custody case — Watson used the PPO as an alienation instrument complementing the custody suspension. Separate motions would be duplicative and wasteful.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** The same judge (McNeill) presides over both cases; the same parties are involved; the PPO is weaponized as a dual barrier to the parent-child relationship alongside the custody suspension; the documented bias applies to Father as a person, not a case number.

---

### Defense 4: "Contempt Show Cause Order is Procedurally Improper"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** The proposed Order to Show Cause is procedurally deficient — it should not contain factual findings about Watson's employment or alienation before the hearing. Barnes will argue the order is argumentative.

**This argument fails because:** An Order to Show Cause is a directive requiring the respondent to appear and explain why sanctions should not be imposed. The recitals in the proposed order (regarding Watson's employment and MCL 722.23(j)) are proposed findings for the court's consideration — the court may adopt, modify, or strike them. MCR 3.606 does not prohibit proposed orders from including proposed findings. The recitals serve the practical purpose of putting Watson on notice of the specific conduct at issue, ensuring due process compliance. The court retains full authority to issue its own order with whatever language it deems appropriate.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** MCR 3.606 (no prohibition on proposed findings in show cause orders); the due process function of providing specific notice of the alleged contemptuous conduct; the court's retained discretion to modify the proposed order.
