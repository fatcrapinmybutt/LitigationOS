## I. INTRODUCTION

Defendant Emily A. Watson has engaged in a systematic, willful, and unrelenting campaign to deny Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors all parenting time with his minor child L.D.W. for more than 571 consecutive days. This is not a case of occasional missed weekends or minor scheduling disputes. This is a case of **total parental alienation** — a complete, deliberate severance of the father-child bond enforced through intimidation, false reports, and abuse of court processes.

Michigan's Legislature has enacted mandatory enforcement provisions in the Support and Parenting Time Enforcement Act precisely to address this type of conduct. The statutory framework — MCL 552.644, MCL 552.645, and MCL 600.1701 — does not give courts discretion to ignore parenting time violations. The statutes use mandatory language: the court **"SHALL"** find a violation occurred when the respondent fails to prove compliance; the court **"MUST"** impose sanctions when bad faith is found.

This Brief establishes the legal framework compelling enforcement and demonstrates that every element of civil contempt is satisfied.

## III. LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A. MCL 600.1701 — Inherent Contempt Authority

MCL 600.1701(a) provides that courts have the power to punish by fine or imprisonment, or both, persons guilty of any neglect or violation of duty or misconduct, including "[d]isobedience of any lawful order of the court." This is the foundational authority for contempt proceedings in Michigan.

### B. MCL 552.644 — Parenting Time Enforcement (Mandatory)

MCL 552.644 establishes a **mandatory** enforcement framework for parenting time violations:

#### 1. Initiation of Proceedings — MCL 552.644(1)

When alternative dispute resolution methods fail to resolve a parenting time dispute, the Friend of the Court "must commence a civil contempt proceeding." Private parties may also initiate contempt proceedings directly.

#### 2. Mandatory Findings — MCL 552.644(3)

> *"For the purposes of [MCL 552.644(2)], 'good cause' includes, but is not limited to, consideration of the safety of a child or party who is governed by the parenting time order."*

The critical statutory mechanism: if the respondent **fails to prove by a preponderance of the evidence** that she complied with or had good cause for violating the parenting time order, the court **"SHALL"** find that parenting time was wrongfully denied.

The word **"SHALL"** is mandatory. *Brausch v. Brausch*, 283 Mich App 339, 348 (2009). The court has no discretion to decline a finding of violation when the respondent fails to carry her burden.

#### 3. Available Sanctions — MCL 552.644(2)

Upon a finding of contempt, the court may impose:
- Additional terms and conditions — MCL 552.644(2)(a)
- Modification of parenting time — MCL 552.644(2)(b)
- **Makeup parenting time** — MCL 552.644(2)(c)
- Fine up to $100 — MCL 552.644(2)(d)
- Jail commitment — MCL 552.644(2)(e)
- Jail with work-release — MCL 552.644(2)(f)
- License suspension — MCL 552.644(2)(g)
- Community corrections — MCL 552.644(2)(h)
- Supervised compliance — MCL 552.644(2)(i)

**If no sanctions are imposed, the court MUST state reasons on the record.** MCL 552.644(3).

#### 4. Mandatory Bad Faith Sanctions — MCL 552.644(6)

When the court finds bad faith:
- **MUST** order sanctions ($250 first offense / $500 second / $1,000 third or subsequent)
- **MUST** order payment of the other party's costs — MCL 552.644(8)

#### 5. Commitment Limits — MCL 552.644(4)

- First finding of contempt: not more than 45 days
- Each subsequent finding: not more than 90 days
- Release when reasonable cause to believe parent will comply

### C. MCL 552.645 — Compensatory Parenting Time

MCL 552.645 provides for compensatory makeup parenting time and license suspension as enforcement mechanisms. If the contemner fails to comply with makeup parenting time and ongoing parenting time schedules, the court may suspend licenses conditioned under MCL 552.644(2)(g). MCL 552.645(3) provides that if a modification motion is timely filed, consolidated hearings must be held.

### D. MCR 3.208 — Procedural Requirements

MCR 3.208(B) governs parenting time contempt procedures:

1. The proceeding commences by motion for an order to show cause — MCR 3.208(B)(1)
2. Notice must include allegations, possible sanctions, and modification rights — MCR 3.208(B)(3)
3. Show cause hearing: minimum 7 days after personal service or 9 days after mailing — MCR 3.208(B)(4)
4. Court may hold hearing without FOC unless evidence requires FOC records — MCR 3.208(B)(5)

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. All Elements of Civil Contempt Are Satisfied

To establish civil contempt, the moving party must demonstrate: (1) a valid court order existed; (2) the contemnor had knowledge of the order; (3) the contemnor violated the order; and (4) the violation was willful. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 97-98 (1987).

**Element 1 — Valid Court Order:** The parenting time order in Case No. 2024-001507-DC is a valid, enforceable order of this Court.

**Element 2 — Knowledge:** Defendant is a party to this action, was served with all orders, and has demonstrated knowledge of the parenting time order by selectively invoking its terms when convenient (e.g., her April 15, 2025 text: "I will not be there on Friday **per our custody order** regarding Easter holiday").

**Element 3 — Violation:** The 571+ days of denied parenting time, documented through AppClose communications, court records, and Plaintiff's verified affidavit, constitute ongoing violations of the parenting time order.

**Element 4 — Willfulness:** Defendant's violations are willful because:
- She has the **ability** to comply (the child resides with her)
- She has offered **no legitimate excuse** (multiple investigations, zero findings)
- Her conduct is **deliberate** (coordinated with Watson family intimidation campaign)
- She has demonstrated **knowledge** of the order (citing it when convenient)
- She has **affirmatively refused** compliance (April 2025 text: "No. I won't be giving you an extra day")

*See also In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 671 (2009) (willfulness established where contemnor had ability to comply and failed to show justification); *MacIntyre v MacIntyre*, 267 Mich App 449 (2005) (affirming contempt for systematic parenting time denial and ordering make-up parenting time).

### B. The Burden Shifts to Defendant Under MCL 552.644(3)

Once Plaintiff establishes a prima facie case of parenting time denial — which the 571+ days of complete cutoff amply demonstrate — the burden shifts to Defendant under MCL 552.644(3) to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that she either complied with the order or had "good cause" for the violation.

Defendant **cannot** meet this burden. The only recognized "good cause" under MCL 552.644(3) relates to "the safety of a child or party." Here, multiple police investigations yielded zero findings, the drug screen was negative, and the HealthWest evaluation returned all zeros. There is **no safety basis** for 571+ days of parenting time denial.

Indeed, the Michigan Court of Appeals has held that "a parent's unsubstantiated fears or allegations, without more, do not constitute good cause for denying parenting time." *See Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31 (2010). Defendant's pattern of making allegations that are consistently not substantiated is precisely the conduct the enforcement statute targets.

### C. MCL 552.644(3) — The Court SHALL Find a Violation

The mandatory language of MCL 552.644(3) compels the result. When Defendant fails to prove compliance or good cause by a preponderance — which she cannot — the statute commands: the court **"SHALL"** find that parenting time was wrongfully denied.

"The Legislature's use of the word 'shall' indicates a mandatory, rather than permissive, directive." *Brausch v. Brausch*, 283 Mich App 339, 348 (2009).

The Michigan Court of Appeals has consistently held that parenting time enforcement provisions must be given their mandatory effect. *See also Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186 (2004) (recognizing the importance of parenting time provisions in Michigan's child custody framework and the court's obligation to enforce them).

### D. Bad Faith Is Established

Defendant's conduct constitutes bad faith under MCL 552.644(6):
- **571+ days** of denial — not an isolated incident but a sustained campaign
- **305 intimidation incidents** by Watson family members — coordinated obstruction
- **Multiple unfounded police reports** — all yielding zero findings against Plaintiff
- **Abuse of ex parte processes** to obstruct parenting time
- **Affirmative refusal** documented in writing (April 2025 AppClose messages)
- **Three jobs lost** by Plaintiff due to jailing and court appearances caused by Defendant's false reports

Upon a finding of bad faith, the sanctions under MCL 552.644(6) are **mandatory** — the court "MUST" impose them.

MCL 552.644(2)(c) specifically authorizes "makeup parenting time" for "wrongfully denied parenting time." Given the unprecedented 571+ days of denial, Plaintiff requests a substantial compensatory parenting time schedule including:

1. **Immediate reinstatement** of regular parenting time
2. **Extended makeup blocks** (additional weekends and holiday time)

The compensatory parenting time framework is not discretionary — it is a core component of the statutory enforcement scheme. *See* MCL 552.644(2)(c); MCL 552.645.

## VII. BEST INTEREST OF THE CHILD REQUIRES ENFORCEMENT

### A. Parenting Time Serves the Child's Best Interest

Michigan's parenting time statute, MCL 722.27a(1), establishes a strong presumption that parenting time is in the child's best interest. MCL 722.27a(3) provides that a child has a **right** to parenting time with a parent unless clear and convincing evidence shows endangerment.

No such evidence exists here. The multiple investigations with zero findings, negative drug screen, and clean evaluation demonstrate that parenting time with Plaintiff poses no risk to L.D.W.

### B. The Duration of Denial Constitutes an Emergency

L.D.W. was born November 9, 2022. At 571+ days of total contact denial, Plaintiff has been excluded from approximately **one-third** of his son's entire life. During this critical developmental window (ages 2-4), children form foundational attachment bonds. *See* MCL 722.23(b) (capacity to provide love, affection, and guidance); MCL 722.23(d) (length and adequacy of home environment). The court cannot restore lost developmental time — only prevent further loss.

### C. Harm From Parental Alienation

Research consistently demonstrates that children subjected to parental alienation suffer:
- Increased anxiety, depression, and behavioral problems
- Attachment disruption and relationship difficulties
- Academic decline
- Long-term psychological harm that worsens with duration

The 571+ day period of total contact denial has passed the threshold where alienation effects become entrenched and increasingly difficult to reverse. Every additional day of denial compounds this harm.

### D. Enforcement Is the Remedy

Enforcement through contempt proceedings — including compensatory parenting time, sanctions, and graduated enforcement — is the only mechanism available to begin repairing the damage to the father-child relationship. Further delay serves only to entrench the alienation.

As the Michigan Supreme Court recognized in *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 195 (2004), "the best interests of the child are served by enforcing parenting time orders" and courts have an affirmative duty to ensure that both parents maintain meaningful relationships with their children.

### E. Graduated Sanctions Are Appropriate

Given the severity and duration of Defendant's contempt, Plaintiff respectfully requests graduated sanctions:

1. **Immediate:** Makeup parenting time schedule — MCL 552.644(2)(c)
2. **Mandatory sanctions:** $1,000 per MCL 552.644(6) (third or subsequent offense)
3. **Attorney fees and costs:** MCL 552.644(8) (mandatory upon bad faith finding)
4. **Conditional commitment:** Jail sentence stayed on condition of compliance — MCL 552.644(2)(e)
5. **License suspension:** MCL 552.644(2)(g) if noncompliance continues
6. **Supervised exchanges:** To prevent Watson family intimidation at exchanges
