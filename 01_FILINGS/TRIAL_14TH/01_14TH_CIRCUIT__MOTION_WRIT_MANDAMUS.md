# PART II: BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF VERIFIED COMPLAINT FOR WRIT OF MANDAMUS

---

## I. STANDARD FOR MANDAMUS RELIEF

27. A writ of mandamus is an extraordinary remedy that may be issued to compel the performance of a ministerial act by a public officer or governmental entity. MCL 600.5701; MCR 3.305.

28. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that mandamus lies where: "(1) the party seeking the writ has a clear legal right to performance of the specific duty sought; (2) the defendant has a clear legal duty to perform the act; (3) the act is ministerial in nature; and (4) no other adequate legal remedy exists." *Taxpayers Allied for Constitutional Taxation v Wayne County*, 450 Mich 119, 129 (1995).

29. A ministerial act is one "which a person performs in a given state of facts in a prescribed manner, in obedience to the mandate of legal authority, and without regard to the exercise of his own judgment upon the propriety of the act being done." *Toan v McGinn*, 271 Mich 28, 34 (1935).

## II. ALL FOUR ELEMENTS ARE MET

### A. Clear Legal Right

30. Plaintiff's right to hearings on pending motions is established by MCR 2.119(E)(1) and the due process clauses of the U.S. Constitution (14th Amendment) and Michigan Constitution (Art. 1, § 17).

31. The fundamental nature of parental rights elevates this right to the highest level of constitutional protection. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982).

32. When a parent's rights have been effectively terminated through an ex parte order that has persisted for 329+ days without hearing, the failure to schedule a hearing is itself a due process violation of the most extreme kind.

### B. Clear Legal Duty

33. MCR 2.119(E)(1) uses mandatory language: motions "will be heard" within specified timeframes. This is not discretionary.

34. MCR 2.003(D)(1) uses mandatory language: the judge "must" either disqualify or refer. There is no option to simply ignore the motion.

35. MCR 3.207(C)(5) requires an evidentiary hearing when parenting time has been suspended ex parte. This hearing has never been held despite 329+ days of suspension.

36. MCR 2.602(D) uses mandatory language: the court "must serve" copies of orders "immediately."

### C. Ministerial Nature

37. None of the duties at issue require the exercise of judicial discretion. Scheduling hearings, processing disqualification motions, producing transcripts, and serving orders are all administrative, ministerial functions governed by clear procedural rules.

### D. No Adequate Alternative Remedy

38. Plaintiff has exhausted all reasonable alternative remedies:
    a. He has filed motions that remain unheard
    b. He has requested hearings that remain unscheduled
    c. He cannot appeal because there is no final order when motions are simply ignored
    d. The passage of each additional day without a hearing causes irreparable harm to the parent-child relationship

39. The extraordinary nature of mandamus relief is warranted by the extraordinary deprivation at issue — 329+ days of total parent-child separation based on an ex parte order that was never subjected to evidentiary review.

## III. CONSTITUTIONAL URGENCY

40. The U.S. Supreme Court has recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

41. When a court deprives a parent of this fundamental right through an ex parte order and then fails to schedule a hearing to review that deprivation for 329+ days, the court has created a constitutional crisis that only mandamus can remedy.

42. Applying the *Mathews v Eldridge* balancing test (424 U.S. 319 (1976)):
    a. **Private interest:** A parent's fundamental liberty interest in the care and custody of their child — the highest category of protected liberty
    b. **Risk of erroneous deprivation:** Extreme — parenting time was suspended based on an ex parte motion containing unsubstantiated allegations that were subsequently contradicted by clinical evidence
    c. **Government interest:** No legitimate government interest is served by refusing to schedule hearings on pending motions

## IV. PREEMPTIVE RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED COUNTERARGUMENTS

### 1. "The Court Has Discretion in Scheduling"

**Rebuttal:** Scheduling hearings is a ministerial, not discretionary, function. MCR 2.119(E)(1) mandates that motions "will be heard" within specified timeframes. The court's duty to hold hearings is not optional. *See Taxpayers Allied*, 450 Mich at 129 ("mandamus lies to compel the performance of a ministerial act"). Moreover, MCR 3.207(C)(5) specifically requires an evidentiary hearing after ex parte custody modification — a requirement that has been violated for 329+ days.

### 2. "Plaintiff Has an Adequate Remedy by Appeal"

**Rebuttal:** Appeal is inadequate because (a) there is no final order to appeal when motions remain unheard and unruled upon; (b) the appellate timeline (months to years) is incompatible with the ongoing daily harm to the parent-child bond; and (c) the very relief sought — scheduling of hearings — cannot be obtained through appellate review. *See Rental Props. Owners Ass'n of Kent County v Kent County Treasurer*, 308 Mich App 498, 517 (2014) (mandamus appropriate where alternative remedy is inadequate).

### 3. "Plaintiff's Filings Are Frivolous or Vexatious"

**Rebuttal:** No vexatious-litigant finding has been entered under MCL 600.5701. A parent whose fundamental rights have been suspended for 329+ days without hearing is not vexatious — he is exercising the only avenue available to vindicate his constitutional rights. The First Amendment and the Due Process Clause protect the right to petition the courts. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971). The characterization of constitutionally protected filings as "frivolous" is itself evidence of the bias that necessitates disqualification.

### 4. "The Ex Parte Orders Were Justified by Emergency Circumstances"

**Rebuttal:** Even if emergency circumstances justified the initial ex parte orders (which they did not — the "emergency" was pre-planned per Albert Watson's admission), no emergency persists 329+ days later. The failure to hold an evidentiary hearing within 14 days as required by MCR 3.207(C)(5) transforms what might have been a valid emergency order into an unconstitutional indefinite deprivation. *See Fuentes v Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67, 81 (1972) (ex parte seizure requires prompt post-deprivation hearing).
