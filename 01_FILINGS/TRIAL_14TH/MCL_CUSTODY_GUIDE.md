## 2. MCL 722.23 — Best Interest Factors (a)–(l) {#2-best-interest-factors}

> **"As used in this act, 'best interests of the child' means the sum total of the following factors to be considered, evaluated, and determined by the court."** — MCL 722.23

### Factor (a): Love, affection, and other emotional ties

**Statutory Text:** The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.

**Application to Our Case:** Andrew maintained consistent, loving relationship until separation. 329+ days of forced separation undermines this factor analysis.

### Factor (b): Capacity to provide love, affection, guidance

**Statutory Text:** The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.

**Application to Our Case:** Andrew demonstrated capacity through daily caregiving before separation.

### Factor (c): Capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care

**Statutory Text:** The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care, and other material needs.

**Application to Our Case:** Andrew maintained stable housing and employment. Material needs always met.

### Factor (d): Length of time in stable environment

**Statutory Text:** The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.

**Application to Our Case:** Child lived with Andrew in stable environment prior to ex parte order disruption.

### Factor (e): Permanence of family unit

**Statutory Text:** The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.

**Application to Our Case:** Both homes should be evaluated for stability and permanence.

### Factor (f): Moral fitness

**Statutory Text:** The moral fitness of the parties involved.

**Application to Our Case:** Review evidence of both parties' conduct and fitness.

### Factor (g): Mental and physical health

**Statutory Text:** The mental and physical health of the parties involved.

**Application to Our Case:** No disqualifying health issues for Andrew.

### Factor (h): Home, school, community record

**Statutory Text:** The home, school, and community record of the child.

**Application to Our Case:** Child's age (1-3) limits school record; community ties to both parents.

### Factor (i): Reasonable preference of child

**Statutory Text:** The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.

**Application to Our Case:** Child too young to express preference (under 3).

### Factor (j): Willingness to facilitate relationship (ANTI-ALIENATION)

**Statutory Text:** The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents.

**Application to Our Case:** 🔴 CRITICAL — Factor J directly addresses alienation. Watson's conduct of 329+ day separation is strong evidence of Factor J violation.

### Factor (k): Domestic violence

**Statutory Text:** Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child.

**Application to Our Case:** PPO proceedings (Lane D) must be evaluated in context.

### Factor (l): Other relevant factors

**Statutory Text:** Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute.

**Application to Our Case:** Court must state on record any additional factors considered.


## 3. MCL 722.27 — Custody Modification & ECE {#3-custody-modification}

### Established Custodial Environment (ECE) — MCL 722.27(1)(c)

> An established custodial environment exists if "over an appreciable time the child naturally looks to the custodian in that environment for guidance, discipline, the necessities of life, and parental comfort."

**Burden of Proof:**
- If a proposed change would alter the ECE → **clear and convincing evidence** required
- If no ECE change → **preponderance of the evidence** standard

### Change of Circumstances — Vodvarka v Grasher Standard

Before modifying custody, the moving party must show either:
1. **Proper cause** — an appropriate ground that has or could have a significant effect on the child's life
2. **Change of circumstances** — conditions that have changed since the last custody order that affect the child's well-being

*Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 1 (2003) — Threshold must be met before court re-evaluates best interest factors.

### Benchbook: Established Custodial Environment

• Exists if child naturally looks to custodian for guidance, discipline, necessities
• Not determined by hours in physical custody
• Can exist with both parents, one parent, or neither
• If established: clear and convincing evidence required to change custody
[TIP] Make specific factual findings
[TIP] Consider child's perspective, not parent's
[TIP] Both parents can have established environment
[TIP] Burden shifts to moving party if established

Related: MCL 722.27

### Benchbook: Modification of Custody

• Requires proper cause or change of circumstances
• Proper cause: matters affecting best interest with significant effect on child
• Change of circumstances: changes since last order
• Moving party bears burden of proof
[TIP] Focus on child impact, not parent preference
[TIP] Require substantial evidence of change
[TIP] Consider totality of circumstances
[TIP] Less scrutiny if no established environment

Related: MCR 3.210, MCL 722.27

### Key Principles

1. **Presumption of reasonable parenting time** — MCL 722.27a(1): "Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child."
2. **Cannot deny without endangerment** — Parenting time may only be denied or restricted if it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health.
3. **Frequency and duration** — Must be sufficient to preserve and foster the parent-child bond.
4. **Specific parenting time** — If parties cannot agree, court must establish specific schedule.

### Benchbook: Presumption of Reasonable Parenting Time

• Strong statutory presumption (MCL 722.27a)
• Cannot restrict without clear and convincing evidence
• Endangerment standard high
• Consider supervised parenting time before denial
[TIP] Mere allegations insufficient
[TIP] Focus on child safety, not parent punishment
[TIP] Supervised PT often appropriate middle ground
[TIP] Review and liberalize as circumstances improve

Related: MCR 3.206, MCL 722.27a(3)

### Benchbook: Eldred v. Ziny Endangerment Standard

• Endangerment must be more than theoretical
• Requires significant risk based on substantial evidence
• Past conduct may support finding if recent/relevant
• Parent's conduct relevant only if affects child
[TIP] Require evidence, not speculation
[TIP] Consider protective conditions
[TIP] Avoid using PT restriction as custody lever
[TIP] Document specific incidents of endangerment


## 5. MCL 722.27a(9) — Mandatory Remedies {#5-mandatory-remedies}

> When parenting time has been wrongfully denied, MCL 722.27a(9) provides **mandatory** remedies:

1. **Makeup parenting time** of the same type as denied (not just additional time)
2. **Economic sanctions** — reimbursement for expenses incurred due to wrongful denial
3. **Modification of parenting time** — the court may modify the existing order
4. **Contempt** — the offending party may be found in contempt
5. **Attorney fees and costs** — the court shall order payment

**Application:** With 329+ days of denied parenting time, Andrew is entitled to ALL of these remedies. The statute uses "shall" — mandatory, not discretionary.
