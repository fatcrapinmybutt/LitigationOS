## II. LEGAL STANDARD -- MCR 2.003

### A. Grounds for Disqualification

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when:

**(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.

**(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 129 S Ct 2252, 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

### B. Procedure

MCR 2.003(D)(1) requires that a motion to disqualify be filed and heard as provided in this rule. The chief judge or another judge must hear the motion if the challenged judge does not grant it.

## IV. JUDICIAL CANON VIOLATIONS

### A. Canon 1 -- Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

Canon 1 requires a judge to "uphold the integrity and independence of the judiciary." Judge McNeill's interference with the HealthWest evaluation, her reliance on illegally obtained evidence, and her pattern of ex parte orders undermining fundamental constitutional rights violate the integrity of the judiciary.

### B. Canon 2 -- Appearance of Impropriety

Canon 2 requires a judge to "avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." The appearance of impropriety standard "is an objective one: would a reasonable, informed observer -- knowing the relevant facts and circumstances -- have a reasonable basis to question the judge's impartiality." A 44% ex parte rate with 100% one-sided outcomes creates an overwhelming appearance of impropriety.

### C. Canon 3 -- Impartial and Diligent Performance

Canon 3 requires a judge to "perform the duties of judicial office impartially and diligently." The documented pattern of unequal treatment, denied motions, and procedural shortcuts demonstrates a failure of impartial performance. The volume of 1127+ documented violations indicates systemic rather than isolated failures.

## V. CASE LAW SUPPORTING DISQUALIFICATION

### A. Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009)

In *Caperton*, the Supreme Court held that "there is a serious risk of actual bias -- based on objective and reasonable perceptions -- when a person with a personal stake in a particular case had a significant and disproportionate influence in placing the judge on the case." While *Caperton* addressed campaign contributions, the principle applies broadly: due process requires recusal whenever objective circumstances create a constitutionally intolerable probability of actual bias.

The statistical evidence in this case -- 52 ex parte orders, 44% rate, 100% favoring one party -- creates precisely the "constitutionally intolerable probability of actual bias" that *Caperton* addresses.

### B. In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133 (1955)

The Supreme Court in *In re Murchison* held that "a fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process" and that "our system of law has always endeavored to prevent even the probability of unfairness." The Court emphasized that justice must not only be done but must "satisfy the appearance of justice." *Id.* at 136.

### C. Liteky v. United States, 510 U.S. 540 (1994)

While *Liteky* held that judicial rulings alone rarely constitute a valid basis for recusal, the Court acknowledged that rulings can demonstrate bias when they "reveal such a high degree of favoritism or antagonism as to make fair judgment impossible." The cumulative record in this case -- 52 one-sided ex parte orders, interference with evaluations, reliance on illegal evidence -- crosses this threshold.

### D. Michigan Authority -- In re Contempt of Dougherty, 429 Mich 81 (1987)

Michigan courts have recognized that judges must maintain not only actual impartiality but the appearance of impartiality. When the record demonstrates a pattern of conduct that would cause a reasonable observer to question the judge's impartiality, disqualification is warranted.

## VIII. SPECIFIC INSTANCES OF BIAS -- DETAILED RECORD

The following specific instances, drawn from the 1,127 documented judicial violations, illustrate the pattern of bias requiring disqualification:

### A. Ex Parte Order Practice

1. **August 8, 2025**: Ex parte suspension of all parenting time without notice to Plaintiff, based on an illegally obtained recording in violation of MCL 750.539c.
2. **Pattern of entry**: Fifty-two (52) ex parte orders entered throughout the case, with no record of reasonable efforts to notify Plaintiff before entry of these orders.
3. **One-sided outcomes**: Every ex parte order entered favored Defendant. Not a single ex parte order was entered in Plaintiff's favor. A neutral adjudicator would be expected to rule in favor of each party at least some of the time.

### B. Interference with Independent Evaluation

1. The Court ordered a HealthWest evaluation, which is intended to be an independent, unbiased assessment.
2. The initial evaluation returned "ALL ZEROS," finding no basis for concern regarding Plaintiff.
3. Rather than accepting this independent finding, Judge McNeill sent a letter to the evaluator -- an action that is extraordinary and inappropriate for a neutral arbiter.
4. Following this communication, the evaluator changed the finding to a "Rule Out" designation.
5. This sequence of events -- independent result unfavorable to the Court's apparent preferred outcome, followed by judicial communication, followed by changed result -- is the hallmark of outcome-driven adjudication, not neutral fact-finding.

### C. Handling of Exculpatory Evidence

1. **Drug screen results**: Plaintiff tested negative for all substances. This exculpatory evidence was not given weight in subsequent proceedings.
2. **Police investigations**: Multiple separate investigations yielded zero findings. These results were not considered grounds for restoring parenting time.
3. **No criminal charges**: Despite Defendant's numerous allegations, no prosecutor has found sufficient basis to file charges. This absence of criminal proceedings was not treated as relevant.
4. A neutral judge confronted with negative drug screens, clean evaluations, and multiple clear investigations would, at minimum, schedule a hearing to reconsider the basis for the parenting time suspension. Judge McNeill did not do so.

### D. Incarceration for Civil Contempt

1. Plaintiff was incarcerated for 59+ days on civil contempt without adequate purge conditions or proportionality review.
2. When liberty is at stake, the right to counsel attaches. *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011). No counsel was appointed.
3. The length and conditions of incarceration exceeded what was necessary or proportionate for civil coercive purposes, suggesting punitive rather than coercive intent.
4. The incarceration further disrupted Plaintiff's employment, housing stability, and ability to maintain relationships with his children -- compounding the very harms this Court's orders were purportedly designed to address.

### E. Retaliation for Protected Activity

1. Following Plaintiff's filing of a JTC complaint, the pattern of adverse rulings intensified.
2. Following Plaintiff's filing of a bar complaint, additional sanctions were imposed.
3. Following Plaintiff's appellate filings, further restrictions were placed on Plaintiff.
4. This escalating pattern of adverse action following protected First Amendment activity (petitioning government for redress of grievances) suggests retaliatory animus. *Perry v. Sindermann*, 408 U.S. 593 (1972); *Mt. Healthy City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Ed. v. Doyle*, 429 U.S. 274 (1977).
