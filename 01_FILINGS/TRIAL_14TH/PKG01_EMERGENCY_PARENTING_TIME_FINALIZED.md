# III. STANDARD OF REVIEW

14. Custody and parenting time decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 91 (2010). An abuse of discretion occurs when the result is so palpably and grossly violative of fact and logic that it evidences not the exercise of will but perversity of will, not the exercise of judgment but defiance thereof. *Id.*

15. Findings of fact are reviewed for clear error. MCR 2.613(C). Here, no findings of fact were made, rendering the standard inapplicable — the absence of required findings is itself legal error.

16. Questions of constitutional law and statutory interpretation are reviewed de novo. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 507 (2003).

## ARGUMENT I: THE STATUTORY PRESUMPTION OF MCL 722.27a WAS VIOLATED

### Issue

17. Whether the complete suspension of Father's parenting time without the required statutory findings violates MCL 722.27a's presumption favoring parenting time and its requirement that suspension be supported by a finding of endangerment.

### Rule

18. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a statutory presumption in favor of parenting time:

> *"It is presumed to be in the best interests of a child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents."*

19. This presumption is not aspirational; it is a substantive legal standard that places the burden on the party seeking restriction. To overcome the presumption, MCL 722.27a(3) requires a showing that "parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

20. MCL 722.27a(7) further provides that the court shall consider "the frequency, duration, and type of parenting time" that serves the child's best interests. The statute contemplates a range of parenting time options — from liberal unsupervised time to supervised visitation — not an all-or-nothing approach.

21. MCL 722.27a(9) provides a mandatory remedy when parenting time is wrongfully denied:

> *"If the court finds that a parent has wrongfully denied the other parent parenting time . . . the court shall . . . [m]ake up the parenting time."*

### Application

22. The trial court's August 8, 2025 orders contain **no finding** that Father's parenting time would "endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." The pre-printed checkbox form order is devoid of any endangerment analysis. This omission is fatal: without a finding of endangerment, the statutory presumption of MCL 722.27a(1) remains unrebutted, and parenting time may not be restricted — let alone eliminated entirely.

23. The only evidence offered to support restriction — unsworn hearsay about alleged "meth use" and an alleged "eviction" — is legally insufficient for two independent reasons:

   a. **Evidentiary insufficiency:** The statements are inadmissible hearsay under MRE 801(c) and MRE 802. No exception applies. Inadmissible evidence cannot support a finding of fact.

   b. **Substantive insufficiency:** Even if the statements were admissible, they are directly contradicted by Father's **NEGATIVE** narcotics screening (*see* Exhibit D) and unsupported by any authenticated documentation.

24. The total elimination of all contact — zero visits, zero phone calls, zero presence in the child's life for 571+ days — is not a "type" of parenting time contemplated by MCL 722.27a(7). It is the abolition of the parental relationship by judicial order. The statute requires the court to consider frequency, duration, and type; the court considered none of these factors.

25. Because the parenting time was wrongfully denied without the required statutory findings, Father is entitled to make-up parenting time under MCL 722.27a(9) for 571+ cumulative days of deprivation.

### Conclusion

26. The suspension violates MCL 722.27a because: (a) no statutory finding of endangerment was made; (b) the statutory presumption favoring parenting time was ignored; (c) the evidence was legally insufficient to overcome the presumption; and (d) the remedy imposed — total elimination of all contact — was disproportionate to any showing made. The suspension must be vacated and parenting time restored.

## ARGUMENT II: THE COURT FAILED TO MAKE REQUIRED MCL 722.23 BEST INTEREST FINDINGS

### Issue

27. Whether the trial court committed reversible error by failing to make findings on any of the twelve MCL 722.23 best interest factors before suspending all parenting time.

### Rule

28. MCL 722.23 requires evaluation of twelve enumerated best interest factors — (a) through (l) — in any custody or parenting time determination. The Michigan Supreme Court has unequivocally held: "Custody decisions require findings under **all** of the best interest factors." *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010) (emphasis in original).

29. MCR 2.517(A)(1) independently requires that in contested matters, the court "find the facts specially and state separately its conclusions of law." The failure to make required findings constitutes reversible error. *Id.*

30. The *Pierron* mandate is not limited to initial custody determinations. Any decision that materially affects the custodial arrangement — including the complete suspension of parenting time — requires best interest analysis. *See* MCL 722.27(1)(c) (court may modify parenting time "for proper cause shown" and "in the best interest of the child").

### Application

31. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders contain **zero findings** on any of the twelve best interest factors. Not one factor was addressed. Specifically:

   - **Factor (a) — Love, affection, and emotional ties:** No finding was made regarding the strong bond between Father and L.D.W., documented by extensive photographic and testimonial evidence. (*See* Exhibit F.)

   - **Factor (b) — Capacity to provide love, affection, and guidance:** No finding was made regarding Father's demonstrated capacity to parent L.D.W.

   - **Factor (c) — Capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care:** No finding was made regarding Father's ability to provide for L.D.W.'s material needs. Father maintains stable housing and employment. (*See* Affidavit of Andrew J. Pigors.)

   - **Factor (d) — Length of time in established custodial environment:** No finding was made regarding the established environment L.D.W. had with Father prior to deprivation.

   - **Factor (e) — Permanence of family unit:** No finding was made.

   - **Factor (f) — Moral fitness:** No finding was made. Father's negative drug screen (*see* Exhibit D) directly addresses this factor in his favor.

   - **Factor (g) — Mental and physical health:** No finding was made.

   - **Factor (h) — Home, school, and community record:** No finding was made.

   - **Factor (i) — Reasonable preference of child:** Not applicable given L.D.W.'s age (3 years).

   - **Factor (j) — Willingness to facilitate parent-child relationship:** This is the single most critical factor in this case, yet no finding was made. Mother filed an unsworn ex parte motion to eliminate all of Father's contact with L.D.W. — the most extreme interference with the parent-child relationship possible. Under *Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004), Factor (j) is "an important consideration" that reflects a parent's respect for the other parent's relationship with the child.

   - **Factor (k) — Domestic violence:** No finding was made.

   - **Factor (l) — Other relevant factors:** No finding was made regarding the impact of 571+ days of separation on a three-year-old child during the critical developmental window.

32. The complete absence of any best interest findings is not a harmless procedural deficiency. It is a failure to apply the substantive legal standard that governs every custody and parenting time decision in Michigan. Without best interest findings, the court's order is an exercise of unbounded discretion, unconstrained by the statutory framework the Legislature enacted to protect children and parents alike.

### Conclusion

33. The complete absence of MCL 722.23 best interest findings constitutes clear legal error requiring reversal under *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92, and MCR 2.517(A)(1). The ex parte orders must be vacated, and any subsequent determination must include findings on all twelve factors.

## ARGUMENT III: FATHER'S FOURTEENTH AMENDMENT DUE PROCESS RIGHTS WERE VIOLATED

### Issue

34. Whether the entry of ex parte orders suspending all parenting time — based on an unverified motion and inadmissible hearsay, without prior notice to Father and without a timely post-deprivation hearing — violates Father's due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment and corresponding Michigan constitutional and procedural protections.

### Rule

35. The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that the right to the care, custody, and companionship of one's child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment:

   > *"The interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."* — *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

   > *"The rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed essential, . . . basic civil rights of man, . . . and rights far more precious . . . than property rights."* — *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972) (internal quotations omitted).

36. When a fundamental right is at stake, the government must provide procedural protections commensurate with the magnitude of the interest. The *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335 (1976) balancing test requires evaluation of: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used; and (3) the governmental interest.

37. In *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753-54 (1982), the Supreme Court held that the private interest of a parent in the care of his child is "commanding" and demands heightened procedural protections:

   > *"The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State."*

38. Michigan court rules provide specific procedural protections for ex parte custody and parenting time orders:

   - MCR 3.207(B)(1): The motion must be supported by "specific facts in a verified pleading or affidavit."
   - MCR 3.206(B)(5): The movant must demonstrate "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage."
   - MCR 3.206(B)(6): A hearing must be scheduled "as soon as possible" after entry of the ex parte order.

### Application

39. Applying the *Mathews* balancing test:

   **First factor — Private interest:** The private interest at stake is of the highest constitutional magnitude: a father's relationship with his three-year-old son. The Supreme Court has consistently placed this interest at the apex of constitutionally protected liberties. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65; *Santosky*, 455 US at 753-54. This is not a case involving property, a government benefit, or a commercial license — it is the severance of a parent-child bond during the most critical period of the child's development.

   **Second factor — Risk of erroneous deprivation:** The risk of erroneous deprivation was maximized by the systematic failure of every applicable procedural safeguard:

   | Required Safeguard | Status | Authority |
   |-------------------|--------|-----------|
   | Verified pleading or affidavit | **VIOLATED** — Motion was unsworn | MCR 3.207(B)(1) |
   | Specific facts showing immediate harm | **VIOLATED** — Only hearsay offered | MCR 3.206(B)(5) |
   | Notice to Father before deprivation | **VIOLATED** — No notice given | Due Process Clause |
   | Child-specific findings of fact | **VIOLATED** — Checkbox form only | MCR 2.517(A)(1) |
   | Hearing "as soon as possible" | **VIOLATED** — 571+ days, no hearing | MCR 3.206(B)(6) |
   | Opportunity to be heard | **VIOLATED** — No hearing held | Due Process Clause |
   | Admissible evidence | **VIOLATED** — Hearsay only | MRE 801, 802 |

   Every procedural safeguard designed to reduce the risk of error was absent. The result was predictable: an erroneous deprivation based on unsworn, inadmissible, and contradicted allegations.

   **Third factor — Governmental interest:** The governmental interest in protecting children is substantial — but that interest is served by the procedural safeguards, not by their abandonment. The verification requirement ensures truthful allegations. The hearing requirement ensures evidentiary testing. The findings requirement ensures principled adjudication. Dispensing with these safeguards does not protect the child; it leaves the child's welfare to the unexamined allegations of one parent.

40. The deprivation here is not merely procedurally defective — it is substantively unjust. Father's narcotics screening was negative. (*See* Exhibit D.) The sole factual basis for the suspension has been affirmatively disproved. Yet the suspension continues, seven months later, without a hearing.

41. The failure to hold a hearing within a reasonable time after entry of the ex parte order independently violates due process. MCR 3.206(B)(6) mandates a hearing "as soon as possible." Seven months and counting is not "as soon as possible" by any standard. The prolonged deprivation without a hearing has created a de facto permanent custody arrangement — one imposed without the procedural protections that even a temporary arrangement requires.

### Conclusion

42. Father's fundamental right to parent his child has been deprived without constitutionally adequate procedure. The ex parte orders were entered on an unverified motion, supported by inadmissible hearsay, without notice, without a hearing, and without findings. The deprivation has persisted for over seven months without any post-deprivation hearing. This is a textbook due process violation. The ex parte orders must be vacated, and parenting time must be restored immediately pending a proper evidentiary hearing with full procedural protections.

## ARGUMENT IV: THE PROPER STANDARD UNDER VODVARKA WAS NOT APPLIED

### Issue

43. Whether the trial court erred by effectively modifying the custodial arrangement without applying the threshold requirements established in *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003).

### Rule

44. In *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509-12 (2003), the Michigan Court of Appeals established that before a court may revisit a custody or parenting time order, the moving party must demonstrate either "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances" that has, or is likely to have, a material effect on the child:

   > *"To establish proper cause, a movant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence the existence of an appropriate ground for legal action to be taken by the trial court. The appropriate ground(s) should be relevant to at least one of the twelve statutory best interest factors, and must be of such magnitude as to have a significant effect on the child's well-being."*

   > *"To establish a change of circumstances, a movant must prove . . . that, since the entry of the last custody order, the conditions surrounding custody of the child, which have or could have a significant effect on the child's well-being, have materially changed."*

45. The *Vodvarka* threshold requirement serves a critical purpose: it prevents the relitigation of custody and parenting time based on trivial or unsubstantiated allegations. It imposes discipline on the modification process by requiring a threshold showing before the court may reopen best interest analysis.

### Application

46. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders effectively modified the parenting time arrangement from whatever time Father previously had to zero contact. This is a modification — indeed, the most extreme modification possible.

47. No *Vodvarka* threshold showing was made. The unsworn motion did not establish "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances" by a preponderance of the evidence. The allegations were:

   a. **Unsubstantiated and contradicted.** The allegation of drug use was refuted by Father's negative narcotics screening. (*See* Exhibit D.)

   b. **Inadmissible.** The allegations consisted of hearsay from unnamed sources. Inadmissible evidence cannot establish the threshold showing required by *Vodvarka*.

   c. **Not of sufficient magnitude.** Even if the allegations were true and admissible (they are neither), an anonymous friend's "concern" about an "eviction" does not rise to the level of a change in circumstances having a "significant effect on the child's well-being."

48. The failure to apply the *Vodvarka* threshold is not a mere procedural irregularity. It allowed the court to eliminate all parenting time based on allegations that would not survive the most basic evidentiary scrutiny — precisely the outcome *Vodvarka* was designed to prevent.

### Conclusion

49. The trial court erred by modifying the parenting time arrangement without requiring the moving party to satisfy the *Vodvarka* threshold of proper cause or change of circumstances. The modification must be reversed.

<a name="argument-v"></a>

## ARGUMENT V: THE COURT FAILED TO EMPLOY THE LEAST RESTRICTIVE MEANS

### Issue

50. Whether the complete elimination of all parenting time — rather than a less restrictive alternative — violates the proportionality requirement established in *Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17 (2010).

### Rule

51. In *Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31-32 (2010), the Michigan Court of Appeals held that any restriction on parenting time must be proportionate to the risk identified:

   > The court must consider less restrictive alternatives before imposing the most severe restriction on parenting time. A complete suspension of parenting time should be ordered only when no less restrictive measure will adequately protect the child.

52. Michigan law provides a range of parenting time options, including but not limited to: (a) supervised parenting time at a neutral location; (b) supervised parenting time by a designated third party; (c) therapeutic supervised visitation; (d) reduced frequency or duration; (e) restrictions on specific activities; and (f) electronic communication. MCL 722.27a(7).

### Application

53. Even assuming, arguendo, that the court had a legitimate basis for concern about Father's parenting time (it did not, as demonstrated above), the complete elimination of all contact was the most extreme remedy available — and the least justified under the circumstances.

54. The court had numerous less restrictive alternatives available:

   | Alternative | Addresses Alleged Concern? | Preserves Parent-Child Bond? |
   |-------------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
   | Supervised visitation at neutral site | Yes | Yes |
   | Third-party supervised visits | Yes | Yes |
   | Random drug testing as condition | Yes | Yes |
   | Reduced frequency with monitoring | Yes | Yes |
   | Therapeutic visitation | Yes | Yes |
   | Phone/video contact | Partially | Partially |
   | **Total elimination of all contact** | **Unnecessary** | **No** |

55. Not one of these alternatives was considered, discussed, or rejected on the record. The court proceeded directly to the most restrictive remedy without any analysis of proportionality. This is precisely the approach condemned in *Shade*.

56. The disproportionate nature of the remedy is further confirmed by the absence of any evidence of harm to the child. There is no allegation — let alone evidence — that L.D.W. was ever harmed, endangered, neglected, or abused while in Father's care. The deprivation was based on speculation about what might occur, not evidence of what did occur.

### Conclusion

57. The complete elimination of all parenting time, without consideration of less restrictive alternatives, violates the proportionality requirement of *Shade v. Wright*. The court was required to impose the least restrictive measure adequate to address any legitimate concern. It imposed the most restrictive measure without any analysis. The suspension must be vacated.

# PROPOSED ORDER GRANTING EMERGENCY MOTION TO RESTORE PARENTING TIME

---

This matter having come before the Court on Plaintiff-Father Andrew J. Pigors's Emergency Motion to Restore Parenting Time and Brief in Support, and the Court having reviewed the motion, brief, supporting affidavit, and exhibits, and the Court being otherwise fully advised in the premises;

---

## FINDINGS OF FACT

The Court finds as follows:

1. Plaintiff-Father Andrew J. Pigors ("Father") and Defendant-Mother Emily A. Watson ("Mother") are the biological parents of the minor child L.D.W. (DOB: November 9, 2022).

2. On August 8, 2025, this Court entered ex parte orders suspending all of Father's parenting time with L.D.W.

3. Father's last contact with L.D.W. was July 29, 2025. As of the date of this Order, Father has been separated from L.D.W. for 571+ cumulative days.

4. Father's narcotics screening returned **NEGATIVE**.

5. The record does not contain sufficient evidence to support a finding that Father's parenting time would endanger L.D.W.'s physical, mental, or emotional health within the meaning of MCL 722.27a(3).

6. No finding of endangerment was made in connection with the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders.

7. No findings were made on any of the twelve MCL 722.23 best interest factors in connection with the August 8, 2025 ex parte orders.

8. The statutory presumption of MCL 722.27a(1) — that it is in the best interests of a child to have a strong relationship with both parents — has not been rebutted.

9. The complete suspension of all parenting time, including the elimination of all contact between Father and L.D.W. for 571+ cumulative days during L.D.W.'s critical developmental period (ages 2-3), is contrary to L.D.W.'s best interests.

10. Less restrictive alternatives to a complete suspension were available and were not considered.

---

## CONCLUSIONS OF LAW

The Court concludes as follows:

A. The statutory presumption of MCL 722.27a(1) favoring parenting time has not been overcome.

B. The evidence is insufficient to support a finding of endangerment under MCL 722.27a(3).

C. The complete suspension of parenting time without MCL 722.23 best interest findings constitutes error. *See Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

D. L.D.W.'s best interests require the immediate restoration of Father's parenting time.

E. Father is entitled to make-up parenting time pursuant to MCL 722.27a(9) for the period of wrongful deprivation.

---

## ORDER

**IT IS HEREBY ORDERED:**

1. **VACATUR.** The ex parte orders entered August 8, 2025, suspending Plaintiff-Father's parenting time, are hereby **VACATED**.

2. **RESTORATION OF PARENTING TIME.** Plaintiff-Father's parenting time with L.D.W. is hereby restored on an equal parenting time basis (50/50), effective immediately upon entry of this Order, pursuant to the Parenting Time Schedule attached hereto or as otherwise agreed by the parties.

3. **PARENTING TIME SCHEDULE.** The parties shall follow a week-on/week-off parenting time schedule as set forth in the Proposed Parenting Time Schedule filed herewith, unless the parties reach a written agreement on an alternative schedule. Father's first week of parenting time shall commence on the first Friday following the entry of this Order.

4. **MAKE-UP PARENTING TIME.** Pursuant to MCL 722.27a(9), Plaintiff-Father is entitled to make-up parenting time for the 571+ cumulative days of deprivation. The parties shall confer and present a proposed make-up schedule within 30 days. If the parties cannot agree, Father may petition the Court for a specific make-up schedule.

5. **TRANSITIONAL PERIOD.** To facilitate the re-establishment of the parent-child bond after the extended separation:

   a. During the first seven (7) days following entry of this Order, Father shall have daily parenting time with L.D.W. for a minimum of four (4) hours per day at a location of Father's choosing;

   b. Commencing on the eighth (8th) day following entry of this Order, the 50/50 parenting time schedule shall take effect.

6. **NO FINDING OF ENDANGERMENT.** The Court specifically finds that the record does not support a finding that Father's parenting time endangers L.D.W.'s physical, mental, or emotional health.

7. **BEST INTERESTS.** The Court finds that restoration of Father's parenting time is in L.D.W.'s best interests, considering the statutory presumption of MCL 722.27a(1), the absence of evidence of endangerment, and the harm to L.D.W. caused by the prolonged separation from Father.

8. **EVIDENTIARY HEARING.** A full evidentiary hearing on custody and parenting time, including findings on all twelve MCL 722.23 best interest factors, shall be scheduled within fourteen (14) days of the entry of this Order.

9. **TRANSPORTATION.** Unless otherwise agreed by the parties, exchanges shall occur at a mutually agreed neutral location, or at the Muskegon County Courthouse, 990 Terrace Street, Muskegon, Michigan 49442.

10. **COMMUNICATION.** Each parent shall ensure that L.D.W. has reasonable telephone or video communication with the other parent during that parent's non-parenting time, at minimum once daily at a reasonable hour.

11. **COMPLIANCE.** Both parties are ordered to comply with this Order. Failure to comply may result in sanctions, including but not limited to a finding of contempt.

# REQUEST FOR EXPEDITED HEARING ON EMERGENCY MOTION TO RESTORE PARENTING TIME

### Filed Pursuant to MCR 2.119(B), MCR 3.207(A), MCR 3.208

## REQUEST

1. Plaintiff-Father Andrew J. Pigors ("Father"), appearing *pro se*, respectfully requests that this Court schedule an **expedited hearing** on Father's Emergency Motion to Restore Parenting Time, to be held **within fourteen (14) days** of the filing of this request, or at the earliest available date on the Court's calendar.

---

## GROUNDS FOR EXPEDITED HEARING

2. This request is based on the following grounds:

### A. Ongoing Irreparable Harm

3. Father has been separated from his minor child Lincoln Watson (DOB: November 9, 2022) for **571+ cumulative days**. The most recent continuous period of total deprivation has been running since **July 29, 2025**.

4. The separation is causing **ongoing, irreparable harm** to the parent-child bond during a critical developmental period (ages 2–3). Every additional day of separation deepens this harm.

5. No monetary remedy can compensate for the permanent loss of the parent-child relationship during this developmental window.

### B. Failure of Procedural Safeguards

6. The ex parte orders of August 8, 2025 were entered on an **unsworn motion** supported only by **inadmissible hearsay** from unnamed sources, in violation of MCR 3.207(B)(1).

7. **No notice** was provided to Father before the deprivation. **No hearing** has been conducted since.

8. MCR 3.206(B)(6) mandates a hearing "as soon as possible" after entry of ex parte custody or parenting time orders. More than **seven months** have elapsed without a hearing. This delay independently violates due process.

### C. Disproved Factual Basis

9. Father's narcotics screening returned **NEGATIVE**, affirmatively disproving the sole factual basis for the parenting time suspension. (*See* Exhibit D.)

10. The continued suspension of all parenting time — based on an allegation that has been disproved by objective testing — is no longer supportable by any standard.

### D. Constitutional Magnitude

11. The right to parent one's child is a **fundamental liberty interest** of the highest constitutional magnitude. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The prolonged deprivation of this right without a hearing demands expedited judicial action.

---

## AUTHORITY FOR EXPEDITED HEARING

12. **MCR 2.119(B)** — Permits the Court to schedule motions on shortened notice when the circumstances warrant.

13. **MCR 3.207(A)** — Provides that motions in domestic relations cases shall be heard "on not less than 9 days' notice" but permits the Court to shorten the notice period in appropriate circumstances.

14. **MCR 3.208** — Provides for post-judgment motions in domestic relations cases, including modification of parenting time.

15. **MCR 3.206(B)(6)** — Requires that when ex parte relief is granted, a hearing shall be scheduled "as soon as possible."

16. **MCL 722.27a** — Establishes the statutory presumption favoring parenting time and provides the framework for modification.

---

## PROPOSED SCHEDULING

17. Father respectfully proposes:

    a. **Hearing date:** Within 14 days of filing, or at the Court's earliest convenience.

    b. **Duration:** Approximately 1–2 hours (motion hearing; not full evidentiary hearing).

    c. **Issues:** (1) Vacatur of August 8, 2025 ex parte orders; (2) Restoration of parenting time; (3) Scheduling of full evidentiary hearing on best interest factors.

    d. **Service:** Father will serve this Request and all supporting documents on Defendant's counsel, Jennifer L. Barnes, Barnes Law Firm PLLC, 880 Jefferson Street Suite B, Muskegon, Michigan 49440, by first-class mail and/or electronic service, immediately upon filing.

---

## CONCLUSION

18. The emergency nature of this matter is established by 571+ days of total separation, a negative drug screen disproving the sole factual basis, and over seven months without a hearing. Father respectfully requests that the Court exercise its authority under MCR 2.119(B) and MCR 3.206(B)(6) to schedule an expedited hearing at the earliest available date.
