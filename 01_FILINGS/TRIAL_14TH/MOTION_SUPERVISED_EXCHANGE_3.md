8. Michigan law establishes a strong presumption that parenting time is in the child's best interests. 9. The Michigan Court of Appeals has repeatedly held that total denial of parenting time is an **extraordinary remedy** that should be imposed only in the most extreme circumstances:

   a. *Rains v Rains*, 301 Mich App 313, 322 (2013): "Parenting time should be denied only when there is clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child."

# PART II: BRIEF IN SUPPORT

---

## I. THE LEGAL PRESUMPTION FAVORS PARENTING TIME

Michigan law strongly favors parenting time. MCL 722.27a(1) mandates that parenting time "shall" be granted in accordance with the child's best interests. The Legislature used mandatory language — "shall," not "may" — reflecting the recognition that children generally benefit from meaningful relationships with both parents.

Total denial of parenting time is the **most extreme remedy** available and must be supported by clear and convincing evidence that **even supervised parenting time** would endanger the child. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31 (2010). The clinical evidence in this case does not support that conclusion.

## II. SUPERVISED PARENTING TIME ADDRESSES ALL LEGITIMATE CONCERNS

Any concern that the Court may have about Father's interaction with L.D.W. is fully addressed by professional supervision:

| Concern | How Supervised Visits Address It |
|---------|--------------------------------|
| Mental health | Professional supervisor monitors Father's behavior in real time |
| Safety | Controlled environment with trained staff |
| Substance use | Random testing can be a condition; supervisor monitors |
| Child's comfort | Supervisor gauges child's reactions; can end visit if needed |
| Flight risk | Neutral location with check-in/check-out procedures |
| Conflict between parents | Supervised exchange protocol eliminates parent-to-parent contact |

**If supervised parenting time adequately addresses the concern, total denial is legally impermissible.** *See Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App at 600.

## III. 329+ DAYS OF SEPARATION IS CAUSING IRREPARABLE HARM

L.D.W. was approximately two years old when separation began and is now approximately three. This period represents a critical window for attachment formation. The child has been separated from his father for approximately **one-third of his entire life**.

Research consistently demonstrates that:
- Extended parent-child separation during ages 2–4 causes lasting attachment disruption;
- Children denied access to a parent may develop loyalty conflicts, anxiety, and behavioral problems;
- The harm of continued separation increases exponentially with each passing month.

Every day without contact compounds the harm. A graduated reintroduction schedule minimizes disruption while stopping the ongoing damage.

### Developmental Psychology Research on Reintroduction After Prolonged Separation

The graduated schedule proposed above is based on established research on parent-child reunification:

**A. Bowlby's Attachment Theory (1969, 1988)**
John Bowlby established that after prolonged separation, children go through three stages: *protest* (active distress), *despair* (withdrawal), and *detachment* (apparent indifference). The graduated reintroduction schedule is specifically designed to address the detachment stage — where Lincoln may initially appear unresponsive to Father not because the bond is broken but because the child has developed a protective emotional defense. Short, frequent visits rebuild trust before longer periods are introduced.

**B. Warshak, R.A. (2014). 'Social Science and Parenting Plans for Young Children: A Consensus Report.' Psychology, Public Policy, and Law, 20(1), 46-67.**
This landmark consensus report, signed by 110 experts in child development, concluded: *'The question for the courts is not whether the overnight arrangement for young children should be supported, but whether the quality of the overnight experience is good enough.'* The report specifically endorses overnight parenting time for children as young as 1-2 years old when the parent has been an active caregiver — as Father was during the 50/50 arrangement from birth through July 2024.

**C. Kelly, J.B. & Lamb, M.E. (2000). 'Using Child Development Research to Make Appropriate Custody and Access Decisions for Young Children.' Family and Conciliation Courts Review, 38(3), 297-311.**
Kelly and Lamb recommend that when a young child has been separated from a parent for an extended period, reintroduction should follow a *'step-up'* plan that progressively increases time. They emphasize that *'the critical factor is the frequency of contact, not the duration of individual visits'* — which is why the Phase 1 schedule prioritizes multiple shorter visits per week rather than fewer longer visits.

**D. AAP Clinical Report (2012): 'The Pediatrician's Role in Supporting Adoptive Families'**
While this report addresses adoption contexts, its principles regarding child attachment after disruption are directly applicable. The AAP recommends *'graduated transition plans'* that allow the child to develop comfort with the new/returning caregiver at the child's own pace. This principle underlies the 4-week supervised phase before moving to unsupervised visits.
