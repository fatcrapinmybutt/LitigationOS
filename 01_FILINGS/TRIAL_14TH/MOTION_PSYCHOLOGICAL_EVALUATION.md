## III. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. Court Authority to Order Evaluations — MCL 722.27(1)(b)

MCL 722.27(1)(b) provides that in a custody dispute, the court may:

> "[O]rder the family independence agency or a private agency to make an investigation and
> report to the court with a recommendation for the resolution of the custody dispute."

This authority extends to ordering psychological evaluations of the parties and the child when
custody is contested. *See Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186 (2004) (recognizing the court's
broad authority under the Child Custody Act to gather evidence bearing on the best interests
of the child).

### B. Best Interest Factors — MCL 722.23

MCL 722.23 establishes 12 factors the court must evaluate in making custody and parenting time
determinations. Several of these factors cannot be properly assessed without professional
psychological evaluation:

- **Factor (a)**: The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties
  involved and the child — *requires assessment of parent-child bond quality and attachment*;
- **Factor (c)**: The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child
  with love, affection, and guidance — *requires clinical assessment of parenting capacity*;
- **Factor (e)**: The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial
  home — *requires evaluation of household dynamics including significant others*;
- **Factor (f)**: The moral fitness of the parties involved — *requires assessment of
  false allegation patterns and their motivation*;
- **Factor (j)**: The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child
  to be of sufficient age — *requires child interview by qualified professional*;
- **Factor (k)**: Domestic violence — *requires professional assessment of allegations
  and counter-allegations*;
- **Factor (l)**: Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant — *including
  parental alienation behaviors, coaching, and the impact of prolonged separation*.

### C. Joint Custody Considerations — MCL 722.27b

MCL 722.27b requires the court to consider factors relevant to joint custody, including
whether the parents can cooperate and communicate regarding the child. A forensic evaluation
is essential to determine each party's capacity for co-parenting and whether barriers to
cooperation are the product of one party's conduct.

### D. Constitutional Due Process

The United States Supreme Court has recognized that the fundamental right to the care and
custody of one's children cannot be conditioned on compliance with mental health treatment
that is not based on any professional evaluation. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000);
*Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982). This Court imposed mental health conditions on
Plaintiff without ever ordering or reviewing any custody evaluation — a practice that raises
serious due process concerns. An independent evaluation will provide the evidentiary
foundation necessary for any conditions this Court may impose.

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. No Professional Evaluation Has Ever Been Conducted in This Case

Despite the severity of the orders entered in this case — including complete suspension of
parenting time for over 226 days — this Court has never ordered a comprehensive custody
evaluation. This is a critical gap. Michigan courts routinely order forensic evaluations in
contested custody cases far less complex than this one. The absence of any professional
assessment means this Court's orders rest entirely on one-sided presentations rather than
neutral, evidence-based findings.

### B. Plaintiff's Clinical Evaluation Demonstrates No Pathology

Plaintiff proactively completed a clinical evaluation at HealthWest on September 4-5, 2025.
The evaluation, conducted by a licensed clinical social worker, found zero risk indicators
across all measured domains and determined that Plaintiff meets the criteria for **neither
Serious Mental Illness nor Severe Emotional Disturbance**. Yet Plaintiff's parenting time
remains suspended. This disparity between clinical findings and court orders underscores
the need for a comprehensive, independent forensic evaluation of **all** parties.

### C. False Allegation Patterns Require Professional Assessment

The case record documents 5 distinct false allegation claims against Defendant. Repeated
false allegations to CPS and law enforcement — particularly when used to manipulate custody
proceedings — may indicate personality pathology, coached behavior, or deliberate parental
alienation strategy. Only a forensic psychologist can assess the motivations behind these
allegations and their impact on L.D.W. *See Sahulka v Sahulka*, unpublished per curiam
(Mich Ct App, discussing relevance of false allegations to custody determination under
best interest factors).

### D. Alienation Indicators Must Be Assessed Using Validated Instruments

Plaintiff has documented 106 parental alienation incidents. The Michigan Court of Appeals
has recognized parental alienation as relevant to the best interest analysis. *See Demski v
Petlick*, 309 Mich App 404, 426 (2015) (factor (c) implicates a parent's willingness to
foster relationship with other parent). A qualified forensic psychologist can administer
validated assessment instruments — including Dr. Amy Baker's 17 Primary Strategies framework
and the Parental Alienation Behavior Scale — to determine:

1. Whether L.D.W. is being subjected to alienating behaviors;
2. The severity and chronicity of any alienation;
3. L.D.W.'s current emotional and psychological state following 226+ days of separation;
4. Recommendations for reunification therapy if warranted.

### E. All Household Members Must Be Included

A custody evaluation limited to the biological parents would be incomplete. Ronald Berry
resides with Defendant and L.D.W. and functions as a significant parental figure. Albert and
Lori Watson provide substantial caregiving. Michigan law recognizes that the court must
consider the "permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home" under
MCL 722.23(e). The evaluator must assess all significant adults in L.D.W.'s environment to
provide a complete picture.

### F. Independent Evaluation Is Necessary Given Structural Conflicts

A motion for judicial disqualification under MCR 2.003 is pending in this case. The
documented procedural irregularities — including ex parte proceedings and one-sided
evidentiary treatment — make it imperative that this Court rely on an independent, qualified
professional rather than continuing to act on contested, unevaluated allegations. An
independent forensic evaluation protects the integrity of the proceedings regardless of the
outcome of the disqualification motion.

### G. The Evaluator Must Be a Qualified Forensic Psychologist

Plaintiff requests that the evaluation be conducted by a licensed forensic psychologist —
not a court-appointed therapist or general practitioner. The complexity of this case,
involving allegations of false reporting, parental alienation, ex parte judicial
communications, and prolonged parent-child separation, requires an evaluator with specific
forensic training, including:

- Competence in custody evaluation methodology (APA Guidelines for Child Custody Evaluations);
- Experience with parental alienation assessment using validated instruments;
- Experience with high-conflict custody disputes;
- No prior relationship with any party, the Court, or the Friend of the Court.
