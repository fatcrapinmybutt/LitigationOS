CONTROLLING RULES AND STANDARDS
PPO violations and contempt.

Domestic-relationship PPOs are issued and enforced under MCL 600.2950 and MCR 3.708.

For alleged PPO violations, the petitioner bears the burden to prove a violation at a show-cause hearing. If criminal contempt is sought (jail as punishment), the elements must be proved beyond a reasonable doubt. MCR 3.708(H)(3).

The court must find that: (a) the PPO was in effect, (b) the terms allegedly violated were clear and unambiguous, and (c) respondent knowingly engaged in conduct that fell within the prohibited behavior.

Rules of Evidence apply.

MCR 3.708(H) incorporates the Michigan Rules of Evidence. Exhibits must be relevant (MRE 401–402), not unfairly prejudicial (MRE 403), and authenticated (MRE 901).

When petitioner relies on written reports or electronic records, those documents must either fall within hearsay exceptions (e.g., business records under MRE 803(6)) or be supported by live testimony.

Ambiguous orders and specific intent.

Contempt requires a violation of a clear and unequivocal court order. Ambiguities are resolved in favor of the accused contemnor.

Courts distinguish between prohibited contact (threats, harassment, direct personal contact) and lawful, narrow communications necessary to comply with court orders or coordinate parenting time.

CHRONOLOGICAL BACKGROUND RELEVANT TO SHOW CAUSE #7
On or about December 2023, this Court issued an ex parte personal protection order (PPO) in favor of Petitioner against Respondent, based on allegations of poisoning, harassment, and mental instability. No criminal charges resulted from these allegations.

Over 2024, Petitioner invoked the PPO repeatedly during parenting exchanges and used PPO extensions to support her position in the custody case. In at least one key incident, Norton Shores Police Department officers concluded there was no PPO violation, and documented that finding in their report.

In the related custody case (2024-001507-DC), this Court later directed Respondent to undergo a mental-health evaluation at HealthWest. Respondent complied. The HealthWest evaluation did not diagnose psychosis or dangerousness and did not recommend suspending parenting time.

The Court also directed the parties to use the co-parenting application AppClose for all communications related to the minor child. Respondent complied, limiting his communications with Petitioner to AppClose, as ordered.

On August 8, 2025, in the custody file, this Court entered an ex parte order suspending all of Respondent’s parenting time. After that order, Respondent remained bound by the PPO and the AppClose directive and had no direct in-person contact with Petitioner.

On October 27, 2025, Petitioner filed a Motion to Modify/Extend PPO and an Affidavit for Show Cause (“Show Cause #7”), alleging that Respondent had “multiple violations” and was “impulsive and erratic” and that the PPO was “the only thing keeping [her] safe.”

Critically, her affidavit identifies only routine, non-threatening communications in AppClose and by text as the alleged “violations.”

Petitioner’s affidavit lists the following as “violations”:

Shortly before or around the time of the ex parte suspension, Respondent texted that “Lincoln and I are going to my step mom’s for the weekend, see ya at 5:30, thanks.”

This message is a standard parenting-time logistics text: it confirms that Respondent is exercising time with his son and sets an exchange time.

This is a polite request to exercise court-ordered time and a short defense of his stability, not a threat or harassment.

AppClose messages (Sept 5, 19, 28, Oct 3, 2025):

September 19: Respondent asks, “May I see my son. At least so I can take him to the park for a couple hours?”

October 3: Respondent writes that he knows parenting time is currently suspended by order, asks Petitioner to voluntarily resume the normal weekend schedule if she wishes to act in good faith rather than alienation, and explicitly states that:

AppClose message (October 18, 2025):

Respondent sends Petitioner a license-plate number (e.g., “ERK5622”), documenting that she was following him in a vehicle at a particular intersection.

This is a single factual notation tied to his own safety and documentation.

None of these communications contain threats, insults, profanity, or any explicit or implicit statement that a reasonable person would interpret as stalking or harassment. They are:

Logistics (scheduling, exchange times).

Good-faith requests to see his child.

A court-ordered evaluation, transmitted in the court-approved app.

A single documented plate number connected to Petitioner’s alleged surveillance of Respondent.

ARGUMENT
A. The cited messages are logistics and compliance, not PPO-prohibited conduct.
The purpose of the PPO is to prevent stalking, harassment, threats, and unwanted contact, not to forbid co-parenting logistics where those logistics are routed through the method the Court itself chose (AppClose) and limited to child-related issues.

Here:

The Court directed the parties to confine communication to AppClose.

Respondent did exactly that.

The content was minimal and child-focused: pickups, holidays, “may I see my son,” and the transmittal of his HealthWest evaluation.

There is no language in the PPO that clearly and unequivocally forbids such court-approved, logistics-only messages.

B. There is no willful violation of a clear order.
Contempt for PPO violations requires:

A clear order stating what contact is forbidden.

Proof beyond a reasonable doubt (for criminal contempt) that Respondent knowingly engaged in conduct that fell within that forbidden category.

Even if the PPO text is broad (e.g., “no contact”), the Court’s later directive to use AppClose for co-parenting communications necessarily narrows and clarifies the scope: some contact is expressly allowed when done through AppClose and limited to the child.

When the Court orders communication through AppClose, and Respondent uses AppClose for:

Scheduling exchanges,

Requesting to exercise parenting time,

Sending a court-ordered evaluation, and

Stating he will seek court relief,

that conduct cannot reasonably be treated as a knowing violation of the PPO. It is compliance.

C. The HealthWest evaluation and AppClose logs show good-faith compliance, not danger.
Respondent complied with the court’s direction to undergo a mental-health evaluation at HealthWest. The resulting report:

Does not diagnose psychosis or dangerousness.

Does not recommend supervised or suspended contact with his child.

Recommends only outpatient supports.

Respondent then sent this evaluation to Petitioner through AppClose, demonstrating both compliance with the court’s mental-health directive and transparency about the results.

Penalizing Respondent for sharing the very evaluation the Court ordered, via the very app the Court ordered, would invert the PPO’s purpose and due-process norms.

D. Previous law-enforcement findings of “no PPO violation” confirm that ordinary logistics are not contempt.
On prior occasions, Petitioner reported alleged PPO violations to police during exchanges. In at least one key incident, the Norton Shores Police Department concluded that no PPO violation occurred and documented that conclusion.

Those findings:

Confirm that ordinary scheduling, presence at agreed exchange locations, and child-related communication do not constitute violations.

Undercut any claim that Respondent should have known that AppClose messages like “Can I see my son?” and “Here is my evaluation as ordered” are somehow forbidden.

E. Using logistics as contempt fodder is a misuse of the PPO.
When routine co-parenting communications—especially those the Court specifically directed—are treated as contempt grounds, the PPO ceases to function as a safety tool and becomes a custody and parenting-time weapon, chilling Respondent’s ability to comply with orders and advocate for his relationship with his child.

MCR 3.708 was not designed to turn every logistics message into a criminal event. It was designed to address genuine threats and stalking. Show Cause #7 misuses the procedure.

REQUESTED RELIEF AND PROPOSED ORDER
Respondent requests that the Court:

Dismiss Show Cause #7 with prejudice.

Enter an order explicitly finding that the cited AppClose and text messages are logistics-only and do not violate the PPO.

Add a safe-harbor clause to the PPO clarifying that logistics and court-ordered compliance communications in AppClose are permitted.
