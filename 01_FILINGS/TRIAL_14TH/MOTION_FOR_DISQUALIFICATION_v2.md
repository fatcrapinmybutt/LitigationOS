## II. LEGAL STANDARD

6. **MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)** provides for disqualification on the ground of bias or prejudice for or against a party or attorney.

7. **MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)** provides that disqualification is warranted where "[t]he judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct."

8. The *Caperton* standard requires disqualification where "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868, 872 (2009). This is an objective inquiry — the question is not whether the judge is *actually* biased, but whether, under the circumstances, "there is an unconstitutional potential for bias." *Id.* at 881.

9. Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct provides: "A judge should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." Canon 2(A) states: "A judge should respect and comply with the law and should act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary."

10. The standard under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii) is objective: whether a reasonable person, fully informed of the facts, would perceive a serious risk of bias or an appearance of impropriety. *See In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 680; 765 NW2d 44 (2009).

11. **MCR 2.003(D)(2)** requires that "[a]n affidavit must accompany the motion," which is provided herein as the Verification section of this filing.

## III. GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

### A. Ex Parte Orders — 24 of 55 Orders (43.6%) Entered Without Notice

#### IRAC Analysis

12. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's pattern of entering ex parte orders in 43.6% of all orders — including five (5) separate ex parte orders on a single day (August 8, 2025) — demonstrates actual bias or the appearance of impropriety warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

13. **Rule:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification where the judge "has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct." Canon 3(A)(4) requires a judge to "accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law." Canon 3(A)(5) prohibits ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters except under narrow circumstances. MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that ex parte orders in domestic-relations matters be supported by "specific facts" in a "verified pleading" demonstrating "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage."

14. **Application:** The record in this case establishes that **24 of 55 total orders (43.6%)** were entered ex parte — all presided over by Judge McNeill, each without the procedural safeguards required by MCR 3.207(B)(1). *See* Docket Sheet, Case No. 2024-001507-DC (42 ex parte events catalogued across the docket). The ex parte pattern spans the entire case:

    - **April 11, 2024:** Ex parte amended PPO entered. MCR 3.207(B)(1).
    - **April 29, 2024:** Ex parte order for custody, parenting time, and child support entered following Facilitative Information Gathering Conference.
    - **July 1, 2024:** Emergency ex parte order granting sole custody to Defendant Watson without full hearing. MCR 3.207(B)(1).
    - **April 25, 2025:** Ex parte order filed in custody case (Case No. 2024-001507-DC).
    - **August 6, 2025:** Ex parte order suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time signed by Judge McNeill.
    - **August 7, 2025:** Emergency ex parte motion filed by Defendant (multiple versions).
    - **August 8, 2025:** Judge McNeill signed **five (5) separate ex parte orders** in a single day suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time without prior notice. MCR 3.207(B)(3).

    Plaintiff was not served with the ex parte motion or order before the August 8, 2025 parenting-time exchange. Plaintiff arrived expecting his regular parenting time and only discovered — after the missed exchange, by contacting the court — that his parenting time had been suspended. This ambush at a scheduled parenting-time exchange, without any notice or opportunity to be heard, is fundamentally incompatible with due process. The ex parte order failed to allege that delaying relief to provide notice would cause immediate and irreparable harm as required by MCR 3.207(B)(1). The subsequent hearing timeline further demonstrates the deprivation:

    - **August 22, 2025:** Interim hearing on ex parte order — Plaintiff ordered to undergo HealthWest assessment.
    - **August 28, 2025:** Second interim hearing — the court refused to view Plaintiff's exculpatory photographs from the July 29, 2025 exchange.
    - **September 4, 2025:** Hearing on Plaintiff's objection to ex parte order (2:30 PM, all parties in person) — nearly a month after the ex parte suspension.
    - **September 22, 2025:** Court ordered Plaintiff to undergo HealthWest assessment; matter adjourned.
    - **October 8, 2025:** Continuation hearing scheduled for October 29 — chambers scheduling, nearly two months after ex parte order.
    - **October 29, 2025:** Belated evidentiary hearing finally held (10:00 AM, all parties in person) — 82 days after the August 8, 2025 ex parte suspension, far exceeding the timeline contemplated by MCR 3.207(B)(3).
    - **November 26, 2025:** Continued hearing; parenting time remains suspended. Order regarding Plaintiff's objection to ex parte order entered.

    The matter was not fully heard until October 29, 2025, far exceeding the 21-day hearing expectation reflected in MCR 3.207(B)(6). Even after that belated hearing, the suspension continued through November 26, 2025 and beyond.

    The ex parte pattern was reinforced by collateral restrictions designed to prevent Plaintiff from challenging these orders. On May 16, 2025, Judge McNeill imposed a $250-per-motion filing bond exclusively on Plaintiff — a pro se father of limited means — while imposing no comparable restriction on Defendant, effectively barring Plaintiff from seeking judicial review of the ex parte orders. *See Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 380-82 (1971) (financial barriers to court access for fundamental rights implicate due process). The asymmetric nature of this restriction — targeting only the party whose rights were suspended by ex parte order — compounds the appearance of bias.

    Additionally, Defendant Watson has been employed by the Kent County Prosecutor’s Office, Family Court Division, as a caseworker for nine years. Under the appearance-of-impropriety standard of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii) and Canon 2, this institutional position within the family court system is relevant context for evaluating the pattern of same-day ex parte grants and off-record communications documented above.

15. **Conclusion:** A pattern in which nearly half of all judicial orders are entered ex parte — without notice to the affected party — cannot be reconciled with Canon 2's requirement that a judge act "in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary." This pattern, standing alone, warrants disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b).

### B. Self-Ruling on Own Disqualification — MCR 2.003(D) Violation

#### IRAC Analysis

16. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's refusal to refer disqualification challenges to the Chief Judge, instead ruling on challenges to her own authority, violates MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)(i) and constitutes grounds for disqualification.

17. **Rule:** MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a) provides that "[f]or courts other than the Supreme Court, the challenged judge shall decide the motion. If the challenged judge denies the motion, (i) in a court having two or more judges, on the request of a party, the challenged judge shall refer the motion to the chief judge, who shall decide the motion de novo." The mandatory language — "shall refer" — means referral is not discretionary once a party requests it. The 14th Circuit Court has more than one judge, making subsection (D)(3)(a)(i) directly applicable.

18. **Application:** In contravention of MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)(i), Judge McNeill has ruled on motions and objections challenging her own authority, impartiality, and procedural decisions without referring those challenges to the Chief Judge for independent review. Specifically:

    - **July 16, 2024:** Plaintiff filed six motions the day before trial challenging custody and procedural issues. Judge McNeill denied all six motions herself, ruling "as they" were filed the day before trial, without substantive consideration or referral per MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)(i).
    - **February 14, 2025:** Plaintiff's motion regarding parenting time — requesting equal parenting time and raising issues with the court's prior orders — was dismissed by Judge McNeill herself "for the reasons stated above," with parenting time to "remain pursuant to the Order entered on August 23, 2024."
    - **May 16, 2025:** At a hearing before Judge McNeill, the court imposed a $250-per-motion filing bond on Plaintiff — effectively ruling on a challenge to her own handling of the case by creating a financial barrier to further challenges.
    - **May 20, 2025:** Judge McNeill entered an order denying Plaintiff's motion regarding custody, parenting time, and child support, again adjudicating challenges to her own prior orders contrary to MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a).
    - **September 4, 2025 through November 26, 2025:** Across three successive hearings (September 4, October 29, and November 26, 2025), Judge McNeill adjudicated Plaintiff's objection to her own August 8, 2025 ex parte order — the very order whose propriety was being challenged — rather than referring the matter to the Chief Judge as required by MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)(i).

    When Plaintiff has raised issues of bias and sought recusal or disqualification, the challenged judge has effectively served as both the accused and the adjudicator. This creates an inherent conflict of interest that the drafters of MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)(i) specifically anticipated and addressed by providing for de novo review by the chief judge. A judge who repeatedly rules on challenges to her own conduct — rather than referring them for independent review — compounds the appearance of impropriety with each such self-ruling.

19. **Conclusion:** Judge McNeill's pattern of self-ruling on challenges to her own authority, rather than referring them to the Chief Judge per MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)(i), independently warrants disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) as a failure to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard.

### C. Refusing to View Exculpatory Evidence — Canon 3(A)(4) Violation

#### IRAC Analysis

20. **Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's refusal to review exculpatory photographic evidence presented by Plaintiff, while relying on ex parte submissions and hearsay from Defendant, demonstrates bias warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

21. **Rule:** Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires that a judge "be patient, dignified, and courteous" to litigants and "accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law." MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification where the judge has failed to adhere to Canon 2's appearance of impropriety standard. Due process under the Fourteenth Amendment requires, at minimum, "an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976).

22. **Application:** During the hearing on August 28, 2025 — an interim hearing on Plaintiff's objection to the ex parte suspension of parenting time under MCR 3.207(B) — Plaintiff attempted to present photographs from the July 29, 2025 parenting-time exchange showing Lincoln in good health and spirits — exculpatory evidence directly contradicting the allegations underlying the ex parte suspension. Judge McNeill refused to view this evidence. This refusal is documented in the record of the August 28, 2025 hearing. The Court's inconsistent evidentiary treatment — accepting Defendant's ex parte submissions while refusing to review Father's exculpatory photographs — is a recurring pattern throughout these proceedings. This refusal was repeated at the September 4, 2025 hearing (2:30 PM, all parties in person), where the court again declined to consider Plaintiff's photographic evidence while sustaining the ex parte suspension. Simultaneously, Judge McNeill received and relied upon a USB recording and mental-health evaluation submitted off-record and ex parte, contrary to the prohibition on ex parte communications about pending matters in Canon 3(A)(5). The HealthWest assessment was ordered on September 22, 2025, with chambers-only instructions emailed to Plaintiff by the judge's secretary — not conducted on the record. The combination of (a) accepting ex parte submissions from one party, (b) refusing to review exculpatory evidence from the other party, and (c) relying on hearsay to sustain the suspension of a fundamental parental right creates an overwhelming appearance that the outcome was predetermined.

23. **Conclusion:** A judge who refuses to view exculpatory evidence offered by one party while simultaneously relying on ex parte submissions from the opposing party has created an appearance of bias so severe that no reasonable observer could maintain confidence in the tribunal's impartiality. Disqualification is warranted under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b).

## IV. CUMULATIVE EFFECT

26. Each of the three grounds set forth above — (A) the ex parte order pattern, (B) self-ruling on disqualification challenges, and (C) refusal to view exculpatory evidence — independently provides grounds for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1). Taken together, their cumulative effect is overwhelming.

27. The *Caperton* Court recognized that disqualification is required where “the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable.” 556 US at 872. Here, a reasonable person confronted with this record — 43.6% ex parte orders entered without notice, a judge who rules on challenges to her own authority rather than referring them to the Chief Judge, and a court that refuses to view one party’s exculpatory evidence while relying on the other party’s ex parte submissions — could not conclude that this tribunal is capable of impartial adjudication. The 204-day-and-counting parent-child separation (since August 8, 2025) without a finding of unfitness, abuse, or neglect provides the devastating context for this pattern.

28. The integrity of the judicial system requires that cases be decided by judges who are, and appear to be, impartial. *In re Murchison*, 349 US 133, 136 (1955) (“A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process.”). The record in this case compels the conclusion that this fundamental requirement cannot be met under the current judicial assignment.
