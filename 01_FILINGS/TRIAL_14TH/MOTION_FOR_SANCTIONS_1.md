### I. INTRODUCTION

1. This Motion seeks sanctions against Defendant Emily A. Watson ("Mother") and her counsel, Jennifer Barnes, for the filing and prosecution of frivolous, vexatious, and factually unsupported pleadings in both the custody proceeding (Case No. 2024-001507-DC) and the PPO proceeding (Case No. 2023-5907-PP).

2. MCR 2.114(D) requires that every document signed by an attorney or party shall be "well grounded in fact" and "warranted by existing law or a good faith argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law."

3. MCR 2.114(E) mandates that "[i]f a document is signed in violation of this rule, the court, on the motion of a party or on its own initiative, shall impose upon the person who signed it, a represented party, or both, an appropriate sanction, which may include an order to pay to the other party the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred because of the filing of the document, including reasonable attorney fees."

4. Defendant and her counsel have filed not fewer than **seven (7) show-cause motions** and multiple PPO petitions containing allegations that are not merely unsubstantiated, but are **affirmatively contradicted** by independent law enforcement investigations conducted by the Norton Shores Police Department.

### IV. COUNSEL'S OBLIGATIONS UNDER MCR 2.114

29. MCR 2.114(B) provides that the signature of an attorney constitutes certification that:
   - (1) The signer has read the document;
   - (2) To the best of the signer's knowledge, information, and belief formed after reasonable inquiry, the document is well grounded in fact;
   - (3) The document is warranted by existing law or a good faith argument for extension, modification, or reversal of existing law; and
   - (4) The document is not interposed for any improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation.

30. Attorney Jennifer Barnes signed and filed pleadings containing the five categories of false allegations described above. A reasonable inquiry — which would include reviewing the **seven or more police investigation reports** finding no violations — would have revealed that these allegations are not "well grounded in fact."

31. Counsel's failure to conduct reasonable inquiry before filing, or alternatively, counsel's decision to file despite knowing the allegations lacked factual support, violates MCR 2.114(B)(2) and MCR 2.114(D).

31A. **Use of Criminally Obtained Evidence as Basis for Relief.** The ex parte motion to suspend parenting time was supported by a USB recording made by Lori Watson without Father's knowledge or consent — a felony under **MCL 750.539d** (Michigan's eavesdropping statute, punishable by up to 2 years imprisonment). Counsel Jennifer Barnes submitted this recording to the court as the centerpiece of the August 2025 emergency relief request. A reasonable inquiry under MCR 2.114(B) would have revealed that: (a) the recording was made without consent of all parties; (b) Michigan is a two-party consent state; and (c) evidence obtained through criminal conduct constitutes fruit of a crime that is inadmissible. *See People v Bednarz*, 360 Mich 552 (1960). Filing a motion relying on evidence known or reasonably knowable to be the product of a felony is sanctionable under both MCR 2.114(E) and **MCR 1.109(E)** (prohibiting documents filed for improper purpose).

32. The repeated nature of these filings — filing the same debunked allegations multiple times — demonstrates that the documents were "interposed for an improper purpose," namely, to harass Father, to cause unnecessary delay, and to gain tactical advantage in custody proceedings, in violation of MCR 2.114(B)(4).

### V. LEGAL STANDARD FOR SANCTIONS

33. Under MCR 2.114(E), sanctions are **mandatory**, not discretionary, when a violation is found: "the court...shall impose...an appropriate sanction."

34. MCR 2.114(F) provides that sanctions may include:
   - (1) Striking the offending pleading or portion thereof;
   - (2) An award of reasonable expenses, including attorney fees;
   - (3) Any other appropriate sanction.

35. MCR 2.625 provides for the taxation of costs against a party who files frivolous claims or defenses.

36. Michigan case law establishes that sanctions are appropriate where a party files pleadings containing allegations that are contradicted by readily available evidence. See *Kitchen v Kitchen*, 465 Mich 654; 641 NW2d 245 (2002) (discussing attorney obligations under court rules).

37. The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that frivolous filings in family court proceedings cause particular harm because they consume judicial resources, increase litigation costs for opposing parties, and — critically — directly harm the children whose best interests the court is charged with protecting.

### VI-A. PREEMPTIVE RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED COUNTERARGUMENTS

**1. "The Filings Were Made in Good Faith"**

Rebuttal: Good faith requires "reasonable inquiry" under MCR 2.114(B)(2). Seven police investigations — all finding no evidence — constitute readily available evidence that a reasonable inquiry would have uncovered. Filing the *same* disproven allegations repeatedly is not good faith; it is sanctionable persistence. *See FMB-First Mich Bank v. Bailey*, 232 Mich App 711, 723 (1998) (sanctions appropriate where attorney failed to conduct reasonable inquiry).

**2. "Mother Has a Right to Seek Court Protection"**

Rebuttal: The right to petition does not extend to knowingly false filings. MCR 2.114(D) specifically prohibits filings "interposed for any improper purpose, such as to harass." Albert Watson's admission — "they want this incident documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order" — proves the filings were instruments of a custody strategy, not legitimate safety concerns. *See Cvengros v Farm Bureau Ins. Co.*, 216 Mich App 261, 266 (1996) (sanctions warranted where claims lack factual basis).

**3. "Pro Se Father Cannot Recover Attorney Fees"**

Rebuttal: MCR 2.114(E) awards "reasonable expenses" — not limited to attorney fees. Pro se litigants may recover reasonable costs and expenses. *See Kay v Ehrler*, 499 US 432, 435 (1991) (discussing costs vs. fees distinction). Additionally, MCL 600.2591 permits recovery of "costs and fees" to any prevailing party against frivolous filings.
