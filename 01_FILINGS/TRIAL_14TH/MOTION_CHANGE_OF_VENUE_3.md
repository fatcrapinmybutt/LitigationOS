## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### GROUND ONE: CHANGE OF VENUE — IMPARTIAL TRIAL IMPOSSIBLE (MCR 2.222(A))

#### Issue

15. Whether the documented pattern of 2,374+ violations, 226 critical-severity ex parte violations, and 329+ days of unadjudicated parent-child separation establish that an impartial trial cannot be had in the 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County, warranting a change of venue under MCR 2.222(A).

#### Rule

16. MCR 2.222(A) provides:

> "The court may order a change of venue of a civil action ... when an impartial trial cannot be had where the action is pending."

17. The standard does not require proof of actual prejudice but rather a showing that the circumstances create a reasonable apprehension that an impartial trial cannot be obtained. *Ingham County Prosecutor v Ingham County Bd of Comm'rs*, 159 Mich App 594, 601; 407 NW2d 46 (1987). The test is an objective one: whether a reasonable person, fully informed of the facts, would entertain a significant doubt about the judge's impartiality. *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996).

#### Application

18. The record in this case does not present a close question. The forensic record contains:

   a. **226 ex parte communication violations rated "critical" severity.** MCR 2.003(C)(1) expressly identifies ex parte communications as a ground for disqualification. The sheer volume — 226 documented instances — transcends any concept of isolated error and establishes a systemic pattern. (R. at PIGORS-0271; Exhibit J, Ex Parte Communication Violation Log)

   b. **146 findings independently warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003**, each rated "critical" severity. These are not borderline cases — they are findings that, individually, would support a disqualification motion. Collectively, they are overwhelming.

   c. **540 benchbook deviations**, including 186 violations of Canon 2(B) (allowing relationships to influence judicial conduct) and 72 violations of Canon 3(A)(4) (failure to maintain impartiality). The Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires judges to "act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary." Canon 2(A). The volume of violations here destroys any such confidence. (R. at PIGORS-0274; Exhibit K, Benchbook Deviation Analysis)

   d. **329+ days of parent-child separation without best-interest analysis.** The failure to analyze even one of the twelve MCL 722.23 factors over nearly a year of total separation is not mere delay — it is a substantive deprivation of Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights and his child's right to a relationship with both parents.

19. **CHESS MODE — Anticipatory Analysis:**

   - **OUR ARGUMENT:** The 2,374+ documented violations, including 226 critical-severity ex parte violations and 146 disqualification-level findings, establish a pattern so pervasive that no reasonable person could conclude an impartial trial is possible. The 329+ day separation without best-interest analysis confirms the Court has abdicated its duty to adjudicate.

   - **THEIR LIKELY DEFENSE:** Defendant will argue that (a) many "violations" are duplicative or reflect the same underlying events; (b) the Court's rulings reflect legitimate exercises of judicial discretion; (c) adverse rulings alone do not establish bias (*Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597; 640 NW2d 321 (2001)); and (d) Plaintiff has not exhausted remedies under MCR 2.003 before seeking venue change.

   - **OUR PRE-REBUTTAL:** (a) Even after accounting for overlap, the violations span **20 distinct forensic categories** across multiple years, multiple hearings, and multiple procedural contexts — they are not duplicative but cumulative. (b) The distinction between judicial discretion and bias collapses when the Court consistently exercises discretion in one direction over 329+ days without conducting the analysis required by statute. The failure to analyze *any* best-interest factor is not discretion — it is abdication. MCR 3.210(C); MCL 722.23. (c) Plaintiff does not rely on adverse rulings alone but on the *pattern* of procedural irregularities (ex parte orders without notice, failure to make findings, failure to enforce parenting time) that systematically disadvantage one party. *Cain*, 451 Mich at 497 (the test is whether a reasonable person would doubt impartiality). (d) Plaintiff has raised disqualification concerns (see Ground Two below) and the motion for venue change is properly filed concurrently under MCR 2.222 — which is an independent procedural vehicle not contingent on exhaustion of MCR 2.003 remedies.

#### Conclusion

20. The documented record establishes, by any objective measure, that an impartial trial cannot be had in the 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County. Venue should be changed to a neutral county pursuant to MCR 2.222(A).

### GROUND TWO: DISQUALIFICATION FOR BIAS OR PREJUDICE (MCR 2.003(C)(1))

#### Issue

21. Whether the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill should be disqualified under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) (bias or prejudice for or against a party) and MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) (objective appearance of partiality), and whether disqualification necessitates a change of venue due to the small size of the 14th Judicial Circuit bench and the pervasive nature of the documented bias.

#### Rule

22. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that disqualification is warranted when:

> "(a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney."
>
> "(b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v Massey*, 556 US 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) has been a material witness in the matter in controversy."

23. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that disqualification must be measured by an objective standard: "whether a reasonable person, informed of the relevant facts, would reasonably question the judge's impartiality." *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996). The appearance of impropriety is sufficient — actual bias need not be proven. *In re MKK*, 286 Mich App 546, 567; 781 NW2d 132 (2009).

#### Application

24. The grounds for disqualification are extensive and documented:

   a. **Ex Parte Violations (226 documented, critical severity).** Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires: *"A judge should accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding, or the person's lawyer, full right to be heard according to law."* The Court signed ex parte orders suspending Plaintiff's parenting time without notice, without verified pleading or affidavit containing specific facts of immediate danger, and without the findings required by MCR 3.207(B). The Ron Berry voicemail was pre-listened in chambers as part of ex parte evidence — evidence Plaintiff had no opportunity to confront or rebut before it influenced judicial decision-making.

   b. **Canon 2(B) Violations (186 documented).** Canon 2(B) provides: *"A judge shall not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct."* The pattern of rulings consistently favoring Defendant — a former Kent County Prosecutor's Office employee with deep connections to the Western Michigan legal community — while systematically denying Plaintiff relief, creates at minimum an appearance that relationships are influencing judicial conduct.

   c. **Failure to Make Required Findings (87 MCR 3.210(C) violations).** The Court's persistent failure to make findings of fact on contested custody issues violates MCR 3.210(C) and *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31-32; 805 NW2d 1 (2010). This is not a single oversight — it is 87 documented instances of the same procedural failure, each depriving Plaintiff of a reviewable record and meaningful appellate remedy.

   d. **One-Sided Pattern of Rulings.** The record demonstrates:
      - Defendant's ex parte motions: granted without hearing or findings.
      - Plaintiff's motions for enforcement: denied or not addressed.
      - Plaintiff's requests for make-up parenting time: denied.
      - Plaintiff's objections to ex parte orders: overruled.
      - Result: 329+ days of complete parent-child separation.

25. **CHESS MODE — Anticipatory Analysis:**

   - **OUR ARGUMENT:** The 186 Canon 2(B) violations, 226 ex parte violations, and 87 MCR 3.210(C) failures — combined with the one-sided ruling pattern and Watson's prosecutorial connections — establish both actual bias and the appearance of bias under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b). Disqualification requires venue change because the 14th Circuit bench is small enough that reassignment within the circuit would not cure the appearance of impropriety.

   - **THEIR LIKELY DEFENSE:** Defendant will argue that (a) the disqualification standard is high and requires more than adverse rulings; (b) Plaintiff's motion is untimely under MCR 2.003(D) if not filed within 14 days of discovering the grounds; (c) the Court's rulings are supported by the record; and (d) judicial immunity protects the Court from allegations of misconduct.

   - **OUR PRE-REBUTTAL:** (a) Plaintiff does not rely on adverse rulings but on documented procedural irregularities — ex parte orders without notice, failure to make required findings, failure to enforce statutory rights — that independently warrant disqualification. *In re MKK*, 286 Mich App at 567 (appearance of impropriety suffices). (b) The pattern of violations is ongoing and cumulative; timeliness runs from the most recent violation, not the first. Each new day of unadjudicated separation renews the ground. (c) The Court's rulings *cannot* be "supported by the record" when the Court has made no findings of fact on contested issues — that is precisely the problem. A decision without findings is not a discretionary ruling; it is an abdication of the judicial function. MCR 3.210(C); *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App at 31-32. (d) Judicial immunity is a defense to damages, not to disqualification or venue change. MCR 2.003 and MCR 2.222 are procedural remedies, not tort claims.

#### Conclusion

26. The Honorable Jenny L. McNeill should be disqualified under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b). Due to the size of the 14th Judicial Circuit bench and the pervasive nature of the documented bias, reassignment within the circuit would be insufficient, and venue must be changed to a neutral county.

### GROUND FOUR: DUE PROCESS VIOLATION — FUNDAMENTAL PARENTAL RIGHTS (U.S. CONST. AMEND. XIV; MCL 722.23)

#### Issue

32. Whether the Court's systematic denial of Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights — including 329+ days of parent-child separation without best-interest analysis, denial of hearings, and failure to enforce parenting time — constitutes a due process violation requiring venue change.

#### Rule

33. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65; 120 S Ct 2054; 147 L Ed 2d 49 (2000). Michigan law codifies this right through MCL 722.23 (best interest factors), MCL 722.27 (custody modification), and MCL 722.27a (parenting time), all of which require specific findings and analysis before a parent's custodial rights may be restricted.

34. Due process in custody proceedings requires, at minimum: (a) notice of proceedings; (b) opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner; (c) an impartial decision-maker; and (d) a decision based on findings supported by the evidence. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335; 96 S Ct 893; 47 L Ed 2d 18 (1976); *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 92-93; 763 NW2d 587 (2009).

#### Application

35. Every component of due process has been violated in this case:

   a. **Notice.** Plaintiff was not served with the ex parte motion or order before the August 8, 2025 scheduled exchange. He arrived expecting his regular parenting time. MCR 3.207(B) requires that ex parte orders be served promptly with notice of hearing rights. The failure of notice is documented in 96 MCR 3.207(B) benchbook violations.

   b. **Opportunity to Be Heard.** The Court signed the ex parte order suspending all parenting time without affording Plaintiff a hearing. When Plaintiff filed objections, they were overruled. The Court has not held a meaningful evidentiary hearing on the best-interest factors. For 329+ days, Plaintiff has been deprived of his parental rights without the "meaningful hearing" required by due process. The 98 documented "PARENTING_TIME_DENIAL" findings and 169 "DUE_PROCESS_VIOLATION" findings confirm the systemic nature of this deprivation.

   c. **Impartial Decision-Maker.** As demonstrated in Grounds One through Three, the decision-maker is not impartial. The 146 MCR 2.003 disqualification findings (critical severity) and 30 BIAS_INDICATOR findings (critical severity) establish that the constitutional requirement of an impartial tribunal has not been met. *Caperton*, 556 US at 884.

   d. **Decision Based on Findings.** The Court has made **zero findings** on the twelve best-interest factors of MCL 722.23(a)–(l). The Court has failed to determine whether an established custodial environment exists under MCL 722.27(1)(c). The Court has not applied the *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 418, 434; 674 NW2d 390 (2003), change-of-circumstances threshold. The absence of findings is not a gap — it is a due process void.

36. **Parental Alienation as Evidence of Venue Failure.** The Court's inaction has enabled a textbook pattern of parental alienation:

   a. Defendant has engaged in documented alienation tactics including: blocking communication between Plaintiff and child; refusing co-parenting communication; false allegations of abuse; coaching the child to reject the other parent; emotional blackmail; and playing the victim. (Source: alienation_tactics table, litigation_context.db).

   b. MCL 722.23(j) — Factor J — requires the Court to consider *"the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent."* The Court's failure to analyze Factor J for 329+ days, while Defendant actively alienates the child, is not neutral — it is an endorsement of alienation. 74 documented "ALIENATION_ENABLEMENT" findings confirm this pattern.

   c. The child, Lincoln, has missed his third birthday, Labor Day, multiple holidays, and months of developmental milestones during the separation period. MCL 722.27a(3) specifically protects holiday and special-event parenting time. The Court has not addressed any of these lost time periods.

37. **CHESS MODE — Anticipatory Analysis:**

   - **OUR ARGUMENT:** The systematic deprivation of Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights — 329+ days without best-interest analysis, without hearing, and without enforcement of statutory parenting-time protections — constitutes a due process violation so severe that only venue change can provide a remedy. The current venue has demonstrated its inability or unwillingness to adjudicate this case in accordance with constitutional requirements.

   - **THEIR LIKELY DEFENSE:** Defendant will argue that (a) the Court's decisions are interlocutory and subject to appellate review, making venue change premature; (b) Plaintiff's due process concerns are actually disagreements with the merits of custody rulings; (c) Plaintiff bears some responsibility for the separation through his own conduct; and (d) constitutional venue-change arguments are properly raised in federal court, not state court.

   - **OUR PRE-REBUTTAL:** (a) Appellate review is not an adequate remedy when the trial court has made no findings to review. An appellate court cannot review a best-interest analysis that was never conducted. The remedy is not review of a deficient decision — it is a venue where the decision will actually be made. (b) Plaintiff is not disagreeing with merits findings — there *are* no findings. The due process violation is the *absence* of adjudication, not the outcome. (c) Any conduct concerns about Plaintiff should have been addressed through the best-interest factors — specifically MCL 722.23(b), (e), (f), (j), and (l). The Court's failure to analyze those factors means it has not actually weighed Plaintiff's conduct against the statutory criteria. An unadjudicated assertion is not a finding. (d) Michigan courts are fully competent to adjudicate constitutional due process claims. *In re Rood*, 483 Mich at 92-93 (applying *Mathews v Eldridge* in Michigan custody context). State courts are co-equal guarantors of constitutional rights. Moreover, MCR 2.222 provides the procedural vehicle; the Constitution provides the substantive right.

#### Conclusion

38. Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights under the Fourteenth Amendment and MCL 722.23 et seq. have been systematically violated for 329+ days. The current venue has demonstrated that it cannot provide the due process protections required by the Constitution. Venue must be changed to a court that will conduct the best-interest analysis required by law.
