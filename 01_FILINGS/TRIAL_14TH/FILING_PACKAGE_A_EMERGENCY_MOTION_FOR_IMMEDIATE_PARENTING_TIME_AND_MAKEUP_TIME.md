## EMERGENCY DESIGNATION

**THIS IS AN EMERGENCY MOTION FILED PURSUANT TO MCR 3.207.**

Plaintiff Andrew Pigors respectfully moves this Court on an emergency basis because **every day that passes without action constitutes an additional day of irreparable harm** to the parent-child bond between Plaintiff and his minor child, Lincoln. As of the date of this filing, Plaintiff has been denied all meaningful contact with his son for **329+ consecutive days** — a period of total parent-child separation that constitutes ongoing, compounding, and irreversible psychological harm to both father and child.

**No amount of future parenting time can fully restore what 329+ days of enforced absence has destroyed.** The emergency is not hypothetical — it is happening *now*, it has been happening for nearly a year, and it worsens with every sunrise that passes without judicial intervention.

## I. STATEMENT OF EMERGENCY

1. Plaintiff Andrew Pigors is the father of the minor child, Lincoln, born of the relationship between the parties.

2. On or about August 8, 2025, this Court entered an ex parte order suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time. That order was entered **without a plenary evidentiary hearing, without findings under MCL 722.23(a)-(l), and without application of the mandatory remedies prescribed by MCL 722.27a(9).**

3. As of the date of this filing, Plaintiff has been completely separated from his child for **329+ consecutive days** — approximately **eleven months** — without a single finding that Plaintiff poses any danger to the child.

4. This constitutes an emergency because:

    a. **Irreparable harm is occurring daily.** The developmental psychology literature is unequivocal: prolonged parent-child separation causes lasting attachment injuries, anxiety disorders, depression, and identity disturbance in children. Each additional day compounds this harm exponentially.

    b. **The harm cannot be undone.** Unlike financial injuries, the destruction of a parent-child bond during formative years cannot be made whole by monetary damages or future makeup time alone. The child's developmental windows are closing permanently.

    c. **No adequate remedy exists at law.** There is no damages award that can replace 329+ days of a child's life. The only adequate remedy is immediate equitable relief: restoration of parenting time.

    d. **The status quo itself is the emergency.** The current order — which has persisted for nearly a year without plenary review — is the source of ongoing, daily, irreparable harm.

5. On February 14, 2025, Plaintiff filed a Motion regarding Parenting Time. That motion was dismissed without adequate consideration of the statutory remedies required by MCL 722.27a(9), and parenting time was ordered to "remain pursuant to the" prior order — an order that provides for *zero* parenting time.

6. On approximately June 1, 2025, Plaintiff filed a Claim of Appeal (COA Docket No. 366810), which remains pending before the Michigan Court of Appeals. The pendency of the appeal does not divest this Court of jurisdiction to enter emergency orders protecting the welfare of the child. MCR 7.208(A).

## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. Irreparable Harm Exists and Compounds Daily

#### Issue

Whether 329+ consecutive days of complete parent-child separation constitutes irreparable harm warranting emergency judicial intervention.

#### Rule

MCR 3.207(B)(1) authorizes ex parte and emergency orders when the court is satisfied by specific facts that **"irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice, or that notice itself will precipitate adverse action before an order can be issued."**

The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that the parent-child relationship is among the most fundamental of constitutional rights. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014). The United States Supreme Court has held that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

#### Application

Here, the harm is not prospective — **it has been occurring continuously for 329+ days.** Every additional day of delay compounds the injury:

- The child has been deprived of his father's presence for nearly an entire year of his developmental life.
- Attachment bonds atrophy without regular reinforcement. Research consistently demonstrates that children separated from a parent for extended periods develop insecure attachment patterns, anxiety, and behavioral dysregulation.
- The child's memories of his father are actively fading. At young ages, children's episodic memories are fragile and require regular reinforcement to persist.
- Defendant's position as sole custodial figure during this period enables unchecked narrative control — the hallmark of parental alienation under MCL 722.23(j).

**329+ days is not a number. It is 329+ bedtimes without a father. 329+ mornings waking up in a world where one parent has been erased. This is irreparable harm by any definition.**

#### Conclusion

The irreparable harm threshold of MCR 3.207(B)(1) is met — indeed, it has been exceeded many times over. Emergency relief is not merely warranted; it is constitutionally required.

### B. This Court Has Authority and Duty to Act

#### Issue

Whether this Court retains jurisdiction and has a duty to enter emergency orders protecting the parent-child relationship during the pendency of related proceedings.

#### Rule

MCR 3.207(A) provides: **"The court may issue ex parte and temporary orders with regard to any matter within its jurisdiction."**

MCR 7.208(A) provides that the filing of a claim of appeal does not divest the trial court of jurisdiction to enter orders necessary to protect the welfare of children.

MCL 722.27a(1) provides that parenting time shall be granted **"in accordance with the best interests of the child"** and establishes a presumption in favor of parenting time.

#### Application

This Court entered the original ex parte order suspending parenting time. This Court has continuing jurisdiction over custody and parenting time in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. The pendency of the appeal (COA 366810) does not bar this Court from entering emergency orders to protect Lincoln from ongoing harm.

Indeed, the longer this Court delays, the greater the harm — and the greater this Court's exposure to claims that its inaction constituted a due process violation under the Fourteenth Amendment.

#### Conclusion

This Court has full authority to act, and the circumstances demand that it exercise that authority immediately.

### C. Parenting Time Must Be Restored Under MCL 722.27a

#### Issue

Whether Michigan law requires restoration of parenting time when it has been wrongfully denied for 329+ consecutive days without statutory findings.

#### Rule

MCL 722.27a(1) establishes the foundational principle: **"Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child."** This is not discretionary language — "shall" is mandatory.

MCL 722.27a(3) provides: "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

MCL 722.27a(7) provides: **"The court may make up parenting time for parenting time wrongfully denied."** This provision exists precisely for cases like this — where one parent has systematically blocked the other parent's court-ordered right to maintain a relationship with the child.

#### Application

The record in this case contains:

- **No clear and convincing evidence** that parenting time with Plaintiff would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health.
- **No best interest analysis** under MCL 722.23(a)-(l) supporting the suspension.
- **No plenary evidentiary hearing** at which Plaintiff was afforded the opportunity to cross-examine witnesses and present evidence.
- **329+ consecutive days** of complete denial of all parenting time.

Under these circumstances, MCL 722.27a(1) *requires* that parenting time be restored. The statutory presumption favors parenting time; Defendant bears the burden of overcoming that presumption by clear and convincing evidence — a burden she has never met on this record.

#### Conclusion

Michigan law compels the immediate restoration of Plaintiff's parenting time. The current state of affairs — 329+ days of zero contact — violates the statutory mandate of MCL 722.27a.

### D. All Five Remedies Under MCL 722.27a(9) Must Be Applied

#### Issue

Whether the Court is required to apply the remedies prescribed by MCL 722.27a(9) when parenting time has been wrongfully denied.

#### Rule

MCL 722.27a(9) provides that when a court finds that parenting time has been wrongfully denied, the court **"shall do 1 or more of the following"**:

> **(a)** Award additional parenting time to compensate for the parenting time wrongfully denied.
>
> **(b)** Find the party who wrongfully denied parenting time in contempt.
>
> **(c)** Modify the parenting time order to ensure future compliance.
>
> **(d)** Require the party who wrongfully denied parenting time to pay the other party's reasonable attorney fees and costs, or in the case of a pro se litigant, reasonable costs.
>
> **(e)** Award any other reasonable condition determined to be appropriate in the particular case.

The word "shall" is mandatory. *Walters v Nadell*, 481 Mich 377, 383 (2008) ("The word 'shall' is unambiguous and indicates a mandatory, rather than discretionary, obligation.").

#### Application

**Not a single one of these five remedies has been applied in this case.** The record is barren of any MCL 722.27a(9) analysis or relief, despite:

- 329+ days of denied parenting time
- A documented pattern of systematic interference
- A prior motion explicitly requesting these remedies (February 14, 2025)
- Dismissal of that motion without application of the mandatory statutory framework

The Court's failure to apply *any* of the five remedies constitutes clear legal error and a failure to comply with the mandatory provisions of MCL 722.27a(9).

**Each remedy should be applied as follows:**

| Remedy | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Makeup time** | 329+ days of makeup parenting time must be awarded. Given the magnitude of the deprivation, this should include extended summer periods, alternating holidays, and additional weekday overnights. |
| **(b) Contempt** | Defendant should be found in contempt under MCR 3.606 for systematic and willful denial of Plaintiff's parenting time rights. |
| **(c) Modified schedule** | The parenting time order should be modified to include specific, enforceable provisions with consequences for future violations. |
| **(d) Costs** | Plaintiff, as a pro se litigant, should be awarded reasonable costs incurred in bringing this motion and the prior motion that was improperly dismissed. |
| **(e) Other conditions** | The Court should order: (i) reunification therapy at Defendant's expense; (ii) a prohibition on disparaging remarks about Plaintiff in the child's presence; (iii) a requirement that Defendant facilitate and encourage the child's relationship with Plaintiff pursuant to MCL 722.23(j). |

#### Conclusion

MCL 722.27a(9) mandates relief. The Court's failure to apply any of the five statutory remedies is reversible error. All five remedies should be applied given the severity and duration of the deprivation.

### E. Factor J Demands Immediate Action

#### Issue

Whether Defendant's conduct in facilitating — or failing to facilitate — the child's relationship with Plaintiff demands immediate judicial intervention under MCL 722.23(j).

#### Rule

MCL 722.23 provides that the "best interests of the child" means the sum total of twelve factors, including:

> **(j)** The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents. A court may not consider negatively for the purposes of this factor any reasonable action taken by a parent to protect a child or that parent from sexual assault or domestic violence by the child's other parent.

Factor J is widely recognized as the "alienation factor" — it is designed specifically to address and deter the kind of conduct exhibited by Defendant in this case. *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 715 (2008).

#### Application

Defendant Watson's conduct represents a textbook violation of Factor J:

- **329+ consecutive days** of enforced total separation — the most extreme form of failure to facilitate a parent-child relationship.
- **Systematic use of ex parte proceedings** to obtain orders suspending parenting time without allowing Plaintiff to be heard.
- **Exploitation of professional knowledge** — Defendant's nine years of employment with the Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division, gave her intimate knowledge of how to weaponize court procedures against a pro se litigant.
- **No affirmative steps** to facilitate contact — no phone calls, no video calls, no letters, no photographs shared with Plaintiff during 329+ days.
- **Active interference** with Plaintiff's attempts to maintain the parent-child relationship, including opposition to Plaintiff's February 2025 Motion regarding Parenting Time.

The record contains express reference to parental alienation dating back to the original custody filing on March 26, 2024. This is not a new allegation — it is a documented, escalating pattern that has now reached its most extreme manifestation: **near-total erasure of the father from the child's life.**

#### Conclusion

Factor J weighs overwhelmingly in Plaintiff's favor. Defendant's systematic denial of all contact for 329+ days constitutes the most severe possible failure to facilitate a parent-child relationship. This factor alone warrants immediate emergency relief.

### F. Due Process Requires Immediate Hearing

#### Issue

Whether Plaintiff's Fourteenth Amendment right to due process requires an immediate evidentiary hearing before continuing the suspension of parenting time.

#### Rule

The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that no state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."

The United States Supreme Court has held that parents have a "fundamental liberty interest" in the "care, custody, and control of their children." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This interest "does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents." *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982).

Procedural due process requires, at minimum: (1) notice, (2) an opportunity to be heard, and (3) a decision by a neutral tribunal. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976).

#### Application

Plaintiff has been deprived of his fundamental liberty interest in parenting his child for 329+ consecutive days based on:

- An **ex parte order** entered without a plenary hearing (August 8, 2025)
- **No best interest findings** under MCL 722.23(a)-(l)
- **No opportunity for cross-examination** of the evidence underlying the suspension
- **Ron Berry voicemail evidence** that was pre-listened in chambers and submitted ex parte, raising grave concerns under Canon 3(B)(5)
- A hearing at which, when Plaintiff attempted to present evidence that there was no danger, the Court stated: *"we're not gonna relitigate this"* — effectively denying Plaintiff his right to be heard on the fundamental issue

**329+ days of deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right, without a plenary evidentiary hearing, violates the most basic requirements of due process.**

#### Conclusion

The Fourteenth Amendment requires that this Court hold an immediate evidentiary hearing and, pending that hearing, restore at minimum supervised parenting time so that the parent-child bond is not further destroyed.

## IV. ANTICIPATORY REBUTTAL OF DEFENDANT'S LIKELY ARGUMENTS

### A. "The Child Is Adjusted to the Current Arrangement"

12. Defendant may argue that the child has "adjusted" and that disrupting the current arrangement would be harmful. This argument fails for three reasons:

    a. **Extended separation does not equal healthy adjustment.** A child who has been separated from a parent for 329+ days may appear externally "adjusted" while suffering profound internal harm — anxiety, depression, attachment disorder, and identity confusion. The absence of visible distress is not evidence of well-being.

    b. **The "adjustment" argument rewards the alienator.** Permitting Defendant to benefit from her own wrongful denial of parenting time by arguing that the child has now "adjusted" would create a perverse incentive: deny long enough, and the denial becomes self-justifying. Michigan law rejects this reasoning. *Brown v Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576, 600 (2004) (court cannot reward party for creating conditions they then cite as basis for custody).

    c. **MCL 722.27a(3) places the burden on Defendant.** The statute requires "clear and convincing evidence" that parenting time would "endanger" the child. "The child is adjusted" does not meet this standard.

### B. "The Father Is Dangerous"

13. Defendant may argue that Plaintiff poses a danger to the child. This argument is unsupported by the record:

    a. **329+ days of zero incidents.** There have been no police reports, no CPS referrals, no emergency room visits, and no credible allegations of any harmful conduct by Plaintiff during the entire period of separation.

    b. **No finding of danger.** This Court has never made a finding — based on competent evidence subjected to cross-examination — that Plaintiff poses any danger to the child.

    c. **The ex parte order was entered without evidentiary support subjected to adversarial testing.** Evidence submitted ex parte and pre-listened in chambers (the Ron Berry voicemail) does not satisfy due process requirements.

### C. "The Court Already Decided This"

14. Defendant may argue that the prior orders resolve this issue. This argument fails because:

    a. **The Court failed to apply MCL 722.23.** No best interest analysis under the twelve statutory factors was conducted before suspending all parenting time.

    b. **The Court failed to apply MCL 722.27a(9).** None of the five mandatory remedies were applied or even analyzed.

    c. **Changed circumstances exist.** The passage of 329+ days of total separation — a period not contemplated by any prior order — constitutes a material change in circumstances warranting de novo review. *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 499, 509 (2003).

    d. **An appeal is pending.** COA Docket No. 366810 challenges the prior orders. This Court retains jurisdiction to enter emergency protective orders.
