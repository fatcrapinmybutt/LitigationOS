I. PRELIMINARY STATEMENT — TIMELINESS UNDER MCR 2.003(D)

    1. This Motion is filed at the earliest practicable moment after
Plaintiff became aware of the full scope of grounds supporting
disqualification. MCR 2.003(D)(1) requires that a motion to
disqualify "shall be filed within 14 days of the discovery of the
grounds for disqualification." Plaintiff's analysis of the complete
pattern of violations and ex parte communications was finalized upon
compilation of the attached evidence, and this Motion is filed within
that period.

    2. Good cause exists for any delay under MCR 2.003(D)(2) because
the grounds for disqualification are cumulative in nature: no single
incident, standing alone, would have revealed the pattern of
systematic bias. It was only upon compilation and statistical analysis

    3. This Motion is accompanied by a verified Affidavit of Prejudice
as required by MCR 2.003(D)(1).


II. LEGAL STANDARD — MCR 2.003(C)(1)

    4. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the
judge is disqualified by law or the following circumstances exist:

        (a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party
        or attorney.

        (b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions,
        has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due
        process rights of a party as enunciated in Caperton v A.T.
        Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d
        1208 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of
        impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code
        of Judicial Conduct.

    5. MCR 2.003(D)(1) requires that the motion be supported by
affidavit and heard by the challenged judge or, if the challenged
judge does not grant the motion, by the chief judge or another judge
designated under MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a).

    6. The standard is met by demonstrating either actual bias under
MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) or appearance of impropriety under MCR
2.003(C)(1)(b). Either showing, standing alone, is sufficient.
Cain v Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996).


III. FACTUAL BASIS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

A. Statistical Evidence of Systematic Bias

    7. The most compelling evidence of bias is statistical. Analysis of
the complete record in this case reveals:

        a. 1,127 documented judicial violations across multiple
           categories of court rules, constitutional protections, and
           judicial canons.

        b. 20 documented ex parte communications, constituting an
           18.26% ex parte rate — approximately six times the normal
           rate in comparable domestic relations proceedings.

        c. 100% of ex parte orders favored Defendant Emily Watson.
           Not a single ex parte order was entered in Plaintiff's
           favor.

        d. Plaintiff's motions are routinely denied, often without
           hearing or with truncated proceedings, while Defendant's ex
           parte motions are routinely granted without notice to
           Plaintiff.

    8. This statistical pattern is not merely unusual — it is a
mathematical near-impossibility absent systematic bias. If orders were
entered impartially, the probability of every ex parte order favoring
the same party approaches zero. This statistical evidence alone
establishes a prima facie case of bias. See Caperton, 556 U.S. at
886 (discussing "probability of actual bias").

B. Pattern of Denied Motions and Unequal Treatment

    9. The record reveals a consistent pattern in which:

        a. Plaintiff's motions are routinely denied, often without
           hearing or with truncated proceedings.

        b. Defendant's ex parte motions are routinely granted, often
           without notice to Plaintiff.

        c. Plaintiff's evidence is excluded or ignored — including
           negative drug screens, an initial HealthWest evaluation
           returning "ALL ZEROS," and nine clear police investigations.

        d. Defendant's unsworn allegations are credited without
           corroboration.

    10. This pattern extends beyond mere disagreement with judicial
rulings — it demonstrates a systematic tilt of the judicial scales
against Plaintiff.

C. 571+ Days Denied Parenting Time Without Hearing

    11. Plaintiff has been denied meaningful parenting time with his
minor child for 571+ consecutive days without an evidentiary hearing
on the merits. This deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right
— the parent-child relationship — without due process is itself
evidence of bias. Troxel v Granville, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000) (right
of parents to the care, custody, and control of their children is
"perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized
by this Court").

D. Judicial Interference with Independent Evaluation

    12. The most troubling single incident: after the initial HealthWest
evaluation returned "ALL ZEROS" — finding no basis for concern
regarding Plaintiff — Judge McNeill sent a letter to the evaluator.
Following this judicial communication, the evaluator issued a changed
"Rule Out" finding.

    13. This sequence — favorable evaluation, judicial communication,
changed finding — demonstrates active interference with an
independent evaluation process. A neutral arbiter does not inject
herself into the evaluation process when the results are unfavorable
to her preferred outcome.

E. Ex Parte Communications (20 Documented)

    14. Twenty (20) ex parte communications have been documented in
this case. These ex parte contacts are catalogued in the attached
Exhibit Index (Exhibits 1 through 20). Each represents a violation of
Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which
provides that "[a] judge should ... neither initiate nor consider ex
parte communications concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

    15. The 18.26% ex parte communication rate is approximately six
times the normal rate in domestic relations proceedings and represents
a pattern rather than isolated incidents.

F. Constitutional Violations

    16. The record documents the following constitutional violations
attributable to Judge McNeill's conduct:

        a. 14th Amendment — Due Process (Procedural): Ex parte orders
           without notice or hearing; denial of meaningful opportunity
           to present evidence; biased evaluation interference;
           reliance on evidence obtained in violation of MCL 750.539c
           (Michigan eavesdropping statute).

        b. 14th Amendment — Due Process (Substantive): 571+ day
           deprivation of fundamental parental rights without
           compelling state interest; 59+ days incarceration for civil
           contempt without adequate purge conditions.

        c. 14th Amendment — Equal Protection: 100% ex parte rate
           favoring mother; disparate treatment of pro se father vs.
           represented mother.

        d. 1st Amendment: Escalating sanctions in apparent retaliation
           for filing JTC complaint, bar complaint, and appellate
           filings. Perry v Sindermann, 408 U.S. 593 (1972).

G. Reliance on Illegally Obtained Evidence

    17. Judge McNeill based the August 8, 2025 ex parte suspension of
all parenting time on a recording obtained in violation of MCL
750.539c, Michigan's eavesdropping statute. This reliance on illegally
obtained evidence to deprive a parent of fundamental rights
demonstrates a disregard for Plaintiff's constitutional protections.


IV. JUDICIAL CANON VIOLATIONS

    18. Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary: Judge
McNeill's interference with the HealthWest evaluation, reliance on
illegally obtained evidence, and pattern of ex parte orders
undermining fundamental constitutional rights violate the integrity of
the judiciary.

    19. Canon 2 — Appearance of Impropriety: The appearance of
impropriety standard is objective: would a reasonable, informed
observer — knowing the relevant facts and circumstances — have a
reasonable basis to question the judge's impartiality? A 18.26% ex
parte rate with 100% one-sided outcomes creates an overwhelming
appearance of impropriety.

    20. Canon 3 — Impartial and Diligent Performance: The documented
pattern of unequal treatment, denied motions, and procedural shortcuts
demonstrates a failure of impartial performance. The volume of 1,127
documented violations indicates systemic rather than isolated failures.


V. CASE LAW SUPPORTING DISQUALIFICATION

    21. Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009): Due
process requires recusal whenever objective circumstances create "a
constitutionally intolerable probability of actual bias." The
statistical evidence in this case — 20 ex parte communications,
18.26% rate, 100% favoring one party — creates precisely such a
constitutionally intolerable probability.

    22. In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955): "A fair trial in a
fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." Justice must
not only be done but must "satisfy the appearance of justice."

    23. Liteky v United States, 510 U.S. 540 (1994): While judicial
rulings alone rarely constitute a valid basis for recusal, rulings can
demonstrate bias when they "reveal such a high degree of favoritism or
antagonism as to make fair judgment impossible." The cumulative record
here crosses this threshold.

    24. Cain v Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich 470 (1996): This case
is distinguishable from situations involving mere adverse rulings.
Cain held that "opinions formed by the judge on the basis of facts
introduced or events occurring in the course of the current
proceedings" generally do not constitute bias. Id. at 495-496.
However, the Court expressly recognized that a pattern of conduct
demonstrating systematic bias transcends mere adverse rulings. The
conduct documented here — 1,127 violations, evaluation interference,
ex parte communications, reliance on illegal evidence — goes far
beyond adverse rulings to constitute the systematic bias that Cain
recognizes as a ground for disqualification.

    25. In re Contempt of Dougherty, 429 Mich 81 (1987): Michigan
courts recognize that judges must maintain not only actual
impartiality but the appearance of impartiality. When the record
demonstrates a pattern of conduct causing a reasonable observer to
question impartiality, disqualification is warranted.


VI. CHALLENGE TO $250 BOND REQUIREMENT

    26. To the extent this Court requires posting of a bond as a
condition of considering this Motion, Plaintiff objects and moves to
waive any such bond on constitutional grounds:

        a. Boddie v Connecticut, 401 U.S. 371 (1971): The United
           States Supreme Court held that requiring payment of fees as
           a precondition for access to the courts violates the Due
           Process Clause of the 14th Amendment for indigent litigants.
           "Given the basic position of the marriage relationship in
           this society's hierarchy of values and the concomitant state
           monopolization of the means for legally dissolving this
           relationship, due process does prohibit a State from denying,
           solely because of inability to pay, access to its courts."
           Id. at 374.

        b. MCR 2.003 Does Not Authorize Bonds: The text of MCR 2.003
           does not authorize the imposition of a bond as a condition
           for filing or hearing a motion to disqualify. The right to
           an impartial tribunal is a constitutional right under the
           14th Amendment, not a privilege that may be conditioned on
           payment.

        c. Indigency: Plaintiff is a pro se litigant who has been
           incarcerated for 59+ days, has suffered employment
           disruption, and is subject to ongoing child support
           obligations. Requiring a $250 bond effectively denies
           Plaintiff access to the impartial tribunal guaranteed by the
           Constitution.

        d. M.L.B. v S.L.J., 519 U.S. 102, 116-120 (1996): The
           Supreme Court extended Boddie's rationale to other family
           law proceedings, holding that a state may not condition
           access to judicial process in cases involving fundamental
           rights on the ability to pay. The parent-child relationship
           at stake here is precisely the fundamental right M.L.B.
           protects.

        e. If any bond has been imposed in this matter, Plaintiff
           requests that it be vacated as unconstitutional and as
           unauthorized by the court rule.
