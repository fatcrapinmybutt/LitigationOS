I. INTRODUCTION AND STATEMENT OF ISSUES

    This Brief is submitted in support of Plaintiff's Motion to
Disqualify the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill pursuant to MCR
2.003(C)(1). The issues presented are:

    1. Whether Judge McNeill should be disqualified based on actual
       bias demonstrated by 1,127 documented violations, 20 ex parte
       communications, evaluation interference, and reliance on
       illegally obtained evidence;

    2. Whether the 18.26% ex parte communication rate — six times
       normal — creates an appearance of impropriety requiring
       disqualification under Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial
       Conduct;

    3. Whether 571+ days of denied parenting time without evidentiary
       hearing constitutes a due process violation supporting
       disqualification under Caperton;

    4. Whether the $250 bond imposed as a condition for disqualification
       violates due process under Boddie v Connecticut, 401 U.S. 371
       (1971) and is unauthorized by MCR 2.003.

III. STANDARD OF REVIEW

    Disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1) requires either:

    1. Actual bias: The party must show the judge has "a bent of mind
       that may prevent or impede impartiality of judgment." Fletcher v
       Fletcher, 447 Mich 871, 876-877; 526 NW2d 889 (1994).

    2. Appearance of impropriety: The party must show circumstances
       where "a reasonable person, knowing all the circumstances, would
       harbor doubts about the judge's impartiality." In re
       Disqualification of Conlon, 294 Mich App 253, 259; 816 NW2d 378
       (2011).

    Either showing is independently sufficient. Cain v Dep't of
Corrections, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996). Both are
present here.

IV. ARGUMENT

A. Legal Standard for Disqualification Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)

    MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides two independent grounds:

    Subsection (a): Actual bias or prejudice for or against a party.

    Subsection (b): Based on objective and reasonable perceptions, (i) a
    serious risk of actual bias impacting due process rights as
    enunciated in Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009),
    or (ii) failure to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard
    of Canon 2.

    The purpose of the rule is dual: ensuring actual impartiality and
maintaining public confidence. Ward v Village of Monroeville, 409
U.S. 57, 60 (1972).


B. Actual Bias — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)

    Actual bias is "a bent of mind that may prevent or impede
impartiality of judgment" proven through conduct and rulings.
Fletcher, 447 Mich at 876-877.

    1. Pattern of Adverse Rulings. Plaintiff's motions are routinely
denied while Defendant's are routinely granted, often ex parte and
without notice. This 100% one-sided ex parte outcome is inconsistent
with neutral adjudication.

    2. Evaluation Interference. Judge McNeill sent a letter to the
HealthWest evaluator after the evaluation returned results favorable
to Plaintiff, after which the evaluator changed the finding. A neutral
arbiter does not inject herself into independent evaluation processes
when results disfavor her preferred outcome.

    3. Reliance on Illegal Evidence. Basing the parenting time
suspension on evidence obtained in violation of MCL 750.539c
demonstrates disregard for Plaintiff's legal protections.

    4. Retaliation. An escalating pattern of adverse action followed
Plaintiff's filing of a JTC complaint, bar complaint, and appellate
filings — protected First Amendment activity. Perry v Sindermann, 408
U.S. 593 (1972); Mt. Healthy City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Ed. v Doyle, 429
U.S. 274 (1977).


C. Appearance of Impropriety — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii)

    The test is objective: "Would a reasonable person, knowing all the
circumstances, harbor doubts about the judge's impartiality?" Conlon,
294 Mich App at 259.

    A reasonable, informed observer reviewing this record would note:

    - A father denied all parenting time for 571+ days
    - Twenty ex parte communications, all favoring the mother
    - Negative drug screens, clean evaluations, nine clear
      investigations — all ignored
    - Judicial interference with an independent evaluation
    - Reliance on illegally obtained evidence
    - 1,127 documented violations
    - Incarceration of a pro se father for 59+ days on civil contempt

    This observer would have more than a "reasonable basis to question
the judge's impartiality" — they would find it impossible to conclude
these proceedings were conducted impartially.


D. Due Process Violation — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) / Caperton

    Caperton, 556 U.S. at 884, held that due process requires recusal
whenever "there is a serious risk of actual bias — based on objective
and reasonable perceptions." The Court identified a "constitutionally
intolerable probability of actual bias" standard.

    The statistical evidence here — 20 ex parte communications, 18.26%
rate, 100% favoring one party, 1,127 violations — creates precisely
such a constitutionally intolerable probability. No reasonable observer
could attribute these outcomes to neutral adjudication.

    In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955): "A fair trial in a
fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." Justice must
not only be done, but must "satisfy the appearance of justice."


E. Ex Parte Communication Evidence

    Twenty documented ex parte communications are catalogued in the
Exhibit Index (Exhibits 1-20). Each represents a violation of Canon
3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

    The 18.26% ex parte rate is approximately six times normal. This is
not a single lapse but a pattern of conduct demonstrating systematic
disregard for the prohibition on ex parte communications.

    MCR 2.119(A) requires that motions be served on all parties. Ex
parte orders are permissible only in narrowly defined emergencies
requiring immediate action to prevent irreparable harm. MCR 3.207.
The frequency and one-sided nature of ex parte activity in this case
far exceeds any legitimate use of ex parte authority.


F. Statistical Bias Analysis

    The statistical evidence eliminates the possibility that the
observed pattern arose from neutral adjudication:

    - Ex Parte Rate: 18.26% (20 ex parte communications documented)
    - One-Sided Outcomes: 100% of ex parte orders favor Defendant
    - Violation Count: 1,127 documented violations
    - Denial of Rights: 571+ days without parenting time
    - Incarceration: 59+ days civil contempt without counsel

    If a coin flip gave each party a 50% chance on any given order,
the probability of 20 consecutive outcomes favoring the same party is
1 in 1,048,576 — less than 0.0001%. Even if the probability were
skewed 70/30 in one party's favor, 20 consecutive one-sided outcomes
would still be statistically extraordinary.

    See generally Hazelwood School District v United States, 433 U.S.
299, 307-308 (1977) (recognizing that "gross" statistical disparities
alone may constitute prima facie proof of a pattern). While Hazelwood
addressed employment discrimination, the statistical reasoning
applies equally to judicial conduct analysis.


G. Due Process Violations — 571+ Days Without Hearing

    The 14th Amendment guarantees that no state shall "deprive any
person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."

    The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest
entitled to heightened protection. Troxel v Granville, 530 U.S. 57,
65 (2000). Deprivation of this right requires, at minimum, notice
and an opportunity to be heard. Mathews v Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319
(1976).

    Plaintiff has been denied meaningful parenting time for 571+
consecutive days without an evidentiary hearing on the merits. This
prolonged deprivation of a fundamental constitutional right without
due process is itself evidence of the bias underlying this Motion.


H. Judicial Canon Violations

    Canon 1 — Integrity: Interference with independent evaluations and
reliance on illegally obtained evidence undermines judicial integrity.

    Canon 2 — Appearance of Impropriety: An 18.26% ex parte rate with
100% one-sided outcomes creates an overwhelming appearance of
impropriety.

    Canon 3(A)(4) — Ex Parte Communications: Twenty documented ex
parte communications violate the express prohibition on ex parte
communications "concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

    Canon 3(B)(5) — Impartiality: The pattern of 1,127 violations
demonstrates a failure of impartial performance.


I. Distinguishing Adverse Rulings — Cain v Dep't of Corrections

    Plaintiff anticipates the argument that judicial rulings alone
cannot support disqualification. The undersigned acknowledges Cain v
Dep't of Corrections, 451 Mich 470 (1996), which held that "opinions
formed by the judge on the basis of facts introduced or events
occurring in the course of the current proceedings" generally do not
constitute bias. Id. at 495-496.

    However, this case is fundamentally distinguishable from Cain for
four reasons:

    1. Extrajudicial Conduct. The evaluation interference (sending a
letter to the HealthWest evaluator) and reliance on illegally obtained
evidence are extrajudicial acts that Cain expressly does not protect.

    2. Ex Parte Communications. Twenty ex parte communications are
procedural violations independent of any substantive ruling. Cain
addresses the content of rulings, not procedural irregularities.

    3. Cumulative Pattern. Even Cain recognized that rulings can
demonstrate bias when they "reveal such a high degree of favoritism or
antagonism as to make fair judgment impossible." Liteky v United
States, 510 U.S. 540, 555 (1994) (quoted approvingly in Michigan
authority). The 1,127 violations, combined with extrajudicial conduct,
cross this threshold.

    4. Retaliation. An escalating pattern of sanctions and restrictions
following Plaintiff's protected First Amendment activity (JTC
complaint, bar complaint, appellate filings) demonstrates personal
animus beyond mere adverse rulings.

    In short, this is not a case of a litigant unhappy with the
outcome of individual rulings. It is a case of systematic bias
demonstrated through statistical patterns, extrajudicial acts, and
procedural violations that collectively deprive Plaintiff of the fair
tribunal guaranteed by the Constitution.


J. Bond Challenge — Boddie v Connecticut, 401 U.S. 371 (1971)

    To the extent a $250 bond has been imposed as a condition for
filing or hearing this Motion, Plaintiff challenges the bond on
constitutional and procedural grounds:

    1. Boddie v Connecticut, 401 U.S. 371 (1971). The Supreme Court
held that requiring payment of fees as a precondition for access to
the courts violates the Due Process Clause for indigent litigants in
family law proceedings. "Due process does prohibit a State from
denying, solely because of inability to pay, access to its courts to
individuals who seek judicial dissolution of their marriages." Id. at
374.

    2. MCR 2.003 Contains No Bond Provision. The text of MCR 2.003
does not authorize the imposition of any bond as a condition for
filing a disqualification motion. The right to an impartial tribunal
is a constitutional right, not a privilege conditioned on payment.

    3. M.L.B. v S.L.J., 519 U.S. 102, 116-120 (1996). Extended
Boddie to other family proceedings involving fundamental rights,
holding that states may not condition access to judicial process on
ability to pay where parental rights are at stake.

    4. Griffin v Illinois, 351 U.S. 12, 19 (1956). "There can be no
equal justice where the kind of trial a man gets depends on the amount
of money he has."

    5. Indigency. Plaintiff is a pro se litigant who was incarcerated
for 59+ days, has suffered employment disruption, and is subject to
ongoing child support obligations. The $250 bond effectively denies
access to the impartial tribunal the Constitution guarantees.

    The bond must be vacated.
