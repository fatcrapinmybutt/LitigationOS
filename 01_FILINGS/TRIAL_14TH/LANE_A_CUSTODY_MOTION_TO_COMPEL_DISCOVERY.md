## II. LEGAL STANDARD

9. **MCR 2.313(A)** provides that "[a] party, on reasonable notice to other parties and all persons affected, may apply for an order compelling disclosure or discovery" when a party fails to respond to interrogatories (MCR 2.313(A)(2)(b)(iii)), fails to respond to document requests (MCR 2.313(A)(2)(b)(iv)), or provides evasive or incomplete responses (MCR 2.313(A)(4)).

10. **MCR 2.302(B)** establishes that the scope of discovery encompasses "any nonprivileged matter that is relevant to any party's claims or defenses and proportional to the needs of the case." Relevance is construed broadly; information need not be admissible at trial if it "appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence."

11. **MCR 2.313(A)(4)** provides that "[f]or purposes of this subrule an evasive or incomplete disclosure, answer, or response must be treated as a failure to disclose, answer, or respond."

12. **MCR 2.313(A)(5)(a)** provides that if the motion to compel is granted, "the court may, after opportunity for hearing, require the party or deponent whose conduct necessitated the motion" to pay the reasonable expenses incurred, including reasonable costs for a pro se litigant.

## V. CHESS-MODE: ANTICIPATED DEFENSES AND REBUTTALS

41. Plaintiff anticipates the following defenses and addresses each:

### Defense 1: "Overbroad and Unduly Burdensome"

42. **Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that the discovery requests are overbroad and impose an undue burden.

43. **Rebuttal:** MCR 2.302(B) permits discovery of relevant, nonprivileged matter "proportional to the needs of the case." ### Defense 2: "Privileged"

44. **Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will assert attorney-client privilege or work product protection over certain communications.

45. **Rebuttal:** The discovery requests do not seek attorney-client communications. They seek communications with court personnel, FOC staff, prosecutors, and Ron Berry — none of whom are Defendant's attorneys. To the extent Defendant asserts privilege, she must produce a privilege log identifying each withheld document with sufficient specificity per MCR 2.302(C). Blanket assertions of privilege are insufficient. *See* MCR 2.302(C).

### Defense 3: "Employment Records Are Irrelevant"

46. **Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that her employment records at the Prosecutor's Office are irrelevant to a custody dispute.

47. **Rebuttal:** Defendant's employment is relevant because: (a) the Court itself cited Defendant's employment status in its findings; (b) Defendant's professional knowledge of family court procedures is directly relevant to the alienation pattern; (c) Defendant's access to prosecutorial resources and relationships bears on the fairness of the proceedings; and (d) MCL 722.23(j) requires evaluation of each party's willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship, which necessarily includes evaluating whether a party has used institutional resources to obstruct that relationship.

### Defense 4: "Privacy / Third-Party Rights"

48. **Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that Berry's records or third-party communications implicate privacy rights.

49. **Rebuttal:** MCR 2.302(C) permits the Court to enter protective orders as appropriate. Plaintiff does not seek public disclosure of private information — Plaintiff seeks production subject to protective order if necessary. The Ron Berry voicemail is already part of the appellate record (COA No. 366810). Berry's connection to the proceedings has been established; his privacy interest does not outweigh the fundamental right of a father to discover the truth about why he has been separated from his child.

### Defense 5: "Discovery Is Being Used for Harassment"

50. **Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will characterize this Motion as harassment by a pro se litigant.

51. **Rebuttal:** This Motion is authorized by MCR 2.313(A) and is the standard procedural mechanism for enforcing discovery obligations. Every category of discovery sought is tied to specific claims, specific Michigan Court Rules, and specific best interest factors. A pro se litigant is entitled to the same discovery rights as a represented party. *See* MCR 2.302(B). The suggestion that a father seeking information about his own child's wellbeing is "harassing" the mother underscores the very dynamic this Motion seeks to expose.

**THIS MATTER** having come before the Court on Plaintiff's Motion to Compel Discovery filed pursuant to MCR 2.313(A), and the Court having reviewed the Motion, the record, and any response filed by Defendant, and being otherwise fully advised in the premises;

**THE COURT FINDS:**

1. Plaintiff served his First Set of Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents, and Requests for Admissions on Defendant pursuant to MCR 2.309, MCR 2.310, and MCR 2.312.

2. Defendant has failed to provide complete responses within the time prescribed by the Michigan Court Rules.

3. The discovery sought is relevant to the parties' claims and defenses and proportional to the needs of the case under MCR 2.302(B).

4. Plaintiff's Motion to Compel is well-taken.

**IT IS HEREBY ORDERED:**

A. Defendant shall serve complete, verified answers to Plaintiff's First Set of Interrogatories (Nos. 1–20) within **fourteen (14) days** of the date of this Order.

B. Defendant shall produce all documents responsive to Plaintiff's Requests for Production (Nos. 1–20) within **fourteen (14) days** of the date of this Order.

C. To the extent Defendant withholds any document on the basis of privilege, Defendant shall produce a **privilege log** within **fourteen (14) days** of the date of this Order, identifying each withheld document with sufficient specificity to permit assessment of the privilege claim, per MCR 2.302(C).

D. Defendant shall bear the reasonable expenses incurred by Plaintiff in bringing this Motion, in the amount of $_______, per MCR 2.313(A)(5)(a).

E. Failure to comply with this Order may result in further sanctions under MCR 2.313(B), including but not limited to: establishment of designated facts, prohibition of defenses, striking of pleadings, default judgment, and/or contempt of court.
