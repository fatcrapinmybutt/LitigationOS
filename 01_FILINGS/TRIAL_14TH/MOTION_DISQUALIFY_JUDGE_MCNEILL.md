## C. LEGAL STANDARD

### 1. MCR 2.003 — Disqualification of Judges

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides in pertinent part:

> **"A judge is disqualified when the judge, with respect to a party, a party's attorney, or the subject matter of a proceeding, has either:**
>
> **(a) a personal bias or prejudice concerning a party or a party's attorney, or**
>
> **(b) personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding."**

The standard for disqualification is whether the judge's conduct "would create in reasonable minds a perception of the court's lack of impartiality." *Crampton v Department of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975). *See also In re MCI Telecom Complaint*, 460 Mich 396, 411 (1999) (applying the reasonable person disqualification standard).

Disqualification is warranted where the record demonstrates "a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible." *Liteky v United States*, 510 US 540, 555 (1994).

Constitutional violations, including those arising from judicial conduct, are reviewed **de novo**. *Griffith v State*, 472 Mich 521; 697 NW2d 895 (2005). Court rule violations are reviewed for abuse of discretion. *Covenant Med. Ctr. v State*, 500 Mich 191; 895 NW2d 490 (2017).

### 2. MCR 2.003(D)(1) — Mandatory Referral to Chief Judge

MCR 2.003(D)(1) provides the mandatory procedure when a challenged judge declines to step aside:

> **"If the challenged judge denies the motion, the moving party may file a motion with the chief judge to have the challenged judge disqualified. The chief judge shall decide the motion de novo."**

This provision is mandatory, not discretionary. The challenged judge is **not** the final arbiter of her own disqualification. A denial by the challenged judge automatically triggers the right to de novo review by the Chief Judge.

### 3. Constitutional Due Process

The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Michigan Constitution Article 1, § 17 guarantee the right to due process of law, which includes the right to a fair tribunal.

In **Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 US 868, 881 (2009)**, the United States Supreme Court held:

> "The Due Process Clause has been implemented by objective standards that do not require proof of actual bias. . . . The question is 'whether, under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness, the interest poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden.'"

In **In re Murchison, 349 US 133, 136 (1955)**, the Court stated:

> **"A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process."**

### 4. Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct — Appearance of Impropriety Standard

- **Canon 1**: A judge shall uphold the integrity and independence of the judiciary.
- **Canon 2 / Rule 2.11**: A judge shall avoid impropriety **and the appearance of impropriety** in all activities; a judge shall disqualify himself or herself in a proceeding in which the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned.
- **Canon 3(A)(3)**: A judge shall be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others with whom the judge deals in an official capacity.
- **Canon 3(A)(4)**: A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications concerning a pending proceeding.
- **Canon 3(B)(2)**: A judge shall afford every person with a legal interest the right to be heard according to law.
- **Canon 3(C)**: A judge shall maintain order and decorum in proceedings (not used as a pretext to silence a party).

**The "Appearance of Impropriety" Standard (Canon 2 / MCJC Rule 2.2):**

The Canon 2 standard does not require proof of actual bias. It requires only that the judge's conduct create **an appearance** of partiality in the mind of a reasonable observer. *See In re Chrzanowski*, 465 Mich 468, 488 (2001) (applying appearance-of-impropriety standard); *In re Haley*, 476 Mich 180, 193 (2006) ("the test is whether the conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception of impropriety").

As the U.S. Supreme Court stated in *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868, 881 (2009): "The Due Process Clause has been implemented by **objective standards that do not require proof of actual bias.**" The inquiry is whether "under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness," the circumstances "pose such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden."

Here, the following facts, viewed through the lens of a reasonable observer, create not merely an "appearance" of impropriety but a **presumption** of it:

| Fact | Appearance Created |
|---|---|
| 44% ex parte order rate, all against Father | Systematic one-sided adjudication |
| Five ex parte orders in one day | Coordinated action, not deliberation |
| "I have represented Party A in the past" | Undisclosed prior relationship |
| Calling Father "a liar" and "crazy" on record | Personal hostility toward a party |
| "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." | Prejudgment of all future claims |
| Secretary communications re: PPO/jail status | Ex parte judicial-party communications |
| 27+ Mother violations, 0 enforcement | Selective favoritism |
| Court letter to HealthWest creating biased clinical finding | Court manufacturing evidence against Father |
| Rusco calling HealthWest to arrange testimony (10/29/2025) | Prohibited ex parte witness coordination |

A reasonable person aware of these facts could not conclude that Father is receiving impartial adjudication. Disqualification is required under Canon 2, under *Caperton*, and under the Due Process Clause.

*See also Liteky v United States*, 510 US 540, 555 (1994) ("opinions formed by the judge on the basis of facts introduced or events occurring in the course of the current proceedings . . . do not constitute a basis for a bias or partiality motion unless they display a **deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible**"). The record here establishes precisely such deep-seated favoritism.
