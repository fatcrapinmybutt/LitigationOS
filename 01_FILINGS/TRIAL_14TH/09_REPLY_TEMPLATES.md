# OPPOSITION REBUTTAL TEMPLATES — 14th Judicial Circuit — Case No. 2024-001507-DC

## Opposition Argument: Mootness — parenting time has been modified/restored
**Court:** ALL

### Our Response
The case is NOT moot. First, the 'capable of repetition yet evading review' exception applies — McNeill has repeatedly issued ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s, and each expires before full appellate review is possible. Friends of the Earth v Laidlaw, 528 US 167 (2000). Second, Father's claim for damages/remedy for the 567+ day separation is live regardless of current status. Third, collateral consequences (custody evaluation bias, bond requirement) persist. The voluntary cessation doctrine also applies — the court could reimpose restrictions at any time.

**Authority:** Friends of the Earth v Laidlaw, 528 US 167 (2000); City of Erie v Pap's AM, 529 US 277 (2000); In re Contempt of Calcutt, 184 Mich App 749 (1990)

**Evidence:** Docket showing repeated ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s; pattern of suspension-restoration-suspension cycle; ongoing bond requirement

## Opposition Argument: Waiver — Father failed to timely object/preserve issues
**Court:** COA

### Our Response
Waiver does not apply for three reasons: (1) Constitutional structural errors are exempt from waiver — denial of right to be heard at ex parte proceedings cannot be 'waived' because Father had no opportunity to object. You cannot waive rights you were denied the chance to exercise. (2) Motion for Reconsideration cured any preservation defects per MCR 2.119(F). (3) Plain error review under Carines remains available for unpreserved issues, and these errors are 'plain' — 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s violating MCR 3.207 is not a close call.

**Authority:** People v Carines, 460 Mich 750 (1999); Rivette v Rose-Molina, 278 Mich App 327 (2008); Walters v Nadell, 481 Mich 377 (2008)

**Evidence:** Docket showing Father was not served/notified of ex parte proceedings; Motion for Reconsideration on file; 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s entered without notice

## Opposition Argument: Adequate remedy at law — Father should exhaust lower court remedies
**Court:** MSC

### Our Response
There is NO adequate remedy at law. The 14th Circuit IS the problem — Judge McNeill has issued 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s, imposed an unconstitutional bond, muted Father at hearings, and self-ruled on her own disqualification. Requiring Father to seek relief from the very judge who has systematically denied his rights is futile and circular. Superintending control (MCR 7.304; Const. 1963, art. 6, Sec. 4) exists precisely for this situation — when a lower court persistently exceeds its authority. MCR 7.306 does not require exhaustion when the lower court has demonstrated inability or unwillingness to follow the law.

**Authority:** Const 1963, art 6, § 4; MCR 7.306; Judicial Attorneys Ass'n v State, 459 Mich 291 (1998); Cheney v Poole, 393 Mich 348 (1975)

**Evidence:** 654 McNeill inconsistent statements; 377 judicial violations documented; 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s; bond requirement; self-ruling on disqualification; muting at hearings

**Court:** COA

### Our Response
Father has standing as a direct party to Case No. 2024-001507-DC. He is the Plaintiff. His parenting time was suspended. His constitutional rights were violated. Standing requires injury-in-fact, causation, and redressability — all are met. The 567+ day separation is concrete injury; McNeill's orders caused it; reversal would remedy it. **Authority:** Lujan v Defenders of Wildlife, 504 US 555 (1992); Boddie v Connecticut, 401 US 371 (1971); MCR 7.204(A)

**Evidence:** Filed Complaint as Plaintiff; orders directly affecting Father's parenting time; Father's own children at issue

## Opposition Argument: Abuse of discretion is a high standard — trial court acted within bounds
**Court:** COA

### Our Response
Even under deferential abuse-of-discretion review, this case presents clear abuse. Abuse of discretion means a result 'so palpably and grossly violative of fact and logic that it evidences perversity of will or defiance of judgment.' Maldonado v Ford, 476 Mich 372 (2006). Severing ALL contact between parent and child for 567+ days without a best-interest hearing, based on ex parte proceedings, while imposing a $250 bond to prevent the parent from even filing motions — that is not the exercise of discretion. That is its antithesis. No reasonable judge would reach this result following proper procedures.

**Authority:** Maldonado v Ford, 476 Mich 372, 388 (2006); Berger v Berger, 277 Mich App 700 (2008); MCL 722.28

**Evidence:** 567+ day separation count; zero BIF hearing; 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s; bond requirement; muting at hearings

## Opposition Argument: Child safety concerns justified the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s
**Court:** ALL

### Our Response
Even if safety concerns existed (they are unsubstantiated), ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s require: (1) verified allegations under MCR 3.207(C)(1), (2) notice within 24 hours under MCR 3.207(C)(2), (3) hearing within 14 days, and (4) specific factual findings. NONE of these requirements were met for any of the 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s. Emily Watson's allegations were unverified, unsworn, and often filed by a non-attorney (Emily Watson's mother, raising UPL concerns under MCR 8.120). The procedure matters — due process is not suspended because one party makes unverified allegations. Stanley v Illinois, 405 US 645 (1972).

**Authority:** MCR 3.207(C)(1)-(2); Stanley v Illinois, 405 US 645 (1972); Fuentes v Shevin, 407 US 67 (1972); MRE 602, 801, 901

**Evidence:** 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s without verification; Emily Watson's unsworn filings; Emily Watson (non-attorney) filing legal documents; no findings of fact in orders
