# JURISDICTION & DUE PROCESS DEFECT MEMORANDUM
## Classification of Void Ex Parte Orders

---

## I. PURPOSE

This memorandum classifies each ex parte order challenged in Plaintiff-Father's Motion to Set Aside Void Ex Parte Orders by specific defect type, constitutional provision violated, and court rule breached. Each order is analyzed under the void ab initio doctrine.

**Father has been separated from his son for 571+ days** since these void orders were entered.

---

## II. VOID AB INITIO DOCTRINE

A void order is a legal nullity — "as though it never existed." *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 107-108 (2009). An order is void — not merely voidable — when entered:

1. Without subject matter or personal jurisdiction; or
2. In violation of constitutional due process of law.

A void judgment confers no rights, imposes no duties, and affords no protection. It may be attacked at any time; there is **no time limit** under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d). *Id.*; MCR 2.612(C)(2).

#### Due Process Denied:

| Defect Type | Description | Constitutional/Rule Authority Violated |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **No Notice** | Father received no prior notice of the motion or hearing. | Const 1963, Art 1, § 17; US Const Amend XIV; MCR 2.107 |
| **No Hearing** | No evidentiary hearing was conducted before or promptly after entry. Father had no opportunity to present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, or contest allegations. | Const 1963, Art 1, § 17; US Const Amend V, XIV; *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545 (1965) |

| **Hearsay Basis** | Order based entirely on inadmissible hearsay allegations in an unsworn motion. No admissible evidence supported the deprivation. | MRE 801, 802; MCR 3.207(B)(1); MCR 1.109(D)(3) |
| **No Sworn Evidence** | Motion was not verified under MCR 1.109(D)(3). No affidavit accompanied filing. | MCR 1.109(D)(3); MCR 3.207(B)(1) |
| **No Findings of Fact** | Checkbox form — no specific, individualized findings explaining factual basis. | MCR 2.517(A)(1) |
| **No Best Interest Analysis** | None of the 12 statutory best interest factors (MCL 722.23) were analyzed. | MCL 722.23 |
| **Disproportionate Remedy** | Most extreme remedy imposed without considering less restrictive alternatives (supervised parenting time, therapeutic visitation). | *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982); *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) |

**Void Ab Initio Determination:** This order is VOID — not voidable. Multiple structural due process failures render it a legal nullity from the moment of entry. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d).

## IV. PATTERN ANALYSIS — SYSTEMIC DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS

The void orders are not isolated incidents. They reflect a **systematic pattern** of entering orders affecting Father's fundamental parental rights without constitutionally adequate process:

| Pattern Element | Evidence |
|-----------------|----------|
| **Orders entered without Father present** | Both the July 1, 2024 and August 8, 2025 orders were entered ex parte — Father was not present and had no opportunity to participate. |
| **Orders entered without notice** | Father received no prior notice of either motion or hearing. The case docket reflects **105 documented violations of MCR 2.107** service requirements. |
| **Orders based on unsworn allegations** | Both orders were entered on unsworn, unverified motions. No affidavits. No sworn testimony. Only inadmissible hearsay. |
| **Checkbox forms instead of findings** | Both orders were entered on pre-printed checkbox forms without specific, individualized findings of fact as required by MCR 2.517(A)(1). |
| **No adversarial testing** | In neither instance was Father afforded the opportunity to cross-examine witnesses, present contrary evidence, or challenge the factual basis for the deprivation. |
| **Exculpatory evidence ignored** | Father's negative drug screen — evidence directly contradicting the unsworn allegations — was neither considered nor addressed. |
| **Most extreme remedy imposed** | Total elimination of parenting time — functionally equivalent to termination of parental rights — was imposed without consideration of less restrictive alternatives. |

This pattern establishes that the due process violations were **structural and recurring**, not incidental.

## V. CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS VIOLATED

### A. Michigan Constitution of 1963, Article 1, § 17

> "No person shall be ... deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law."

Father's fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his child was deprived without due process. The Michigan Due Process Clause provides protections "at least as extensive" as those of the federal Constitution. *In re Carmody*, 475 Mich 1088 (2006).

### B. United States Constitution, Amendment V

> "No person shall be ... deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."

### C. United States Constitution, Amendment XIV, § 1

> "No State shall ... deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."

A parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). This fundamental right cannot be eliminated through ex parte proceedings based on unsworn allegations without notice or hearing. *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 552 (1965).

## VII. CONCLUSION

Both identified ex parte orders are **void ab initio** under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d). They were entered in violation of Father's fundamental due process rights under Const 1963, Art 1, § 17 and US Const Amend V and XIV. The pattern of proceeding without notice, without hearing, without sworn evidence, and without Father present — while ignoring exculpatory evidence — constitutes a systematic deprivation of constitutional rights.

Father has been separated from his son for **571+ days** as a direct result of these void orders. No valid legal basis supports this separation.
