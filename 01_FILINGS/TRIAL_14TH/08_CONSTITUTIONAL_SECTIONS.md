## Due Process — Procedural (14th Amendment)

### Issue
Whether Judge McNeill violated Father's procedural due process rights by entering 24 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s, suspending parenting time without notice or hearing, and denying Father the opportunity to be heard.

### Rule
The Fourteenth Amendment provides: "nor shall any State deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1. Procedural due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before deprivation of a protected interest. Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). A parent's right to the care, custody, and control of their children is a fundamental liberty interest. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982). Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). Michigan law requires that ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s in custody cases meet strict requirements under MCR 3.207(C)(2), including specific findings of irreparable injury.

## Due Process — Substantive (14th Amendment)

### Issue
Whether the 567+ day separation of Father from his child, without a best-interest-factor hearing or change-of-circumstances finding, violates substantive due process.

### Rule
Substantive due process protects fundamental rights from government interference regardless of procedural protections. Washington v. Glucksberg, 521 U.S. 702, 720 (1997). The parent-child relationship is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982)." Troxel, 530 U.S. at 65. Any state action that effectively terminates parental rights must satisfy strict scrutiny. Santosky v. Kramer, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982). Under Michigan law, modification of custody requires a showing of proper cause or change of circumstances (*Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499; 675 NW2d 847 (2003)) AND a best-interest analysis under MCL 722.23(a)-(l). Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer, 259 Mich. App. 499 (2003).

## Right to Fair and Impartial Tribunal (14th Amendment)

### Issue

### Rule

When a party files a motion to disqualify, the Chief Judge — not the challenged judge — must decide the motion. MCR 2.003(D). Self-ruling on one's own disqualification violates both MCR 2.003(D) and due process. The appearance of impropriety is sufficient grounds. Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009).
