                  II. CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK

A. Substantive Due Process — Fundamental Parental Rights

    The United States Supreme Court has unequivocally held that
parental rights are among the most fundamental of all liberty
interests:

    "The interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of
    their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental
    liberty interests recognized by this Court."
    Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

    "The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the
    care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate
    simply because they have not been model parents or have lost
    temporary custody of their child to the State."
    Santosky v. Kramer, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

    "A parent's interest in the companionship, care, custody, and
    management of his or her children rises to a constitutionally
    protected level."
    Stanley v. Illinois, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972).

    The Court traced this principle through over a century of
precedent: Meyer v. Nebraska, 262 U.S. 390 (1923); Pierce v.
Society of Sisters, 268 U.S. 510 (1925); Wisconsin v. Yoder,
406 U.S. 205 (1972). These rights demand the highest procedural
protections.

    A 571+ day deprivation of all parenting time, based on ex parte
orders entered without notice or hearing, and in the face of
overwhelming exculpatory evidence, cannot withstand constitutional
scrutiny under any standard of review.

B. Procedural Due Process — Right to Notice and Hearing

    Before the government may deprive a person of a fundamental
right, procedural due process requires at minimum: (1) adequate
notice; (2) an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in
a meaningful manner; and (3) a decision by a neutral and detached
adjudicator. Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).

    Plaintiff's parenting time was suspended via ex parte orders
entered without notice or hearing, in direct violation of these
constitutional minima. The absence of these protections renders the
deprivation constitutionally infirm.

C. Equal Protection

    The Equal Protection Clause requires that similarly situated
individuals be treated alike. The docket in this case reveals a
stark pattern: an 18.26% ex parte rate, with 100% of ex parte
orders favoring Defendant/Mother and 0% favoring Plaintiff/Father.

    This pattern raises serious equal protection concerns and
suggests gender-based discrimination in violation of Craig v. Boren,
429 U.S. 190 (1976), and its progeny. At minimum, this Court should
scrutinize any further deprivation of Plaintiff's parental rights
with heightened attention to procedural fairness.


             III. MICHIGAN STATUTORY PRESUMPTION

A. MCL 722.27a — Parenting Time Presumption

    MCL 722.27a(1) provides: "Parenting time shall be granted in
accordance with the best interests of the child."

    MCL 722.27a(3) provides: "A child has a right to parenting
time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and
convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical,
mental, or emotional health."

    This presumption has not been rebutted. To the contrary, every
objective measure clears Plaintiff:

    - Drug screen: NEGATIVE
    - HealthWest evaluation: ALL ZEROS (initial finding)
    - Multiple police investigations: ZERO findings
    - Criminal charges: NONE filed
    - No substantiated child welfare findings against Plaintiff

    No clear and convincing evidence — or any credible evidence at
all — has been presented establishing that parenting time with
Plaintiff would endanger L.D.W.

B. MCR 3.207 — Parenting Time Procedures

    MCR 3.207(B) requires that when parenting time is contested,
the court shall consider the factors in MCL 722.27a(7). The rule
further requires that any modification or restriction of parenting
time follow established procedures, including proper notice,
hearing, and findings on the record.

    These procedures were not followed. No proper hearing was held.
No evidence-based findings were entered. No statutory factors were
considered on the record.

C. Shade v. Wright — Modification Standard

    In Shade v. Wright, 291 Mich App 17 (2010), the Court of
Appeals held that the standard for modifying parenting time is less
stringent than for custody modification. Proper cause or change of
circumstances must be shown, but the threshold is lower. Where a
parent demonstrates systematic interference with parenting time,
modification is warranted.

    Here, 571+ days of total deprivation, combined with
overwhelming exculpatory evidence and a documented pattern of
alienation, far exceeds the threshold for modification.

             VIII. CONTEMPT AUTHORITY — MCL 552.644

    MCL 552.644(2) provides a comprehensive remedial framework for
parenting time violations. When the court finds that a parent has
violated a parenting time order without good cause, it may impose
any combination of the following:

    - Additional terms and conditions (MCL 552.644(2)(a))
    - Modification of the parenting time order (MCL 552.644(2)(b))
    - Makeup parenting time (MCL 552.644(2)(c))
    - Fine up to $100 per incident (MCL 552.644(2)(d))
    - Commitment to county jail (MCL 552.644(2)(e))
    - Community corrections (MCL 552.644(2)(h))
    - Supervision with conditions (MCL 552.644(2)(i))

    Where a finding of bad faith is made, MCL 552.644(6) MANDATES
sanctions and MCL 552.644(8) MANDATES costs. Defendant's pattern of
interference plainly constitutes bad faith.

    The Court should also note that MCL 552.645 provides for
license suspension upon noncompliance with makeup parenting time
orders, and MCL 552.644(7) provides that fines and sanctions
constitute judgments upon entry.
