# PART I: INTRODUCTION AND RELIEF SOUGHT

---

**NOW COMES** Plaintiff, Andrew James Pigors, appearing *in propria persona*, and respectfully moves this Honorable Court, pursuant to MCL 722.24, MCR 3.916, and MCL 722.27(1)(c), for entry of an Order appointing a qualified **Guardian Ad Litem ("GAL")** for the minor child, **L.D.W.**, to conduct an independent investigation of the child's best interests under MCL 722.23.

This Motion is brought because the child's best interests are not adequately protected by either party in this highly contested custody matter. The minor child has been completely separated from Plaintiff-Father for more than **226 consecutive days** without any independent, neutral investigation into the child's welfare, the veracity of the allegations that precipitated that separation, or the impact of prolonged paternal absence on the child's development. No person in these proceedings currently owes a duty of loyalty to the child. A GAL is urgently needed to serve as the child's independent advocate and to provide this Court with a neutral, professionally investigated assessment of the child's best interests.

# PART III: LEGAL STANDARD

---

## A. MCL 722.24 — Appointment of Guardian Ad Litem

15. MCL 722.24(1) provides:

    > "If, at any time in the proceeding, the court determines that the child's best interests are not adequately protected by the parties, the court shall appoint a guardian ad litem for the child."

16. The Legislature's use of the mandatory **"shall"** — rather than the permissive "may" — indicates that appointment is **required** once the court determines that the child's best interests are not adequately protected. This is not a matter of judicial discretion; it is a statutory mandate.

17. MCL 722.24(2) further specifies that a guardian ad litem shall be an attorney admitted to practice in Michigan with the authority and duty to advocate for the child's best interests and to make recommendations regarding custody, parenting time, and other matters relating to the child.

## B. MCR 3.916 — Guardian Ad Litem Duties and Appointment

18. MCR 3.916(A) provides that the court may appoint a lawyer-guardian ad litem for a child in a custody dispute. Under MCR 3.916, the GAL:

    a. Has the authority and duty to advocate for the child's best interests;

    b. May file pleadings, present evidence, examine witnesses, and make arguments on the child's behalf;

    c. Shall conduct an independent investigation;

    d. Shall file a written report with recommendations at least seven (7) days before any hearing at which custody or parenting time is at issue.

## C. MCL 722.23 — Best Interest Factors

19. The GAL's investigation should encompass all twelve (12) best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23:

    a. The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties and the child;

    b. The capacity and disposition of the parties to give the child love, affection, and guidance;

    c. The capacity and disposition of the parties to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care, and other material needs;

    d. The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity;

    e. The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home;

    f. The moral fitness of the parties;

    g. The mental and physical health of the parties involved;

    h. The home, school, and community record of the child;

    i. The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference;

    j. The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent;

    k. Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against the child;

    l. Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular custody dispute.

20. Given the complexity of this case and the number of contested factual issues bearing on each of these factors, an independent GAL investigation is essential to providing the Court with reliable information.

## D. MCL 722.27(1)(c) — Court's Authority

21. MCL 722.27(1)(c) further authorizes the court to utilize any reasonable and appropriate remedy to protect the child's best interests, including the appointment of a guardian ad litem. This broad remedial authority underscores the Court's power to appoint a GAL at any stage of the proceedings.

# PART IV: ARGUMENT

---

## A. The Child's Best Interests Are Not Adequately Protected

22. No person in these proceedings currently owes a duty of loyalty to the minor child, L.D.W.:

    a. **Plaintiff-Father** appears *in propria persona*. While Plaintiff acts in what he believes are the child's best interests, he is a party to the litigation, not a neutral advocate for the child, and lacks legal training.

    b. **Defendant-Mother** is a party whose litigation interests may or may not align with the child's best interests. Defendant's prior attorney, Jennifer Barnes (P55406), has withdrawn from representation. Even when represented, Defendant's counsel owed a duty of loyalty to Defendant — not to L.D.W.

    c. **The Friend of the Court** (FOC, Pamela Rusco) provides recommendations to the Court but does not serve as the child's independent legal advocate with authority to file pleadings, examine witnesses, and present evidence on the child's behalf.

    d. **No one represents L.D.W.** The child has no voice in proceedings that have resulted in 226+ days of complete separation from his father.

23. This is precisely the circumstance contemplated by MCL 722.24(1), which mandates GAL appointment when "the child's best interests are not adequately protected by the parties."

## B. The Structural Judicial Conflict Demands Independent Oversight

24. The Berry-McNeill structural conflict — the familial connection between the presiding judge and a person residing in the child's home — creates a heightened need for an independent fact-finder whose credibility and neutrality cannot be questioned on the basis of any actual or perceived bias.

25. A GAL appointed from outside the existing relationships in this case provides essential structural integrity. The GAL's investigation and recommendations will be free from any taint of bias, providing the Court (whether Judge McNeill or any successor judge) with a reliable foundation for custody and parenting time decisions.

26. This is particularly important given the pending disqualification motion. Whether the case proceeds before Judge McNeill or is reassigned, a GAL's independent findings will serve the child's interests and judicial efficiency regardless of which judge ultimately presides.

## C. Seven Unsubstantiated Allegations Require Independent Verification

27. The pattern of seven unsubstantiated allegations detailed in Part II, Section B above demonstrates a contested factual landscape that cannot be reliably resolved without independent investigation. Each allegation, standing alone, is serious. Collectively, the pattern raises significant questions about whether these allegations serve the child's interests or serve as litigation tools.

28. A GAL has the investigative authority to:

    a. Interview Defendant-Mother about the factual basis for each allegation;

    b. Review police reports, CPS records, and medical records independently;

    c. Assess whether the allegations are supported by evidence or constitute a pattern of false reporting;

    d. Advise the Court on the credibility of the allegations and their relevance to custody and parenting time.

## D. 226+ Days of Separation Without Investigation Is Unconscionable

29. L.D.W. has been completely separated from his father for more than 226 consecutive days. During this period:

    a. No independent investigation has been conducted into the child's welfare;

    b. No neutral professional has assessed the impact of complete paternal absence on the child's development;

    c. No one has independently verified whether the conditions that allegedly warranted separation actually exist;

    d. The child's developmental needs — including attachment formation, language development, and identity development — have gone unmonitored by any neutral party.

30. The American Psychological Association recognizes that both parents contribute uniquely to child development, and that denial of access to a parent causes measurable harm. For a child of L.D.W.'s age, 226 days represents a substantial fraction of the child's entire life and encompasses critical developmental milestones.

31. A GAL can assess the current impact of this separation on L.D.W. and make evidence-based recommendations for the child's best interests going forward.

## E. Parental Alienation Requires Specialized Assessment

32. The 106 documented incidents of alienation-consistent conduct require professional assessment by a neutral investigator. Parental alienation is a recognized concern in Michigan custody law, and factor (j) of the best interest analysis — the willingness and ability of each party to facilitate a close and continuing parent-child relationship — directly addresses this issue.

33. A GAL can observe the child's interactions with each parent, interview household members, review the documented incidents, and provide the Court with a professional assessment of whether alienation is occurring and what remedial measures, if any, are appropriate.

## F. Ex Parte Orders Without Hearing Demand Independent Corrective

34. The ex parte orders that initiated the 226+ day separation were entered without an evidentiary hearing and without affording Plaintiff-Father the opportunity to be heard. When custody determinations are made on one-sided information, a GAL provides essential corrective oversight by conducting the independent investigation that the ex parte process, by its nature, cannot provide.

35. A GAL's investigation ensures that future orders are based on complete, independently verified information rather than untested allegations.

## G. The Pro Se Imbalance Further Supports Appointment

36. Plaintiff is a *pro se* litigant without legal training. While the Court has an obligation to apply the same rules to all parties, the practical reality is that a self-represented father is at a significant disadvantage in formally presenting evidence bearing on the child's needs. A GAL compensates for this structural inequality by conducting an independent investigation focused on the child's interests — not on either party's litigation strategy.
