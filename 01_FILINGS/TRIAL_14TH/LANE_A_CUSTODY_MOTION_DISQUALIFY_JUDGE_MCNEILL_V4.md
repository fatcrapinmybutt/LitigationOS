# PART I: MOTION

**NOW COMES** Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors, appearing *pro se*, and respectfully moves for the disqualification of the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill from all further proceedings in this matter pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b), and in support thereof states as follows:

## A. Grounds for Disqualification

1. MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge:

   > **(a)** Is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney;
   >
   > **(b)** Has personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding.

2. Plaintiff asserts both grounds (a) and (b) based on the pattern of conduct documented below.

## B. Pattern of Actual Bias — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)

### (i) Ex Parte Handling of Evidence in Violation of Canon 3(A)(4)

3. On multiple occasions, Judge McNeill received and considered evidence **off the record and ex parte**, including but not limited to a USB recording and mental health evaluation materials, in violation of Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which bars ex parte communications on the merits of pending matters except in narrow circumstances inapplicable here.

4. The judicial record contains evidence of ex parte submissions accepted and considered by the court without notice to Father and without the opportunity for Father to examine, challenge, or rebut the evidence. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) is triggered whenever a judge obtains personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts outside the courtroom proceeding.

### (ii) Entry of Ex Parte Orders Without Required Procedural Safeguards

5. On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill signed **five (5) separate ex parte orders** on a single day, all without prior notice to Father, based on an **unsworn, unverified** motion by Defendant. The orders resulted in the **complete elimination** of Father's parenting time — the most extreme remedy available short of termination of parental rights.

6. The motion did not comply with MCR 3.207(B)(1) (no verification under oath), the orders did not comply with MCR 2.517(A)(1) (no specific findings of fact), and the court did not comply with MCR 3.206(B)(6) (no timely post-deprivation hearing scheduled).

7. A reasonable, objective person would question the impartiality of a judge who signs five ex parte orders on a single day based on an unsworn motion, imposes the most extreme sanction available, and then fails to schedule a timely hearing — all while one party has had no opportunity to be heard.

### (iii) Refusal to Consider Exculpatory Evidence

8. At hearing, Father attempted to present **photographs** showing L.D.W. happy and healthy during parenting time exchanges. Judge McNeill **refused to review** this evidence, dismissing it as unnecessary.

9. Father also attempted to present his **NEGATIVE narcotics screening** — directly rebutting Mother's hearsay allegation of "meth use." A neutral judge would, at minimum, consider exculpatory evidence before making the extreme determination to suspend all parenting time. Refusal to consider exculpatory evidence that directly contradicts the sole basis for the challenged orders demonstrates prejudgment.

### (iv) Characterization of Legal Advocacy as Harassment

10. On the record, Judge McNeill stated words to the effect: *"The court finds that this continued pattern of questioning is meant to harass the defendant."*

11. Father's "continued pattern of questioning" was the exercise of his **right to cross-examine adverse witnesses** — a fundamental component of due process guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment and Const 1963, art 1, § 17. Characterizing a pro se litigant's exercise of cross-examination rights as "harassment" reveals a predisposition against Father's right to present his case and tests credibility.

12. *See Lombardo v. Lombardo*, 202 Mich App 151, 159; 507 NW2d 788 (1993) ("A judge's bias or prejudice may be shown by the judge's conduct during proceedings.").

### (v) Systematic One-Sided Credibility Determinations

13. The pattern across the entire case demonstrates systematic crediting of Mother's claims while dismissing Father's evidence:

| Mother's Submission | Court Action | Father's Submission | Court Action |
|-------------------|--------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Unsworn hearsay allegations | Accepted, ex parte orders signed | NEGATIVE narcotics screen | Refused to consider |
| Anonymous "friend" concerns | Credited as basis for orders | Photographs of healthy child | Refused to review |
| Unverified "emergency" motion | Five orders signed same day | Motion to Restore Parenting Time | No hearing scheduled for 329+ days |
| Claims of danger | Taken at face value | Detailed written objections | Characterized as "frivolous" |

14. This is not a case of close calls or balanced weighing of evidence. It is a pattern of predetermined outcomes in which Mother's allegations are accepted without scrutiny and Father's rebuttals are dismissed without consideration. A reasonable, objective person would conclude that the outcome was decided before the evidence was weighed.

### (vi) Facilitation of Parental Alienation

15. Judge McNeill's conduct has directly facilitated a **parental alienation** campaign by Defendant Emily Watson — who has been employed by the **Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division**, as a caseworker for nine (9) years. MCL 722.23(j) requires courts to evaluate each parent's willingness to "facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004). Rather than enforcing MCL 722.27a(9)'s **mandatory** remedies for Defendant's documented withholding of parenting time beginning March 26, 2024, Judge McNeill rewarded the alienation by suspending Father's parenting time entirely. After **329+ days** of total parent-child separation, the court's failure to act constitutes judicial facilitation of parental alienation — conduct that undermines the appearance of impartiality required by Canon 2.

### (vii) Quantified Pattern from the Record

16. Analysis of the case record documented in the litigation database reveals:

   - **6,512 court impeachment items** documenting judicial conduct patterns inconsistent with neutrality (court speaker entries showing ex parte handling, unsupported findings, procedural shortcutting)
   - **32 evidence entries** classified as DUE_PROCESS_VIOLATION, 20 attributed to COURT action
   - **15 entries** classified as IMPEACHMENT_MATERIAL attributed to COURT
   - Multiple documented instances of CANON 3(A)(4) violation indicators
   - Benchbook violation entries documenting failures to follow required procedures

## C. Personal Knowledge of Disputed Facts — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)

16. By receiving and considering evidence off the record and ex parte — including the USB recording and mental health evaluation — Judge McNeill acquired **personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts** outside the trial or hearing process. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification when a judge has such knowledge.

17. This personal knowledge cannot be "unlearned." Once a judge has personally reviewed disputed evidence ex parte, the judge's ability to make impartial credibility determinations and factual findings is irremediably compromised.

## D. Legal Standard for Disqualification

18. The test for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) is whether a reasonable, objective person, knowing the facts, would have a reasonable basis to question the judge's impartiality. *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597; 640 NW2d 321 (2001).

19. The Michigan Judicial Tenure Commission Standards provide additional guidance. Canon 2 requires judges to "avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety." Canon 3(A)(4) bars ex parte communications. Canon 3(A)(5) requires that judges "accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding . . . the right to be heard according to law."

20. Each incident documented above independently raises a reasonable question about impartiality. Cumulatively, they compel disqualification.

# PART I-A: ANTICIPATED DEFENSE & REBUTTAL (CHESS-MODE)

> **Strategic Framework:** Disqualification motions face the highest resistance. Judge McNeill herself must rule on the motion initially (MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a)), creating an inherent structural bias. Barnes (P55406) will argue the motion is meritless to preserve the favorable status quo. Each defense below is pre-rebutted with pinpoint authority from the adversary_models database and Michigan Court Rules.

---

### Defense 1: "Motion is Untimely — Should Have Been Filed Earlier"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** Father has participated in proceedings before Judge McNeill for over a year without objection. The disqualification motion is untimely and constitutes waiver of the right to seek recusal. Barnes will cite the delay between the earliest complained-of conduct and this motion.

**This argument fails because:** The adversary_models database specifically identifies this attack (attack_type: TIMELINESS) and provides the authoritative rebuttal: "Pattern of bias only became clear through cumulative ex parte violations. MCR 2.003(D)(1) allows motion 'as soon as the ground becomes known.'" *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996). The individual incidents — ex parte evidence handling, refusal to consider exculpatory evidence, characterization of cross-examination as harassment — formed a recognizable pattern only as they accumulated. A pro se litigant cannot be expected to identify judicial bias from a single ruling; the pattern became actionable only when the cumulative weight of 329+ days of separation, 6,512 court impeachment items, and systematic one-sided credibility determinations coalesced into an unmistakable showing. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656 (2009).

**Record evidence refuting defense:** The timeline of escalating conduct (individual incidents spanning months); Father's pro se status and limited knowledge of disqualification procedures; the adversary_models database documenting that the "pattern only became clear through cumulative ex parte violations."

---

### Defense 2: "Adverse Rulings Alone Are Insufficient to Show Bias"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** Father's motion is merely a dissatisfied litigant who disagrees with unfavorable rulings. Adverse rulings, standing alone, do not demonstrate bias or prejudice sufficient for disqualification. *People v Stevens*, 498 Mich 162 (2015).

**This argument fails because:** The adversary_models database specifically addresses this attack (attack_type: INSUFFICIENT_SHOWING) and provides the rebuttal: "24 ex parte orders (44% rate), 66 documented ex parte communications, denial of hearings — this exceeds 'adverse rulings' and constitutes structural bias." *People v Stevens*, 498 Mich 162; *Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001). This is not about disagreement with outcomes — it is about documented procedural violations: (a) receiving evidence ex parte in violation of Canon 3(A)(4); (b) signing five ex parte orders on a single day based on an unsworn motion; (c) refusing to consider exculpatory evidence (NEGATIVE narcotics screen, photographs); (d) characterizing constitutionally protected cross-examination as "harassment"; (e) failing to schedule a hearing for 329+ days in violation of MCR 3.206(B)(6). These are systemic procedural failures, not close calls on the merits.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** 6,512 court impeachment items; 32 evidence entries classified as DUE_PROCESS_VIOLATION (20 attributed to COURT); the unsworn ex parte motion accepted without scrutiny; the NEGATIVE narcotics screen refused; the 329+ day hearing delay.

---

### Defense 3: "Waiver by Continued Participation in Proceedings"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** By continuing to file motions, attend hearings, and participate in proceedings before Judge McNeill, Father waived his right to seek disqualification. He should have raised the issue at the first sign of bias.

**This argument fails because:** The adversary_models database addresses this attack (attack_type: WAIVER) and provides the rebuttal: "Self-represented litigant was unaware of right to disqualification. MCR 2.003 does not require pre-emptive withdrawal. Litigant raised concerns at earliest opportunity upon understanding the pattern." *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656 (2009). A pro se litigant is held to the same legal standards as an attorney but is not presumed to have the same knowledge of procedural mechanisms. MCR 2.003(D) does not impose a waiver penalty for participating in proceedings — participation is required; absence would result in default. The alternative Barnes proposes — that Father should have stopped attending hearings — would have resulted in ex parte proceedings and default judgment, compounding the harm.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** Father's pro se status throughout; no prior legal training or experience with disqualification procedures; Father's consistent attendance at every hearing (demonstrating diligence, not waiver); MCR 2.003(D)(1) tolling the filing deadline to when "the ground becomes known."

---

### Defense 4: "The Court Was Exercising Legitimate Judicial Discretion"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** Every action complained of — the ex parte orders, evidence rulings, courtroom management — falls within the judge's legitimate discretion. Judges have broad discretion in family court matters, and Father's disagreement with those decisions does not constitute bias.

**This argument fails because:** The adversary_models database addresses this attack (attack_type: JUDICIAL_DISCRETION) and provides the rebuttal: "24 ex parte orders (44% rate across 55 hearings) cannot be characterized as 'discretion.' 101 CRITICAL impeachment items, 66 ex parte communication violations, and 20 transcript-order contradictions demonstrate persistent pattern." MCR 9.104; *In re Haley*, 476 Mich 180 (2006). Judicial discretion does not encompass: (a) receiving evidence ex parte in violation of Canon 3(A)(4); (b) accepting an unsworn motion as a "verified pleading" under MCR 3.207(B)(1); (c) refusing to consider directly exculpatory evidence (NEGATIVE narcotics screen); (d) failing to comply with mandatory MCR 3.206(B)(6) hearing requirements for 329+ days. These are not discretionary judgment calls — they are violations of mandatory procedural rules.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** MCR 3.207(B)(1) (mandatory verification requirement — violated); MCR 3.206(B)(6) (mandatory hearing requirement — violated for 329+ days); MCR 2.517(A)(1) (mandatory findings requirement — violated); Canon 3(A)(4) (mandatory prohibition on ex parte communications — violated).

---

### Defense 5: "Personal Knowledge Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) Applies Only to Extra-Judicial Knowledge"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) disqualification for "personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts" applies only to knowledge acquired outside the judicial proceedings, not to information received during the case. Barnes will argue the judge simply reviewed filings and evidence in the normal course.

**This argument fails because:** The ex parte review of the USB recording and mental health evaluation occurred **outside** of any hearing at which Father was present — this is the very definition of extra-judicial knowledge acquisition. Canon 3(A)(4) bars ex parte communications precisely because they give the judge knowledge of facts that the adversary has not had the opportunity to challenge. Once Judge McNeill reviewed disputed evidence ex parte, she acquired "personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts" within the meaning of MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). This knowledge cannot be "unlearned" and irremediably compromises the judge's ability to make impartial factual findings. *See Armstrong v Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App at 597 (the test is whether "a reasonable, objective person" would question impartiality).

**Record evidence refuting defense:** The documented ex parte receipt of the USB recording; the mental health evaluation reviewed without Father's presence; 66 documented ex parte communication violations in the case record; Canon 3(A)(4) prohibiting the very conduct that occurred.

---

### Defense 6: "If Denied, Father Has No Immediate Appellate Remedy"

**Watson/Barnes may argue:** Even if the motion is denied, Father has no immediate right to appeal and must wait until final judgment to challenge the denial — effectively trapping him before the same judge.

**This argument fails because:** MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a) specifically provides that if the challenged judge denies the motion, the judge "shall forward the motion and supporting affidavit to the chief judge, who shall decide the motion de novo." This is an automatic escalation mechanism — not discretionary. If the Chief Judge also denies, Father may seek leave to appeal under MCR 7.203(B). Additionally, the denial itself becomes part of the appellate record in COA 366810, preserving the issue for review. The existence of this escalation mechanism is itself evidence that the rules anticipate judicial resistance to disqualification — and provide a remedy for it.

**Record evidence refuting defense:** MCR 2.003(D)(3)(a) (mandatory forwarding to Chief Judge upon denial); the pending appeal COA 366810 (preserving all issues for appellate review); the comprehensive record documenting the factual basis for disqualification.
