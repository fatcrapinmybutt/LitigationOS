## I. INTRODUCTION

This motion seeks emergency ex parte relief and restoration of parenting time that has been wrongfully suspended for a period exceeding one hundred ninety-two (192) days without legal justification. The plaintiff, the father of minor child L.D.W. (age 5), requests immediate restoration of his constitutionally protected parenting rights and court-ordered parenting time schedule. This matter presents a clear case of parental alienation combined with violation of statutory requirements for suspension of parenting time under Michigan law.

## III. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. Emergency Relief Standards Under MCR 3.207(B)(4)

MCR 3.207(B)(4) permits the Court to grant emergency ex parte relief without notice to the opposing party when:

1. There is reasonable likelihood of success on the merits;
2. Irreparable injury will result if relief is not granted immediately; and
3. The injury outweighs any injury to the party against whom relief is sought.

This motion satisfies all three criteria.

**Likelihood of Success:** Plaintiff has a clear statutory right to parenting time absent good cause and clear and convincing evidence of endangerment. *MCL 722.27a(8)*. No such evidence exists. The prior court order awarded 50/50 parenting time, and absent modification by proper court process, that order remains in effect.

**Irreparable Injury:** The deprivation of parenting time causes irreparable harm to both the parent-child relationship and to the child's psychological and emotional well-being. Each day of suspended parenting time cannot be recovered. The child has already lost six and one-half months of meaningful contact with his father. Continued suspension will cause ongoing psychological harm to the child and permanent damage to the parent-child relationship.

**Balance of Interests:** The child's best interests clearly favor restoration of parenting time with both parents. The statutory framework strongly favors substantial parenting contact with both parents. *MCL 722.23(h)*. The court's paramount concern is the best interests of the child, and deprivation of contact with a fit, willing parent is antithetical to those interests.

### B. MCL 722.27a(8) - Mandatory Relief for Parenting Time Violations

MCL 722.27a(8) is a mandatory statutory provision that states:

> *"The court shall not suspend or eliminate the contact between a child and the parent except for good cause shown by clear and convincing evidence."*

The statute is unambiguous and contains no exceptions for emergency situations, parental complaints, or unsubstantiated allegations.

**Application to this case:**

1. **No Good Cause:** Defendant has offered only conclusory allegations without factual support. Parental discomfort, disagreement over child-rearing decisions, or an ex parte request are not "good cause" for suspension.

2. **No Clear and Convincing Evidence:** The highest evidentiary standard short of criminal proof has not been met. Defendant has not provided an affidavit, not testified under oath, and has not presented any credible evidence of endangerment.

3. **Violation of Due Process:** Plaintiff was denied a hearing, an opportunity to be heard, presentation of evidence, and confrontation of accusations—all required by due process under the Fourteenth Amendment.

### C. MCL 722.27(1)(c) - Modification Standards

Even if the Court were to consider this a modification (rather than a restoration of an illegally suspended order), the statutory standard under MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires:

> *"A change of circumstances so substantial and continuing as to make the terms unconscionable."*

No such showing has been made. The circumstances surrounding Plaintiff and the child have not materially changed. Defendant's subjective dissatisfaction does not constitute changed circumstances.

### D. Best Interests of the Child Standard - MCL 722.23

The Court must consider the best interests of the child under MCL 722.23. The statutory factors include:

- Love, affection, and emotional ties with each parent
- Capacity and willingness to provide love, affection, guidance, and material needs
- Length and stability of any existing parent-child relationships
- Moral fitness and capacity to parent
- Substantial parenting time and the likely effect of any change
- The child's adjustment to home, school, and community
- The child's preference (age-appropriate)
- Stability and continuity

Every factor weighs toward restoration of Plaintiff's parenting time. Plaintiff maintains deep emotional bonds with his child. The wrongful six-and-a-half-month suspension disrupts the stability and continuity the child has previously known. Continued suspension is directly contrary to the statutory best interests standard.

### E. Constitutional Protection

The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000). Suspension of parenting time without a hearing and without clear and convincing evidence violates this fundamental right.

Michigan courts have consistently recognized this fundamental right. See *Sands v. Sands*, 444 Mich. 21 (1993) (protecting parental rights absent clear and convincing evidence of parental unfitness).
