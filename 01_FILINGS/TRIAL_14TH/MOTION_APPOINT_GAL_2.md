## I. STATUTORY AUTHORITY

1. MCL 722.27a provides:

   > "If, at any time in the proceeding, the court determines that the child's best interests are not adequately protected by the parties, the court shall appoint a guardian ad litem for the child."

2. MCR 3.916(A) further provides:

   > "The court may appoint a lawyer-guardian ad litem for a child in a custody dispute in an action for divorce or separate maintenance, an action in which custody is an issue, or a proceeding in which a guardian is appointed."

3. MCL 722.27a specifies that a GAL shall:

   > "(a) Be an attorney admitted to practice in this state...
   > (b) Have the authority and duty to advocate for the child's best interests and to make recommendations to the court regarding custody, parenting time, and other matters relating to the child."

4. The appointment of a GAL is particularly appropriate — and arguably mandatory — in cases involving allegations of parental alienation, mental health disputes, and prolonged parent-child separation.

## I. LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A. Michigan's GAL Statute — MCL 722.27a

Michigan law provides that the court **"shall"** appoint a GAL when it determines that the child's best interests are not adequately protected by the parties. MCL 722.23. The use of the mandatory "shall" — as opposed to "may" — indicates that appointment is **required** once the threshold finding is made.

### B. MCR 3.916 — Duties and Authority

Under MCR 3.916, a lawyer-guardian ad litem:
- Has the authority and duty to advocate for the child's best interests;
- May file pleadings, present evidence, examine witnesses, and make arguments on the child's behalf;
- Shall make an independent investigation;
- Shall file a written report with recommendations.

### C. When Appointment Is Appropriate

Michigan courts have held that a GAL should be appointed when:

1. There are **serious allegations** regarding the fitness of one or both parents. *See Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186 (2004).

2. The case involves **allegations of abuse, neglect, or alienation** that require independent investigation.

3. There is a **significant power imbalance** between the parties (e.g., one party represented, one pro se).

4. The child's **developmental stage** makes it impossible for the child to express his own preferences.

5. The proceedings have become **highly adversarial** to the point where the child's interests are subordinated to the parents' litigation goals.

**All five factors are present in this case.**

## II. APPLICATION TO THIS CASE

### A. The "He Said/She Said" Problem

The central challenge in this case is that the Court has been asked to resolve competing narratives without independent investigation:

- **Mother says:** Father is mentally ill, dangerous, using drugs. *Evidence: Police officer lay opinion, Watson family police reports.*
- **Father says:** Mother is alienating, making false allegations, using court as weapon. *Evidence: HealthWest clinical findings, Watson family admission of pre-planned filing, 519 alienation findings.*

Neither party's perspective is neutral. Neither party's counsel owes a duty to the child. A GAL resolves this by providing an **independent, neutral investigation** focused solely on L.D.W.'s best interests.

### B. The Child Cannot Speak for Himself

L.D.W. is approximately three years old. He cannot express a preference. He cannot describe his experience of separation from his father. He cannot report whether he has been told negative things about his father. He cannot articulate the impact of 329+ days of complete paternal absence during his most critical developmental years.

A GAL gives this child a voice.

### C. A GAL Protects the Court's Integrity

Appointment of a GAL in high-conflict cases protects the Court from the appearance of relying on one-sided information. Given the extraordinary procedural history — 44% ex parte order rate, 329+ days without evidentiary hearing, prohibited ex parte communications by court staff — a GAL provides the independent oversight necessary to ensure that future orders are based on complete, verified information.

## III. CONCLUSION

The appointment of a Guardian Ad Litem is in the best interests of L.D.W. and is arguably mandatory under MCL 722.23 given the current posture of this case. The child's interests are not adequately represented by either party. The competing allegations require independent investigation. The child cannot speak for himself. A GAL removes the "he said/she said" dynamic and provides the Court with neutral, professionally investigated recommendations focused solely on L.D.W.'s welfare.
