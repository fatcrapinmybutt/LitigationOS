## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. CHANGED CIRCUMSTANCES WARRANT TERMINATION OF THE PPO

#### Issue

Whether changed circumstances since entry of the PPO warrant termination under MCL 600.2950(12).

#### Rule

MCL 600.2950(12) provides that "[a] court shall modify or rescind a personal protection order upon a finding that the personal protection order is no longer needed." The use of the word "shall" indicates a mandatory duty. *See* *Smitter v. Thornapple Twp.*, 494 Mich 121, 137 (2013) ("shall" in a statute is mandatory, not permissive).

The Michigan Court of Appeals has interpreted this provision to require a showing of changed circumstances. *Hayford v. Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324, 325-326 (2008). Relevant changed circumstances include:

- The passage of significant time without incident;
- The absence of domestic violence during the pendency of the PPO;
- Stabilization of the respondent's living situation;
- The dissipation of the conditions that gave rise to the PPO.

*Id.* at 326-327.

#### Application

Every relevant indicator of changed circumstances is present in this case:

**Passage of time.** Approximately two to three years have elapsed since entry of the PPO. During this entire period, no domestic violence incidents have occurred.

**No domestic violence.** The central purpose of a PPO under MCL 600.2950 is to protect against domestic violence. *Pickering*, 268 Mich App at 7. Where no domestic violence has occurred during the pendency of the PPO, the factual predicate for the order has dissipated.

**Stable housing.** Movant maintains a permanent, stable residence. This demonstrates the type of stabilization that courts consider relevant to changed circumstances.

**Negative drug screen.** Movant has submitted to voluntary drug screening with negative results, further demonstrating fitness and the absence of risk factors.

**Original allegations unsubstantiated.** The original PPO was based upon allegations that were never tested through adversarial proceedings. The absence of corroborating evidence—no police reports, no criminal charges, no hospital records—combined with the passage of time without incident, undermines the continued need for the PPO.

#### Conclusion

The record establishes changed circumstances warranting termination of the PPO. Under MCL 600.2950(12), the Court "shall" rescind the PPO upon such a finding.

### B. THE PPO HAS BEEN WEAPONIZED AS A CUSTODY TOOL

#### Issue

Whether the PPO has been misused as a custody and parenting-time tool, contrary to the protective purpose of MCL 600.2950.

#### Rule

The Michigan Legislature enacted MCL 600.2950 for the purpose of protecting victims of domestic violence from actual or threatened physical harm. *Pickering v. Pickering*, 268 Mich App 1, 7 (2005). A PPO is a protective remedy. It is not a custody order, a parenting-time order, or a child-placement tool.

Custody and parenting-time matters are governed by a separate statutory scheme: the Child Custody Act, MCL 722.21 *et seq.*, and MCR 3.207. These provisions require courts to consider the statutory best-interest factors of MCL 722.23 before restricting a parent's access to a child. *See* *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508-509 (2003).

The Michigan Court of Appeals has recognized the danger of PPO misuse in custody disputes. In *Hayford*, the Court noted that the circumstances of the case warranted scrutiny of whether the PPO served its intended protective purpose or had been employed as a litigation tactic. 279 Mich App at 327.

#### Application

The evidence in this case establishes that the PPO has been weaponized:

**The PPO is the mechanism of child separation.** The PPO does not merely restrict contact between the parties; it operates as the instrument through which Movant has been completely separated from Lincoln. This is a custody function, not a protective function.

**Circumvention of the Child Custody Act.** By using the PPO to prevent Father-child contact, Petitioner has effectively obtained a *de facto* no-contact custody order without satisfying the requirements of MCL 722.23. The best-interest factors have never been applied to the question of whether Movant should be separated from Lincoln.

**Pattern of misuse.** The record reflects a sustained pattern of leveraging the PPO to block parenting time, frustrate custody proceedings, and maintain the status quo of Father's exclusion from Lincoln's life. This pattern is inconsistent with a genuine need for protection and is consistent with strategic misuse.

**No domestic violence.** The absence of any domestic violence during the pendency of the PPO further supports the conclusion that the PPO serves a custody function rather than a protective function.

The Michigan Supreme Court has held that a court "must consider whether the facts actually support the issuance of a PPO or whether the PPO is merely being used as a sword rather than a shield." *TM v. MZ*, 501 Mich 312, 339 (2018). Here, the PPO is being used as a sword.

#### Conclusion

The PPO has been converted from a domestic-violence protection mechanism into a custody-control tool. This misuse contravenes the statutory purpose of MCL 600.2950 and constitutes an independent ground for termination.

### C. THE PPO IS UNCONSTITUTIONALLY OVERBROAD AS APPLIED

#### Issue

Whether the PPO, as applied, unconstitutionally infringes upon Movant's fundamental right to parent his child.

#### Rule

The right of a parent to direct the care, custody, and control of a child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The Michigan Supreme Court has likewise recognized parental rights as fundamental. *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247, 257 (2009).

When a governmental action infringes upon a fundamental right, it must be narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. *See* *Washington v. Glucksberg*, 521 US 702, 720 (1997). A PPO that operates to sever the parent-child bond must satisfy this heightened standard. *Pickering*, 268 Mich App at 8.

An order is unconstitutionally overbroad when it restricts more conduct than is necessary to achieve its legitimate purpose. *See* *Broadrick v. Oklahoma*, 413 US 601, 612 (1973).

#### Application

**Complete severance of the parent-child relationship.** The PPO, as applied, has resulted in the near-total elimination of Movant's relationship with Lincoln. Father has been unable to exercise parenting time, attend medical appointments, participate in educational decisions, or maintain any meaningful contact with his child during a critical developmental period.

**No narrow tailoring.** The PPO is not narrowly tailored. If the Court's concern is Petitioner's safety, less restrictive alternatives exist that would protect Petitioner while preserving the parent-child relationship:

- Supervised parenting time at a neutral location;
- Modification of the PPO to exclude Lincoln (i.e., allowing Father-child contact while maintaining restrictions on direct contact with Petitioner);
- A structured custody/parenting-time order under MCL 722.27a with appropriate safeguards;
- Exchange protocols through a third party.

**Absence of evidentiary support.** The PPO was entered without a full evidentiary hearing. The severance of a fundamental right requires, at minimum, clear and convincing evidence that the restriction is necessary to prevent harm. No such evidence is in the record.

**Disproportionate restriction.** The complete elimination of a parent-child relationship is the most severe restriction the state can impose short of termination of parental rights. The Juvenile Code requires clear and convincing evidence and extensive procedural protections before parental rights may be terminated. MCL 712A.19b. The PPO achieves the functional equivalent of termination without any of these safeguards.

#### Conclusion

The PPO is unconstitutionally overbroad as applied. It completely severs a fundamental right without evidentiary support, without narrow tailoring, and without the procedural protections that the severity of the restriction demands.

---

### D. DUE PROCESS: THE PPO WAS ENTERED AND CONTINUED WITHOUT AN ADEQUATE EVIDENTIARY HEARING

#### Issue

Whether Movant has been denied due process of law by the entry and continuation of the PPO without a full evidentiary hearing.

#### Rule

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires that a person receive notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard before being deprived of a protected liberty interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976). The right to parent is a protected liberty interest. *Troxel*, 530 US at 65.

MCL 600.2950(12) provides that a respondent may petition the court to modify or rescind a PPO, and MCR 3.707(B) establishes the procedural framework for such hearings. At such a hearing, the petitioner bears the burden of establishing, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the PPO remains necessary. *Hayford*, 279 Mich App at 326.

#### Application

**No adversarial hearing at entry.** The PPO was entered without a contested hearing at which Movant was afforded the opportunity to confront witnesses, cross-examine the petitioner, and present evidence in his own defense.

**Deprivation of a fundamental right.** The PPO deprives Movant of his fundamental right to parent Lincoln. When a fundamental right is at stake, due process demands heightened procedural protections. *See* *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753-754 (1982).

**Continuation without review.** The PPO has remained in effect for approximately two to three years without a substantive evidentiary hearing on whether it continues to be warranted.

**Request for hearing.** Movant now formally requests a full evidentiary hearing pursuant to MCL 600.2950(12) and MCR 3.707(B) at which Petitioner must establish, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the PPO remains necessary.

#### Conclusion

Movant has been denied due process. A full evidentiary hearing is constitutionally required.

### E. NO ACTUAL DOMESTIC VIOLENCE SUPPORTS THE PPO

#### Issue

Whether the PPO is supported by evidence of actual domestic violence, where the original allegations were unsubstantiated and no domestic violence has occurred during the pendency of the order.

#### Rule

MCL 600.2950(1) authorizes issuance of a PPO upon a showing that the respondent has engaged in or threatens to engage in domestic violence. "Domestic violence" is defined by reference to MCL 400.1501 and includes physical harm, placing a person in fear of physical harm, and sexual assault. MCL 600.2950(1).

A PPO must be supported by competent evidence. *Grew v. Knox*, 265 Mich App 308, 313-314 (2005). Conclusory allegations, without supporting evidence, are insufficient to sustain a PPO. *Id.*

#### Application

**Original allegations unsubstantiated.** The record does not contain independent evidence—such as police reports, medical records, photographs of injuries, or third-party witness statements—corroborating the allegations of domestic violence that formed the basis for the PPO.

**No domestic violence during the pendency.** In the two to three years since the PPO was entered, no domestic violence of any kind has occurred. No criminal charges have been filed. No police reports have been generated. This sustained absence of domestic violence undermines the continued necessity of the PPO.

**Absence of threat.** Movant does not pose a threat to Petitioner's safety. The passage of time without incident, stable housing, and a clean drug screen collectively demonstrate the absence of risk.

#### Conclusion

The PPO is not supported by evidence of actual domestic violence. The original allegations were unsubstantiated, and no domestic violence has occurred during the pendency of the order. The PPO should be terminated.

---

### F. CORRELATION BETWEEN PPO AND CUSTODY: THE PPO IS THE MECHANISM OF CHILD SEPARATION

#### Issue

Whether the PPO operates as the mechanism through which Movant is separated from his child, such that its continuation is contrary to the child's best interests.

#### Rule

Michigan law recognizes that a child's best interests are served by maintaining a strong relationship with both parents. MCL 722.23(a), (b), (c), (j). The Child Custody Act presumes that parenting time is in the child's best interest. MCL 722.27a(1).

When a PPO operates to sever the parent-child relationship, the court must consider whether continuation of the PPO is consistent with the child's best interests. *See* *Pickering*, 268 Mich App at 8; *Hayford*, 279 Mich App at 327.

#### Application

**PPO = child separation.** The PPO is the direct cause of Father's separation from Lincoln. Without the PPO, Movant would be entitled to seek parenting time through the Child Custody Act's best-interest framework. The PPO short-circuits this analysis entirely.

**Harm to Lincoln.** Lincoln has been deprived of a relationship with his father since approximately one year of age. Developmental literature and Michigan caselaw recognize that the absence of a parental relationship during formative years causes harm to the child. *See* MCL 722.23(j) (willingness to facilitate a close and continuing parent-child relationship); MCL 722.23(l) (domestic violence as a factor, but one that must be weighed against all other factors).

**Best-interest factors not applied.** The PPO operates as a *de facto* custody order, yet none of the MCL 722.23 best-interest factors have been applied to the question of Father's separation from Lincoln. If the Court were to apply the best-interest framework, the absence of domestic violence, Father's stable housing, and Father's clean drug screen would weigh in favor of parenting time.

**Direct correlation.** The PPO is not merely incidental to Father's separation from Lincoln. It is the mechanism through which that separation is maintained. Termination of the PPO would remove the primary barrier to Father-child contact and permit the custody question to be resolved through the proper statutory framework.

#### Conclusion

The PPO operates as the mechanism of child separation. Its continuation is contrary to Lincoln's best interests and circumvents the statutory framework for custody determination. The PPO should be terminated to permit the custody question to be resolved under the Child Custody Act.

## III. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. Statutory Authority: MCL 600.2950(12)

10. MCL 600.2950(12) provides:

> "A court shall modify or rescind a personal protection order upon a finding that the personal protection order is no longer needed."

11. The movant bears the burden of establishing changed circumstances warranting modification or termination. *Hayford v. Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324, 325-326 (2008).

### B. Standard of Review

12. A trial court's decision on a motion to modify or terminate a PPO is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. *Pickering v. Pickering*, 268 Mich App 1, 4 (2005). An abuse of discretion occurs when the court's decision falls outside the range of principled outcomes. *Maldonado v. Ford Motor Co.*, 476 Mich 372, 388 (2006).

---

## IV. ARGUMENT

### ARGUMENT I: CHANGED CIRCUMSTANCES WARRANT TERMINATION OF THE PPO

#### Issue

Whether changed circumstances since entry of the PPO warrant termination pursuant to MCL 600.2950(12).

#### Rule

MCL 600.2950(12) mandates that a court "shall modify or rescind" a PPO upon finding it is "no longer needed." The Michigan Court of Appeals has held that a PPO may be terminated upon a showing of changed circumstances. *Hayford*, 279 Mich App at 326. Changed circumstances include, but are not limited to: the passage of time without incident, the absence of domestic violence, stability in the respondent's living situation, and the dissipation of the conditions that gave rise to the PPO. *Id.*

#### Application

The following changed circumstances exist:

(a) **No domestic violence has occurred.** Since entry of the PPO, Movant has not engaged in any act of domestic violence against Petitioner or any other person. No police reports, criminal charges, or convictions exist against Movant for domestic violence during the pendency of the PPO.

(b) **Passage of time without incident.** Approximately two to three years have elapsed since entry of the PPO. During this period, Movant has demonstrated compliance with the law and the absence of the alleged threat that purportedly justified the PPO.

(c) **Stable living situation.** Movant maintains a stable residence at 1977 Whitehall Rd, Lot 17, Laketon Township, Michigan 49445. (*See* Affidavit of Andrew J. Pigors.)

(d) **Negative drug screen.** Movant has submitted to drug screening, with negative results, further demonstrating fitness and changed circumstances. (*See* Exhibit Index.)

(e) **Original allegations unsubstantiated.** The original PPO petition was premised upon allegations that were never adjudicated in a full evidentiary hearing and that Movant contests as unsubstantiated.

#### Conclusion

Changed circumstances warrant termination of the PPO under MCL 600.2950(12). The PPO is no longer needed.

---

### ARGUMENT II: THE PPO HAS BEEN WEAPONIZED AS A CUSTODY TOOL, CONTRARY TO STATUTORY PURPOSE

#### Issue

Whether the PPO has been misused as a custody and parenting-time tool, contrary to the protective purpose of MCL 600.2950.

#### Rule

The purpose of MCL 600.2950 is to protect victims of domestic violence from actual or threatened harm. *Pickering v. Pickering*, 268 Mich App 1, 7 (2005). A PPO is not intended to serve as a substitute for custody or parenting-time orders. *See* MCL 722.27a (governing parenting time); MCR 3.207(B) (governing custody proceedings). The Michigan Court of Appeals has cautioned against the misuse of PPOs as tactical weapons in custody disputes. *Hayford*, 279 Mich App at 327.

#### Application

The record in this matter establishes a pattern of PPO misuse:

(a) The PPO has been the primary instrument used to prevent Movant from exercising parenting time with Lincoln (DOB 11/9/2022). The PPO operates not as a protective measure against domestic violence, but as a *de facto* no-contact custody order.

(b) A documented pattern of weaponization is present. The record reflects systematic use of the PPO to restrict Movant's access to his child, including leveraging PPO enforcement to frustrate custody proceedings. (*See* Brief in Support of Motion; Exhibit Index.)

(c) There is a direct correlation between the PPO and the denial of parenting time. The PPO is the mechanism through which Movant has been separated from Lincoln.

(d) The use of a PPO as a custody tool circumvents the statutory framework established by the Child Custody Act, MCL 722.21 *et seq.*, and the procedural protections of MCR 3.207, which require consideration of the best-interest factors under MCL 722.23 before restricting parenting time.

#### Conclusion

The PPO has been weaponized as a custody tool, contrary to the statutory purpose of MCL 600.2950. This misuse constitutes an independent ground for termination or modification.

---

### ARGUMENT III: THE PPO IS UNCONSTITUTIONALLY OVERBROAD AS APPLIED

#### Issue

Whether the PPO, as applied, unconstitutionally infringes upon Movant's fundamental right to parent his child.

#### Rule

The United States Supreme Court has recognized the fundamental right of parents to direct the care, custody, and control of their children. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Michigan law likewise recognizes this fundamental right. *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247, 257 (2009).

A PPO that restricts parental rights must be narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. *See* US Const, Am XIV; Const 1963, art 1, §§ 2, 17. When a PPO operates to sever a parent-child relationship without an evidentiary basis, it is unconstitutionally overbroad as applied. *See* *Pickering*, 268 Mich App at 8.

#### Application

(a) The PPO, as applied, completely severs Movant's relationship with his minor child Lincoln. Movant has been unable to exercise parenting time, attend Lincoln's medical appointments, participate in educational decisions, or maintain any meaningful parent-child bond.

(b) No evidentiary hearing has been conducted at which Movant was afforded the opportunity to confront witnesses and present evidence. The PPO was entered based on uncontested allegations.

(c) The PPO is not narrowly tailored. A less restrictive alternative—such as supervised parenting time, modification of the PPO to exclude the minor child, or a structured custody order—would protect Petitioner's legitimate safety interests while preserving Movant's fundamental parental rights.

(d) The state's interest in preventing domestic violence, while compelling, does not justify the permanent severance of the parent-child bond absent clear and convincing evidence of risk to the child. No such evidence has been presented.

#### Conclusion

The PPO is unconstitutionally overbroad as applied because it completely severs Movant's fundamental right to parent Lincoln without adequate evidentiary support and without narrow tailoring.

---

### ARGUMENT IV: DUE PROCESS REQUIRES AN EVIDENTIARY HEARING

#### Issue

Whether Movant has been denied due process by the entry and continuation of the PPO without a full evidentiary hearing.

#### Rule

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires that before a person is deprived of a fundamental right, the person must receive notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976). Michigan law provides that a respondent to a PPO is entitled to request a hearing. MCL 600.2950(12); MCR 3.707(B). A hearing at which the petitioner bears the burden of establishing the PPO by a preponderance of the evidence is constitutionally required when fundamental rights are at stake. *Pickering*, 268 Mich App at 9.

#### Application

(a) Movant's right to parent his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment.

(b) The PPO was entered on the basis of Petitioner's unsworn or uncontested allegations without a full adversarial hearing at which Movant could confront witnesses and present evidence.

(c) The continuation of the PPO for approximately two to three years without a substantive evidentiary hearing on changed circumstances compounds the due process violation.

(d) Movant now requests a full evidentiary hearing at which Petitioner must establish, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the PPO remains necessary. MCL 600.2950(12); *Hayford*, 279 Mich App at 326.

#### Conclusion

Due process requires a full evidentiary hearing on this Motion. Movant has been deprived of a fundamental right without adequate procedural protections.
