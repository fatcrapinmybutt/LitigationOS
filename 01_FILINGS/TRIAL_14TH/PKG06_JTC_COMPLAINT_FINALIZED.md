## V. CANONS OF JUDICIAL CONDUCT VIOLATED

### A. MCJC Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

Canon 1 requires that a judge shall uphold the integrity and independence of the judiciary. A judiciary of integrity is one where decisions are reached through application of law to fact, not through bias, prejudice, or predetermination.

**Judge McNeill violated Canon 1 by:**

1. **Entering orders reflecting personal bias rather than legal analysis.** Judge McNeill used derogatory characterizations in official court orders, including referring to Complainant's positions as "crazy" and labeling Complainant a "liar." Such language in judicial orders is incompatible with the integrity of the judiciary and evidences personal animus rather than dispassionate adjudication.

2. **Systematically depriving a parent of custody rights without the findings required by MCL 722.23.** Michigan's best-interest factors exist to ensure judicial integrity in custody determinations. Judge McNeill's failure to analyze or apply these statutory factors — while simultaneously depriving Complainant of parental rights — demonstrates decisions driven by something other than legal analysis.

3. **Issuing sanctions as retaliation for the exercise of legal rights.** Judge McNeill imposed $250 sanctions against Complainant for filing motions — a constitutionally protected activity. This chilling of access to the courts undermines the independence of the judiciary by discouraging litigants from seeking lawful redress.

### B. MCJC Canon 2 — Avoidance of Impropriety and Appearance of Impropriety

Canon 2 requires that a judge shall avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities. A judge shall act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary.

**Judge McNeill violated Canon 2 by:**

1. **Engaging in ex parte contacts and communications.** Complainant has documented 150 instances of ex parte violations, including orders entered without hearing and communications with one party outside the presence of the other. Each instance creates the appearance — and the reality — of impropriety.

2. **Exhibiting an observable pattern of one-sided rulings.** When a judge's rulings consistently favor one party across 1,127 documented decision points, the appearance of impartiality is destroyed. The statistical pattern documented herein compels the conclusion that an objective observer would reasonably question Judge McNeill's impartiality.

3. **Issuing ex parte PPOs at a rate of 44%.** Nearly half of all protective orders in these cases were entered without notice or hearing. This rate far exceeds any reasonable baseline and creates a profound appearance of impropriety.

### C. MCJC Canon 3 — Impartiality, Diligence, and Competence

Canon 3 requires that a judge shall perform judicial duties impartially and diligently. Specifically:

- **Canon 3(B)(5):** A judge shall perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice and shall not manifest bias or prejudice by words or conduct.
- **Canon 3(B)(7):** A judge shall accord every person the right to be heard according to law.
- **Canon 3(B)(8):** A judge shall not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications.

**Judge McNeill violated Canon 3 by:**

1. **Manifesting bias through words in official orders.** The use of "crazy" and "liar" in judicial orders constitutes bias manifested by words in violation of Canon 3(B)(5).

2. **Denying the right to be heard.** Complainant was deprived of custody without hearing, had motions denied without analysis, and was sanctioned for exercising the right to file motions. These actions violate Canon 3(B)(7).

3. **Permitting and relying upon ex parte communications.** The 150 documented ex parte violations directly contravene Canon 3(B)(8).

4. **Failing to apply mandatory legal standards.** Judge McNeill failed to make findings under MCL 722.23 (best-interest factors) despite depriving Complainant of custody — a failure of both diligence and competence.

5. **Ignoring exculpatory evidence.** Judge McNeill disregarded Complainant's negative drug screen, reflecting predetermined conclusions rather than diligent evaluation of evidence.

## B. BIAS AND PREJUDICE

**167 MCR 2.003 Disqualification Violations | Applicable Standard: MCJC Canon 2, Canon 3(B)(5), MCR 2.003(C)(1)**

### Legal Standard

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification of a judge where the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party. MCJC Canon 3(B)(5) prohibits a judge from manifesting bias or prejudice by words or conduct.

The test is objective: whether a reasonable person would have a well-grounded fear of receiving unfair treatment. *Cain v. Michigan Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470 (1996).

### Pattern Description

Judge McNeill's conduct across 1,127 documented violations establishes a pattern of systematic bias against Complainant. The bias manifests in four observable forms: (1) derogatory language in official orders; (2) one-sided application of legal standards; (3) predetermined outcomes; and (4) differential treatment of the parties.

### Specific Instances

#### Category B-1: Derogatory Language in Orders

1. **Use of "Crazy"** — Judge McNeill characterized Complainant's legal positions using the word "crazy" in official court orders. This language has no place in judicial writing and evidences personal animus rather than legal analysis. It is bias manifested by words in direct violation of Canon 3(B)(5).

2. **Use of "Liar"** — Judge McNeill labeled Complainant a "liar" in official court orders. A judicial determination that testimony is not credible is substantively different from a personal attack labeling a party a liar. The latter reflects prejudice, not adjudication.

#### Category B-2: One-Sided Application of Legal Standards

3. **Selective Evidentiary Standards** — Judge McNeill applied heightened evidentiary scrutiny to Complainant's submissions while accepting opposing party's representations without similar scrutiny. Evidence favorable to Complainant — including a negative drug screen — was disregarded without explanation.

4. **Asymmetric Motion Practice** — Complainant's motions were denied without substantive analysis or were met with sanctions, while opposing party's requests were granted routinely, often ex parte.

#### Category B-3: Predetermined Outcomes

5. **Checkbox Orders Without Findings** — Judge McNeill issued orders using checkbox forms without the factual findings or legal analysis required by MCR 2.517. The use of checkbox orders in complex custody matters — without any written findings — suggests outcomes were predetermined rather than adjudicated.

6. **Disregard of Exculpatory Evidence** — Complainant's negative drug screen was ignored. When a court disregards evidence favorable to one party without explanation, it evidences predetermination.

#### Category B-4: Differential Treatment

7. **Sanctions for Filing Motions** — Complainant was sanctioned $250 for the act of filing motions. Filing motions is a constitutionally protected exercise of the right of access to courts. Sanctioning a party for exercising this right, in the absence of a finding of frivolousness under MCR 1.109(E), constitutes differential treatment and retaliation.

### Legal Standard

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, §17 of the Michigan Constitution require that no person be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. A parent's right to the care and custody of a child is a fundamental liberty interest. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394 (2014).

MCR 2.517 requires that in actions tried without a jury, the court shall find the facts specially, state separately its conclusions of law, and direct entry of the appropriate judgment.

### Pattern Description

Judge McNeill systematically deprived Complainant of fundamental due process protections in proceedings that resulted in the deprivation of his parental rights — a liberty interest of the highest constitutional order.

### Specific Instances

#### Category C-1: Custody Deprivation Without Hearing

#### Category C-2: Motions Denied Without Analysis

#### Category C-3: Right to Be Heard Denied
