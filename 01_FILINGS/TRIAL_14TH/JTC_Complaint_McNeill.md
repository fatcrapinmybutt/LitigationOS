## I. INTRODUCTION

1. Andrew J. Pigors ("Complainant" or "Father"), appearing pro se, respectfully submits this formal complaint to the Michigan Judicial Tenure Commission ("Commission") pursuant to Article 6, § 30 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963 and MCR 9.104–9.120, requesting investigation and appropriate disciplinary action against the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill ("Respondent Judge" or "Judge McNeill") of the 14th Judicial Circuit Court, Muskegon County, Michigan.

2. This complaint is based upon personal knowledge, review of court records, and documented events occurring in Case Nos. 2024-001507-DC and 2023-5907-PP. The misconduct alleged herein is not a mere disagreement with judicial rulings on the merits, but rather a pattern of procedural irregularities, denial of due process, ex parte communications, and sustained bias that individually and collectively violate the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct ("MCJC") and undermine public confidence in the judiciary.

3. Complainant respectfully acknowledges that the Commission's jurisdiction extends to judicial conduct rather than the correctness of judicial decisions. The allegations herein are directed at Judge McNeill's conduct, demeanor, procedures, and adherence to the canons of judicial ethics—not the ultimate merits of the underlying custody or protection order disputes.

## IV. STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. Background and Procedural History

9. In or about 2023, a Personal Protection Order ("PPO") petition was filed in Case No. 2023-5907-PP. The PPO was issued against Complainant and has been maintained by Judge McNeill despite a lack of adequate evidentiary basis.

10. In or about 2024, custody proceedings were initiated in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. Judge McNeill was assigned to preside over these proceedings.

11. Throughout these interrelated proceedings, Complainant has appeared pro se—without the benefit of legal counsel—while the opposing party has been represented by attorney Rusco ("Opposing Counsel").

### B. Issuance and Maintenance of PPO Without Adequate Evidentiary Basis

12. Judge McNeill issued and/or maintained the Personal Protection Order in Case No. 2023-5907-PP despite the existence of seven (7) or more police reports, none of which contained findings of abuse, violence, or threatening conduct by Complainant.

13. The police reports, which are a matter of public record and available for the Commission's review, consistently failed to substantiate the allegations underlying the PPO petition. No law enforcement agency made a finding of abuse or threat attributable to Complainant.

14. Despite this evidentiary record, Judge McNeill sustained the PPO, effectively using it as a basis to restrict and ultimately deny Complainant's parenting time with his child—a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clauses of the United States and Michigan Constitutions.

### C. Denial of Parenting Time for 329+ Days Without Proper Hearing

15. As of the date of this complaint, Complainant has been denied parenting time with his minor child for a period exceeding three hundred and twenty-nine (329) consecutive days.

16. This prolonged denial of parenting time occurred without a full evidentiary hearing at which Complainant was afforded the opportunity to present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, and be heard on the record regarding the best interests of the child.

17. Michigan law establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time. MCL 722.27a(1) provides that parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child. MCL 722.27a(7) further mandates that the court shall order makeup parenting time when a parent is wrongfully denied parenting time.

18. Judge McNeill made no safety findings on the record that would justify the complete suspension of parenting time for a period exceeding eleven months. No credible evidence of abuse, neglect, or endangerment was adduced at any hearing to support such an extreme deprivation.

19. Judge McNeill failed to order makeup parenting time as required by MCL 722.27a(7), compounding the deprivation and demonstrating a disregard for the statutory framework governing parenting time.

### D. Failure to Consider Best Interest Factors

20. MCL 722.23 enumerates twelve specific best interest factors that a court must consider and make findings upon when making custody and parenting time determinations. These factors include, among others, the love and affection between parent and child, the capacity of the parties to provide for the child, the mental and physical health of the parties, and the willingness of each party to facilitate a close relationship between the child and the other parent.

21. Upon information and belief, Judge McNeill failed to make adequate findings on the statutory best interest factors before imposing what amounts to a complete deprivation of Complainant's parenting time—one of the most severe restrictions a family court can impose.

22. The failure to apply the statutory best interest framework before restricting parental rights is not merely legal error; when it reflects a pattern of predetermined outcomes and disregard for mandatory procedures, it constitutes judicial misconduct.

### E. Disregard of HealthWest Psychological Evaluation

23. Complainant underwent a psychological evaluation conducted by HealthWest, a recognized community mental health provider in the Muskegon County area. The HealthWest evaluation cleared Complainant—that is, it did not identify psychological conditions, behavioral risks, or safety concerns that would warrant the denial or restriction of parenting time.

24. Despite the HealthWest clearance, Judge McNeill continued to deny Complainant parenting time and credited unsubstantiated allegations from the opposing party over the professional findings of the HealthWest evaluation.

25. A judge's discretion to weigh evidence does not extend to ignoring material, favorable evidence without explanation or to crediting uncorroborated allegations over professional clinical findings—particularly in the absence of a full evidentiary hearing at which credibility can be properly tested.

### F. Denial of Motions Without Hearing or Explanation

26. On multiple occasions, Judge McNeill denied Complainant's motions without scheduling hearings and without providing adequate written explanations for the denials.

27. Pro se litigants are entitled to reasonable access to the court and to have their properly filed motions considered on the merits. While courts have discretion in managing their dockets, the repeated denial of motions without hearing or explanation—particularly motions affecting fundamental parental rights—creates the appearance of bias and denies the pro se litigant a meaningful opportunity to be heard.

28. The pattern of summary denial of Complainant's motions stands in contrast to the treatment afforded to motions filed by Opposing Counsel, further reinforcing the appearance of partiality.

### G. Ex Parte Communications

29. Upon information and belief, Judge McNeill engaged in ex parte communications with Opposing Counsel Rusco in connection with one or both of the above-referenced cases.

30. MCR 2.410 and Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct strictly prohibit ex parte communications between a judge and a party or attorney, except in narrow, specifically defined circumstances not applicable here.

31. Any ex parte communication between Judge McNeill and Opposing Counsel Rusco—whether in person, by telephone, by electronic means, or through intermediaries—regarding substantive or procedural matters in these cases constitutes a serious violation of judicial ethics and due process.

32. The existence of ex parte communications is supported by scheduling irregularities, unexplained procedural developments, and outcomes that suggest advance coordination between the court and Opposing Counsel, of which Complainant was not informed and to which he was not given an opportunity to respond.

### H. Imposition of $250 Bond Requirement on Indigent Pro Se Litigant

33. Judge McNeill imposed a requirement that Complainant post a $250 bond as a condition of pursuing relief in these proceedings.

34. Complainant is indigent and appearing pro se. The imposition of a $250 bond effectively created a financial barrier to Complainant's access to the court and to the vindication of his parental rights.

35. Michigan law and constitutional principles require that courts not impose financial conditions that effectively deny indigent litigants access to justice, particularly in proceedings involving fundamental rights such as the parent-child relationship. See Boddie v. Connecticut, 401 U.S. 371 (1971) (due process prohibits states from denying access to courts based on inability to pay in cases involving fundamental interests).

36. The imposition of the bond requirement on an indigent pro se litigant, in the context of a case involving fundamental parental rights, demonstrates insensitivity to access-to-justice concerns and contributes to the appearance of bias against the unrepresented party.

### I. Credibility Determinations Without Evidentiary Hearing

37. Judge McNeill made credibility determinations—specifically, crediting the allegations of the opposing party while discounting Complainant's evidence and representations—without conducting a full evidentiary hearing.

38. Credibility determinations are among the most consequential judicial findings, particularly in family law matters. They require live testimony, the opportunity for cross-examination, and careful observation of witness demeanor. Making such determinations on the papers alone, or based on unsworn representations of counsel, violates fundamental principles of due process and fair adjudication.

### J. Pattern of Hostility Toward Pro Se Litigant

39. Throughout these proceedings, Judge McNeill has demonstrated a pattern of hostility, impatience, and dismissiveness toward Complainant as a pro se litigant.

40. While judges are not required to serve as advocates for pro se parties, they are required by Canon 3(A)(3) to accord every person the full right to be heard according to law. The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that pro se litigants are entitled to be treated with dignity and to have their filings and arguments considered on the merits, even if they are not presented with the technical precision of a trained attorney.

41. The pattern of hostility is evidenced by: (a) the repeated denial of motions without hearing; (b) the dismissive treatment of Complainant's evidence, including the HealthWest clearance; (c) the imposition of financial barriers; (d) the contrast between the court's treatment of Complainant and its treatment of represented Opposing Counsel; and (e) the overall trajectory of rulings that systematically disadvantaged Complainant without adequate evidentiary or procedural basis.

## V. VIOLATIONS OF THE MICHIGAN CODE OF JUDICIAL CONDUCT

### A. Canon 1 — Integrity and Independence of the Judiciary

42. Canon 1 provides: "An independent and honorable judiciary is indispensable to justice in our society. A judge should participate in establishing, maintaining, and enforcing, and should personally observe, high standards of conduct so that the integrity and independence of the judiciary may be preserved."

43. **Violation — Failure to Uphold Public Confidence Through Biased Proceedings:** Judge McNeill's pattern of conduct in these cases—including the denial of parenting time for 329+ days without hearing, the disregard of favorable psychological evidence, the imposition of financial barriers on an indigent litigant, and the appearance of ex parte communications—individually and collectively undermines public confidence in the integrity of the judiciary. When a father is denied access to his child for nearly a year without a full evidentiary hearing and without adequate findings on the record, the public's confidence that the courts operate fairly and impartially is eroded.

44. **Violation — Pattern of Predetermined Outcomes:** The systematic nature of the rulings against Complainant—denial of motions without hearing, dismissal of favorable evidence, maintenance of parenting time restrictions without safety findings, and financial barriers—creates a strong inference that outcomes in these cases were predetermined rather than the product of neutral, impartial adjudication. This pattern is inconsistent with the judicial independence that Canon 1 requires.

### B. Canon 2 — Avoiding Impropriety and the Appearance of Impropriety

45. Canon 2(A) provides: "A judge should respect and comply with the law and should conduct himself or herself at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary."

46. Canon 2(B) provides: "A judge should not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment. A judge should not lend the prestige of the judicial office to advance the private interests of others."

47. **Violation — Ex Parte Communications with Opposing Counsel:** As set forth in paragraphs 29–32 above, Judge McNeill engaged in or permitted ex parte communications with Opposing Counsel Rusco. Such communications constitute impropriety per se and create an appearance of impropriety that cannot be dispelled, regardless of whether the communications actually influenced judicial decisions. See In re Justin, 490 Mich 394 (2012) (discussing the gravity of ex parte communications as judicial misconduct).

48. **Violation — Appearance of Partiality Toward Opposing Party:** The totality of Judge McNeill's conduct in these cases—the asymmetric treatment of the parties, the crediting of unsubstantiated allegations from the represented party over professional clinical findings favorable to the pro se party, and the imposition of barriers to access on the unrepresented party—creates a pervasive appearance of partiality that violates Canon 2. The appearance of impartiality is as important as actual impartiality. See Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868 (2009).

49. **Violation — Failure to Disclose Relationships or Conflicts:** Upon information and belief, Judge McNeill failed to disclose any personal, professional, or social relationships or other circumstances that may give rise to a conflict of interest or appearance of partiality in these proceedings. Canon 2 requires a judge to proactively address potential conflicts, and the failure to do so—particularly when combined with the appearance of partiality described herein—constitutes a separate violation.

### C. Canon 3 — Performing Duties Impartially and Diligently

50. Canon 3 sets forth specific, enforceable standards governing how a judge must perform judicial duties.

#### Canon 3(A)(3) — Failure to Accord Full Right to Be Heard

51. Canon 3(A)(3) provides: "A judge should accord to every person who is legally interested in a proceeding, or the person's lawyer, full right to be heard according to law."

52. Judge McNeill violated Canon 3(A)(3) by: (a) denying Complainant's motions without scheduling hearings or providing adequate explanation; (b) making credibility determinations and findings affecting parental rights without affording Complainant a full evidentiary hearing; (c) denying parenting time for 329+ days without a hearing at which Complainant could present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, and argue his position; and (d) demonstrating a pattern of hostility and dismissiveness that effectively silenced Complainant's participation in the proceedings.

53. The right to be heard is not a mere formality—it is the bedrock of due process. When a judge systematically denies a pro se litigant meaningful opportunities to be heard on matters affecting his fundamental parental rights, the violation is not merely procedural but strikes at the core of the judicial function.

#### Canon 3(A)(4) — Ex Parte Communications

54. Canon 3(A)(4) provides: "A judge should not initiate, permit, or consider ex parte communications, or consider other communications made to the judge outside the presence of the parties concerning a pending or impending proceeding."

55. As set forth in paragraphs 29–32 above, Judge McNeill violated Canon 3(A)(4) by engaging in or permitting ex parte communications with Opposing Counsel Rusco. This violation is independently established and does not depend upon proof that the communications influenced case outcomes—the mere occurrence of such communications violates the Canon.

#### Canon 3(B)(5) — Failure to Perform Duties Without Bias or Prejudice

56. Canon 3(B)(5) provides that a judge shall perform judicial duties without bias or prejudice and shall not, by words or conduct, manifest bias or prejudice based upon, among other things, socioeconomic status.

57. Judge McNeill's imposition of a $250 bond requirement on an indigent pro se litigant, combined with the systematic disadvantaging of the unrepresented party relative to the represented party, manifests bias based on socioeconomic status and representation status. The pattern of conduct described herein demonstrates prejudice against Complainant as an indigent pro se father.

#### Canon 3(B)(8) — Failure to Dispose of Matters Promptly and Efficiently

58. Canon 3(B)(8) requires a judge to "dispose of all judicial matters promptly, efficiently, and fairly."

59. The denial of parenting time for 329+ consecutive days without resolving the underlying disputes through proper hearing and findings constitutes a failure to dispose of this matter promptly and efficiently. The prolonged deprivation of a fundamental right without resolution is itself a form of injustice, as justice delayed is justice denied—particularly for a child whose relationship with a parent is being severed by the passage of time.

#### Additional Canon 3 Violations — Findings Not Supported by Evidence

60. Judge McNeill made findings and entered orders that were not supported by the evidence of record. Specifically: (a) the maintenance of the PPO despite seven or more police reports with no abuse findings; (b) the restriction of parenting time without safety findings; (c) the crediting of unsubstantiated allegations over the HealthWest psychological clearance; and (d) credibility determinations made without evidentiary hearing.

61. While judges have broad discretion in weighing evidence, the exercise of that discretion must be grounded in an evidentiary record developed through fair procedures. When a judge makes findings that are directly contradicted by the only professional evidence in the record (the HealthWest clearance) and supported only by unsworn, unsubstantiated allegations from an adverse party, the resulting orders are the product of bias rather than judicial discretion.

### D. Canon 5 — Extrajudicial Activities

62. Canon 5 governs a judge's extrajudicial activities and requires that such activities not cast reasonable doubt on the judge's capacity to act impartially, demean the judicial office, or interfere with the proper performance of judicial duties.

63. Complainant reserves the right to supplement this complaint with additional allegations under Canon 5 should the Commission's investigation reveal that Judge McNeill's conduct in these cases was influenced by extrajudicial relationships, activities, or interests not currently known to Complainant.
