## PART IV: LEGAL ARGUMENT

### ARGUMENT I: The Ex Parte Suspension Violates MCL 722.27a

#### Issue

21. Whether the complete suspension of Father's parenting time without the required statutory findings violates MCL 722.27a.

#### Rule

22. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a **statutory presumption** that parenting time is in the child's best interests:

> *"It is presumed to be in the best interests of a child to have a strong relationship with both of his or her parents."*

23. This presumption may be overcome only upon specific findings. MCL 722.27a(3) provides that parenting time shall be restricted **only** if it is shown that "parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

24. MCL 722.27a(7) further requires the court to consider "the frequency, duration, and type of parenting time" that serves the child's best interests. A complete suspension with zero contact is not a "type" of parenting time — it is the abolition of it.

#### Application

25. The trial court made **no finding** that Father's parenting time would "endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health" as required by MCL 722.27a(3). The checkbox form order contains no such language.

26. The only evidence offered — unsworn hearsay about "meth use" (contradicted by a NEGATIVE drug screen) and "eviction" (unauthenticated) — is legally insufficient to overcome the MCL 722.27a(1) statutory presumption in favor of parenting time.

27. Even if the allegations had been proved (they were not), the appropriate remedy would have been **supervised parenting time or other restrictions** — not total elimination. Michigan courts have consistently held that the most restrictive remedy should be used only as a last resort. *Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 31-32; 805 NW2d 1 (2010).

#### Conclusion

28. The suspension violates MCL 722.27a because no statutory finding of endangerment was made, the statutory presumption in favor of parenting time was ignored, and the remedy imposed (total elimination) was disproportionate to the unsubstantiated allegations.

---

### ARGUMENT II: No Best Interest Findings Were Made Under MCL 722.23

#### Issue

29. Whether the trial court erred by failing to make any findings on the MCL 722.23 best interest factors before suspending all parenting time.

#### Rule

30. MCL 722.23 requires the court to evaluate twelve best interest factors — (a) through (l) — when making any custody or parenting time determination. "Custody decisions require findings under **all** of the best interest factors." *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92; 782 NW2d 480 (2010).

31. MCR 2.517(A)(1) requires that in contested matters, the court must "find the facts specially and state separately its conclusions of law."

#### Application

32. The August 8, 2025 orders contain **ZERO findings** on any of the twelve best interest factors. Not one. The checkbox form is devoid of any analysis of:

   - **Factor (a):** The strong love, affection, and emotional ties between Father and L.D.W. — the Court's own trial findings found this factor "equal between the parties";
   - **Factor (b):** Father's demonstrated capacity to provide guidance and parenting — also found "equal" at trial;
   - **Factor (c):** Father's ability to provide for L.D.W.'s needs — also found "equal" at trial;
   - **Factor (d):** The length of time the child has lived in a satisfactory environment — L.D.W. had an established custodial environment with Father;
   - **Factor (f):** The moral fitness of the parties — no finding of unfitness against Father;
   - **Factor (g):** Mental and physical health — Father's HealthWest assessment (received September 12, 2025) was favorable, reporting "no issues with employment" and "support of friends";
   - **Factor (h):** The child's reasonable preference — L.D.W. is too young to express preference;
   - **Factor (j): MOST CRITICALLY — Mother's demonstrated UNWILLINGNESS to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship**, as evidenced by filing the ex parte motion to eliminate ALL contact;
   - **Factor (l):** Any other relevant factor, including the **329+ day separation** and its devastating impact on the child.

33. The absence of any best interest findings is not a harmless technicality — it is the failure to apply the substantive legal standard that governs every custody and parenting time decision in Michigan.

#### Conclusion

34. The complete absence of MCL 722.23 best interest findings constitutes clear legal error requiring reversal. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92.

### ARGUMENT III: Due Process — Fundamental Right Violated Without Adequate Procedure

#### Issue

35. Whether the complete suspension of Father's parenting time for 214+ days without a merits hearing violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

#### Rule

36. The right to the care, custody, and companionship of one's child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 US 645, 651 (1972).

37. MCR 3.206(B)(5)–(6) establishes the Michigan procedural requirements for ex parte custody/parenting time orders: (1) specific facts showing immediate and irreparable injury; (2) verified pleading or affidavit; (3) a hearing scheduled "as soon as possible" after entry.

38. MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that the motion be supported by "specific facts in a verified pleading or affidavit."

#### Application

39. Every procedural safeguard was violated:

| Required Safeguard | Status | Authority |
|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| Verified pleading or affidavit | **VIOLATED** — unsworn motion | MCR 3.207(B)(1) |
| Specific facts showing immediate harm | **VIOLATED** — only hearsay | MCR 3.206(B)(5) |
| Notice to Father before deprivation | **VIOLATED** — no notice | Due Process Clause |
| Child-specific findings of fact | **VIOLATED** — checkbox form | MCR 2.517(A)(1) |
| Hearing "as soon as possible" | **VIOLATED** — 214+ days, no merits hearing | MCR 3.206(B)(6) |
| Opportunity to be heard | **VIOLATED** — no full evidentiary hearing | Due Process Clause |

40. The private interest at stake — the parent-child relationship — is of the highest constitutional magnitude. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753-54 (1982). The risk of erroneous deprivation was maximized by the absence of every procedural safeguard. The governmental interest in protecting children is served by the safeguards, not by their abandonment.

#### Conclusion

41. Father's fundamental right to parent his child was deprived without adequate procedure. The ex parte orders must be vacated, and parenting time must be restored immediately pending a proper hearing.

### ARGUMENT IV: The Trial Court Failed to Schedule a Timely Post-Ex Parte Hearing

#### Issue

42. Whether the trial court abused its discretion by allowing the ex parte suspension to persist for 214+ days since last contact without a merits hearing.

#### Rule

43. MCR 3.206(B)(6) mandates that after entry of an ex parte order, the court "shall" schedule a hearing "as soon as possible." This is not permissive — it is mandatory. Ex parte orders are emergency measures, not permanent custody arrangements.

#### Application

44. **214+ days** since last contact is not "as soon as possible." It is a wholesale abdication of the court's duty under MCR 3.206(B)(6).

45. Despite Father's Motion to Restore Parenting Time and subsequent filings notifying the court that the ex parte suspension was causing ongoing, escalating harm, no merits hearing was scheduled.

46. The effect of this delay has been to create a de facto permanent custody arrangement — one in which Mother has sole, uncontested physical custody and Father has been completely eliminated from L.D.W.'s life. This court-created status quo will prejudice any future hearing, as the passage of time itself becomes evidence against Father.

#### Conclusion

47. The trial court's failure to hold a timely hearing violates MCR 3.206(B)(6) and constitutes an independent ground for vacating the ex parte orders.

### ARGUMENT VI: Factor (j) — Parental Alienation by Defendant

#### Issue

54. Whether Defendant's conduct in seeking and obtaining total elimination of Father's parenting time through unsworn ex parte proceedings, combined with the 329+ days of enforced separation, constitutes relevant evidence under MCL 722.23(j).

#### Rule

55. MCL 722.23(j) requires the court to consider "the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192; 680 NW2d 835 (2004), recognized Factor (j) as "an important consideration" in custody disputes.

#### Application

56. Defendant's conduct demonstrates a deliberate, sustained effort to destroy the father-child bond:
   - Filed an **unsworn** ex parte motion seeking the most extreme remedy — total suspension;
   - Made no effort to propose any intermediate remedy (supervised visits, conditions);
   - Has maintained the complete separation for **329+ days** without offering any alternative;
   - Previously withheld L.D.W. during holidays and scheduled parenting time;
   - Leveraged her nine (9) years of employment as a caseworker in the **Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division** — institutional knowledge of family court procedures used to initiate and sustain alienation.

57. The court's failure to enforce MCL 722.27a(9)'s mandatory remedies has allowed the alienation to continue unchecked. MCL 722.27a(9) provides that the Court "*shall* do 1 or more of the following" when parenting time is wrongfully denied — the word "shall" is mandatory, not discretionary. *Walters v. Nadell*, 481 Mich 377, 383 (2008).

#### Conclusion

58. Defendant's conduct is highly probative of Factor (j) alienation and must be weighed against her in any best interest analysis. The court's refusal to apply MCL 722.27a(9) mandatory remedies is clear legal error.

MCL 722.27a(7) provides: "If a child's parenting time with a parent is substantially interrupted or unreasonably denied by the other parent or by the court, the court shall do 1 or more of the following: (a) Grant additional parenting time to the aggrieved parent to make up for the parenting time that was denied."
