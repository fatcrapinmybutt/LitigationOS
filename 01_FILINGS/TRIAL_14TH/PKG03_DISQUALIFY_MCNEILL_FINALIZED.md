### III. LEGAL STANDARD

#### A. Federal Constitutional Standard

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires disqualification in cases where the probability of actual bias is constitutionally intolerable, even without proof of actual bias. *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 883-84. This principle derives from the foundational requirement that "[a] fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." *In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955).

The *Caperton* Court articulated an objective standard: whether, "under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness," the interest at stake "poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden if the guarantee of due process is to be adequately implemented." 556 U.S. at 883-84 (quoting *Withrow v. Larkin*, 421 U.S. 35, 47 (1975)).

#### B. Michigan Standard — MCR 2.003(C)(1)

Michigan Court Rule 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification on the following grounds relevant here:

> **(a)** The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
>
> **(b)** The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868; 129 S Ct 2252; 173 L Ed 2d 1208 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct.

The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that the right to a fair and impartial judge is "fundamental to our system of justice." *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich. 470, 497 (1996). The standard is objective: "whether a reasonable person would perceive a significant risk of bias." *Id.* at 495.

In *Crampton v. Department of State*, 395 Mich. 347, 351 (1975), the Michigan Supreme Court recognized that due process requires a "fair and impartial decision maker" and that "the appearance of justice is as important as justice itself."

### IV. ARGUMENT

#### A. The Caperton Due Process Framework Requires Disqualification

The *Caperton* framework applies where the totality of circumstances creates a constitutionally intolerable probability of bias. 556 U.S. at 884. The Court emphasized that the inquiry is objective and does not require proof of actual bias — only that "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 872 (quoting *Withrow*, 421 U.S. at 47).

The record here satisfies this standard. The combination of:

- **150 documented ex parte violations**, representing a 44% ex parte order rate;
- **Derogatory language** ("crazy," "liar") directed at a party within judicial orders;
- **Retaliatory sanctions** imposed for exercising the right to file motions;
- **Systematic one-sided adjudication**, denying Father's motions without substantive analysis while granting Mother's requests;
- **Failure to apply mandatory legal standards** (MCL 722.23 best-interest factors); and
- **Disregard of exculpatory evidence** (negative drug screens)

— collectively demonstrate a probability of bias that no reasonable observer could dismiss. Under *Caperton*, this level of systematic deviation from neutral adjudication compels disqualification as a matter of constitutional law.

#### B. Michigan Disqualification Standards Under MCR 2.003

##### 1. Personal Bias or Prejudice — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a)

Personal bias or prejudice sufficient for disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) is established where the judge's conduct reflects an "attitude or state of mind that prevents impartial judgment." *Cain*, 451 Mich. at 495.

The use of words "crazy" and "liar" in judicial orders is direct evidence of personal animus. Such characterizations are not legal conclusions; they are pejorative labels reflecting the Court's subjective assessment of a party's character, not findings of fact based on evidence.

The imposition of $250 in sanctions for filing motions, absent an MCR 1.109(D) or MCR 2.625 frivolousness finding, further demonstrates personal bias. The logical inference from sanctioning a party for exercising fundamental legal rights is that the Court views Father's participation in the proceedings as unwelcome — the definition of prejudice.

##### 2. Serious Risk of Actual Bias — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i)

Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i), disqualification is warranted where there exists "a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton*."

The documented 44% ex parte order rate establishes this risk. In a proceeding governed by the adversarial system, a rate of ex parte adjudication approaching one-half of all orders is fundamentally incompatible with due process. *See* MCR 2.119(A) (requiring notice of motions); MCR 3.207 (limiting ex parte relief in domestic relations cases to genuine emergencies).

##### 3. Appearance of Impropriety — MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii)

Under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(ii), disqualification is also warranted where the judge "has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct."

As set forth in Section E below, the record demonstrates multiple Canon 2 violations.

#### C. Specific Instances of Judicial Bias

The following specific instances, drawn from the record and supported by the accompanying Affidavit and Exhibits, illustrate the pattern of bias:

##### Instance 1: Ex Parte Order of August 8, 2025

The Court entered an order on or about August 8, 2025, affecting Father's custodial rights. This order was entered without prior notice to Father, without an opportunity for Father to be heard, and without a finding of emergency circumstances as required by MCR 3.207. The order was based entirely on an ex parte communication from Mother or Mother's counsel. This violated Father's right to notice and an opportunity to be heard. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976).

##### Instance 2: Derogatory Language in Court Orders

The Court characterized Father as "crazy" and a "liar" in written orders. Judicial orders are public documents that carry the authority and imprimatur of the court. The inclusion of pejorative, non-legal characterizations in such documents is inconsistent with the requirement of judicial impartiality and evidences personal hostility toward Father. *See* MCJC Canon 3(A)(3) ("A judge should be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others with whom the judge deals in an official capacity.").

##### Instance 3: Retaliatory Sanctions

The Court imposed $250 in sanctions against Father for filing motions. The sanctions were not preceded by a finding that Father's filings were frivolous under MCR 1.109(D), nor were they authorized under any other specific sanctions provision. The timing and context of the sanctions — imposed after Father exercised his right to seek judicial relief — create an objective inference of retaliation.

##### Instance 4: Disregard of Negative Drug Screen Evidence

Father submitted negative drug screen results as evidence in support of his position. The Court failed to address this evidence in its rulings, while simultaneously crediting unsubstantiated allegations of substance abuse against Father. A neutral tribunal would evaluate all competent evidence; the selective disregard of evidence favorable to one party is an indicator of bias. *See* *Gates v. Gates*, 256 Mich. App. 420, 432 (2003) (trial court must consider all relevant evidence in custody determinations).

##### Instance 5: Failure to Apply MCL 722.23

Michigan's Child Custody Act requires the trial court to evaluate and make findings on each of the enumerated best-interest factors in MCL 722.23 when making custody determinations. *See* MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich. App. 499, 508-09 (2003). The Court's systematic failure to make these required findings, while entering orders modifying custody and parenting time, constitutes a procedural deficiency that, in context, further evidences the Court's departure from neutral adjudication.

##### Instance 6: Systematic Denial Without Analysis

Father's motions have been systematically denied without substantive analysis. A comparison of the Court's treatment of Father's motions versus Mother's motions reveals a statistically significant disparity. This pattern is inconsistent with impartial adjudication and supports a finding of bias.

#### E. Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct Violations

##### 1. MCJC Canon 2 — Impropriety and Appearance of Impropriety

Canon 2 provides: "A judge should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities."

Canon 2(A) further provides: "A judge should respect and comply with the law and should act at all times in a manner that promotes public confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the judiciary."

The documented pattern of ex parte communications, derogatory language, and retaliatory sanctions violates Canon 2 because it undermines public confidence in the impartiality of the proceedings. A reasonable, informed observer would not have confidence that Father is receiving a fair hearing before this Court.

**Documented Canon 2 violations:** 150 instances of MCJC Canon violations have been identified in the record.

##### 2. MCJC Canon 3 — Impartiality and Diligence

Canon 3(A)(3) requires: "A judge should be patient, dignified, and courteous to litigants, jurors, witnesses, lawyers, and others with whom the judge deals in an official capacity."

The use of "crazy" and "liar" in judicial orders violates the courtesy and dignity requirements of Canon 3(A)(3).

Canon 3(A)(4) provides: "A judge should accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding, or that person's lawyer, the right to be heard according to law."

The 150 documented ex parte violations and the 44% ex parte order rate establish a systematic failure to accord Father the right to be heard, in violation of Canon 3(A)(4).

Canon 3(A)(5) prohibits ex parte communications concerning a pending or impending proceeding. The 150 documented instances of ex parte violations constitute a pervasive pattern of non-compliance with Canon 3(A)(5).

### III. GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

#### A. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

7. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) requires disqualification where a judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney."

9. The Court has entered orders based on ex parte communications on no fewer than 150 documented occasions, representing a 44% ex parte order rate. This rate is statistically anomalous and inconsistent with the requirements of MCR 2.119 and due process.

   **(b) Derogatory Characterizations in Judicial Orders**

11. The Court has employed the terms "crazy" and "liar" in reference to Father within judicial orders. Such language is inconsistent with the objective, dispassionate tone required of judicial decision-making and evidences personal animus rather than reasoned adjudication. *See* MCJC Canon 2(A) (requiring conduct that promotes public confidence in the judiciary); MCJC Canon 3(A)(3) (requiring patience, dignity, and courtesy).

   **(c) Retaliatory Sanctions for Exercising Legal Rights**

12. The Court imposed $250 in sanctions against Father for filing motions in this proceeding. The right to petition the court for relief is fundamental. *See* U.S. Const. amend. I; Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 3. Imposing monetary sanctions for the exercise of this right, absent a finding that specific filings were frivolous under MCR 1.109(D) or MCR 2.625, evidences a retaliatory motive constituting bias.

   **(d) Systematic Denial of Father's Motions Without Substantive Analysis**

13. The Court has systematically denied Father's motions without conducting the substantive analysis required by applicable legal standards. In custody matters, the court is required to make findings on each of the best-interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23. The Court's failure to do so, while granting Mother's requests, demonstrates a pattern of one-sided adjudication.

   **(e) Disregard of Exculpatory Evidence**

14. The Court has disregarded negative drug screen results favorable to Father while relying on unsubstantiated allegations against him. The selective consideration of evidence — crediting allegations against one party while ignoring objective evidence favorable to that party — constitutes an indicator of bias. *See* *Cain*, 451 Mich. at 495-96.

#### B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — Personal Knowledge of Disputed Facts

15. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification where a judge "has personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding."

16. The volume and nature of the 150 documented ex parte contacts create a reasonable inference that the Court has obtained information outside the record. A judge who has received such extensive off-record communications necessarily possesses personal knowledge of disputed facts that were not subject to adversarial testing. *See* *In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955) ("[N]o man can be a judge in his own case and no man is permitted to try cases where he has an interest in the outcome.").
