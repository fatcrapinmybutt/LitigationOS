## I. MCR 2.119(B)(7) REQUIREMENTS

MCR 2.119(B)(7) permits a party to seek emergency relief upon a showing that:

1. **Irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result** from the delay necessitated by following regular motion procedures;
2. **The applicant has made a reasonable effort** to notify the adverse party; and
3. **The motion is supported** by an affidavit setting forth facts showing the necessity for emergency relief.

Each of these requirements is satisfied here.

# PART III: BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF EMERGENCY MOTION

## I. CONSTITUTIONAL IMPERATIVE

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that parental rights are among the most fundamental of all constitutional rights:

> "The interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children -- is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

> "The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State." *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

> "A parent's interest in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children rises to a constitutionally protected level." *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972).

These rights demand the highest procedural protections. A 571+ day deprivation of all parenting time, based on ex parte orders entered without notice or hearing, and in the face of overwhelming exculpatory evidence, cannot withstand constitutional scrutiny under any standard of review.

## II. MICHIGAN STATUTORY PRESUMPTION

Michigan law creates a strong presumption in favor of parenting time:

> "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(3).

This presumption has not been rebutted. Every objective measure -- drug screen, psychological evaluation, police investigations -- confirms that parenting time with Plaintiff poses no danger to the children. The burden is on the party opposing parenting time to demonstrate danger by clear and convincing evidence, and that burden has not been met.

## III. EMERGENCY STANDARD IS MET

The standard for emergency relief under MCR 2.119(B)(7) is satisfied:

1. **Irreparable harm**: The ongoing destruction of the father-child relationship constitutes irreparable harm that no remedy can undo. See *Mich. Coalition of Radioactive Material Users, Inc. v. Griepentrog*, 945 F.2d 150, 154 (6th Cir. 1991) (harm is irreparable when it cannot be adequately compensated by monetary damages).

2. **No adequate remedy**: The passage of time in a child's life cannot be restored by any court order. *Elrod v. Burns*, 427 U.S. at 373.

3. **Likelihood of success**: Plaintiff has demonstrated, through objective evidence, that he is a fit and capable parent. The exculpatory evidence is overwhelming and uncontradicted.

4. **Public interest**: The public interest favors maintaining parent-child relationships and ensuring due process in family court proceedings. *Parham v. J.R.*, 442 U.S. 584, 602 (1979).

The American Psychological Association, the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, and leading child development researchers uniformly recognize that:

At 571+ days and counting, this case has crossed every recognized threshold for intervention. Further delay is not a neutral act -- it actively deepens the harm.
