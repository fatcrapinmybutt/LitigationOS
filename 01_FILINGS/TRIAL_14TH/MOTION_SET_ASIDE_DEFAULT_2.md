## I. LEGAL STANDARD — MCR 2.612(C)(1)

MCR 2.612(C)(1) provides:

> "On motion and on just terms, the court may relieve a party or the legal representative of a party from a final judgment, order, or proceeding for the following reasons:
> (a) Mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.
> (b) Newly discovered evidence which by due diligence could not have been discovered in time to move for a new trial under subrule 2.611(B).
> (c) Fraud (whether heretofore denominated intrinsic or extrinsic), misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party.
> (d) The judgment is void.
> (e) The judgment has been satisfied, released, or discharged; a prior judgment on which it is based has been reversed or otherwise vacated; or it is no longer equitable that the judgment should have prospective application.
> (f) Any other reason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment."

The standard for relief under MCR 2.612(C)(1) is whether "justice requires setting aside the judgment." *Heugel v Heugel*, 237 Mich App 471, 478 (1999). The movant must demonstrate both a meritorious defense and a legally cognizable ground for relief. *Shawl v Spence Brothers, Inc.*, 280 Mich App 213, 221 (2008).

### MCR 2.612(C)(1) — All Applicable Grounds

Relief from judgment is warranted under multiple subsections of MCR 2.612(C)(1):

| Subsection | Ground | Application |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect** | Father was served Friday 08/08 for Monday 08/11 hearing — zero business days to prepare. Any failure to mount adequate defense resulted from the impossible timeline, not neglect. |
| **(b) Newly discovered evidence** | HealthWest evaluation (09/05/2025) finding zero pathology across all domains; Albert Watson's police admission re pre-planned ex parte; 2nd assessment bias from court letter |
| **(c) Fraud, misrepresentation, or other misconduct** | Emily's false allegations of 'meth use,' 'ongoing drug use,' 'extreme paranoia' — all contradicted by HealthWest evaluation; Albert Watson's pre-planning of the ex parte filing |
| **(d) Judgment is void** | Orders entered without MCR 3.207(B) written findings and without MCR 3.207(C)(5) evidentiary hearing — jurisdictional prerequisites not met |
| **(f) Any other reason justifying relief** | 329+ days without evidentiary hearing; 25%+ of child's life in complete separation from father; cumulative pattern of constitutional violations |

## II. EACH GROUND IS INDEPENDENTLY SUFFICIENT

### A. Void Orders Require No Balancing — Ground (d)

Relief under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) is **mandatory** when an order is void. *See Pastrick v Gen. Tel. Co.*, 162 Mich App 243, 247 (1987) ("A void judgment is a legal nullity and must be vacated upon motion.").

An order entered in violation of due process is void. The ex parte orders at issue were entered:
- Without notice to Father (violating MCR 2.119(B));
- Without findings of endangerment (violating MCR 3.207(B));
- Without a hearing within 14 days (violating MCR 3.207(C)(5));
- Without any hearing on the merits for 329+ days;
- In violation of Michigan Const Art. 1, § 17 and US Const, Amend. XIV.

These orders are void as a matter of law and must be vacated.

### Void Order Doctrine — Expanded Analysis

Under Michigan law, an order is void ab initio — not merely voidable — when it is entered without jurisdiction or in fundamental violation of due process. See In re Rood, 483 Mich 73, 107-108 (2009) (*'A void judgment is a nullity and may be vacated at any time'*).

**The ex parte orders are void, not merely voidable, because:**

1. **Jurisdictional defect — MCR 3.207(B) non-compliance:** MCR 3.207(B) requires *written findings* that the child's health, safety, or welfare would be endangered by continuation of the existing arrangement. These findings are a *jurisdictional prerequisite* to entry of an ex parte order modifying custody or parenting time. No such findings were made. Without them, the court lacked authority to enter the orders.

2. **Structural due process violation:** The failure to hold an evidentiary hearing within 14 days (MCR 3.207(C)(5)) is not a mere procedural defect — it is a structural constitutional violation. The Supreme Court in Fuentes v. Shevin, 407 U.S. 67, 82 (1972) held that post-deprivation procedures are constitutionally required to accompany ex parte deprivations. The 329+ day delay in providing any hearing transforms what might have been a temporary emergency measure into an unconstitutional permanent deprivation.

3. **Fruit of the poisonous tree:** The ex parte orders were procured through false statements (Emily's 'meth use' allegation with no drug test, 'extreme paranoia' contradicted by Psychosis=0, 'ongoing drug use' contradicted by Substance=0). An order procured by fraud on the court is void. See People v. Burge, 722 NW2d 470 (Mich App 2006).

4. **Temporal analysis:** A void order may be challenged at any time — there is no statute of limitations. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) (fraud) and (f) (void judgment) provide independent bases for relief, and the one-year limitation in MCR 2.612(C)(2) does not apply to subdivisions (d), (e), or (f).

### B. Fraud on the Court Requires Vacatur — Ground (c)

Where a judgment is obtained through fraud, the court has an **independent duty** to vacate it, regardless of any other considerations. *Draggoo v Draggoo*, 223 Mich App 415 (1997).

Albert Watson's admission to police — that the family planned to use the police report to obtain an ex parte custody order the next day — constitutes direct evidence that the August 8, 2025 orders were obtained through a pre-planned scheme rather than genuine emergency. This is fraud upon the court.

### C. Newly Discovered Clinical Evidence Is Dispositive — Ground (b)

The HealthWest evaluation is not merely "new" — it is **dispositive**. It directly contradicts every factual allegation upon which the ex parte orders were based. A professional clinical evaluation finding zero danger, zero psychosis, zero substance abuse, and lowest-severity anxiety constitutes newly discovered evidence that requires a different outcome.

### D. The Interest of Justice Demands Relief — Ground (f)

The Michigan Supreme Court has held that MCR 2.612(C)(1)(f) should be "sparingly invoked" but applied in "extraordinary circumstances." *Heugel v Heugel*, 237 Mich App at 479. The circumstances here are extraordinary:
- 329+ days of parent-child separation without evidentiary hearing
- 52+ ex parte orders, 44% of all orders
- Clinical evidence categorically refuting every allegation
- Documented fraud and pre-planning
- Prohibited ex parte communications by court staff
- Child in critical developmental period suffering irreparable harm

## III. PLAINTIFF HAS A MERITORIOUS DEFENSE

Father's meritorious defense to the ex parte orders consists of:

1. **Clinical Evidence:** HealthWest evaluation finding no psychosis, no substance abuse, no danger, lowest severity level.
2. **13+ Years of Voluntary Therapy:** Services of Hope engagement since 2012 contradicts "untreated mental illness" narrative.
3. **Full Compliance:** Father attended every evaluation, submitted results promptly, and followed all court directives.
4. **Pre-Planned Attack:** The ex parte motion was not a response to genuine emergency but a pre-planned legal maneuver.
5. **No Drug Test:** Despite "meth use" allegation, no drug screen was ever ordered or conducted.

## IV. CONCLUSION

Every ground under MCR 2.612(C)(1) supports vacatur of the ex parte orders. The void-order ground alone is dispositive. The cumulative weight of all six grounds makes vacatur not merely appropriate but constitutionally required.
