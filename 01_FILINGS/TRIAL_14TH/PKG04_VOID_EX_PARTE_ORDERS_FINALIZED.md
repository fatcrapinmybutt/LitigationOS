## I. INTRODUCTION

This Brief is submitted in support of Plaintiff-Father's Motion to Set Aside Void Ex Parte Orders pursuant to MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) and (f). The central question presented is whether ex parte orders that suspend all of a father's parenting time — entered without notice, without sworn evidence, without specific findings of fact, and without a best interest analysis — are void ab initio.

The answer, under both Michigan and federal constitutional law, is yes.

The distinction between void and voidable orders is not academic. A voidable order is one entered by a court with jurisdiction that may contain error but retains legal force unless reversed on appeal. A void order is a legal nullity — "as though it never existed." *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 107-108 (2009). The orders at issue here are void because they were entered in violation of the most fundamental requirements of due process: notice, an opportunity to be heard, and an evidentiary basis for the deprivation of a constitutionally protected right.

## III. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. MCR 2.612(C)(1) — Relief from Judgment or Order

MCR 2.612(C)(1) provides:

> "On motion and on just terms, the court may relieve a party or the legal representative of a party from a final judgment, order, or proceeding for the following reasons:
> ...
> (d) the judgment or order is void;
> ...
> (f) any other reason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment or order."

### B. Void vs. Voidable — The Critical Distinction

The Michigan Supreme Court has drawn a clear line between void and voidable judgments:

> "A void judgment is a legal nullity. It is as though it never existed." *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 107-108 (2009).

A judgment is **voidable** when it is entered by a court with jurisdiction but contains some error of law or procedure that may warrant reversal. A judgment is **void** when:

- The court lacked **subject matter jurisdiction**; or
- The court lacked **personal jurisdiction** over the parties; or
- The judgment was entered in violation of **due process of law**.

*See In re Rood*, 483 Mich at 107-108; *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 39-40 (1992); *Restatement (Second) of Judgments* § 12 (1982).

A void judgment may be challenged at any time. It confers no rights, imposes no duties, and affords no protection. *Id.*

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. The Identified Orders Are Void, Not Merely Voidable

The ex parte orders at issue are not merely erroneous — they are void. The distinction is dispositive.

An erroneous order is one where a court with jurisdiction makes a mistake of law or fact. Such an order is voidable: it retains legal force unless reversed through the ordinary appellate process.

A void order is one entered without jurisdiction or in violation of due process. It has no legal effect from the moment of its entry — void ab initio.

The orders at issue were entered in violation of the most basic requirements of procedural due process:

| Due Process Requirement | July 1, 2024 Order | August 8, 2025 Order |
|-------------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Prior notice to Father | ❌ Not provided | ❌ Not provided |
| Opportunity to be heard | ❌ Not provided | ❌ Not provided |
| Sworn evidence or verified pleading | ❌ Not provided | ❌ Not provided |
| Specific findings of fact | ❌ Checkbox form | ❌ Checkbox form |
| Best interest analysis (MCL 722.23) | ❌ Not conducted | ❌ Not conducted |
| Evidentiary hearing (before or promptly after) | ❌ Not conducted | ❌ Not conducted |
| Less restrictive alternatives considered | ❌ Not addressed | ❌ Not addressed |

Every procedural safeguard designed to protect the fundamental right to parent was absent. These are not minor procedural defects. They are structural failures that render the orders void.

### B. Due Process Analysis Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*

The United States Supreme Court established the framework for evaluating procedural due process in *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). The *Mathews* balancing test requires consideration of three factors:

> (1) The **private interest** affected by the official action;
> (2) The **risk of erroneous deprivation** of that interest through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional procedural safeguards; and
> (3) The **government's interest**, including the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional procedural requirements.

*Id.* at 335.

#### Factor 1: The Private Interest — Fundamental Right to Parent

The private interest at stake is the "oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by" the Supreme Court: the right to the care, custody, and companionship of one's child. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

This interest is not merely "important" — it is **fundamental**:

- "The interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children ... is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65.

- "The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents." *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

- The Michigan Supreme Court recognizes this right as "of the highest order." *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014).

**This factor weighs decisively in Father's favor.** No private interest is more strongly protected.

#### Factor 2: Risk of Erroneous Deprivation — Exceedingly High

The procedures employed here — or rather, the absence of procedures — created an unacceptable risk of erroneous deprivation:

**a. Unsworn, inadmissible hearsay.** The motion underlying the August 8 Order was not sworn and was not supported by affidavit. The factual assertions contained therein are hearsay — inadmissible in any proceeding. *MRE 801, 802.* An order based entirely on inadmissible evidence is necessarily unreliable.

**b. No adversarial testing.** Father was given no opportunity to cross-examine witnesses, present contrary evidence, or challenge the assertions in the motion. Cross-examination is "the greatest legal engine ever invented for the discovery of truth." *5 Wigmore, Evidence* § 1367 (Chadbourn rev. 1974). Its absence fatally undermines the reliability of the proceeding.

**c. Pre-printed checkbox forms.** The orders were entered on standardized forms that require only the checking of boxes, without any requirement of specific, individualized findings. Such forms create a structural risk of erroneous deprivation by eliminating the judicial obligation to articulate the factual basis for the decision.

**d. No consideration of alternatives.** The most extreme remedy — total elimination of parenting time — was imposed without any analysis of less restrictive alternatives. Supervised visitation, therapeutic visitation, graduated reintroduction, or other intermediate measures were neither considered nor addressed. This "all or nothing" approach maximizes the risk of disproportionate deprivation.

**The probable value of additional safeguards — notice, a hearing, sworn testimony, cross-examination, specific findings — is obvious and enormous.** These are not novel procedural innovations. They are the most basic tools of procedural due process.

**This factor weighs decisively in Father's favor.**

#### Factor 3: Government Interest — Adequately Served by Less Restrictive Means

The government's interest in protecting children is legitimate, substantial, and not disputed. But this interest does not require — and is not served by — the complete elimination of parenting time without due process.

The government's interest in child safety is **adequately served** by procedures that include:

- **Prior notice** to the non-moving party (or at minimum, notice within 24-48 hours with an expedited hearing);
- **An evidentiary hearing** at which sworn testimony is presented and subject to cross-examination;
- **Specific findings of fact** explaining the basis for the court's determination;
- **Consideration of less restrictive alternatives** such as supervised parenting time.

These safeguards impose **no significant fiscal or administrative burden** on the court system. They are already required by existing court rules (MCR 3.207(B)(1), MCR 2.517(A)(1), MCL 722.23). The government's interest in child safety is fully served by enforcing the procedures already on the books.

**This factor does not support the procedures — or lack thereof — employed here.**

#### Conclusion of *Mathews* Analysis

All three *Mathews* factors weigh in Father's favor. The private interest is the strongest recognized in law. The risk of erroneous deprivation is at its zenith. The government's interest is adequately served by existing procedural safeguards that were not employed. The procedures used — ex parte action on unsworn motions, without notice, hearing, sworn evidence, or specific findings — are constitutionally inadequate.

### C. Michigan Constitutional Analysis: Art. 1, § 17

Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963 provides:

> "No person shall be ... deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law."

The Michigan Supreme Court has held that the Michigan Due Process Clause provides protections "at least as extensive" as those of the federal Due Process Clause. *In re Carmody*, 475 Mich 1088 (2006); *People v. Sierb*, 456 Mich 519, 523 (1998).

Father's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is a liberty interest protected by Art. 1, § 17. The ex parte deprivation of this interest, without notice, hearing, or sworn evidence, violates the Michigan Constitution independently of any federal constitutional analysis.

The Michigan Court Rules themselves codify the due process protections required by Art. 1, § 17:

- **MCR 2.107**: Service of process and notice requirements.
- **MCR 3.207(B)(1)**: Verified pleadings or sworn testimony in domestic relations actions.
- **MCR 2.517(A)(1)**: Specific findings of fact in bench proceedings.
- **MCL 722.23**: Best interest analysis in custody and parenting time determinations.

The systematic violation of these rules is not merely a procedural irregularity — it is a constitutional violation.

### D. Federal Constitutional Analysis: Amendments V and XIV

The Fourteenth Amendment provides:

> "No State shall ... deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."

The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his or her child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment:

- *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923)
- *Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510 (1925)
- *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972)
- *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982)
- *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)

In *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545 (1965), the Supreme Court addressed the precise issue presented here: whether a parent's rights can be affected by court proceedings conducted without notice to the parent. The Court held:

> "A fundamental requirement of due process is 'the opportunity to be heard.' ... It is an opportunity which must be granted at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Id.* at 552.

The Court further held that after-the-fact review does not cure the constitutional defect:

> "[T]he right to be heard before being condemned to suffer grievous loss of any kind ... is a principle basic to our society." *Id.* (quoting *Joint Anti-Fascist Refugee Committee v. McGrath*, 341 U.S. 123, 168 (1951) (Frankfurter, J., concurring)).

In *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971), the Court held that due process requires meaningful access to courts in proceedings affecting fundamental family interests. A parent cannot be deprived of fundamental rights through proceedings to which he was not a party and of which he had no notice.

The ex parte orders at issue violate the core holdings of *Armstrong*, *Boddie*, *Mathews*, and *Santosky*.

#### 3. Common Deficiency: Disproportionate Remedy

Both orders imposed the most extreme remedy available — total elimination of parenting time — without any consideration of proportionality. The Supreme Court has recognized that the "total destruction" of a parent-child relationship requires the most exacting procedural protections:

> "The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State. Even when blood relationships are strained, parents retain a vital interest in preventing the irretrievable destruction of their family life." *Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 753.

Father's negative drug screen (Exhibit PKG04-005) further demonstrates the absence of an evidentiary basis for the most extreme remedy. Evidence that directly contradicts the basis for the deprivation was neither considered nor addressed.

### F. No Time Limit on Challenging Void Orders

MCR 2.612(C)(2) imposes a "reasonable time" limitation on motions under subsections (a)-(c) and a one-year limitation on motions under subsections (a)-(c). However, **subsection (d) — void judgments — is not subject to any time limitation**:

> "A motion under this subrule ... must be made within a reasonable time, and, for reasons (a), (b), and (c), within one year after the judgment, order, or proceeding was entered or taken." MCR 2.612(C)(2).

Subsection (d) is conspicuously excluded from this time limitation. This exclusion reflects the settled principle that a void judgment "is a legal nullity" that "may be attacked at any time." *In re Rood*, 483 Mich at 108; *see also Restatement (Second) of Judgments* § 12 cmt. a (1982) ("A judgment that is void is in legal effect no judgment at all.").

Father's motion to set aside the void ex parte orders is therefore timely regardless of when it is filed.

Every procedural safeguard that the United States Constitution, the Michigan Constitution, and the Michigan Court Rules require before a parent may be deprived of the fundamental right to the care, custody, and companionship of his child was absent.

These orders are not erroneous. They are void. They are legal nullities. They should be set aside.

## II. VOID AB INITIO DOCTRINE

A void order is a legal nullity — "as though it never existed." *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 107-108 (2009). An order is void — not merely voidable — when entered:

## II. LEGAL STANDARD — VOID AB INITIO DOCTRINE

12. MCR 2.612(C)(1) provides in relevant part:

> *"On motion and on just terms, the court may relieve a party or the legal representative of a party from a final judgment, order, or proceeding for the following reasons:*
> *(d) the judgment or order is void;*
> *(f) any other reason justifying relief from the operation of the judgment or order."*

13. A judgment or order is **void** — not merely voidable — when it is entered without jurisdiction or in violation of due process of law. *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 107-108 (2009).

14. "A void judgment is a legal nullity. It is as though it never existed." *Id.*

15. There is **no time limit** on challenging a void order. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) is not subject to the "reasonable time" limitation applicable to other subsections. *See* MCR 2.612(C)(2); *In re Rood*, 483 Mich at 108.

## III. THE IDENTIFIED ORDERS ARE VOID — NOT MERELY VOIDABLE

### A. No Constitutionally Adequate Notice

16. The Due Process Clauses of the United States Constitution (Amendments V and XIV) and the Michigan Constitution (Art. 1, § 17) require that no person be deprived of a protected liberty interest without notice and an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976).

17. A parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of his or her child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014).

18. "A fundamental requirement of due process is 'the opportunity to be heard.' It is an opportunity which must be granted at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 552 (1965).

19. In *Armstrong*, the United States Supreme Court held that a father's rights could not be terminated through adoption proceedings conducted without notice to the father, even where the father later had an opportunity to seek to set aside the decree. The Court held that after-the-fact review was not a constitutionally adequate substitute for prior notice. *Id.* at 550-552.

20. Father received **no notice** before entry of the August 8, 2025 Order and the July 1, 2024 Order. The deprivation of his fundamental parental rights occurred without any opportunity to be heard prior to the deprivation.

23. MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that motions in domestic relations actions be supported by verified pleadings or sworn testimony establishing the factual basis for the relief sought.

24. The motions underlying the identified ex parte orders were **not sworn** and were **not supported by affidavit or verified pleading**.

25. An order entered on the basis of inadmissible hearsay, without sworn evidence, lacks a constitutionally adequate evidentiary foundation and is void for want of due process. *See Greene v. Lindsey*, 456 U.S. 444, 449 (1982).

### C. No Specific Findings of Fact

26. MCR 2.517(A)(1) requires that in actions tried without a jury, the court "find the facts specially and state separately its conclusions of law."

27. The identified orders were entered on **pre-printed checkbox forms** without any specific, individualized findings of fact explaining the factual and legal basis for the extraordinary relief of terminating all parenting time.

28. Checkbox forms do not satisfy the requirement of specific findings. A court must articulate the factual basis for its decision, particularly where fundamental rights are at stake. *See In re Rood*, 483 Mich at 107-108.

29. MCL 722.23 enumerates the best interest factors that must be considered in custody and parenting time determinations.

30. None of the identified orders contains an analysis of the statutory best interest factors. The total elimination of Father's parenting time — the most extreme remedy available — was imposed without any consideration of less restrictive alternatives, such as supervised parenting time.

### E. Disproportionate Remedy

31. Even where concerns about a child's safety exist, due process requires that the remedy be proportionate to the risk. The complete elimination of all parenting time is the functional equivalent of termination of parental rights and must be supported by clear and convincing evidence. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 747-748 (1982).

32. No evidence — let alone clear and convincing evidence — was presented to support total elimination of Father's parenting time. Less restrictive alternatives (e.g., supervised parenting time, therapeutic visitation) were neither considered nor addressed.

# ORDER VACATING VOID EX PARTE ORDERS

---

At a session of said Court held in the Muskegon County Courthouse,
City of Muskegon, County of Muskegon, State of Michigan,

on this _______ day of _________________, 20___.

**PRESENT: Honorable _________________________________, Circuit Court Judge**

---

This matter having come before the Court on Plaintiff-Father's Motion to Set Aside Void Ex Parte Orders pursuant to MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) and (f), and the Court having reviewed the motion, brief in support, and exhibits, and having heard the arguments of the parties, and being otherwise fully advised in the premises;

**THE COURT FINDS:**

1. The Ex Parte Order entered on or about **August 8, 2025**, suspending all of Father's parenting time, was entered without constitutionally adequate notice to Father, without an evidentiary hearing, without sworn evidence or verified pleading as required by MCR 3.207(B)(1), without specific findings of fact as required by MCR 2.517(A)(1), and without analysis of the best interest factors as required by MCL 722.23.

2. The Ex Parte Order entered on or about **July 1, 2024**, affecting Father's custodial rights, was entered without constitutionally adequate notice to Father, without an evidentiary hearing, without sworn evidence or verified pleading, and without specific findings of fact.

3. These orders were entered in violation of Father's rights under the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, § 17 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963.

4. Pursuant to MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d), the identified orders are **void ab initio**.

---

**IT IS HEREBY ORDERED:**

**A.** The Ex Parte Order entered on or about **August 8, 2025** is hereby **VACATED** as void ab initio and is of no legal force or effect from the date of its entry.

**B.** The Ex Parte Order entered on or about **July 1, 2024** is hereby **VACATED** as void ab initio and is of no legal force or effect from the date of its entry.

**C.** Any additional ex parte orders entered in this action affecting Father's custodial or parenting time rights without constitutionally adequate notice and opportunity to be heard are hereby **VACATED** as void ab initio.

**D.** The parenting time schedule in effect immediately prior to the **first** void ex parte order is hereby **RESTORED** as the operative parenting time schedule, effective immediately, pending further order of this Court.

**E.** An **evidentiary hearing** on custody and parenting time shall be scheduled within _______ days of the date of this Order. At such hearing:

   (i) Both parties shall have the right to present sworn testimony and documentary evidence;

   (ii) Both parties shall have the right to cross-examine witnesses;

   (iii) The Court shall make specific findings of fact on the record addressing each of the best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23;

   (iv) Any modification of parenting time shall be supported by admissible evidence and shall consider the least restrictive means of addressing any legitimate safety concern.

**F.** Pending the evidentiary hearing, neither party shall seek to modify the restored parenting time schedule except upon verified motion supported by sworn evidence demonstrating an immediate and irreparable risk of harm to the minor child(ren), with notice to the opposing party.

---

**IT IS SO ORDERED.**
