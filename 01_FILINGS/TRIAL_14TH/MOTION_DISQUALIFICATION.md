## MOTION FOR DISQUALIFICATION OF JUDGE JENNY L. McNEILL

---

**NOW COMES** Plaintiff-Father, Andrew J. Pigors, appearing *pro se*, and respectfully moves this Court for the disqualification of the Honorable Jenny L. McNeill from all further proceedings in this matter, pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) and (b), and in support thereof states as follows:

---

### I. INTRODUCTION

1. This Motion is filed pursuant to MCR 2.003(C)(1), which provides grounds for disqualification where a judge is personally biased or prejudiced for or against a party, or has personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding.

2. Father brings this Motion based on a documented pattern of judicial conduct that, viewed objectively, establishes an impermissible degree of bias requiring disqualification under Michigan Court Rules, the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, and the Due Process Clauses of the United States and Michigan Constitutions. U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1; Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 17.

3. This Motion is timely filed pursuant to MCR 2.003(D).

---

### II. JURISDICTION AND AUTHORITY

4. This Court has jurisdiction to hear motions for disqualification pursuant to MCR 2.003.

5. The Michigan Supreme Court has held that MCR 2.003 provides the exclusive procedural mechanism for disqualification of a judge in Michigan courts. *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich. 470, 494 (1996).

6. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that due process requires disqualification where the probability of actual bias is too high to be constitutionally tolerable. *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868, 872 (2009).

---

### III. GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION

#### A. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) — Personal Bias or Prejudice

7. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(a) requires disqualification where a judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney."

8. The record in this case establishes pervasive bias against Father through the following objective indicators:

   **(a) Pattern of Ex Parte Orders Without Notice or Hearing**

9. The Court has entered orders based on ex parte communications on no fewer than 150 documented occasions, representing a 44% ex parte order rate. This rate is statistically anomalous and inconsistent with the requirements of MCR 2.119 and due process.

10. On or about August 8, 2025, the Court entered an ex parte order materially affecting Father's custodial rights without providing notice, an opportunity to be heard, or any showing of the emergency circumstances required to justify ex parte relief under MCR 3.207.

   **(b) Derogatory Characterizations in Judicial Orders**

11. The Court has employed the terms "crazy" and "liar" in reference to Father within judicial orders. Such language is inconsistent with the objective, dispassionate tone required of judicial decision-making and evidences personal animus rather than reasoned adjudication. *See* MCJC Canon 2(A) (requiring conduct that promotes public confidence in the judiciary); MCJC Canon 3(A)(3) (requiring patience, dignity, and courtesy).

   **(c) Retaliatory Sanctions for Exercising Legal Rights**

12. The Court imposed $250 in sanctions against Father for filing motions in this proceeding. The right to petition the court for relief is fundamental. *See* U.S. Const. amend. I; Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 3. Imposing monetary sanctions for the exercise of this right, absent a finding that specific filings were frivolous under MCR 1.109(D) or MCR 2.625, evidences a retaliatory motive constituting bias.

   **(d) Systematic Denial of Father's Motions Without Substantive Analysis**

13. The Court has systematically denied Father's motions without conducting the substantive analysis required by applicable legal standards. In custody matters, the court is required to make findings on each of the best-interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23. The Court's failure to do so, while granting Mother's requests, demonstrates a pattern of one-sided adjudication.

   **(e) Disregard of Exculpatory Evidence**

14. The Court has disregarded negative drug screen results favorable to Father while relying on unsubstantiated allegations against him. The selective consideration of evidence — crediting allegations against one party while ignoring objective evidence favorable to that party — constitutes an indicator of bias. *See* *Cain*, 451 Mich. at 495-96.

#### B. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) — Personal Knowledge of Disputed Facts

15. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) requires disqualification where a judge "has personal knowledge of disputed evidentiary facts concerning the proceeding."

16. The volume and nature of the 150 documented ex parte contacts create a reasonable inference that the Court has obtained information outside the record. A judge who has received such extensive off-record communications necessarily possesses personal knowledge of disputed facts that were not subject to adversarial testing. *See* *In re Murchison*, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955) ("[N]o man can be a judge in his own case and no man is permitted to try cases where he has an interest in the outcome.").

---

### IV. CONSTITUTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

17. The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment establishes a constitutional floor for judicial disqualification that exists independently of MCR 2.003. *Caperton*, 556 U.S. at 876.

18. In *Caperton*, the Supreme Court held that recusal is constitutionally required when, "under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness," the risk of actual bias is "too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Id.* at 883-84 (internal quotation marks omitted).

19. The pattern documented herein — 1,127 violations across multiple categories including 377 at CRITICAL severity, a 44% ex parte order rate, derogatory language in orders, retaliatory sanctions, and systematic one-sided adjudication — satisfies the *Caperton* standard.

### V. TIMELINESS

20. This Motion is filed pursuant to MCR 2.003(D), which requires that a motion for disqualification be filed "within 14 days of the discovery of the grounds for disqualification." Father brings this Motion within the required timeframe of discovering the full scope of the grounds set forth herein.
