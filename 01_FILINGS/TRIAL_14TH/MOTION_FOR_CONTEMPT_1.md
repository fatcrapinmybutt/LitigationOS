## II. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. This Court Has Contempt Authority Over Violations of Its Parenting Time Orders

#### Issue

Whether this Court possesses the authority to hold Defendant in contempt for willful violation of parenting time orders.

#### Rule

MCR 3.606(A) provides:

> *"For a contempt committed outside the immediate view and presence of the court, on a proper showing on ex parte motion supported by affidavits, the court shall either (1) order the accused person to show cause, at a reasonable time specified in the order, why that person should not be punished for the alleged misconduct; or (2) issue a bench warrant for the arrest of the person."*

MCL 600.1701 grants Michigan circuit courts broad contempt powers, including the power to punish for *"[d]isobedience, resistance, or obstruction of the execution of an order."*

MCR 3.208(B) further provides that *"[t]he friend of the court is responsible for initiating proceedings to enforce an order or judgment for support, parenting time, or custody"* and that *"MCR 3.606 governs contempt proceedings under MCL 600.1701."*

#### Application

This Court entered a Parenting Time Order on August 23, 2024. (See Final Judgment, 8/23/2024; LC Record.) That order has been in continuous effect. *See* Order of this Court (dismissing Plaintiff's February 14, 2025 Motion and confirming: *"Parenting Time shall remain pursuant to the Order entered on August 23, 2024"*; LC Record). Despite the existence of this binding order, Defendant has facilitated **zero parenting time** for 329+ consecutive days. (See Calendar Exhibit B, calculating consecutive days from August 8, 2025 to present.) This is a textbook contempt: a clear, existing order has been willfully disobeyed.

#### Conclusion

This Court has unambiguous authority under MCR 3.606 and MCL 600.1701 to issue an Order to Show Cause and, upon hearing, to find Defendant in contempt.

### B. Defendant's Violation Is Willful and Contumacious

#### Issue

Whether Defendant's failure to comply with this Court's parenting time orders constitutes willful contempt.

#### Rule

To establish civil contempt, the moving party must demonstrate: (1) the existence of a clear and definite court order; (2) the contemnor's knowledge of the order; and (3) the contemnor's willful failure to comply. *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 668 (2009). A party's conduct is "willful" when it reflects a deliberate or intentional disregard of the court's order rather than an inability to comply. *Brausch v Brausch*, 283 Mich App 339, 348 (2009).

#### Application

All three elements are satisfied here:

**(1) Clear and Definite Order.** This Court entered a Parenting Time Order on August 23, 2024, which remains in effect by this Court's own confirmation. (See Final Judgment, 8/23/2024; Order re: Parenting Time Motion, 2025, confirming "Parenting Time shall remain pursuant to the Order entered on August 23, 2024"; LC Record.) The order's terms are clear and definite.

**(2) Knowledge.** Defendant has actual knowledge of the order. Defendant was a party to the proceedings, was represented by counsel (Jennifer L. Barnes, P55406), and has actively litigated in this case—including requesting the very ex parte orders that suspended Plaintiff's time. (See Watson Ex Parte Motion, 8/7/2025: "NOW COMES Defendant, Emily Watson, files the Emergency Ex Parte Motion to Suspend Parenting Time"; Exhibit A.) Defendant cannot claim ignorance.

**(3) Willful Failure to Comply.** The evidence establishes not mere non-compliance but a *pattern of deliberate obstruction*:

- **329+ consecutive days** of zero contact — this is not accidental; (See Calendar Exhibit B.)
- Defendant affirmatively *requested* ex parte orders to suspend parenting time; (See Exhibit A, Watson Ex Parte Motion, 8/7/2025: "NOW COMES Defendant, Emily Watson, files the Emergency Ex Parte Motion to Suspend Parenting Time.")
- Defendant failed to provide notice of parenting time cancellations as required; (See Pigors Declaration re: Service Failure, Exhibit D; MCL 722.27a(9).)
- Defendant engaged in ambush tactics at parenting-time exchanges; (See Exhibit D: "I was not served with the ex parte motion or order before an August 8, 2025 exchange.")
- Defendant exploited PPO proceedings to erect additional barriers; (See Docket, PPO Case No. 2023-5907-PP; Exhibit C, showing 24 ex parte orders — 44% of all orders.)
- The pattern is escalating, not diminishing—moving from restricted time to total severance. (See Docket Timeline: 7/1/2024 ex parte custody order → 9/15/2024 PPO → 8/8/2025 total suspension.)

The duration alone (329+ days) establishes willfulness. No parent who intends to comply with a parenting time order produces 329 consecutive days of zero contact. This is not inability—it is a deliberate choice. (R. at PIGORS-0417; Docket Entry No. 3, Final Judgment of Custody and Parenting Time, 8/23/2024)

#### Conclusion

Defendant's conduct constitutes willful, deliberate, and contumacious contempt of this Court's orders. A finding of contempt is warranted.

### C. Defendant's Conduct Constitutes Parental Alienation Under MCL 722.23(j)

#### Issue

Whether Defendant's systematic denial of parenting time constitutes a violation of the best-interest-of-the-child standard, specifically Factor (j) of MCL 722.23.

#### Rule

MCL 722.23 provides that courts shall consider, among other factors:

> *"(j) The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents."*

This factor is commonly referred to as the "alienation factor" and is given substantial weight in custody and parenting time determinations. *See Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457 (1996) (emphasizing the importance of facilitating the child's relationship with both parents).

#### Application

Defendant has demonstrated **zero willingness and zero ability** to facilitate the parent-child relationship between Plaintiff and the minor child:

- 329+ consecutive days of no contact in any form; (See Calendar Exhibit B; Docket Timeline.)
- Affirmative steps to *prevent* contact through ex parte orders, PPO proceedings, and procedural obstruction; (See Exhibit A, Watson Ex Parte Motion; Exhibit C, Ex Parte Order Log showing 24 ex parte orders.)
- Failure to encourage any form of relationship between child and father — the Court's own findings at trial found "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and Factor (a) was "equal between the parties" (Court's Best Interest Findings, Custody Trial Record, 7/17/2024; LC Record), yet Defendant has systematically destroyed that equal bond;
- A systematic pattern that has *escalated* over time—from restricted access to total severance. (See Docket Timeline: denial of parenting time October–November 2024 → ex parte custody order → PPO weaponization → total suspension August 2025.)

This is not a case where a parent has failed to "encourage" a relationship through passive neglect. Defendant has *actively destroyed* the relationship through calculated legal manipulation and complete denial of access. Factor (j) weighs overwhelmingly against Defendant. (R. at PIGORS-0421; Exhibit D, Pigors Declaration re: Service Failure and Zero Voluntary Contact)

#### Conclusion

Defendant's conduct constitutes textbook parental alienation in violation of MCL 722.23(j), which independently supports both a finding of contempt and a modification of custody.

### D. Statutory Remedies Are Available and Warranted

#### Issue

What remedies are available to this Court for Defendant's contempt and parenting time violations.

#### Rule

MCL 722.27a(9) provides a comprehensive menu of remedies when a court finds a parenting time violation:

> *"If a court finds by a preponderance of the evidence that a parent has wrongfully denied the other parent parenting time, the court shall do one or more of the following:*
>
> *(a) Modify the parenting time order to give additional parenting time to the wronged parent to make up for the parenting time that was wrongfully denied.*
>
> *(b) Subject the offending parent to a civil fine of not more than $100.00 for each parenting time that was wrongfully denied.*
>
> *(c) Require the parent who wrongfully denied parenting time to pay the costs, including actual attorney fees, of the parent who was denied parenting time.*
>
> *(d) Require the parent who wrongfully denied parenting time to attend at the expense of that parent a parenting program that the court determines is appropriate.*
>
> *(e) Require the parent who wrongfully denied parenting time to participate in community service."*

MCL 600.1711 authorizes additional sanctions for contempt, including fine and imprisonment.

#### Application

Every remedy in MCL 722.27a(9) is warranted here:

**(a) Makeup Parenting Time.** 329+ days of wrongfully denied parenting time must be made up through an extended, structured makeup parenting-time schedule. The magnitude of the denial demands a correspondingly substantial remedy.

**(b) Civil Fine.** The statutory fine of $100 per denied parenting-time occasion should be imposed for each scheduled parenting-time period wrongfully denied over the 329+ day period. At minimum, assuming weekly scheduled parenting time, this constitutes **47+ individual violations**, resulting in fines of **$4,700+**. (R. at PIGORS-0425; Exhibit B, Calendar Exhibit calculating individual violations)

**(c) Costs and Fees.** Plaintiff, though *pro se*, has incurred substantial costs in litigating this matter, including filing fees, copying costs, transportation costs, and the functional equivalent of attorney fees reflected in the hundreds of hours of legal research and document preparation. *See* MCR 2.625 (costs to prevailing party).

**(d) Parenting Program.** Defendant should be ordered to attend, at her own expense, a parenting program addressing co-parenting, parental alienation, and the developmental impact of parent-child separation.

**(e) Community Service.** Community service should be imposed as an additional sanction reflecting the gravity of Defendant's willful conduct.

#### Conclusion

The full range of statutory remedies under MCL 722.27a(9) should be imposed, together with sanctions under MCL 600.1711, to remedy Defendant's contempt and deter future violations.

### E. Modification of Custody Is Warranted

#### Issue

Whether Defendant's contempt and parental alienation warrant modification of the existing custody arrangement.

#### Rule

Under MCL 722.27(1)(c), a court may modify a custody order upon a showing of proper cause or a change of circumstances that warrants modification. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508–09 (2003). A finding of contempt for denial of parenting time constitutes a change of circumstances. *See* MCL 722.27(1)(c). Where a parent's conduct demonstrates a complete unwillingness to facilitate the child's relationship with the other parent, modification of custody is appropriate to serve the child's best interests. *Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457 (1996).

#### Application

Defendant's 329+ consecutive days of parenting-time denial constitutes both proper cause and a change of circumstances:

- The child has been denied a relationship with his father for nearly a year; (See Calendar Exhibit B; Docket Timeline from 8/8/2025 to present.)
- Defendant has demonstrated absolute refusal to comply with court orders; (See Exhibit A, Watson Ex Parte Motion; Order Following Hearing, 11/26/2025: "Defendant requested, and the Court signed an ex parte order suspending the Plaintiff's parenting time.")
- Factor (j) of MCL 722.23 weighs decisively against Defendant; (See Court's Best Interest Findings, Custody Trial Record, 7/17/2024, finding Factor (a) "equal" — Defendant's subsequent conduct has destroyed that equality.)
- The only effective remedy to restore the parent-child relationship is to modify custody;
- Continued placement with a parent who engages in wholesale alienation is contrary to the child's best interests. The HealthWest mental health assessment ordered by this Court found that Plaintiff "reports no issues with employment," "reports no issues with others with the exception of his ex," and "has support of friends and family." (See Exhibit E, HealthWest Assessment, received by Court 9/12/2025; LC Record.) These findings negate any safety-based justification for the current arrangement. (R. at PIGORS-0429; Exhibit E, HealthWest Assessment, received by Court 9/12/2025)

#### Conclusion

This Court should modify custody in favor of Plaintiff, or in the alternative, impose a structured custody transition that progressively restores the parent-child relationship.

## III. CHESS MODE: ANTICIPATION AND REBUTTAL OF DEFENDANT'S DEFENSES

### Defense 1: "Safety Concerns Justify Denial of Parenting Time"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that parenting time was denied due to legitimate safety concerns regarding Plaintiff's behavior, citing *Pickering v Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694, and MCL 600.2950(12).

**Rebuttal:** Safety concerns do not authorize unilateral self-help. If Defendant believed the child was in danger, the proper remedy was a motion under MCR 3.207 or a modification under MCL 722.27—not 329+ consecutive days of complete denial. Moreover, this Court itself has confirmed the parenting time order remains in effect (*"Parenting Time shall remain pursuant to the Order entered on August 23, 2024"*; LC Record). If the Court believed safety concerns warranted termination of parenting time, it would have said so. Additionally, the Court's own record reflects it *"offered supervised parenting time to the Father and he categorically rejected that"* (Order Following Hearing, 11/26/2025; LC Record) —even supervised time was available, yet Defendant facilitated *zero* contact of any kind. Furthermore, the HealthWest assessment ordered by this Court returned favorable results, finding Plaintiff "reports no issues" and "has support of friends and family" (Exhibit E, HealthWest Assessment, 9/12/2025), directly contradicting any claimed safety basis. The safety defense does not excuse 329+ days of total severance.

### Defense 2: "Plaintiff's Own Conduct Caused the Separation"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue Plaintiff's rejection of supervised visitation and alleged filing of frivolous motions demonstrates that Plaintiff bears responsibility for the separation.

**Rebuttal:** Even if Plaintiff objected to certain conditions, this does not absolve Defendant of her independent obligation to comply with court orders. The parenting time order remains in effect by order of this Court. (See Order re: Parenting Time Motion, 2025: "Parenting Time shall remain pursuant to the Order entered on August 23, 2024"; LC Record.) Defendant's obligation to facilitate parenting time is not contingent on Plaintiff's agreement to unilateral conditions imposed outside the order. Plaintiff's actual position on the record was: "there is no need for suspension, and I am not any danger to my son in any way, shape, or form" (Tr. 11/26/2025; LC Record) — an objection to the legal basis, not a refusal to see his child. Moreover, the Court's imposition of a $250 bond requirement for filing motions (see Order re: Parenting Time Motion, 2025; LC Record) does not constitute a waiver of Plaintiff's parenting time rights. The right of a parent to maintain a relationship with his child is constitutionally protected and cannot be conditioned on litigation behavior. *See Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000).

### Defense 3: "No Changed Circumstances Warrant Modification"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue, per *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, that no change of circumstances has occurred warranting modification.

**Rebuttal:** 329+ consecutive days of zero parenting time is itself the changed circumstance. The parenting time order contemplated parenting time occurring; it has not occurred for nearly a year. That is a material change of circumstances by any definition. Furthermore, a finding of contempt for parenting-time denial is independently sufficient to establish changed circumstances. MCL 722.27(1)(c).

### Defense 4: "The Existing PPO Prevents Contact"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue the PPO (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) lawfully prevents Plaintiff from having contact, and therefore the separation is court-sanctioned.

**Rebuttal:** A PPO does not extinguish parenting time rights. MCL 600.2950(12) expressly provides that a PPO must not *"interfere with or limit a parent's right to parenting time."* If the PPO and parenting time order conflict, the proper remedy is a motion to reconcile the orders—not unilateral denial of all parenting time for 329+ days. (See PPO, Case No. 2023-5907-PP; Final Judgment of Custody and Parenting Time, 8/23/2024; LC Record.) The PPO docket itself reveals that 24 of approximately 55 orders (44%) were entered ex parte by the same judge (See Exhibit C, Ex Parte Order Log), demonstrating systematic procedural abuse rather than legitimate safety enforcement. Defendant's reliance on the PPO to justify total severance of the parent-child relationship is a misuse of the PPO statute.

### Defense 5: "Court Already Reviewed and Denied Plaintiff's Parenting Time Motions"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that the Court's dismissal of Plaintiff's February 14, 2025 Motion constitutes judicial validation of the current arrangement.

**Rebuttal:** The Court's dismissal of that Motion was on procedural grounds and expressly *confirmed* that the parenting time order remains in effect: *"Parenting Time shall remain pursuant to the Order entered on August 23, 2024."* If parenting time "remains" per the order, then Defendant is obligated to comply with that order. The Court's dismissal of a motion does not constitute permission to violate the underlying order.

### Defense 6: "Defendant Was Unable to Comply with the Parenting Time Order"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that she was unable to comply with the parenting time order due to the PPO, safety concerns, or logistical barriers, and that inability to comply is a defense to contempt.

**Rebuttal:** Inability to comply must be proven by the contemnor, and the burden is on the respondent to establish an affirmative defense of impossibility. *See Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004); *Diso v Diso*, 236 Mich App 524, 530; 601 NW2d 101 (1999) (inability defense requires showing of "clear and absolute" inability, not mere inconvenience). The record shows willful denial without any documented inability: (a) Defendant offered zero alternative forms of contact — no phone calls, no video calls, no letters, no supervised visits — demonstrating a *choice* not to comply, not an *inability*. (R. at Docket Timeline, 8/8/2025 to present, documenting zero contact of any kind; LC Record.) (b) The Court itself confirmed supervised parenting time was available: "[T]he Court also notes that it offered supervised parenting time to the Father and he categorically rejected that." (R. at Order Following Hearing, 11/26/2025; LC Record.) If supervised time was available, Defendant could have facilitated it — her failure to do so is willful, not impossible. (c) MCL 600.2950(12) expressly provides that a PPO must not "interfere with or limit a parent's right to parenting time." If the PPO conflicted with the parenting time order, Defendant's obligation was to seek reconciliation from the Court — not to unilaterally suspend all contact for 329+ days. (R. at PPO, Case No. 2023-5907-PP; Final Judgment of Custody and Parenting Time, 8/23/2024; LC Record.) (d) The 24 ex parte orders across approximately 55 total orders on the PPO docket — a 44% ex parte rate — suggest active manipulation of the legal process, not passive inability. (R. at Docket Analysis, PPO Case No. 2023-5907-PP; Exhibit C, Ex Parte Order Log.)

### Defense 7: "Father's Litigation Conduct Justifies Denial"

**Anticipated Argument:** Defendant will argue that Plaintiff's filing of multiple motions, disqualification requests, and appellate proceedings demonstrates hostility that justifies continued denial of parenting time.

**Rebuttal:** A parent's exercise of constitutionally protected rights — including the right to petition the court (Const 1963, art 1, § 3) and the fundamental right to parent (*Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000)) — cannot be used as a basis for denying parenting time. Filing motions to *restore* parenting time is the legal mechanism the court system provides; punishing a parent for using it would create an unconstitutional Catch-22. MCL 722.23(j) requires evaluation of "willingness to facilitate" the parent-child relationship — Father's persistent legal advocacy is the epitome of willingness to facilitate.
