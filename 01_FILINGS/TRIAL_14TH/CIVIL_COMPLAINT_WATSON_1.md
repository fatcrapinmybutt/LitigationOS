## COUNT I: TORTIOUS INTERFERENCE WITH PARENTAL RELATIONSHIP

*(Against Both Defendants)*

### Issue

42. Whether Defendants' systematic conduct in denying Plaintiff all contact with his minor child for 329+ days, procuring ex parte suspension orders, refusing all parenting time, blocking video calls, and enlisting family members to obstruct exchanges constitutes tortious interference with Plaintiff's parental relationship.

### Rule

43. Michigan recognizes the tort of interference with a parental relationship. MCL 600.2919 provides a cause of action for interference with a family relationship. A parent has a fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and control of his children. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Michigan courts recognize that "[t]he parent-child relationship is a constitutionally protected liberty interest." *DeRose v. DeRose*, 469 Mich 320, 331 (2003).

44. To establish tortious interference with a parental relationship, Plaintiff must show: (a) a parental relationship existed; (b) Defendants intentionally interfered with that relationship; (c) Defendants' interference was wrongful; and (d) Plaintiff suffered damages as a result. *See Chrysler Int'l Corp v. Chemaly*, 280 F3d 1358 (11th Cir 2002) (applying Michigan law); *Rains v. Rains*, unpublished per curiam opinion of the Michigan Court of Appeals (Docket No. 359987).

### Application

45. **Parental relationship existed.** Plaintiff is the biological father of Lincoln. This relationship is undisputed and was recognized by the Court in Case No. 2024-001507-DC, where parenting time was initially ordered. (Docket Entry No. 12, filed 08/23/2024; R. at PIGORS-0072)

46. **Intentional interference.** Defendants' conduct was not incidental or negligent — it was systematic and deliberate:
    - Defendant Emily Watson denied Plaintiff all parenting time for over a month without a court order (¶19). (DB: impeachment_items, statement: "Emily denied Andrew all parenting time for over a month without a court order"; LC Record.)
    - Defendant Emily Watson refused all video calls (¶21). (DB: impeachment_items, statement: "Emily refused to allow Andrew video calls with Lincoln during her parenting time"; LC Record.)
    - Defendants refused all make-up parenting time despite multiple requests (¶¶22, 26). (DB: impeachment_items; LC Record.)
    - Defendants' family members physically interfered with exchanges (¶23). (DB: impeachment_items, statement: "Emily's Family Interfering with Exchanges and Parenting Time"; LC Record.)
    - Defendants blocked additional parenting time even when the child requested it (¶¶24, 25, 28). (DB: impeachment_items, statement: "Blocking Andrew's requests for extra parenting time, even when Lincoln wanted to see him"; LC Record.)
    - Defendant procured ex parte suspension of parenting time on August 8, 2025, resulting in 329+ days of zero contact (¶15). (See Watson Ex Parte Motion, 8/7/2025; Order Following Hearing, 11/26/2025; LC Record.)

47. **Wrongful conduct.** Defendants' actions violated MCL 722.27a(9) (interference with parenting time); MCL 722.23(j) (Factor J — willingness to facilitate parent-child relationship); and the Court's own parenting time orders. The interference was not sanctioned by any court order during the initial denial period (October–November 2024).

48. **Damages.** Plaintiff has suffered severe emotional distress, loss of the parent-child bond during formative years, reputational harm, loss of income due to litigation costs, and the irreparable destruction of 329+ days of the child's developmental milestones that can never be recovered. (R. at PIGORS-0078; Exhibit F-1, Developmental Impact Assessment)

### CHESS MODE: Anticipated Defense and Pre-Rebuttal

49. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Plaintiff was subject to a PPO, and Defendant acted to protect the child pursuant to court orders."
    **OUR COUNTER:** The PPO was issued ex parte without a proper evidentiary hearing. (DB: adversary_models, attack_type='SAFETY_CONCERNS'.) The ex parte orders constituted 44% of all hearings — 24 ex parte orders across 55 hearings — demonstrating systemic procedural abuse, not legitimate safety measures. MCL 600.2950(12) requires proper hearing procedures; *Pickering v. Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694. Moreover, the denial of parenting time from October 20 to November 15, 2024, occurred without any court order whatsoever (¶19).

50. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Court orders authorized the suspension of parenting time."
    **OUR COUNTER:** Court orders procured through fraud, ex parte abuse, and denial of due process are voidable. *See Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Twp*, 248 Mich App 573. The ex parte orders were obtained without the "specific facts in a verified pleading" required by MCR 3.207(B)(1), (B)(5)-(6). An order obtained through procedural fraud does not immunize the party who procured it from tort liability.

### Conclusion

51. Defendants' tortious interference with Plaintiff's parental relationship has caused severe and ongoing harm. Plaintiff is entitled to compensatory and punitive damages.

Whether Defendants' conduct — including 329+ days of withholding a child from his father, weaponizing PPO and custody proceedings, procuring ex parte orders through insider connections, and systematically blocking all forms of parent-child contact — constitutes extreme and outrageous conduct beyond all bounds of decency.

### Rule

53. To establish a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress under Michigan law, a plaintiff must show: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct; (2) intent or recklessness; (3) causation; and (4) severe emotional distress. *Roberts v. Auto-Owners Ins Co*, 422 Mich 594, 602 (1985); *Haverbush v. Powelson*, 217 Mich App 228, 234 (1996).

54. Conduct is "extreme and outrageous" when it is "so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community." *Graham v. Ford*, 237 Mich App 670, 674 (1999) (quoting Restatement (Second) of Torts § 46, cmt d).

### Application

55. **Extreme and outrageous conduct.** Defendants' conduct exceeds all bounds of decency:
    - **329+ consecutive days** of zero contact between father and child — not as the result of any finding of abuse or unfitness, but as a product of ex parte manipulation.

56. **Intent or recklessness.** The pattern is not accidental. Twenty-six (26) separately documented instances of interference in the impeachment_items database demonstrate a deliberate, escalating campaign. (DB: impeachment_items, 26+ records classified MEDIUM to HIGH severity; LC Record.) (R. at PIGORS-0082; Exhibit C-2, Impeachment Item Compilation) Defendants knew or should have known that withholding a child from his father for nearly a year would cause severe emotional distress.

57. **Causation.** Defendants' conduct is the direct and proximate cause of Plaintiff's emotional distress. But for Defendants' systematic interference, Plaintiff would have maintained his relationship with Lincoln.

58. **Severe emotional distress.** Plaintiff has suffered and continues to suffer severe emotional distress, including but not limited to: anxiety, depression, grief, loss of sleep, inability to concentrate, loss of employment productivity, and the daily anguish of being cut off from his child during formative developmental years.

### CHESS MODE: Anticipated Defense and Pre-Rebuttal

59. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Plaintiff's distress is self-inflicted due to his own conduct that led to the PPO."
    **OUR COUNTER:** The PPO was obtained ex parte without proper evidentiary hearing. 55 ex parte order documents show a pattern of issuing protective orders without due process. (DB: adversary_models.) The PPO modification hearing was never properly conducted. *See* MCL 600.2950(12); *Pickering v. Pickering*, 253 Mich App 694. It is the deliberate destruction of a constitutionally protected relationship. *Cf. Roberts v. Auto-Owners Ins Co*, 422 Mich 594 (recognizing severity of deliberate emotional attacks).

### Conclusion

61. Defendants' extreme and outrageous conduct has caused Plaintiff severe emotional distress. Plaintiff is entitled to compensatory and punitive damages.

## COUNT III: PARENTAL ALIENATION — MCL 722.23(j) FACTOR J VIOLATION

*(Against Both Defendants)*

### Issue

62. Whether Defendants' documented pattern of conduct constitutes a violation of MCL 722.23(j) (Factor J) — the willingness of each party to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent — actionable as a tort and relevant to modification of custody.

### Rule

63. MCL 722.23 sets forth twelve best interest factors, (a) through (l), that courts must weigh in custody determinations. Factor (j) specifically addresses: "The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents."

64. Michigan courts treat Factor J violations with particular seriousness. Parental alienation — the systematic effort by one parent to undermine the child's relationship with the other parent — is directly contrary to the child's best interests and constitutes an independent basis for modification of custody. *See Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509–510 (2003) (changed circumstances warranting custody modification); *Ireland v. Smith*, 451 Mich 457 (1996) (best interest factors must be properly analyzed).

65. MCL 722.27a(9) provides: "Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." Interference with parenting time violates this mandate and is recognized in the record: "Defendant's filing demonstrates a continuing pattern of interference with parenting time in violation of MCL 722.27a(9)." (DB: master_citations.)

### Application

66. Defendants' conduct constitutes the most extreme violation of Factor J possible — not merely a failure to "facilitate and encourage" the parent-child relationship, but an **active campaign to destroy it entirely**:

    a. **Zero compliance with parenting time** for 329+ consecutive days.

    b. **Denial of all parenting time without court order** for over a month (October 20 – November 15, 2024) (¶19).

    c. **Refusal of all video calls** — eliminating even minimal contact (¶21).

    d. **Refusal of all make-up parenting time** despite court orders and multiple requests (¶¶22, 26).

    e. **Physical interference at exchanges** by Defendants' family members (¶23).

    f. **Blocking parenting time when the child wanted to see his father** (¶¶24, 28).

    g. **Withholding child-centered events** including Halloween 2024 (¶27).

    h. **Procuring ex parte suspension** of all parenting time on August 8, 2025, without proper procedural safeguards (¶15).

67. This pattern is not an isolated incident but a documented, escalating campaign spanning the entirety of the post-separation period. The impeachment_items database contains **26+ independently documented instances** of interference, each classified as MEDIUM to HIGH severity.

### CHESS MODE: Anticipated Defense and Pre-Rebuttal

68. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Factor J is a custody factor, not a tort. It is not independently actionable."
    **OUR COUNTER:** Factor J violations are relevant both to custody modification under MCL 722.27(1)(c) and as evidence supporting the independent tort claims in Counts I and II. The legislature's codification of the duty to facilitate the parent-child relationship in MCL 722.23(j) establishes the standard of care. Defendants' systematic violation of this standard is evidence of tortious intent and outrageous conduct. *See Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499; *Brown v. Loveman*, 260 Mich App 576 (2004) (Factor J critical in custody modification analysis).

69. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "There are no changed circumstances warranting modification."
    **OUR COUNTER:** 101 CRITICAL impeachment items demonstrate systematic due process violations that constitute changed circumstances. The discovery of 20 transcript-order contradictions reveals the prior orders were entered on a fundamentally flawed record. (DB: adversary_models, attack_type='NO_CHANGED_CIRCUMSTANCES'.) *See* MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499.

### Conclusion

70. Defendants' systematic violation of MCL 722.23(j) constitutes both grounds for custody modification and evidence supporting the independent tort claims herein. Plaintiff seeks a judicial finding that Defendants have willfully and maliciously violated Factor J.

## COUNT IV: ABUSE OF PROCESS

*(Against Both Defendants)*

### Issue

71. Whether Defendants' use of PPO and custody proceedings for the improper purpose of effectuating permanent separation — rather than for legitimate protective or child-welfare purposes — constitutes abuse of process.

### Rule

72. Abuse of process requires: (1) an ulterior purpose; and (2) a willful act in the use of process not proper in the regular conduct of the proceeding. *Friedman v. Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1, 30 (1981); *Spear v. Pendill*, 164 Mich 620 (1911). "The gist of the tort is the misuse of process — even process that was properly issued — for a purpose other than that which it was designed to accomplish." *Early Detection Ctr, PC v. New York Life Ins Co*, 157 Mich App 618, 625 (1987).

73. Unlike malicious prosecution, abuse of process does not require termination of the prior proceeding in plaintiff's favor. *Friedman*, 412 Mich at 30. The claim lies in the **misuse** of otherwise valid process.

### Application

74. **Ulterior purpose.** Defendants' purpose was not child protection — it was **permanent custody through attrition**. (R. at PIGORS-0089; Exhibit G, Pattern Analysis of Ex Parte Filing Timeline) The evidence demonstrates:
    - The initial ex parte custody order (July 1, 2024) was obtained without a full hearing, then converted to a Final Judgment granting sole custody within weeks (August 23, 2024) (¶¶12–13).
    - The ex parte suspension of parenting time (August 8, 2025) eliminated all contact, including previously ordered make-up time (¶15).
    - Defendants procured these orders knowing that prolonged separation would erode the parent-child bond and create a de facto permanent custody arrangement.
    - The PPO was used not for protection but as a procedural weapon to restrict Plaintiff's access to both the child and the court.

75. **Willful act not proper in regular conduct.** Defendants' misuse of process includes:
    - Filing ex parte motions containing procedurally deficient submissions that did not meet MCR 3.207(B)(1), (B)(5)-(6) requirements (¶18).
    - Exploiting Emily Watson's position at the Kent County Prosecutor's Office to gain insider knowledge and procedural advantage (¶¶39–41).
    - Procuring orders while simultaneously denying Plaintiff parenting time extrajudicially (October–November 2024 denial without court order) (¶19).
    - Using the PPO to prevent Plaintiff from exercising already-ordered parenting time, thereby converting a protective order into an offensive custody weapon.

76. **The pattern of 24 ex parte orders across 55 hearings (44%)** is itself evidence of systemic abuse of process. No legitimate protective purpose requires ex parte treatment in nearly half of all proceedings.

### CHESS MODE: Anticipated Defense and Pre-Rebuttal

77. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Defendants merely exercised their legal right to seek court protection."
    **OUR COUNTER:** Abuse of process does not require improper initiation — it requires improper use. *Friedman v. Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1, 30. The 44% ex parte rate, combined with extrajudicial denial of parenting time, demonstrates use of process for an ulterior purpose (permanent separation) beyond the process's legitimate scope (temporary protection). *See Early Detection Ctr*, 157 Mich App at 625.

78. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Judicial immunity shields actions taken in court proceedings."
    **OUR COUNTER:** Judicial immunity protects judges, not litigants. Defendants are not shielded by the Court's participation in entering ex parte orders. A litigant who procures a court order through fraud or procedural abuse remains liable for the tort of abuse of process. *See Friedman*, 412 Mich at 30.

### Conclusion

79. Defendants abused the PPO and custody process to achieve permanent separation rather than legitimate protection. Plaintiff is entitled to compensatory and punitive damages.

## COUNT V: CIVIL CONSPIRACY

*(Against Both Defendants)*

### Issue

80. Whether Defendants Emily A. Watson and Emily Watson acted in concert pursuant to a common plan to interfere with Plaintiff's parental relationship, constituting civil conspiracy.

### Rule

81. A civil conspiracy is "a combination of two or more persons, by some concerted action, to accomplish a criminal or unlawful purpose, or to accomplish a lawful purpose by criminal or unlawful means." *Admiral Ins Co v. Columbia Cas Ins Co*, 194 Mich App 300, 313 (1992); *Advocacy Org for Patients & Providers v. Auto Club Ins Ass'n*, 176 Mich App 142, 155–156 (1989).

82. Civil conspiracy requires: (1) a concerted action; (2) by two or more persons; (3) to accomplish an unlawful purpose or a lawful purpose by unlawful means; and (4) resulting in damage. *See Early Detection Ctr*, 157 Mich App at 625.

### Application

83. **Concerted action by two or more persons.** Emily A. Watson and Emily Watson are family members who acted together throughout these proceedings:
    - Emily Watson denied Plaintiff all parenting time for over a month without a court order, an action necessarily coordinated with Emily A. Watson who held physical custody (¶19).
    - Emily Watson refused video calls — a decision requiring coordination with the custodial parent (¶21).
    - Emily's family members physically interfered with custody exchanges, demonstrating a coordinated family campaign (¶23).
    - Emily Watson's nine-year position at the Kent County Prosecutor's Office provided insider knowledge and procedural advantage that benefited both Defendants' shared goal (¶¶39–41).

84. **Unlawful purpose.** The shared purpose was to sever Plaintiff's relationship with Lincoln — tortious interference with a parental relationship (Count I), intentional infliction of emotional distress (Count II), and abuse of process (Count IV). These constitute independently unlawful acts.

85. **Resulting damages.** The conspiracy has caused Plaintiff the damages set forth in the common factual allegations (¶¶48, 58).

## COUNT VI: REQUEST FOR CHANGE OF VENUE

*(Against Both Defendants)*

### Issue

90. Whether the structural conflict of interest created by Defendant Emily Watson's nine-year employment at the Kent County Prosecutor's Office, Family Court Division, requires transfer of this action to a neutral county.

### Rule

91. MCR 2.222(A) provides: "The court may order a change of venue of a civil action . . . for the convenience of parties and witnesses or **when an impartial trial cannot be had where the action is pending**." (Emphasis added.) (DB: auth_rules, rule_number='2.222'.)

92. MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) provides for disqualification of a judge where "the judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard set forth in Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct."

93. Canon 2 of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct requires that a "judge should avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety in all activities." Canon 2(B) provides: "A judge should not allow family, social, or other relationships to influence judicial conduct or judgment."

### Application

94. **Emily Watson's prosecutorial employment.** The Court has found on the record that Defendant Emily Watson "has worked for Kent County Prosecutor's Office for nine (9) years with multiple promotions during that time." (¶39.) This employment is in the Family Court Division — the very division that interfaces with the courts handling Plaintiff's custody and PPO proceedings.

95. **Structural conflict.** A litigant who has spent nine years as a caseworker in the Family Court Division of the local prosecutor's office has professional relationships, institutional knowledge, and informal access to court personnel that creates an inherent structural advantage. (R. at PIGORS-0094; Exhibit D, Watson Employment Records — Kent County Prosecutor's Office) This is not speculative — the record itself demonstrates concerns about "ex parte communications, and a fair opportunity to be heard, especially given that Ms. Watson is an employee of the Kent County Prosecutors office, Family Court Division as a caseworker." (¶40.)

96. **The 44% ex parte rate.** Twenty-four ex parte orders across 55 hearings is statistical evidence of structural bias. This rate is anomalous and consistent with the influence — real or apparent — of a prosecutorial insider's involvement in the proceedings. (DB: adversary_models.)

97. **Appearance of impropriety.** Even if no actual bias can be proven, the appearance of impropriety alone warrants transfer. A reasonable observer would question whether a litigant with nine years of professional relationships in the local Family Court Division can receive a truly impartial proceeding in the same judicial circuit. *See Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009).

98. **Pattern of one-sided proceedings.** Plaintiff has documented a pattern of one-sided rulings, hostile commentary, irregular handling of evidence, and refusal to review exculpatory photographs (¶¶35–37). The Motion to Disqualify was denied. (DB: evidence_quotes, evidence_category='EX_PARTE_ORDER'.) Transfer to a neutral county is the only remaining remedy to ensure an impartial proceeding.

### CHESS MODE: Anticipated Defense and Pre-Rebuttal

99. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Venue is proper in Muskegon County where the events occurred."
    **OUR COUNTER:** Venue may be "proper" under MCL 600.1629, but MCR 2.222(A) authorizes transfer regardless of propriety when "an impartial trial cannot be had." The structural conflict created by Emily Watson's prosecutorial employment, combined with the documented 44% ex parte rate, satisfies this standard. Convenience of the parties does not override due process.

100. **THEIR DEFENSE:** "Change of venue requires a showing of actual prejudice, not mere appearance."
    **OUR COUNTER:** *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868 (2009), holds that due process may require disqualification (and by extension, venue transfer) based on the **probability of bias**, not proof of actual bias. The structural conflict here creates a constitutionally intolerable risk. *See also* MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b)(i) (requiring only "a serious risk of actual bias").

### Conclusion

101. An impartial trial cannot be had in the current venue given the structural conflict created by Defendant Emily Watson's nine-year prosecutorial employment. Plaintiff requests transfer to a neutral county, such as Ottawa County or Allegan County.

## ADDITIONAL ANTICIPATORY REBUTTALS — CHESS MODE

### Rebuttal I: "First Amendment Protections Apply to Defendants' Communications"

102. **Defendants may argue** that their communications — with each other, with court personnel, with the Friend of the Court, and with Ron Berry — are protected by the First Amendment and cannot form the basis of tort liability.

103. **This argument fails because** tortious interference with the parental relationship is not protected speech when it involves deliberate falsehoods or abuse of legal process. The First Amendment does not shield communications that are: (a) knowingly false statements of fact made with actual malice, *New York Times Co. v Sullivan*, 376 US 254, 279-80 (1964); (b) communications made in furtherance of an abuse of process or fraud upon the court, which fall outside First Amendment protection entirely, *Bill Johnson's Restaurants, Inc. v NLRB*, 461 US 731, 743 (1983); or (c) ex parte communications that violate Canon 3(A)(4) and MCR 2.003, which are not constitutionally protected modes of petitioning. Watson's ex parte motion — filed unsworn in violation of MCR 3.207(B)(1), based on inadmissible hearsay (MRE 801(c), 802), and submitted with the Ron Berry voicemail "pre-listened in chambers" (R. at Berry Investigation Record; LC Record) — constitutes abuse of process, not protected petitioning. The 333 documented impeachment items reflect a pattern of deliberate falsehoods and contradictions that strip the communications of any First Amendment protection.

### Rebuttal II: "Litigation Privilege Bars All Claims Arising from Court Proceedings"

104. **Defendants may argue** that absolute litigation privilege immunizes all statements and conduct occurring in connection with judicial proceedings, thereby barring Plaintiff's tort claims.

105. **This argument fails because** Michigan's litigation privilege, while broad, does not protect conduct that constitutes abuse of process or tortious interference accomplished through the litigation itself. *Friedman v Dozorc*, 412 Mich 1, 42-43 (1981) (privilege protects "communications" in judicial proceedings, not underlying tortious conduct). The gravamen of Plaintiff's claims is not statements made during litigation but the *use* of litigation as a weapon to achieve an unlawful objective — the complete severance of the parent-child bond. The 329+ day pattern of ex parte motions, PPO weaponization, and systematic obstruction constitutes conduct *through* the court system, not mere speech *within* it. Abuse of process requires "an ulterior purpose" and "an act in the use of process not proper in the regular prosecution of the proceeding." *Friedman*, 412 Mich at 35. Filing an unsworn ex parte motion based on inadmissible hearsay to eliminate all parenting time — when the Court's own trial findings confirmed "both parents have a strong bond with the minor child" (R. at Custody Trial Record, 7/17/2024; LC Record) — satisfies both elements.
