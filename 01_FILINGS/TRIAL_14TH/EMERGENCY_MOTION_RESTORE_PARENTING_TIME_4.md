## IV. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. Michigan Law Creates a Strong Presumption in Favor of Parenting Time

46. The Michigan Child Custody Act establishes a clear and strong presumption that parenting time with both parents serves the best interests of the child. MCL 722.27a(1) provides:

> "Parenting time shall be granted in frequency, duration, and type reasonably calculated to promote a strong relationship between the child and the parent granted parenting time."

47. This presumption reflects the Legislature's recognition — consistent with decades of social science research and constitutional law — that children benefit from maintaining strong, ongoing relationships with both parents.

48. The presumption in favor of parenting time can only be overcome by a showing that parenting time would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health. MCL 722.27a(3). No such showing has been made in this case. To the contrary, the HealthWest evaluation and Dr. DeAugustine's clearance affirmatively establish that Father poses no risk to L.D.W.

49. As the Michigan Court of Appeals held in *Shade v. Wright*, 805 N.W.2d 1, 7 (Mich. App. 2011), a court must make specific findings based on the statutory best interest factors before modifying or restricting parenting time. A blanket denial of all parenting time without such findings is legally insufficient and constitutes an abuse of discretion.

50. In *Vodvarka v. Grasher*, 259 Mich. App. 499, 512 (2003), the Court of Appeals established that before a court may revisit a custody or parenting time arrangement, the moving party must demonstrate proper cause or a change of circumstances. Here, the dramatic change of circumstances — namely, Father's complete denial of parenting time for 329+ days — more than satisfies this threshold and compels judicial intervention.

### B. Analysis of Best Interest Factors Under MCL 722.23

51. MCL 722.23 sets forth twelve (12) factors that the Court must consider in determining the best interests of the child. Father respectfully submits the following analysis of each factor:

#### Factor (a): The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child.

52. Father has a deep, genuine, and well-documented love for L.D.W. Prior to the denial of parenting time, Father was actively and lovingly involved in L.D.W.'s life. The forced 329+ day separation has not diminished Father's love and emotional connection to his son; it has only deepened Father's resolve to restore the relationship. **This factor favors Father or, at minimum, is neutral**, as both parents are presumed to have emotional ties to the child absent evidence to the contrary.

#### Factor (b): The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any.

53. Father has the full capacity and disposition to provide love, affection, and guidance to L.D.W. The HealthWest psychological evaluation and Dr. DeAugustine's clearance confirm Father's psychological fitness and capacity to parent. No professional evaluation has found otherwise. **This factor favors Father**, as Father has demonstrated his capacity and willingness through professional evaluation, while Mother's conduct in obstructing the parent-child relationship calls her disposition regarding Factor (b) into question.

#### Factor (c): The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care recognized and permitted under the laws of this state in place of medical care, and other material needs.

54. Father has the capacity and disposition to provide for L.D.W.'s material needs, including food, clothing, and medical care. Father's commitment to providing for L.D.W. is evidenced by his sustained and costly efforts to restore parenting time. **This factor is neutral** as between the parties, absent specific evidence that either parent is unable to meet the child's material needs.

#### Factor (d): The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity.

55. L.D.W. has been in Mother's physical custody during the 329+ day period of denied parenting time. However, an environment in which a child is deliberately alienated from a loving parent is not a "satisfactory" environment within the meaning of this factor. The Michigan Supreme Court has recognized that parental alienation undermines the stability and quality of a child's environment. *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich. 81 (2010). **This factor weighs against Mother**, whose alienation conduct has created an environment that is harmful to L.D.W.'s long-term emotional development.

#### Factor (e): The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes.

56. Father seeks parenting time, not a change of primary physical custody at this time. Father offers a stable, permanent home environment in which L.D.W. can exercise parenting time. **This factor is neutral** as it pertains primarily to custody rather than parenting time.

#### Factor (f): The moral fitness of the parties involved.

57. Father's moral fitness is established by his clean record — seven (7) or more investigations with zero findings of abuse or misconduct — and by the HealthWest psychological clearance. Mother's moral fitness is called into question by her systematic obstruction of court-ordered parenting time, her instrumentalization of the legal system to deny Father's rights, and the Watson family's coordinated alienation campaign. In *Brown v. Loveman*, 260 Mich. App. 576, 590 (2004), the Court of Appeals recognized that a parent's interference with the other parent's relationship with the child is relevant to the moral fitness inquiry. **This factor favors Father.**

#### Factor (g): The mental and physical health of the parties involved.

58. Father has been professionally evaluated and cleared by Dr. DeAugustine at HealthWest. No mental or physical health condition has been identified that would impair Father's ability to parent L.D.W. No competing professional evaluation contradicts this finding. **This factor favors Father or is neutral.**

#### Factor (h): The home, school, and community record of the child.

59. L.D.W. is approximately two (2) years of age and does not yet have a school record. The child's community record is limited at this age. Father is committed to supporting L.D.W.'s educational and community development as the child grows. **This factor is neutral** given the child's young age.

#### Factor (i): The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference.

60. L.D.W. is approximately two (2) years old and is not of sufficient age to express a meaningful preference. **This factor is inapplicable** at this time.

#### Factor (j): The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents. A court may not consider negatively for the purposes of this factor any reasonable action taken by a parent to protect a child or that parent from sexual assault or domestic violence by the child's other parent.

61. This factor is the most significant in the present case. Mother has demonstrated an absolute unwillingness to facilitate any relationship between L.D.W. and Father. For 329+ consecutive days, Mother has denied all parenting time, all communication, and all contact. This is not a case of minor scheduling conflicts or occasional difficulties — this is a complete, deliberate, and sustained campaign to eliminate Father from L.D.W.'s life.

62. The Michigan Supreme Court in *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich. 81, 92 (2010), emphasized that Factor (j) is of critical importance and that a parent who actively undermines the child's relationship with the other parent acts contrary to the child's best interests.

63. #### Factor (k) (continued): Parental alienation as a form of emotional abuse.

Mother's 329+ day denial of all parenting time, combined with the coordinated Watson family alienation campaign, constitutes emotional abuse of L.D.W. by depriving him of a relationship with his father during the most formative years of his development.

#### Factor (l): Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody or parenting time dispute.

### C. The Extended Denial of Parenting Time Constitutes a Violation of Father's Constitutional Rights

73. The United States Supreme Court has long recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

74. In *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982), the Supreme Court held that the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment protects the fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child, and that before a state may sever this interest entirely, it must provide fundamentally fair procedures.

75. The 329+ day denial of all parenting time in this case constitutes a *de facto* termination of Father's parental relationship with L.D.W. While Father's legal rights as a parent have not been formally terminated, the practical effect of 329+ days of zero contact is indistinguishable from termination for a child of L.D.W.'s age.

76. This *de facto* termination has occurred without: (a) a finding of unfitness by clear and convincing evidence; (b) a full evidentiary hearing on the question of whether parenting time would endanger L.D.W.; (c) the appointment of counsel for Father as required in termination proceedings; or (d) any of the other procedural safeguards that the Due Process Clause mandates before such a drastic interference with parental rights.

77. The constitutional violation is ongoing and continuing. Each additional day of denied parenting time compounds the constitutional injury and deepens the irreparable harm to both Father and L.D.W.

### D. Father Is Entitled to Makeup Parenting Time Under MCL 722.27a(7)

78. MCL 722.27a(7) provides specific remedies for the wrongful denial of parenting time:

> "If a child's parent . . . has been wrongfully denied parenting time . . . the court shall do 1 or more of the following: (a) Grant additional parenting time to compensate for the parenting time wrongfully denied."

79. The statute further authorizes the court to modify the custody or parenting time order, hold the offending party in contempt, and award actual costs and attorney fees. MCL 722.27a(7)(b)-(d).

80. Father has been wrongfully denied 329+ consecutive days of parenting time. Under MCL 722.27a(7)(a), Father is entitled to makeup parenting time reasonably calculated to compensate for this extended deprivation and to restore the parent-child bond.

81. Given the extraordinary length of the denial and the young age of L.D.W., makeup parenting time should be structured in a manner that provides for a graduated reintroduction period followed by an expanded parenting time schedule that exceeds what would otherwise be ordered, in order to compensate for the lost time and to counteract the effects of parental alienation.

### E. The Friend of the Court Has Failed to Protect Father's Parenting Time Rights

82. MCR 3.208 establishes the role of the Friend of the Court in domestic relations proceedings, including the responsibility to investigate and make recommendations regarding parenting time and to assist in the enforcement of parenting time orders.

83. Despite Father's documented efforts to report the denial of parenting time and to seek enforcement assistance, the Friend of the Court has failed to take effective action to ensure Father's parenting time rights are protected.

84. Father respectfully requests that the Court direct the Friend of the Court to conduct a thorough investigation of the denial of parenting time and to make recommendations consistent with the legal analysis set forth in this motion.

### F. Parental Alienation and the Best Interests of L.D.W.

85. The Michigan Supreme Court's decision in *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich. 81 (2010), provides a comprehensive framework for analyzing cases involving parental alienation. The Court recognized that a parent's efforts to undermine the child's relationship with the other parent weigh heavily against that parent in the best interest analysis.

86. Mother's conduct in this case goes far beyond the type of alienation addressed in *Pierron*. While *Pierron* involved a parent who was undermining the relationship, Mother has completely eliminated the relationship. For 329+ days, L.D.W. has had no contact whatsoever with Father — no visits, no phone calls, no video calls, no letters, no communication of any kind.

87. The developmental psychology literature is unequivocal that the complete absence of a parent during early childhood causes lasting developmental harm. 88. The coordinated involvement of the Watson family — Albert Watson, Lori Watson, and Cody Watson — in the alienation campaign demonstrates that this is not a case of one parent making isolated poor decisions, but rather a deliberate, family-wide strategy to permanently sever L.D.W.'s relationship with Father.

### G. The Seven Unfounded Investigations Confirm Father's Fitness

89. Father has been the subject of seven (7) or more investigations triggered by reports or allegations associated with Mother or the Watson family. Every single investigation has concluded without any finding of abuse, neglect, or criminal conduct by Father.
