## III. LEGAL STANDARDS FOR TEMPORARY RELIEF

Under MCR 7.205(F)(2), this Court may grant immediate consideration when an order will have significant effects within a short time frame. Under MCR 7.209 and its inherent authority, this Court may stay enforcement of trial-court orders and grant other temporary relief to preserve the status quo and prevent irreparable harm while an application is pending.

In deciding whether to grant interim relief, this Court considers:

1. **Likelihood of success on the merits;**
2. **Irreparable harm to the movant absent relief;**
3. **Harm to the opposing party or the child if relief is granted;** and
4. **Public interest and systemic concerns** (including adherence to court rules, statutory standards, and due process).

In the child-custody context, these factors are evaluated through the lens of the Child Custody Act's presumption favoring parent-child relationships and the overarching "best interests of the child" standard.

## IV. APPLICATION OF THE FACTORS

### A. Likelihood of Success on the Merits

As detailed in the Brief on Appeal (incorporated by reference), Appellant is likely to succeed on at least five independent grounds:

**1. Ex Parte Procedural Violations.** The August 6–8, 2025 ex parte suspension violated MCR 3.207(B) and the 2025 amendments requiring an evidentiary hearing before changing an established custodial environment. There were no verified allegations of immediate and irreparable harm, no prompt evidentiary hearing (90-day delay vs. 21-day requirement), and no best-interest findings.

**2. Evidentiary Framework Errors.** The trial court unlawfully discounted the court-ordered HealthWest evaluation (September 4, 2025 clean baseline) while relying on an unauthenticated "second" evaluation Father never underwent, contrary to MRE 901, 702, and 703.

**3. Suppression of Defense Evidence.** The court excluded Father's evidence (HealthWest report, AppClose logs, police clarifications, recordings), blocked motion practice, and issued orders without statutory findings — depriving Father of a meaningful opportunity to be heard in violation of *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65–66 (2000), and *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404–412; 852 NW2d 524 (2014).

**4. PPO Weaponization.** PPO contempt proceedings were used to ratchet up custody and parenting-time restrictions outside the Child Custody Act framework, contrary to *Hunter v Hunter*, 484 Mich 247, 265–268; 771 NW2d 694 (2009), and MCR 3.708, while similar conduct by the other parent went unaddressed.

**5. Judicial Bias.** The Rusco warrant email, the Martini prejudgment email, off-record clinician coordination, on-record hostility, and systematic exclusion of Father's evidence collectively establish an appearance of bias and probability of actual bias under MCR 2.003(C), *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497; 548 NW2d 210 (1996), and *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co*, 556 US 868, 877–878 (2009).

These are not close calls at the margins of discretion; they are structural errors that directly implicate statutory and constitutional protections.

### B. Irreparable Harm Absent Relief

The ongoing total suspension of parenting time causes irreparable harm in multiple ways:

- The parent-child relationship deteriorates with each week of forced separation; L.D.W. is three years old and in a critical developmental window;
- L.D.W.'s perception of Father is being shaped by absence and by a narrative of risk unsupported by tested evidence;
- The longer the separation persists, the more the unlawful "status quo" can be invoked to oppose reinstatement;
- Father has already lost two jobs and two homes as a direct consequence of the court's enforcement trajectory;
- Father has served 59 days in jail — every additional day of separation compounds the constitutional injury.

No later award of make-up parenting time can fully restore the lost months of ordinary, developmental contact during a formative period of L.D.W.'s life.

### C. Harm to Appellee or the Child if Interim Relief Is Granted

Interim relief can be tailored to minimize risk, if any, while restoring contact:

- Parenting time can be reinstated under the last non-suspensive order (which the court previously deemed safe for 438 consecutive days);
- Exchanges can be conducted at neutral locations or via third-party supervisors;
- Communication rules can be clarified to respect PPO boundaries while permitting necessary logistics.

There is no clear and convincing evidence that ordinary parenting time endangers L.D.W.'s physical, mental, or emotional health. If such evidence existed, the court would have produced it in a proper evidentiary hearing. Instead:

- The court operated 438 days of 50/50 custody without incident;
- The HealthWest September 4, 2025 evaluation — the only court-ordered evaluation Father actually completed — found no safety basis to suspend contact; and
- The 7-day "Rule-out Delusional Disorder" flip is speculative, with the clinician acknowledging inability to distinguish factual concerns from delusional ones.

### D. Public Interest and Systemic Concerns

Granting interim relief promotes:

- Adherence to MCR 3.207 and MCR 3.210's safeguards against casual use of ex parte orders that alter custodial environments;
- Proper application of the Child Custody Act's presumption favoring parent-child contact;
- Meaningful due-process protections for self-represented litigants in high-stakes domestic cases;
- Public confidence that custody outcomes will not be driven by untested ex parte narratives, unauthenticated documents, off-record coordination between court staff and prosecutors, or explicit hostility to a party's attempts to seek relief; and
- Judicial accountability — the newly discovered Rusco emails and Martini prejudgment communication demand prompt appellate oversight to protect the integrity of the tribunal.
