## III. PROPER CAUSE AND CHANGE OF CIRCUMSTANCES

11\. Before modifying a custody order, the Court must first determine whether the moving party has demonstrated either proper cause or a change of circumstances. MCL 722.27(1)(c); *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508-509 (2003).

12\. "Proper cause" means one or more appropriate grounds that have or could have a significant effect on the child's life sufficient to warrant a reevaluation of the custody order. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 511.

13\. A "change of circumstances" requires conditions that have or could have a significant effect on the child's well-being since the entry of the last custody order. *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 513; *Pierron v. Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 93 (2010).

14\. Father demonstrates the following changes of circumstances and proper cause, each of which independently satisfies the threshold requirement and which collectively present an overwhelming case for modification:

### A. Ex Parte Custody Reversal Without Due Process

15\. On July 17, 2025, the prior court (Hon. Jenny L. McNeill, now subject to a pending disqualification motion, Filing #3) entered an ex parte order that reversed the established 50/50 custody arrangement and awarded Mother sole physical custody — **without providing Father notice or an opportunity to be heard.**

16\. This ex parte reversal eliminated 438 days of established shared custody in a single stroke, without any evidentiary hearing, without testimony, and without Father's participation. This constitutes a deprivation of Father's due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment and Const 1963, art 1, § 17. *See Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) (three-factor balancing test for procedural due process); *Fuentes v. Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67, 80 (1972) (prior notice and hearing required before deprivation of protected interest); MCR 3.207(A) (requirements for ex parte orders in domestic relations proceedings).

16a\. The ex parte order was predicated in part on a recording obtained by Lori Watson (Mother's relative and fellow Kent County employee, Employee ID 1190) in apparent violation of MCL 750.539c (felony eavesdropping — punishable by up to two years' imprisonment). Evidence obtained through illegal eavesdropping constitutes "fruit of the poisonous tree" and should not serve as the basis for custody modification. *See Mapp v. Ohio*, 367 U.S. 643 (1961); *Wong Sun v. United States*, 371 U.S. 471 (1963); *People v. Beavers*, 393 Mich 554 (1975).

16b\. The pattern of ex parte orders in this case is itself a change of circumstances. The record reflects a 44% ex parte order rate in proceedings involving these parties, with **100% of ex parte orders favoring Mother** — a statistical pattern that is facially inconsistent with neutral adjudication. *See Craig v. Boren*, 429 U.S. 190 (1976) (intermediate scrutiny for gender-based classifications); *United States v. Virginia*, 518 U.S. 515 (1996) (exceedingly persuasive justification required for gender-based disparate treatment).

## IV. BEST INTEREST OF THE CHILD — MCL 722.23 FACTORS

29\. If the Court finds proper cause or a change of circumstances (which Father respectfully submits is self-evident), the Court must then evaluate the best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23. *Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004).

30\. Where the proposed modification would change the established custodial environment, the moving party bears the burden of proof by clear and convincing evidence. *Harvey*, 470 Mich at 194. Where modification would not change the established custodial environment, the burden is by a preponderance of the evidence. *Id.*

31\. Father submits that the **established custodial environment** was the 50/50 arrangement that existed for 438 days, and that **restoring** that arrangement would restore — not change — the established custodial environment. Therefore, the preponderance standard applies.

### Factor (a): Love, Affection, and Other Emotional Ties

32\. Father maintained an active, loving, and engaged parenting relationship with L.D.W. throughout the 438 days of 50/50 custody. Father participated in L.D.W.'s daily care, feeding, bathing, bedtime routines, and developmental activities. The emotional bond between Father and L.D.W. was strong and well-documented during the shared custody period.

33\. This bond has now been severed for more than 230 days — not by Father's choice, but by court order entered without hearing. Every additional day of separation inflicts further harm on the parent-child bond. **This factor favors Father and modification.**

### Factor (b): Capacity to Give Love, Affection, and Guidance; Continuation of Education

## VI. ESTABLISHED CUSTODIAL ENVIRONMENT

75\. Under Michigan law, an established custodial environment exists when "over an appreciable time the child naturally looks to the custodian in that environment for guidance, discipline, the necessities of life, and parental comfort." MCL 722.27(1)(c).

76\. The established custodial environment in this case was the **50/50 shared custody arrangement** that existed for 438 days (May 5, 2024 through July 17, 2025). During this period:

   a. L.D.W. looked to **both parents** for guidance, comfort, and the necessities of life;

   b. L.D.W. had two homes and was integrated into the routines of both households;

   c. The arrangement was of sufficient duration — over 14 months — to constitute an established custodial environment with both parents. *See Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 193 (2004).

77\. The ex parte order of July 17, 2025 **destroyed** the established custodial environment by unilaterally removing Father from L.D.W.'s life without notice or hearing.

78\. Father's motion seeks to **restore** the established custodial environment — not to change it. Accordingly, Father bears the burden of proving that restoration is in L.D.W.'s best interests by a **preponderance of the evidence**, not by clear and convincing evidence. *Harvey*, 470 Mich at 194.

79\. In the alternative, even under the clear and convincing evidence standard, the evidence overwhelmingly supports restoration of the 50/50 arrangement, as set forth in the best interest analysis above.
