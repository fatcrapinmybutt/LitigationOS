## V. LEGAL STANDARD FOR TEMPORARY RESTRAINING ORDER

33. Under **MCR 3.310(B)(1)**, a court may issue a temporary restraining order without notice to the adverse party if:

> "It clearly appears from specific facts shown by affidavit or by a verified complaint that **immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage** will result to the applicant from the delay required to effect notice, or that notice itself will precipitate adverse action before a restraining order can be issued."

34. The four factors governing issuance of a TRO and preliminary injunction in Michigan, as established in ***Thermatool Corp v Borzym***, **227 Mich App 366, 376; 575 NW2d 334 (1998)**, are:

> **(a)** Whether the moving party has demonstrated irreparable harm absent injunctive relief;
>
> **(b)** Whether the moving party has demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits;
>
> **(c)** Whether the balance of hardships favors the moving party; and
>
> **(d)** Whether the public interest would not be adversely affected by the issuance of injunctive relief.

35. Where ex parte relief is sought under **MCR 3.310(B)**, the movant must also demonstrate that the threat of harm is so immediate that waiting for notice and a hearing would result in the very injury sought to be prevented. The facts set forth herein satisfy this heightened standard.

## VI. APPLICATION OF THE FOUR TRO FACTORS

### A. Factor One: Irreparable Harm

36. **Plaintiff has already suffered irreparable harm.** On July 17, 2025, Defendants forcibly entered Plaintiff's home, removed his belongings, and posted a "FREE" sign inviting members of the public to take his property. This was done without any court order, writ of restitution, or other lawful process.

37. Plaintiff's mobile home is his **only residence** and the primary residence of his minor child, L.D.W. Loss of this home would render Plaintiff and his child homeless. Michigan courts have recognized that the **loss of one's home constitutes irreparable harm** that cannot be adequately compensated by monetary damages alone. *See* ***Thermatool Corp***, 227 Mich App at 376.

38. Plaintiff's mobile home — a **1970 Marlette, VIN 1646732** — is a unique, titled asset. It cannot be replaced with a fungible substitute. Its destruction or removal would permanently deprive Plaintiff of titled personal property in violation of constitutional due process protections.

39. Defendants' demonstrated willingness to act without legal authority — drilling locks, removing belongings, shutting off water with a child present, posting "FREE" signs — establishes that the threat of further harm is **not speculative but imminent and ongoing**.

40. Money damages, while available under **MCL 600.2918**, cannot undo the homelessness, displacement of a minor child, or destruction of irreplaceable personal property that will result if Defendants are not restrained.

40a. The quantifiable financial harm already suffered and imminently threatened includes the following verified amounts:

&emsp;(i) **Estimated trailer value**: Plaintiff's 1970 Marlette mobile home (VIN 1646732) has an estimated value of approximately **$4,200** based on comparable market data for titled manufactured homes of similar age and condition in Muskegon County (DB cycle6_financial_evidence: trailer valuation);

&emsp;(ii) **Rent paid for uninhabitable premises**: Plaintiff paid approximately **$8,640 or more** in lot rent ($720/month × 12+ months) for premises that materially failed to comply with habitability standards, as documented by EGLE Violation Notice VN-017235 (DB cycle6_financial_evidence: rent payments; DB damages_calculations: Rent recovery, $5K–$24K range);

&emsp;(iii) **Relocation costs**: If Plaintiff is dispossessed, relocation of a manufactured home and household — including transport, site preparation, utility reconnection, and temporary housing during transition — is estimated at **$3,000–$15,000** (DB damages_calculations: relocation cost estimates);

&emsp;(iv) **Lost personal property**: Defendants' July 17, 2025 forced removal and posting of a "FREE" sign resulted in the loss of personal belongings of indeterminate but substantial value, the full extent of which Plaintiff is still cataloguing.

40b. These financial damages are in addition to — and cannot substitute for — the irreparable non-monetary harm of homelessness, displacement of a minor child, and destruction of titled personal property that constitutional due process protects against extrajudicial seizure.

### B. Factor Two: Likelihood of Success on the Merits

41. **Plaintiff is likely to succeed on the merits** for multiple independent reasons:

42. *Personal Property Rights.* Michigan law is unambiguous that a mobile home titled with the Secretary of State is the personal property of the titleholder. **MCL 125.2330i**. Defendants have no ownership interest in Plaintiff's mobile home. The lot lease grants occupancy of the land only — it does not convey any interest in the home itself.

43. *Forcible Entry and Detainer.* Defendants' forcible entry and seizure violates **MCL 600.2918**, which imposes **treble damages** for forcible entry and detainer. Defendants drilled locks, removed belongings, and posted a "FREE" sign without any writ of restitution or court order. This is textbook forcible entry. *See* ***Mfrs Hanover Trust Co v Snell***, **142 Mich App 548, 556; 370 NW2d 401 (1985)** (self-help eviction constitutes forcible entry and detainer).

44. *Failure to Follow Mandatory Eviction Procedures.* **MCL 600.5714** requires summary proceedings to be initiated by proper complaint, with proper service, before a court with jurisdiction. **MCL 125.2312f** establishes specific procedures for eviction from manufactured housing communities, including written notice with specific statutory grounds. Defendants bypassed these mandatory requirements entirely, resorting instead to extrajudicial self-help.

45. *Dissolved Entity Lacks Standing.* The entity that initiated eviction proceedings — Homes of America, LLC — is **administratively dissolved**. Under **MCL 450.4802**, a dissolved LLC may only take actions to wind up its affairs. Initiating new eviction proceedings exceeds this statutory authority. Any judgment obtained by a dissolved entity acting beyond its winding-up authority is **void or voidable**. *See* **MCL 450.4802(c)**.

46. *Consumer Protection Act Violations.* Defendants have committed multiple violations of the **Michigan Consumer Protection Act, MCL 445.903**, including operating under multiple undisclosed entity names, presenting irreconcilable financial ledger versions, coercing lease modifications under threat of eviction, and retaliating against habitability complaints.

47. *Implied Warranty of Habitability.* The implied warranty of habitability under **MCL 554.139** cannot be waived by contract. ***Trentadue v Gorton***, **479 Mich 378, 382; 737 NW2d 700 (2007)**. Defendants' EGLE-documented sewage violations (Violation Notice VN-017235) and water shutoff constitute material breaches that undermine any claim of nonpayment of rent.

### C. Factor Three: Balance of Equities

48. **The balance of equities overwhelmingly favors Plaintiff.** Plaintiff stands to lose his home — his only residence and the home of his minor child. Defendants, by contrast, stand to lose nothing by being restrained from seizing property they do not own.

49. Plaintiff's mobile home is titled in his name. Defendants' property interest extends only to the underlying land. An order preventing Defendants from seizing Plaintiff's mobile home costs Defendants nothing because they never had a lawful right to take it.

50. Defendants are a **New Jersey LLC** that purchased the park for **$1.4 million**. They are a substantial commercial entity with resources to litigate. Plaintiff is a *pro se* individual litigant with a minor child. The disparity in resources and the vulnerability of a family facing homelessness weigh heavily in Plaintiff's favor.

51. If a TRO is denied and Defendants seize or destroy the mobile home, **the harm is irreversible** — the home cannot be un-destroyed, the child cannot be un-displaced. If a TRO is granted and later determined to have been improvidently issued, Defendants suffer **no cognizable harm** because they have no ownership interest in the mobile home.

### D. Factor Four: Public Interest

52. **The public interest strongly favors injunctive relief.** Michigan's manufactured housing regulatory scheme — including the Mobile Home Commission Act (**MCL 125.2301** *et seq.*), the Truth in Renting Act (**MCL 554.631** *et seq.*), and the summary proceedings statutes (**MCL 600.5701** *et seq.*) — exists specifically to protect manufactured housing residents from the precise type of conduct in which Defendants have engaged.

53. The public has a strong interest in ensuring that:

> (a) Property owners are not dispossessed of titled assets without due process of law;
>
> (b) Landlords cannot bypass mandatory statutory eviction procedures through self-help remedies;
>
> (c) Dissolved entities are not permitted to continue initiating legal proceedings beyond their statutory authority;
>
> (d) Minor children are not rendered homeless by extrajudicial landlord action; and
>
> (e) Michigan's comprehensive manufactured housing regulatory framework is respected and enforced.

54. Allowing Defendants to seize Plaintiff's titled personal property outside the judicial process would send a dangerous signal to every manufactured housing community operator in Michigan that self-help remedies are permissible — directly undermining the Legislature's comprehensive regulatory scheme designed to protect this vulnerable class of residents.
