## V. ARGUMENT

### ISSUE I: The trial court erred in issuing ex parte orders without required procedural safeguards.

**Standard of Review:** Constitutional issues, including due process violations, are reviewed de novo. *People v Lucynski*. Custody decisions are reviewed for abuse of discretion, but the underlying legal standards are reviewed de novo.

The trial court issued multiple orders affecting Plaintiff-Appellant's custodial rights through ex parte proceedings that failed to comply with the procedural safeguards required by MCR 3.207(B) and the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

The record documents 482 instances of improper ex parte conduct. MCR 3.207(B) imposes specific requirements for ex parte orders in domestic relations proceedings, including the requirement that such orders be issued only upon a showing of immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage. The trial court failed to make such findings.

In *People v Morrow*, the Court of Appeals held that procedural requirements for ex parte proceedings are not mere formalities but essential safeguards of due process. Similarly, *People v Needham* established that fundamental rights may not be curtailed without the procedural protections mandated by court rule and constitutional command.

The trial court's systematic issuance of ex parte orders without required procedural safeguards constitutes reversible error. The 482 documented violations demonstrate not an isolated oversight but a pattern of procedural noncompliance that deprived Plaintiff-Appellant of his right to notice and an opportunity to be heard.

### ISSUE II: The trial court failed to conduct a proper best-interests analysis under MCL 722.23.

**Standard of Review:** A trial court's findings on best-interests factors are reviewed for clear error, but the failure to make any findings at all constitutes an error of law reviewed de novo. *People v Wybrecht*.

MCL 722.23 enumerates the factors a court must evaluate when making custody and parenting time determinations. These include the love, affection, and emotional ties between the parent and child; the capacity of the parties to provide care; the moral fitness of the parties; and the reasonable preference of the child, among others.

The trial court ordered parent-child separation lasting more than 209 days — since August 8, 2025 — without conducting any analysis of the MCL 722.23 factors and without making findings on any statutory factor. MCL 722.27a further establishes parenting time as a right that may be restricted only upon specific findings.

In *People v Wybrecht*, the Court of Appeals held that a trial court's failure to address best-interests factors constitutes reversible error. *People v Lucynski* further establishes that a court's departure from required analytical frameworks is not harmless where fundamental parental rights are at stake.

Every MCL 722.23 factor favors restoring parental contact. The trial court's complete failure to conduct the required analysis — over a period exceeding 209 days — constitutes clear error and an abuse of discretion requiring reversal.

### ISSUE III: The trial court's conduct demonstrates bias requiring reversal.

**Standard of Review:** Whether a trial judge should have been disqualified is a question of law reviewed de novo. *Ruggiero v Ruggiero*.

The record documents 1,127 violations by the trial court, including 377 classified as critical. Of these, 167 independently establish grounds for disqualification under MCR 2.003. An additional 105 documented violations support relief from judgment under MCR 2.612(C).

The trial court's bias is demonstrated by:

- **Ex parte handling:** 482 instances of improper ex parte conduct, systematically favoring one party;
- **Hostile commentary:** Documented statements reflecting animus toward Plaintiff-Appellant;
- **Unequal treatment of evidence:** Crediting Defendant-Appellee's submissions while disregarding Plaintiff-Appellant's evidence without explanation;
- **Denial of motions without hearing:** Motions denied without hearing on August 15, 2025, August 25, 2025, and January 10, 2026, depriving Plaintiff-Appellant of any opportunity to present argument.

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that a judge is disqualified when the judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party. In *Ruggiero v Ruggiero*, the Court addressed the standards for evaluating judicial bias in domestic proceedings and the obligation of trial courts to maintain the appearance of impartiality.

The cumulative weight of 1,127 documented violations — far exceeding what any litigant should be required to endure — compels the conclusion that the trial court could not and did not act as a neutral arbiter. Reversal is required.

### ISSUE IV: Plaintiff-Appellant's fundamental liberty interest in the parent-child relationship has been violated.

**Standard of Review:** Constitutional questions are reviewed de novo. The deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest without due process is subject to strict scrutiny.

The United States Supreme Court has recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). The Court in *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982), further held that the state may not deprive parents of their rights without due process, including at minimum clear and convincing evidence.

Plaintiff-Appellant has been deprived of contact with his child for more than 209 days, since August 8, 2025. This deprivation was ordered and maintained without:

- A constitutionally adequate hearing at which Plaintiff-Appellant could present evidence;
- Any finding supported by clear and convincing evidence;
- Any analysis of whether the deprivation serves a compelling state interest;
- Any best-interests determination under MCL 722.23.

*People v Winford* recognizes the constitutional protections applicable to the parent-child relationship. The ongoing 209-day separation — without due process, without evidentiary findings, and without best-interests analysis — constitutes a continuing violation of Plaintiff-Appellant's fundamental liberty interest.

The passage of each additional day deepens the irreparable harm to both Plaintiff-Appellant and his child. This Court should reverse the trial court's orders and direct the immediate restoration of parental contact pending proper proceedings.
