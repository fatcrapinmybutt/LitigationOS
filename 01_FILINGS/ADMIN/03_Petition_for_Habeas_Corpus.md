### B. The Constitution Guarantees Habeas Corpus for This Deprivation

The Michigan Constitution provides in unequivocal terms: "The privilege of the writ of habeas corpus shall not be suspended unless in case of rebellion or invasion the public safety may require it." Const 1963, Art 1, § 12. The federal Constitution provides the same guarantee. US Const, Art I, § 9, cl 2.

Habeas corpus is the Great Writ—the foundational remedy against unlawful restraint of liberty. When a person, including a child, is held under color of law but the underlying orders were constitutionally deficient, habeas corpus is the proper vehicle to challenge that restraint. The writ extends to cases of child custody where the custodial arrangement is maintained by void or voidable court orders. See MCL 600.4301 et seq.

### C. The Underlying PPO Orders Are Void or Voidable

The Personal Protection Orders issued under MCL 600.2950a that form the basis of the child's restraint from Petitioner were issued with 126 documented violations of PPO procedure. These violations include but are not limited to: ex parte issuance without the procedural safeguards required by MCR 3.708; denial of Petitioner's motions to modify or terminate without hearing; and continuation of orders despite changed circumstances.

An ex parte PPO entered without compliance with the mandatory procedural requirements of MCR 3.708 is void ab initio. A void order has no legal force or effect and cannot serve as lawful authority for the continued restraint of a child from a parent. The 126 documented procedural violations demonstrate a systematic pattern of non-compliance that renders these orders unenforceable as a matter of law.

### D. Petitioner's Fundamental Liberty Interest Has Been Violated

The United States Supreme Court has recognized that "[t]he liberty interest at issue in this case—the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children—is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). This fundamental right is protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

In Santosky v Kramer, 455 US 745, 753 (1982), the Court held that due process requires heightened procedural protections before the State may completely sever the parent-child bond. The Court emphasized that "the fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents."

Here, Petitioner has been completely deprived of all contact with his child for 209+ days—a deprivation functionally equivalent to termination of parental rights—without any of the heightened procedural protections that due process demands.
