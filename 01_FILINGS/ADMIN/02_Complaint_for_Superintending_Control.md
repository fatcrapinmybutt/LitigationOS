## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. This Court's Superintending Control Authority Compels Intervention

Under Const 1963, Art 6, § 4, the Michigan Supreme Court possesses "general superintending control over all courts." This authority is not merely appellate; it is an independent constitutional power to correct lower courts that act beyond their jurisdiction or fail to perform clear legal duties. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 434 (1993). MCR 7.304 provides the procedural vehicle for invoking this power through a complaint for superintending control.

The standard is met when a lower court has "(1) acted beyond its jurisdiction, (2) proceeded despite lacking jurisdiction, or (3) failed to perform a clear legal duty." *Id.* As demonstrated below, Judge McNeill's conduct satisfies all three prongs.

### B. The Lower Court Has Systematically Failed in Its Judicial Duties

The record before this Court is not one of isolated error. OMEGA analysis of the complete case record documents **1,127 judicial violations** across the proceedings in Case No. 2024-1507-DC before Judge Jenny L. McNeill. Of these, **377 are classified as critical severity**. The violations break down as follows:

- **482 ex parte violations**, reflecting a pattern of one-sided communication and decision-making outside Plaintiff's presence or knowledge;
- **167 instances** warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003, including documented bias, prejudgment, and hostile commentary directed at Plaintiff;
- **161 instances** of procedural misconduct, including the systematic denial of motions without hearing;
- **126 violations** related to Personal Protection Order proceedings; and
- **105 notice violations**, depriving Plaintiff of the opportunity to be heard.

This volume and pattern of violations is not a matter of judicial discretion—it is a systematic failure to perform the duties required by Michigan law and court rules. No litigant should be required to endure over one thousand documented violations before obtaining relief.

### C. Systematic Ex Parte Violations Mandate This Court's Intervention

Of the 1,127 total violations, **482 involve ex parte communications or proceedings**—the single largest category. MCR 3.207(B) governs ex parte orders in domestic relations cases and imposes strict procedural requirements to protect the due process rights of both parties. The lower court has treated these requirements as optional.

Ex parte violations are uniquely corrosive to the judicial process because they deprive one party of the fundamental right to be heard. When a court conducts 482 such violations in a single case, it has not merely erred—it has abandoned the adversarial framework upon which our system of justice depends. This Court's superintending control is the only mechanism capable of correcting a pattern this pervasive.

Plaintiff, proceeding pro se, has been systematically denied the ability to present his case.
