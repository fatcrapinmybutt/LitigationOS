## VI. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY — EXCEPTIONS APPLICABLE

82. Plaintiff acknowledges the general doctrine of **absolute judicial immunity** from damages under § 1983. *See Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11 (1991) (per curiam); *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356 (1978).

83. However, judicial immunity does **not** bar the claims asserted herein because two recognized exceptions apply:

### A. Non-Judicial Acts

84. Certain of Defendant McNeill's actions were **non-judicial in character**, even though taken from the bench:

    (a) The entry of five ex parte orders on a single day without any showing of emergency constitutes administrative, not judicial, conduct. *See Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 229 (1988) (distinguishing judicial from administrative acts);

    (b) Self-ruling on a disqualification motion directed at herself, rather than referring it to the Chief Judge as required by MCR 2.003(D), constitutes a non-judicial act;

    (c) Systematic exclusion of a party from proceedings — through denial of notice, denial of hearings, and blanket dismissal of motions — is not a function "normally performed by a judge" but rather the conduct of an "administrator" depriving a litigant of access. *Id.* at 227.

### B. Complete Absence of Jurisdiction

85. Defendant McNeill acted in the **complete absence of all jurisdiction** when she:

    (a) Entered ex parte orders without complying with the mandatory prerequisites of MCR 3.207(C)(2), which vest jurisdiction to enter ex parte relief only upon a verified showing of "immediate and irreparable injury";

    (b) Effectively terminated Plaintiff's parental rights — the most drastic state action recognized in the law — through a series of ex parte orders that provided none of the constitutional protections required for termination proceedings under *Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 747–48;

    (c) The functional termination of parental rights without following mandatory procedural requirements strips the court of jurisdiction to take such action. *See Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522, 541–42 (1984).

### C. Declaratory and Injunctive Relief

86. Even if judicial immunity bars damages against Defendant McNeill personally, § 1983 claims proceed for **declaratory and injunctive relief** under *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984), as modified by the Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1996. Declaratory relief remains available.

## VII. QUALIFIED IMMUNITY — DEFEAT

87. Individual-capacity Defendants McNeill and Barnes may assert **qualified immunity**. Plaintiff demonstrates that qualified immunity does not apply because the rights at issue were **clearly established** at the time of the violations.

88. By 2023–2025, it was **clearly established** that:

    (a) A parent has a fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his child. *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65; *Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 753; *Lassiter v. Department of Social Services*, 452 U.S. 18 (1981);

    (b) The state may not sever or significantly curtail parenting time without constitutionally adequate process. *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972); *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102 (1996);

    (c) Due process requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before deprivation of a protected liberty interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976); *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 U.S. 306, 314 (1950);

    (d) Ex parte relief is an extraordinary remedy requiring a genuine showing of immediate and irreparable injury. *Granny Goose Foods, Inc. v. Brotherhood of Teamsters*, 415 U.S. 423, 438–39 (1974);

    (e) Government officials may not retaliate against individuals for exercising their First Amendment right to petition. *Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378, 394 (6th Cir. 1999) (en banc);

    (f) The Equal Protection Clause prohibits treating similarly situated litigants differently without rational basis. *Village of Willowbrook v. Olech*, 528 U.S. 562 (2000).

89. **No reasonable official** in the position of any individual-capacity Defendant could have believed:

    (a) That entering 44% of all orders without notice was constitutionally permissible;

    (b) That eliminating all parent-child contact for 209+ days through ex parte orders — without an evidentiary hearing, without sworn testimony, and without compliance with MCR 3.207(C)(2) — was lawful;

    (c) That dismissing six pending motions without adjudication comported with due process;

    (d) That retaliating against a litigant for filing motions and pursuing appellate remedies was constitutionally protected conduct;

    (e) That the asymmetric treatment of similarly situated parties was rational.

90. Accordingly, qualified immunity does not shield any individual-capacity Defendant from liability for the constitutional violations alleged herein.
