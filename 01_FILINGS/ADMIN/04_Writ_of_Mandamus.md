## III. LEGAL AUTHORITY

- **Const 1963, Art 6, § 4** — "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts; power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs." This constitutional provision vests the Michigan Supreme Court with the power to issue writs of mandamus compelling lower court judges to perform their legal duties.

- **MCR 3.305** — Governs mandamus actions in Michigan. Establishes that mandamus is appropriate to compel the performance of a duty specifically enjoined by law when the party seeking relief has a clear legal right, the respondent has a clear legal duty, and the act sought to be compelled is ministerial in nature.

- **MCR 7.304** — Governs original proceedings in the Michigan Supreme Court, including complaints for mandamus. Provides the procedural framework for invoking this Court's original jurisdiction to issue extraordinary writs.

- **MCR 2.119** — Establishes requirements for motion practice, including the requirement that courts schedule and hold hearings on properly filed motions. This rule imposes a clear ministerial duty to afford parties the opportunity to be heard before a court rules on their motions.

- **MCR 2.003** — Sets forth the standards and procedures for judicial disqualification, including mandatory disqualification for bias or prejudice. When grounds for disqualification exist, the judge has a clear legal duty to follow the disqualification procedure, including referral to the chief judge. This duty is ministerial, not discretionary.

- **MCL 722.23** — Enumerates the twelve best-interest-of-the-child factors that courts **must** consider and make findings on in custody and parenting time determinations. The duty to analyze and apply these factors is mandatory and ministerial; a court may not simply ignore them.

- **MCL 722.27a** — Governs parenting time and establishes a presumption that parenting time with both parents serves the child's best interests. Courts have a clear legal duty to consider and provide for parenting time consistent with this presumption.

- ***Teasel v Department of Mental Health*, 419 Mich 390 (1984)** — The Michigan Supreme Court established the controlling standard for mandamus: the petitioner must demonstrate (1) a clear legal right to the performance of the specific duty sought, (2) a clear legal duty on the part of the respondent to perform that act, (3) that the act is ministerial in nature (involving no exercise of discretion), and (4) that no other adequate legal remedy exists. This is the seminal mandamus case in Michigan.

- ***Taxpayers for Michigan Constitutional Government v State*, 471 Mich 306 (2004)** — Reaffirmed that mandamus is an extraordinary remedy available "only when there is a clear legal duty, a clear legal right, and no other adequate legal remedy."

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. Respondent Has a Clear Legal Duty to Act

Mandamus relief requires, as a threshold matter, the existence of a clear legal duty on the part of the respondent to perform a specific act. *Teasel v Department of Mental Health*, 419 Mich 390, 405 (1984). Respondent Judge McNeill owes at least four distinct clear legal duties relevant to this case, each of which is ministerial rather than discretionary:

**1. Duty to Hold Hearings on Properly Filed Motions (MCR 2.119).** Michigan Court Rule 2.119 governs motion practice and requires courts to schedule hearings on properly filed motions before ruling. This is not a discretionary choice — the right to be heard before a tribunal disposes of a claim is a foundational requirement of due process. A court that denies a motion without hearing has failed to perform a ministerial act required by the court rules.

**2. Duty to Follow Disqualification Procedures (MCR 2.003).** MCR 2.003 establishes mandatory procedures when grounds for disqualification are presented. When a party files a motion for disqualification supported by factual allegations of bias, the judge must either disqualify herself or refer the motion to the chief judge for determination. MCR 2.003(D). This is a mandatory procedural duty, not a matter of judicial discretion. A judge cannot simply ignore disqualification grounds.

**3. Duty to Conduct Best-Interests Analysis (MCL 722.23).** In any custody or parenting time proceeding, the court **must** evaluate and make findings on each of the twelve statutory best-interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23. This duty is imposed by the Legislature and is ministerial in that the court has no discretion to skip the analysis entirely. A court that separates a parent from a child for 209+ days without conducting any best-interests analysis has failed to perform a duty specifically enjoined by law.

**4. Duty to Consider Parenting Time (MCL 722.27a).** MCL 722.27a establishes a presumption that parenting time with both parents is in the child's best interest. A court has a clear legal duty to consider and provide for parenting time consistent with this statutory framework. The complete elimination of parenting time without findings supporting that result is a failure to perform this duty.

Each of these duties is "specifically enjoined by law" within the meaning of mandamus jurisprudence. None involves the exercise of judicial discretion that would render mandamus inappropriate. The question is not how the court should rule, but whether the court must act at all — and the answer, in each instance, is yes.

### B. Respondent Has Failed to Perform These Duties

The record demonstrates that Respondent has not merely erred in the exercise of discretion — she has refused to perform the ministerial acts required of her.

**Refusal to hold hearings.** Respondent denied Plaintiff's properly filed motions on three documented occasions without any hearing:
- **August 15, 2025**: Motion denied without hearing.
- **August 25, 2025**: Motion denied without hearing.
- **January 10, 2026**: Motion denied without hearing.

At no point has Respondent afforded Plaintiff a hearing on any motion. MCR 2.119 does not grant courts discretion to simply deny motions without hearing the movant. This is a refusal to perform a ministerial duty.

**Failure to follow disqualification procedures.** Despite 167 documented instances of conduct warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003 — including bias, prejudgment, hostile commentary directed at Plaintiff, and ex parte handling — Respondent has neither recused herself nor referred the matter to the chief judge for disqualification review as required by MCR 2.003(D). The record reflects not one instance of compliance with disqualification procedures despite overwhelming grounds.

**Failure to conduct best-interests analysis.** During the entire 209+ day period of parent-child separation, Respondent has not conducted or documented any analysis of the twelve best-interest factors required by MCL 722.23. No findings of fact have been made. No factor-by-factor evaluation has occurred. A child has been separated from a parent for over seven months without the statutory analysis the Legislature deemed essential to such decisions.

**Failure to consider parenting time.** Respondent has effectively eliminated Plaintiff's parenting time for over 209 consecutive days without any finding that parenting time would not be in the child's best interest, as would be required to overcome the presumption established in MCL 722.27a.

### C. These Duties Are Ministerial, Not Discretionary

Mandamus lies only to compel the performance of ministerial duties — that is, duties "where the law prescribes and defines the duty to be performed with such precision and certainty as to leave nothing to the exercise of discretion or judgment." *Teasel*, 419 Mich at 405.

The duties at issue here meet this standard:

- The duty to hold hearings on motions under MCR 2.119 is defined with precision: a properly filed motion triggers the right to a hearing. The court has no discretion to refuse to hold the hearing entirely.
- The duty to follow MCR 2.003 disqualification procedures is mandatory: when grounds are presented, the judge must either recuse or refer. There is no third option of ignoring the motion.
- The duty to apply MCL 722.23 is statutory and specific: the court must consider and make findings on each of twelve enumerated factors. The court has discretion in weighing the factors, but has no discretion to skip the analysis altogether.
- The duty to consider parenting time under MCL 722.27a is statutory: the court must consider parenting time and apply the statutory presumption. Eliminating parenting time entirely without findings is not an exercise of discretion — it is a refusal to act.

Plaintiff does not ask this Court to dictate how Respondent should rule. Plaintiff asks this Court to compel Respondent to perform the acts the law requires her to perform — to hold hearings, to follow disqualification procedures, to conduct the mandated analysis, and to consider parenting time. These are the very definition of ministerial duties appropriate for mandamus relief.

### D. No Other Adequate Legal Remedy Exists

*Teasel* requires that the petitioner have no other adequate legal remedy before mandamus may issue. 419 Mich at 405. Plaintiff has exhausted every alternative avenue:

**1. Court of Appeals.** Plaintiff's appeal is pending before the Michigan Court of Appeals as Case No. 366810. However, the appellate process is inherently retrospective — it reviews decisions already made. It cannot compel the trial court to hold hearings it has refused to hold, conduct analyses it has refused to conduct, or follow procedures it has ignored. The Court of Appeals can reverse; it cannot compel prospective performance of ministerial duties. Moreover, the ordinary appellate timeline is constitutionally inadequate given the ongoing daily deprivation of Plaintiff's fundamental parental rights.

**2. Judicial Tenure Commission.** Plaintiff has filed a complaint with the JTC. However, the JTC's jurisdiction is limited to judicial discipline. It cannot enter orders restoring parenting time, vacating improper rulings, compelling hearings, or requiring disqualification. The JTC investigates; it does not provide remedial relief.

**3. Lower Court Remedies.** Every motion Plaintiff has filed in the 14th Circuit Court has been denied without hearing. The court that has refused to perform its duties cannot reasonably be expected to order itself to comply. Returning to the lower court would be futile.

Only this Court's mandamus authority can compel Respondent to perform the specific ministerial duties she has refused to perform. No other remedy is adequate.

Where a public official systematically refuses to perform ministerial duties, mandamus is the appropriate remedy to compel compliance with the law.

Mandamus is particularly appropriate where delay in compelling performance of a legal duty causes continuing irreparable harm. The emotional and psychological bond between parent and child deteriorates with each day of enforced absence. No monetary award can compensate for this loss. The urgency of this matter demands that this Court exercise its mandamus authority without delay.
