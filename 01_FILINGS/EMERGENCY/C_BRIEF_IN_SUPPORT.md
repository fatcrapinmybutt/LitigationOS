## II. STANDARD OF REVIEW FOR EMERGENCY MOTIONS

### A. MCR 2.119(B)(7) Standard

Emergency relief under MCR 2.119(B)(7) is warranted when:

1. **Irreparable injury, loss, or damage** will result from the delay required by normal motion procedures;
2. **Reasonable effort** has been made to notify the opposing party; and
3. **An affidavit** sets forth facts establishing the necessity for emergency relief.

The standard for "irreparable injury" in the context of parental rights is well established: the loss of a parent-child relationship during critical developmental years constitutes irreparable harm per se because it cannot be compensated by any monetary award or future court order. Michigan Coalition of Radioactive Material Users, Inc. v. Griepentrog, 945 F.2d 150, 154 (6th Cir. 1991) (harm is irreparable when it "cannot be adequately compensated" by damages).

### B. The Four-Factor Test for Injunctive/Emergency Relief

Michigan courts apply a four-factor test when evaluating emergency relief:

1. **Likelihood of success on the merits** -- Plaintiff must demonstrate a substantial likelihood of prevailing on the underlying claim.
2. **Irreparable harm** -- Plaintiff must show that irreparable injury will result absent emergency relief.
3. **Balance of equities** -- The balance of hardships must favor the movant.
4. **Public interest** -- Emergency relief must serve the public interest.

Each of these factors is overwhelmingly satisfied here, as demonstrated below.

## III. CONSTITUTIONAL ANALYSIS

### A. Substantive Due Process

The Fourteenth Amendment protects the fundamental right of parents to the care, custody, and control of their children. This right has been recognized and reaffirmed by the United States Supreme Court for over a century:

**Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000):** "The interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children -- is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." The plurality opinion traced this right through Meyer v. Nebraska, 262 U.S. 390 (1923), Pierce v. Society of Sisters, 268 U.S. 510 (1925), Prince v. Massachusetts, 321 U.S. 158 (1944), Wisconsin v. Yoder, 406 U.S. 205 (1972), and Quilloin v. Walcott, 434 U.S. 246 (1978), demonstrating an unbroken century of recognition.

**Santosky v. Kramer, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982):** "The fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State." The Court held that the state must prove its case for terminating parental rights by at least "clear and convincing evidence" -- a standard that has not been met here.

**Stanley v. Illinois, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972):** "The private interest here, that of a man in the children he has sired and raised, undeniably warrants deference and, absent a powerful countervailing interest, protection." The Court struck down an Illinois statute that presumed unwed fathers to be unfit, holding that due process requires an individualized determination of fitness before parental rights may be terminated.

**Application to this case:** A 571+ day deprivation of all parenting time, based on ex parte orders entered without notice or hearing, and in the face of overwhelming exculpatory evidence (multiple investigations/zero findings, negative drug screen, ALL ZEROS evaluation), constitutes a de facto severance of the parent-child relationship without the constitutional protections required by these authorities.

### B. Procedural Due Process -- Mathews v. Eldridge Analysis

Under Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), the adequacy of procedural protections is determined by balancing three factors:

1. **The private interest affected:** The interest at stake -- a parent's relationship with his child -- is among the most fundamental liberty interests recognized by the Constitution. Santosky, 455 U.S. at 758-59 ("a parent's desire for and right to the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children is an important interest that undeniably warrants deference").

2. **The risk of erroneous deprivation through existing procedures:** The risk here is not theoretical -- it has materialized. The ex parte procedures used to deprive Plaintiff of parenting time provided no notice, no hearing, and no opportunity to respond. The result is a 571+ day deprivation based on allegations that multiple investigations have uniformly disproven.

3. **The governmental interest:** The state's interest in protecting children is compelling, but it does not extend to depriving a parent of all contact with his child based on unsubstantiated allegations when every investigation has cleared the parent. The state's interest is in protecting children from actual danger, not in facilitating strategic litigation by one parent against another.

**Conclusion:** The Mathews balancing test overwhelmingly favors Plaintiff. The private interest at stake is of the highest constitutional order. The risk of erroneous deprivation through ex parte procedures has been demonstrated. And the governmental interest does not support continued deprivation when no evidence of danger exists.

### C. Equal Protection Analysis

The pattern of 52 ex parte orders with a 100%/0% grant rate disparity raises serious equal protection concerns. Under Craig v. Boren, 429 U.S. 190 (1976), classifications based on gender must serve important governmental objectives and be substantially related to the achievement of those objectives. The statistical pattern in this case -- combined with the complete absence of evidence supporting the restrictions on the father -- supports an inference that the disparate treatment is based on gender rather than the merits. See also Mississippi University for Women v. Hogan, 458 U.S. 718 (1982); United States v. Virginia, 518 U.S. 515 (1996).

## IV. MICHIGAN STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

### A. MCL 722.27a -- The Parenting Time Presumption

Michigan law creates one of the strongest parenting time presumptions in the nation:

**MCL 722.27a(1):** "Parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child."

**MCL 722.27a(3):** "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

This statutory scheme places the burden squarely on the party opposing parenting time. The party seeking to restrict parenting time -- here, Defendant Emily Watson -- must demonstrate, by the heightened "clear and convincing evidence" standard, that parenting time would "endanger" the child. This burden has not been met. multiple investigations, a negative drug screen, and an ALL ZEROS evaluation collectively establish, beyond any reasonable dispute, that parenting time with Plaintiff does not endanger A.P.

### B. MCL 722.27a(6) -- Parenting Time Violations

MCL 722.27a(6) provides that when parenting time is wrongfully denied, the court "shall" order makeup parenting time and "may" award costs, expenses, and attorney fees. This provision reflects the Legislature's determination that the denial of court-ordered parenting time is a serious matter warranting remedial action.

## V. CASE LAW SUPPORTING RESTORATION

### A. Michigan Case Law

**Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 499 (2003):**

Vodvarka is the leading Michigan case on modification of parenting time. The Court of Appeals held that before the court may modify parenting time, it must first determine whether the moving party has demonstrated "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances." The court defined "proper cause" as "one or more appropriate grounds that have or could have a significant effect on the child's life sufficient to warrant a review of the custody order." Id. at 512.

Here, 571+ days of total denial of parenting time based on ex parte orders that were entered without due process protections, and in the face of uniformly exculpatory evidence, constitute proper cause for modification that exceeds any threshold recognized in Vodvarka.

**Harvey v. Harvey, 470 Mich 186 (2004):**

In Harvey, the Michigan Supreme Court emphasized that the court must consider all relevant factors when making custody and parenting time determinations, and that the best interest of the child is the paramount concern. The Court criticized the trial court for failing to make adequate findings on the record. Here, no adequate findings were made before parenting time was suspended -- the suspension was entered ex parte without any evidentiary record.

**Dailey v. Dailey, 2005 WL 1272849 (Mich Ct App 2005):**

The Court of Appeals reversed a trial court's parenting time restriction where the restriction was not supported by sufficient evidence on the record. The court emphasized that restrictions on parenting time must be based on competent evidence, not mere allegations.

**Hunter v. Hunter, 484 Mich 247 (2009):**

The Michigan Supreme Court held that ex parte custody and parenting time orders are disfavored and may only be entered upon a showing of immediate and irreparable harm to the child. The procedural protections required by Hunter were not followed in this case.

### B. Federal Case Law

**Bartell v. Lohiser, 215 F.3d 550 (6th Cir. 2000):**

The Sixth Circuit recognized that state interference with parental rights triggers constitutional scrutiny. The court held that parents have a fundamental right under the Due Process Clause to make decisions regarding the care, custody, and control of their children, and that state action that interferes with this right must satisfy heightened scrutiny.

**Catz v. Chalker, 142 F.3d 279 (6th Cir. 1998):**

The Sixth Circuit held that the right to familial association is a fundamental right protected by the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, and that government interference with this right must be justified by a compelling state interest.

**Elrod v. Burns, 427 U.S. 347, 373 (1976):**

The Supreme Court observed that "the loss of First Amendment freedoms, for even minimal periods of time, unquestionably constitutes irreparable injury." This principle applies with even greater force to the loss of fundamental parental rights, which the Court has described as among the oldest and most precious of all liberty interests.

**Parham v. J.R., 442 U.S. 584, 602 (1979):**

The Court recognized that the public interest favors maintaining parent-child relationships and ensuring due process in proceedings that affect those relationships.

## VII. MCR 3.207 REQUIREMENTS ANALYSIS

MCR 3.207 governs interim orders in domestic relations cases and imposes specific requirements:

### A. MCR 3.207(A) -- Motion Required

An order affecting custody or parenting time must be based on a motion supported by an affidavit. Here, the original ex parte suspension was entered without the procedural protections required by this rule, including adequate notice to the non-moving party.

### B. MCR 3.207(B) -- Emergency Orders

MCR 3.207(B) permits emergency orders only upon a showing that:

1. An emergency exists that could significantly affect the health or safety of a child;
2. An affidavit supports the claimed emergency; and
3. The moving party has made a good faith effort to provide notice to the other party.

The continuing suspension fails all three prongs: (1) multiple investigations have found no emergency affecting the child's health or safety; (2) the factual basis underlying the original affidavit has been disproven; and (3) the initial order was entered without adequate notice.

### C. MCR 3.207(C) -- Duration Limitations

Emergency ex parte orders are intended to be temporary -- they provide stopgap protection during a genuine emergency until a hearing can be held. A 571+ day ex parte suspension without an evidentiary hearing far exceeds any reasonable interpretation of the temporary nature of emergency orders. The continued enforcement of this order without a hearing violates both the letter and spirit of MCR 3.207.

The cumulative error doctrine holds that multiple errors, even if individually harmless, may collectively prejudice a party to the point where a new proceeding or corrective relief is required. 1. **Ex parte suspension without notice or hearing** -- violation of procedural due process

## XI. RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED OBJECTIONS

### A. "The PPO Justifies Continued Denial"

**Response:** A PPO is not a custody order and does not, by itself, justify the indefinite denial of parenting time. MCL 600.2950 governs PPOs; MCL 722.27a governs parenting time. These are separate statutory schemes with separate standards. Even if the PPO were valid (which Plaintiff contests), it does not relieve the Court of its obligation to independently assess parenting time under the best interest standard. Moreover, multiple investigations have found no factual basis for the allegations underlying the PPO.

### B. "Plaintiff Was Found in Contempt"

**Response:** The contempt findings were based on alleged violations of orders that were themselves entered without due process. Contempt of Dougherty, 429 Mich 81 (1987), requires that the underlying order be valid before contempt may be imposed. A contempt finding based on a constitutionally deficient order cannot serve as an independent basis for denying parenting time.

### C. "The Court Previously Considered and Denied Parenting Time"

**Response:** No court has conducted a full evidentiary hearing with testimony and cross-examination on the question of parenting time. The "consideration" of parenting time to date has consisted entirely of ex parte proceedings in which Plaintiff had no opportunity to participate. This does not satisfy due process.

### D. "Supervised Visitation Is Not Necessary Because There Is No Safety Concern"

**Response:** Plaintiff agrees that there is no safety concern -- that is precisely why parenting time should be restored. Plaintiff nevertheless proposes supervised visitation as an initial transitional step, not because supervision is necessary for safety, but because it provides an additional safeguard during the reunification process and may ease the Court's concerns.

### E. "It Has Been Too Long -- Resuming Contact Would Be Disruptive"

**Response:** This argument rewards the party who wrongfully denied parenting time. The passage of time is not a defense to the violation of constitutional rights -- it is evidence of the magnitude of the harm. Developmental psychology research uniformly supports gradual reunification after prolonged separation, which is exactly what Plaintiff proposes. The longer the Court delays, the more difficult reunification becomes. This is a reason to act now, not to delay further.

### F. "Plaintiff Has Not Shown Changed Circumstances"

**Response:** Under Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 499 (2003), the standard for modification is "proper cause" or "change of circumstances." Here, the circumstances have changed dramatically: multiple investigations have returned zero findings since the original suspension. The exculpatory evidence that has accumulated -- negative drug screen, ALL ZEROS evaluation, zero CPS findings -- constitutes a massive change in the evidentiary landscape. Moreover, the passage of 571+ days without a hearing is itself a changed circumstance demanding judicial action.
