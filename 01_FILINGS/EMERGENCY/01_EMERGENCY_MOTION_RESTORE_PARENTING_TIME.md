## IV. LEGAL ARGUMENT

### A. The Statutory Presumption Favors Restoration of Parenting Time

15. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes that "parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child (MCL 722.23)." The statute creates a **presumption** in favor of parenting time.

16. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that "[a] parenting time order shall not be considered a change in established custodial environment."

17. No party has produced **clear and convincing evidence** that parenting time with Plaintiff would endanger the child. The complete absence of adverse findings after multiple investigations conclusively demonstrates that parenting time is safe.

### B. Due Process Requires an Evidentiary Hearing Before Suspension

18. Under the Fourteenth Amendment, a parent's right to the care, custody, and companionship of their child is a **fundamental liberty interest**. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982). *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972).

19. Before this fundamental right may be restricted, due process requires, at minimum:
    - **Notice** of the allegations
    - An **opportunity to be heard** at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner
    - The right to **present evidence** and **cross-examine witnesses**
    - *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976)

20. None of these protections were afforded to Plaintiff before his parenting time was suspended. The suspension was entered ex parte, without notice, without hearing, and without any opportunity for Plaintiff to respond.

21. Michigan courts have consistently held that ex parte custody order (MCR 3.207(B))s are **disfavored** and may only be entered upon a showing of **immediate and irreparable harm**. *See* MCR 3.207(B); *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247 (2009).

### C. The Extended Duration of Suspension Is Constitutionally Impermissible

22. Even if the initial ex parte suspension were justified (it was not), the continuation of a **total suspension for 571+ days** without an evidentiary hearing violates both procedural and substantive due process.

23. The Sixth Circuit has recognized that state interference with parental rights triggers strict scrutiny. *Bartell v. Lohiser*, 215 F.3d 550 (6th Cir. 2000); *Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279 (6th Cir. 1998).

24. No compelling state interest justifies 571+ days of complete parental severance where zero investigations have found any risk to the child.
