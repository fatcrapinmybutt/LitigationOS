## MOTION TO PRESERVE EVIDENCE

---

**NOW COMES** Plaintiff/Father, Andrew J. Pigors, appearing in propria persona, and pursuant to MCR 2.119 and MCR 2.302, hereby moves this Honorable Court for entry of an Order requiring all parties to preserve evidence relevant to this matter. In support thereof, Plaintiff states as follows:

### I. INTRODUCTION

1. This matter involves disputes concerning custody, parenting time, and related family law issues in which documentary and electronic evidence is of critical importance to the fair adjudication of the parties' respective claims and defenses.

2. Plaintiff has a reasonable, good-faith basis to believe that relevant evidence may be at risk of alteration, destruction, or loss absent court intervention.

3. This Motion is filed to ensure the integrity of the evidentiary record and to protect the due process rights of all parties.

### II. LEGAL AUTHORITY

#### A. Michigan Court Rules

4. MCR 2.302(A) provides that parties may obtain discovery regarding any matter not privileged that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action. The scope of discoverable material necessarily presupposes the preservation of such material.

5. MCR 2.313(B) authorizes the Court to impose sanctions, including adverse inference instructions, for failure to comply with discovery obligations, which includes the duty to preserve evidence once litigation is reasonably anticipated.

#### B. Common Law Duty to Preserve

6. Michigan courts recognize a common law duty to preserve evidence once a party knows or should know that the evidence is relevant to pending or reasonably anticipated litigation. *Brenner v. Kolk*, 226 Mich App 149, 160 (1997).

7. The duty to preserve attaches at the moment litigation is reasonably anticipated and extends to all evidence that a party knows or reasonably should know is relevant to the action. This duty applies to electronically stored information ("ESI") with equal force.

#### C. Spoliation Doctrine

8. Spoliation of evidence is the intentional or negligent destruction, alteration, or concealment of evidence. Where spoliation is established, Michigan courts may impose remedies including adverse inference instructions, exclusion of evidence, default judgment, or dismissal. *Bloemendaal v. Town & Country Sports Ctr., Inc.*, 255 Mich App 207, 213 (2002).

### IV. RISK OF SPOLIATION

18. Plaintiff has identified specific risks that relevant evidence may be destroyed, altered, or rendered unavailable absent a preservation order:

    a. Electronic communications, particularly text messages and social media content, are inherently transient and may be automatically deleted, overwritten, or lost through device changes, application updates, or intentional deletion.

    b. Third-party service providers may have limited data retention periods, after which records may be permanently destroyed in the ordinary course of business.

    c. The adversarial nature of this proceeding creates an inherent incentive for parties to eliminate unfavorable evidence.

    d. Absent a court order, there is no enforceable mechanism to compel ongoing preservation by the opposing party or third-party custodians.

19. The risk of evidence loss is not speculative. The categories of evidence identified above are directly relevant to the central issues in this case, including the best interests of the child(ren) as defined by MCL 722.23, and the loss of any such evidence would be prejudicial to the fair resolution of this matter.

---

### V. RELIEF REQUESTED

**WHEREFORE,** Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Honorable Court:

A. Enter an Order requiring all parties to immediately preserve all evidence identified in Section III above;

B. Enter an Order prohibiting all parties from destroying, altering, concealing, or otherwise disposing of any evidence relevant to this matter;

C. Enter an Order requiring all parties to take affirmative steps to ensure the preservation of electronically stored information, including but not limited to implementing litigation holds, suspending automatic deletion protocols, and preserving backup copies;

D. Enter an Order authorizing appropriate sanctions, including adverse inference instructions, in the event of non-compliance;

E. Grant such other and further relief as this Court deems just and equitable.
