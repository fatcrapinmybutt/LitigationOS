## III. LEGAL AUTHORITY AND ARGUMENT

### A. The August 8, 2025 Ex Parte Order Is Void Due to Fraud Upon the Court — MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c)

13. MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c) provides that a court may relieve a party from a final judgment or order based on "[f]raud (whether heretofore denominated intrinsic or extrinsic), misrepresentation, or other misconduct of an adverse party."

14. The Michigan Court of Appeals has held that there is **no time limit** for bringing a motion to set aside a judgment procured by fraud upon the court. *Souden v Souden*, 303 Mich App 406; 844 NW2d 151 (2013); MCR 2.612(C)(2) (one-year limitation inapplicable to fraud upon the court).

15. Defendant's August 8, 2025 ex parte motion contained a **material misattribution**: the motion attributed a statement regarding alleged substance use to a police officer, when in fact, upon information and belief, the officer's report does not support this characterization. This misattribution was central to the Court's decision to suspend all parenting time without a hearing.

16. Under MRPC 3.3(d), a lawyer (or self-represented party) presenting a matter ex parte to a tribunal has a duty to "inform the tribunal of all material facts known to the lawyer that will enable the tribunal to make an informed decision, whether or not the facts are adverse." Defendant's failure to disclose the actual content of the officer's report, and affirmative mischaracterization thereof, constitutes fraud upon the court.

17. The misattributed statement constitutes an admission by a party-opponent under MRE 801(d)(2)(A) — Defendant's own characterization, offered against Defendant, is admissible as non-hearsay and reveals the deliberate nature of the misrepresentation.

### B. The Ex Parte Order Violates MCR 3.207(B) in Multiple Respects

18. MCR 3.207(B) governs emergency ex parte orders in domestic relations cases. The August 8, 2025 order fails to comply with this rule in at least **seven independently dispositive ways**:

   **(i) No Clear and Convincing Evidence of Endangerment.** MCL 722.27a(3) requires that to restrict parenting time, there must be "clear and convincing evidence that parenting time . . . would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." No such evidence exists on the record. Zero law enforcement investigations have produced adverse findings against Father. A favorable HealthWest mental health evaluation dated approximately August 5, 2025, was disregarded by the Court.

   **(ii) No Specific Facts of Irreparable Harm.** MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that an ex parte motion "state specific facts showing that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice." Defendant's motion did not meet this threshold.

   **(iii) No Established Custodial Environment Findings.** MCR 3.207(B)(1)(a)(i)-(ii) (effective September 1, 2025) requires specific findings regarding the established custodial environment before an ex parte order may alter custody or parenting time.

   **(iv) No Evidentiary Hearing Within 21 Days.** MCR 3.207(B)(1)(b) requires that an evidentiary hearing be scheduled within 21 days of an ex parte order. The evidentiary hearing was not held until **September 4, 2025 — 27 days** after the ex parte order, exceeding the mandatory deadline by 6 days. [V — calculated from docket dates 08/08 to 09/04]

   **(v) No Three-Day Service.** MCR 3.207(B)(2) requires that the ex parte order and motion be served on the opposing party within 3 days. On information and belief, service was not timely effectuated.

   **(vi) No Mandatory Notice Language.** MCR 3.207(B)(6) requires that an ex parte order contain specific notice language informing the affected party of their right to file an objection. On information and belief, the order lacks this language.

   **(vii) No Findings of Fact.** MCR 2.517(A) requires that in all actions "the court shall find the facts specially and state separately its conclusions of law." The ex parte order was entered without any written findings of fact supporting the extraordinary relief granted.

### C. Binding Precedent Requires Vacatur — Barretta v Zhitkov

19. In *Barretta v Zhitkov*, 348 Mich App 539; 19 NW3d 420 (2023), the Michigan Court of Appeals held that the failure to conduct an evidentiary hearing before modifying custody or parenting time based on an ex parte motion constitutes **clear legal error**. The court in *Barretta* vacated the trial court's orders suspending the mother's parenting time and remanded for proceedings consistent with due process requirements, emphasizing that even "temporary" suspensions that effectively modify custody require evidentiary findings.

20. Here, the ex parte order of August 8, 2025, was entered and enforced for **27 days** before any evidentiary hearing was held — exceeding even the 21-day outside limit imposed by MCR 3.207(B)(1)(b). The *Barretta* holding squarely mandates vacatur.

### D. Constitutional Due Process Requires Immediate Relief

21. The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a parent's interest in the care, custody, and companionship of their child is a **fundamental liberty interest** protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

22. The Michigan Supreme Court has likewise recognized this right. *In re JK*, 468 Mich 202, 210 (2003) (holding that a parent's right to direct the care and custody of their child is a fundamental right under both the state and federal constitutions).

23. Under the three-factor balancing test of *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976):

   **(a)** The private interest at stake — the parent-child bond — is among the most fundamental liberty interests recognized in our constitutional order.

   **(b)** The risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used — an ex parte order based on a misattributed statement, without findings of fact, without timely hearing, and without proper service — is **extreme**.

   **(c)** The government's interest — protecting child welfare — is served by an evidentiary hearing at which both parties present evidence, not by indefinite ex parte suspension.

24. All three *Mathews* factors weigh heavily in favor of Father. The 236-day deprivation without adequate process is constitutionally intolerable.

### E. The Statutory Presumption Favors Parenting Time — MCL 722.27a

25. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes that "parenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." This creates a **statutory presumption** in favor of parenting time.

26. The burden falls on the party seeking restriction to demonstrate, by **clear and convincing evidence**, that parenting time would endanger the child. MCL 722.27a(3). No such showing has been made. Multiple law enforcement contacts have produced **zero adverse findings** against Father. Father's HealthWest mental health evaluation was favorable.

27. Under MCL 722.23(j) — the "willingness to facilitate" best-interest factor — Defendant's conduct in weaponizing the ex parte process to achieve total parental exclusion weighs **heavily** against Defendant's position.
