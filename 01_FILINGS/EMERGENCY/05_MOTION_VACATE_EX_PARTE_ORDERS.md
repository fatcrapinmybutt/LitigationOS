## I. AUTHORITY

1. **MCR 2.612(C)(1)** — Relief from judgment or order on grounds including:
   - **(a)** Mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect
   - **(b)** Newly discovered evidence
   - **(c)** Fraud, misrepresentation, or other misconduct
   - **(d)** The judgment is void
   - **(f)** Any other reason justifying relief

2. **MCR 3.207(B)** — Ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s in domestic relations cases require a showing of "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage" and must be narrowly tailored.

3. **Fourteenth Amendment, U.S. Constitution** — No state shall deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.

4. **Michigan Const. Art. 1, §17** — No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.

## II. ORDERS SOUGHT TO BE VACATED

5. Plaintiff seeks to vacate **all ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s** entered in this matter that resulted in:
   - Suspension of parenting time

   - Any sanctions flowing from the void ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s

## III. GROUNDS FOR VACATUR

### A. The Orders Are Void Ab Initio — MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d)

7. An order is **void** when the court lacked jurisdiction, when the order was entered in violation of due process, or when the process by which it was obtained was fundamentally flawed. *Resurgent Capital Servs LP v. Eaton*, 322 Mich App 578 (2018).

8. Each of the 52 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s is void because:

   a. **No meaningful notice** was provided to Plaintiff before entry, violating *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 U.S. 306 (1950).

   b. **No opportunity to be heard** was afforded to Plaintiff at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner, violating *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).

   c. **No showing of immediate irreparable harm** was made as required by MCR 3.207(B). The allegations underlying the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s were contradicted by multiple investigations yielding zero findings.

   d. **No adequate post-deprivation hearing** was provided within a reasonable time to review the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s, violating *Fuentes v. Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67 (1972).

### B. The Orders Were Obtained Through Fraud/Misrepresentation — MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c)

9. The ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s were obtained through material misrepresentations to the court:
   - Allegations of danger unsupported by any investigation findings
   - Failure to disclose exculpatory evidence (negative drug screens, cleared investigations)
   - Under MCR 3.310(C)(2), the petition for a PPO must be "supported by a sworn statement setting forth specific facts to satisfy the court." On information and belief, the sworn statements contained material falsehoods.

10. Under *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971), due process requires that deprivation of fundamental rights not be based on unverified, uncontested allegations.

### C. The Orders Violate Fundamental Liberty Interests

11. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982). Parental rights are **fundamental liberty interests** protected by the Due Process Clause. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982). *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972).

12. Deprivation of fundamental rights requires, at minimum:
   - **Clear and convincing evidence** standard — *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982)
   - **Strict scrutiny** by the court — *Bartell v. Lohiser*, 215 F.3d 550 (6th Cir. 2000)
   - **Narrowly tailored** restrictions — the least restrictive means to address any genuine safety concerns

13. None of these constitutional standards were met. The ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s imposed **total deprivation** (zero contact) based on **zero evidence** of danger, representing the most extreme possible interference with the least possible justification.

### D. The Ex Parte Process Was Structurally Defective

14. The 14-day hearing requirement under MCR 3.310(C)(3) was not satisfied — hearings were not timely held or were insufficient to constitute meaningful review.

15. The cumulative effect of 52 ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s creates a **structural due process violation**: the repeated use of ex parte process to achieve what should require full adversarial proceedings constitutes an end-run around due process. *See Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319.

16. Under *Hunter v. Hunter*, 484 Mich 247, ex parte custody order (MCR 3.207(B))s are **disfavored** and require extraordinary justification that was never provided here.

### E. Fruit of the Poisonous Tree

17. All orders flowing from the void ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s are themselves void, including:
   - Contempt findings based on alleged violations of void orders
   - Incarceration orders (59+ days) predicated on void underlying orders
   - Sanctions and fines imposed based on conduct governed by void orders
   - Child support modifications premised on custody arrangements flowing from void orders

18. Under the "fruit of the poisonous tree" doctrine, remedies built upon a void foundation cannot stand. *See Wong Sun v. United States*, 371 U.S. 471 (1963) (though criminal, the principle applies to civil proceedings where fundamental rights are at stake).

## IV. MICHIGAN CASELAW SUPPORTING VACATUR

19. Michigan courts have vacated ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s under comparable circumstances:

   - *Hayford v. Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324 — PPO reversed for procedural defects
   - *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 — Due process requires access and opportunity to be heard
   - *Fuentes v. Shevin*, 407 U.S. 67 — Pre-deprivation hearing required for significant property/liberty interests
   - *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 — Procedure for parental rights deprivation must satisfy due process
