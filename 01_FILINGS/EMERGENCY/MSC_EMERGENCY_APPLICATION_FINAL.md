## II. FACTS REQUIRING IMMEDIATE RELIEF

### A. The August 8, 2025 Mass Ex Parte Orders

6. On August 8, 2025, Judge Jenny L. McNeill of the 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County, entered **five (5) separate ex parte orders on a single day** that collectively suspended ALL of Father's parenting time with LDW. (Ex. A — Aug. 8, 2025 Orders.)

7. Father was not served with the emergency ex parte motion before the orders were entered. Father arrived at the scheduled parenting-time exchange on August 8, 2025, expecting his regular time with his son. Only after the missed exchange — by calling the court — did Father discover that his parenting time had been suspended. (Ex. B — Father's Declaration.)

8. The ex parte motion was filed by Defendant Emily A. Watson ("Mother") and was premised entirely on unsworn allegations. The motion did not contain:

   a. Specific facts establishing immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage as required by MCR 3.207(B)(1);

   b. A verified pleading or affidavit as required by MCR 3.207(B)(5);

   c. Any analysis of the twelve best-interest factors under MCL 722.23(a)–(l); or

   d. Any admissible evidence meeting MRE 602 (personal knowledge), MRE 801 (hearsay), or MRE 901 (authentication).

9. The allegations that underpin the suspension have been systematically analyzed and are contradicted by the record. The Court itself previously found that "[b]oth parents have a strong bond with the minor child" and that this factor was "equal between the parties." No intervening event — supported by admissible evidence — justifies the complete reversal of this finding.

### B. The Pattern of Ex Parte Abuse

10. The August 8, 2025 orders are not an isolated incident. **Twenty-four (24) of fifty-five (55) total orders in this case — 43.6% — were entered ex parte.** All 24 were entered by Judge McNeill. All 24 were entered without notice to Father. All 24 favored Mother. (Ex. C — Ex Parte Order Analysis.)

11. The database of record in this case documents **1,127 judicial violations** by Judge McNeill, including:

### C. The $250 Filing Bond — Blocking All Access to Justice

12. On or about May 16, 2025, Judge McNeill ordered: "Plaintiff must pay a bond of $250 (for each motion) before he may file another motion." (Ex. D — Bond Order.)

13. This order effectively bars Father — a pro se litigant — from all access to the lower court. Father cannot petition for restoration of parenting time, cannot move for reconsideration, and cannot seek any relief from the very orders that have separated him from his child for 567+ days.

14. The United States Supreme Court has held that a State may not condition access to its courts on the ability to pay fees where fundamental rights are at stake. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371, 380–81 (1971). The parental liberty interest is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). A $250-per-motion bond that prevents a parent from seeking restoration of his relationship with his child is unconstitutional under *Boddie*.

### D. No Best-Interest-Factor Hearing — Ever

15. In 567+ days of complete separation, no court has conducted a hearing on the twelve best-interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23(a)–(l). No findings of fact have been entered. No evidence has been weighed.

16. MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires that before a court modifies custody or parenting time, it must determine whether an established custodial environment exists and, if so, whether clear and convincing evidence supports the modification. This has never been done.

17. All twelve best-interest factors have been analyzed against the evidentiary record, with **519 evidence links** mapped across the factors. The record overwhelmingly supports Father's fitness and the child's need for a relationship with both parents. Factor (a) — love, affection, and emotional ties — was found "equal between the parties" by the Court's own order. Factor (j) — willingness to facilitate a close relationship — heavily favors Father, who has consistently sought parenting time while Mother has systematically withheld it.

### E. Silencing of Father at Hearings

18. Father has been systematically silenced at court hearings. In Father's own words, documented in the record: "I was muted, cut off, or told the judge would not look at further filings, effectively closing the courtroom door to a self-represented parent." (Ex. E — Father's Declaration.)

19. At multiple hearings, Judge McNeill muted Father's microphone, cut him off mid-objection, and instructed him not to file further materials because she would not review them. This conduct violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and Canon 3(A)(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, which requires a judge to "accord to every person who has a legal interest in a proceeding . . . the right to be heard according to law."

### F. Self-Ruling on Disqualification

20. Father filed a motion to disqualify Judge McNeill pursuant to MCR 2.003(C). Rather than referring the motion to the Chief Judge as required by MCR 2.003(D), Judge McNeill ruled on her own disqualification motion and denied it. This is a direct violation of MCR 2.003(D), which mandates: "The challenged judge shall refer the motion to the chief judge."

### G. The Child's Suffering

21. Father has stated in the record: "My three-year-old son has been deprived of regular, loving contact with his father for months at a time. A genuine father-child bond, previously recognized by the court as a [strong bond]" is being systematically destroyed. (Ex. F — Father's Hearing Statement.)

22. The child is losing irreplaceable developmental time. Attachment theory and child developmental science establish that the period between ages 2 and 5 is critical for the formation of secure parent-child bonds. The complete severing of this bond during this window causes measurable, lasting psychological harm that cannot be remediated after the fact.
