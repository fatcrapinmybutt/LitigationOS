## IV. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. MCR 3.606 — Contempt in Domestic Relations Cases

42. **MCR 3.606** governs contempt proceedings in domestic relations cases. The rule provides:

> **(A)** For purposes of this rule, "contempt" consists of any act or omission that is a failure to obey a court order entered in a domestic relations case.

> **(B)** A person may be found guilty of contempt if:
> (1) there is a court order;
> (2) the person knew of the order;
> (3) the person violated the order; and
> (4) the violation was willful.

MCR 3.606(A)-(B) [ANDREW_REQUIRED: VERIFY - — exact text of current rule].

43. Under MCR 3.606, the moving party bears the initial burden of establishing by a **preponderance of the evidence** that a valid court order existed, the respondent had knowledge of the order, and the respondent failed to comply with the order. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 93; 413 NW2d 392 (1987) [ANDREW_REQUIRED: VERIFY - — correct citation and holding].

44. Once the moving party establishes these elements, the burden shifts to the respondent to demonstrate that noncompliance was not willful — i.e., that the respondent was unable to comply despite making reasonable, good-faith efforts to do so. *See In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656, 668; 765 NW2d 44 (2009) Will v. Michigan, 491 U.S. 58.

### B. MCL 600.5701 — Contempt Power

45. **MCL 600.5701** sets forth the statutory authority of Michigan courts to punish contempt. The statute provides, in relevant part:

> The supreme court, circuit court, and all other courts of record, have power to punish by fine or imprisonment, or both, persons guilty of any neglect of, or resistance to, any process, rule, or order of the court, or any misbehavior in the presence of the court, or so near thereto as to directly tend to interrupt its proceedings, or any other contempt of the authority of the court.

MCL 600.5701-(g) [ANDREW_REQUIRED: VERIFY - — exact text of relevant subsections].

46. The purpose of civil contempt is not punitive but **coercive** — it is designed to compel compliance with a court order and to vindicate the authority of the court. *See In re Contempt of Robertson*, 209 Mich App 433, 436; 531 NW2d 763 (1995) [ANDREW_REQUIRED: VERIFY - Context unclear, manual review needed].

47. In a civil contempt proceeding, the contemnor holds the "keys to the jail" — she can purge herself of contempt by complying with the court's order. *See In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 93 [ANDREW_REQUIRED: VERIFY - Context unclear, manual review needed].

### C. MCL 722.27a(7) — Parenting Time Enforcement

48. **MCL 722.27a(7)** specifically addresses the enforcement of parenting time orders and provides a range of remedies:

> If a child's parent, without good cause, fails to comply with a parenting time order, the court shall order the parent to make up the parenting time. In addition, the court may:
>
> (a) Modify the parenting time order;
>
> (b) Order the parent to pay reasonable costs and attorney fees incurred by the prevailing party;
>
> (c) Find the parent in contempt under section 1701 of the revised judicature act;
>
> (d) Order the parent to perform community service;
>
> (e) Take any other action that the court considers necessary or appropriate;
>
> (f) Modify the custody order.

MCL 722.27a(7)(a)-(f) [ANDREW_REQUIRED: VERIFY - — exact text of current statute].

49. Notably, MCL 722.27a(7) creates a **presumption** that parenting time violations shall result in makeup parenting time at a minimum.
