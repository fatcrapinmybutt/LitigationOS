## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. The Trial Court Violated Due Process (14th Amendment; Mich. Const. Art. I, § 17)

The fundamental requirement of due process is the opportunity to be heard "at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). Parental rights are "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982)." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

The trial court deprived Plaintiff-Appellant of his fundamental parental rights by:
- Entering an ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) without prior notice
- Relying on evidence never served on Plaintiff-Appellant
- Reviewing evidence outside any hearing
- Denying Plaintiff-Appellant the opportunity to confront and respond to evidence

These actions constitute a clear violation of procedural due process. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982).

### B. MCR 3.206(C) Was Violated

MCR 3.206(C) governs ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s in domestic relations cases and requires:
- A showing of irreparable injury, loss, or damage
- Notice to the adverse party (or a showing that notice is impossible)
- Specific findings of fact

The trial court failed to comply with these requirements. No evidence of irreparable injury was presented beyond unsubstantiated allegations. No notice was provided to Plaintiff-Appellant.

### C. Irreparable Harm Requires Emergency Relief

Plaintiff-Appellant's parenting time has been suspended since August 8, 2025 — a period of [ANDREW_REQUIRED: calculate months]. This ongoing separation constitutes **irreparable harm** to both Plaintiff-Appellant and his minor child:

- The parent-child bond is being destroyed by enforced separation
- The child is being deprived of a relationship with his/his father
- Plaintiff-Appellant has been labeled "unfit" without evidentiary basis
- No adequate remedy at law exists for the loss of time with a child

### D. Mandamus Is Warranted

A writ of mandamus is appropriate where:
1. The party seeking the writ has a clear legal right to the performance of the duty sought (parental rights; due process)
2. The respondent has a clear legal duty to perform the act (follow MCR 3.206(C); provide notice)
3. The act is ministerial (compliance with court rules is mandatory, not discretionary)
4. No other adequate legal remedy exists (the ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B)) cannot be adequately remedied by post-hoc proceedings that have already been denied)

All four elements are satisfied here.
