## STANDARD OF REVIEW

This Court reviews petitions for superintending control de novo. *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975). Under MCR 7.305(B), superintending control is proper when:

1. The petitioner has no adequate legal remedy; **and**
2. The lower court's exercise of power was unauthorized by law.

Additionally, this Court may exercise superintending control when the issues are of significant public interest or when the lower court's conduct constitutes an "abuse of judicial power" tantamount to a "failure to perform a clear legal duty." *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 441-42.

The standard is not whether the lower court committed reversible error on a single issue, but whether the *cumulative pattern* of the lower court's conduct warrants this Court's supervisory intervention.

## ARGUMENT

### I. THIS COURT'S CONSTITUTIONAL SUPERINTENDING AUTHORITY IS PLENARY AND REQUIRES EXERCISE HERE

The Michigan Constitution vests this Court with "general superintending control over all courts." Const 1963, art 6, § 4. This authority is not a discretionary grant that can be exercised or withheld at will. It is a constitutional *obligation* — an essential structural safeguard in Michigan's judicial system.

This Court has recognized that its superintending control power "extends to the entire range of supervisory authority necessary to ensure the proper and efficient administration of justice." *Judges of the 74th Judicial District v. Bay County*, 385 Mich 710, 727 (1971). The power is "plenary" — it is not limited to the specific writs of mandamus, prohibition, or habeas corpus, but encompasses the full scope of supervisory authority over lower courts. *Id.* at 728.

In *In re Hatcher*, this Court confirmed that superintending control "is the proper remedy when a lower court has committed an abuse of judicial power that is tantamount to a failure to perform a clear legal duty, and there is no other adequate remedy." 443 Mich at 441-42.

More recently, this Court reaffirmed the breadth of its superintending authority in *Plumbers & Pipefitters Local 190 v. Dep't of Licensing & Regulatory Affairs*, 508 Mich 401, 418 (2021), holding that superintending control reaches any exercise of governmental power that exceeds lawful authority, and that this Court's supervisory jurisdiction is not constrained to traditional mandamus or prohibition categories. In *Dep't of Transp v. Tomkins*, 481 Mich 1061 (2008), this Court exercised superintending control to vacate lower court orders entered without lawful authority, confirming that orders issued in violation of procedural prerequisites are subject to summary correction through this Court's plenary power.

The instant case presents precisely such a situation. The lower court has not merely committed isolated errors — it has *systematically failed* to perform its core judicial functions:

- It has failed to enforce mandatory statutes (MCL 552.644);
- It has failed to ensure due process (ex parte proceedings at 44% (24 of 55 orders));
- It has failed to exercise contempt power within constitutional limits;
- It has erected financial barriers to the disqualification remedy;
- It has allowed a child to be separated from a fit parent for 200+ days.

This is not a case for appellate error correction. This is a case for structural judicial oversight — the very purpose of the superintending control power.

No lesser remedy — mandamus compelling a specific act, habeas adjudicating custody, or appellate review of a single order — can address the *totality* of the lower court's dysfunction. Only superintending control provides this Court with the authority to:

1. Remove the presiding judge;
2. Vacate multiple unconstitutional orders;

4. Order emergency parenting time;
5. Establish a case management schedule;
6. Impose ongoing compliance monitoring.

This is precisely the "entire range of supervisory authority" that the Constitution envisions. *Bay County*, 385 Mich at 727.

### II. THE LOWER COURT'S SYSTEMATIC DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS REQUIRE MSC INTERVENTION

Due process is not a suggestion. It is a constitutional command — binding on every court at every level. U.S. Const, amend XIV, § 1; Const 1963, art 1, § 17.

The *Mathews v. Eldridge* framework requires consideration of three factors: (1) the private interest at stake; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation under existing procedures and the probable value of additional safeguards; and (3) the government's interest. 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976).

Applying *Mathews* here:

**(1) The private interest:** The parent-child relationship is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). No interest protected by due process is more fundamental. The child's reciprocal interest in maintaining a relationship with both parents is equally profound. *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972).

**(2) Risk of erroneous deprivation:** The risk is not theoretical — erroneous deprivation has *already occurred*. Petitioner has been deprived of all contact with his child for 200+ days without any finding of unfitness, dangerousness, or abuse. The lower court's existing procedures — including ex parte proceedings at 44% (24 of 55 orders), contempt without counsel, and financial barriers to disqualification — have proven wholly inadequate to protect Petitioner's rights.

**(3) The government's interest:** The government has no legitimate interest in the continuation of proceedings that violate due process. To the contrary, the state has a compelling interest in ensuring that its courts function lawfully and impartially. *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 42 (1992).

The due process violations in this case are not isolated incidents — they form a pattern:

**a. Ex parte proceedings.** Notice and an opportunity to be heard are the "essential elements" of due process. *Goldberg v. Kelly*, 397 U.S. 254, 267-68 (1970). Judge McNeill has conducted approximately 44% (24 of 55 orders) of proceedings without notice to or participation by Petitioner. This is a systemic violation that cannot be cured by appellate reversal of individual orders — it requires structural intervention.

**b. Contempt without counsel.** When civil contempt results in actual incarceration, due process requires, at minimum, "substitute procedural safeguards" including notice of the charges, an opportunity to respond, and consideration of the contemnor's ability to comply. *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431, 448 (2011). Petitioner was jailed for 59+ days without adequate procedural protections.

**c. Orders without notice.** Substantive orders affecting custody, parenting time, and financial obligations have been entered without providing Petitioner notice or an opportunity to be heard, violating MCR 2.119 and fundamental due process.

**d. Void orders — 24 ex parte orders lacking MCR 2.119(B) notice.** An order entered without the procedural prerequisites mandated by due process and the court rules is void, not merely voidable. *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 49 (1992). MCR 2.119(B) requires that written motions be served at least nine days before hearing; ex parte orders under MCR 3.207 are narrowly limited to genuine emergencies involving immediate, irreparable injury. The lower court issued twenty-four (24) ex parte orders — 44% (24 of 55) of all proceedings — substantively modifying custody, parenting time, and child support without the notice required by MCR 2.119(B). These were not narrow emergency measures; they were substantive adjudications of fundamental parental rights conducted without adversarial presentation. Because these orders were entered without constitutionally required notice, they are void ab initio and should be vacated in their entirety. The sheer volume — twenty-four orders — demonstrates that this was not occasional emergency action but a systematic practice of deciding contested matters unilaterally, in direct contravention of both constitutional due process and Michigan's court rules.

The cumulative effect of these violations is a proceeding that bears no resemblance to the "fair hearing" that due process demands. *Toan v. McGinn*, 271 Mich App 291, 296 (2006). Individual appellate review of individual orders cannot cure the systemic nature of the problem. Superintending control is necessary.

### III. MANDATORY STATUTORY DUTIES CANNOT BE REFUSED — MCL 552.644 COMPELS ENFORCEMENT

MCL 552.644 is not ambiguous. It provides:

> "If the court determines that a custodial parent or agency has failed to comply with a parenting time order, the court **shall** do 1 or more of the following . . ."

The word "shall" in a statute creates a mandatory duty. *Harvey v. Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 189 (2004) ("The word 'shall' indicates a mandatory and imperative duty."). When a statute imposes a mandatory duty on a court, the court's refusal to perform that duty is an unauthorized exercise of power that this Court may correct through superintending control. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 441.

The factual predicate for MCL 552.644's mandatory duty is undisputed:

| Element | Record |
|---------|--------|
| **Custodial parent** | Emily A. Watson |
| **Parenting time order** | Entered by 14th Circuit Court |
| **Failure to comply** | 305 documented violations |
| **Court determination** | Judge McNeill has been presented with evidence of violations |
| **Court action under § 644** | **None** — zero enforcement actions taken |

MCL 552.644 provides a menu of mandatory remedies when a violation is found, including: compensatory parenting time, modification of the parenting time order, civil fine, contempt, and attorney fees. The court "shall" do "1 or more" of these. The statute does not say "may." It does not say "at the court's discretion." It says "shall."

Judge McNeill's refusal to enforce parenting time despite 305 documented violations is a refusal to perform a clear, mandatory, statutory duty. This refusal is an "unauthorized" exercise of judicial power — or more precisely, an unauthorized *non-exercise* — that constitutes precisely the kind of "abuse of judicial power" that superintending control was designed to correct.

The consequence of this refusal is not abstract. A child has been separated from his father for 200+ consecutive days. Each day of non-enforcement is a day of developmental harm, a day of alienation, a day of childhood irretrievably lost. The lower court's statutory duty is clear, its violation is documented, and the harm is ongoing and irreparable.

This Court should exercise its superintending authority to compel compliance with MCL 552.644 and to ensure that the mandatory duty to enforce parenting time is fulfilled.

### IV. THE CONTEMPT ABUSE PATTERN EXCEEDS THE BOUNDS OF LAWFUL JUDICIAL POWER

This Court has drawn a clear constitutional line between civil and criminal contempt. Civil contempt is coercive — it compels compliance. Criminal contempt is punitive — it punishes past conduct. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 96-97 (1987). The distinction is not merely academic — it determines the procedural protections to which the contemnor is entitled.

When civil contempt sanctions lose their coercive character, they become criminal punishment, and the contemnor is entitled to full criminal procedural protections — including the right to counsel, the presumption of innocence, and proof beyond a reasonable doubt. *Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 97.

The lower court's use of contempt power against Petitioner exhibits all the hallmarks of punishment, not coercion:

**A. Duration.** Petitioner was jailed for 59+ days. Prolonged incarceration on civil contempt raises a strong inference that the sanction has lost its coercive purpose and become punitive. The longer the incarceration, the weaker the coercive justification.

**B. Purge conditions.** Civil contempt must include a purge condition — something the contemnor can do to secure release. The purge condition must be within the contemnor's present ability to perform. When purge conditions are set beyond the contemnor's ability, the sanction is effectively a fixed sentence — which is punishment, not coercion. Petitioner's purge conditions included financial requirements that he, as an indigent litigant who had lost his job, could not satisfy.

**C. Lack of procedural protections.** *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431 (2011), held that even in civil contempt proceedings that result in incarceration, due process requires at minimum "substitute procedural safeguards" — including notice, opportunity to respond, and a judicial finding that the contemnor has the ability to comply. *Id.* at 448. Petitioner was not afforded these protections.

**D. Asymmetry.** The most telling evidence of abuse is the stark asymmetry: Petitioner was jailed for 59+ days, while Watson's 305 documented violations of parenting time resulted in zero enforcement actions. This asymmetry is inexplicable as even-handed justice. It is consistent only with punitive targeting of one party.

**E. Collateral consequences.** Petitioner lost 3 jobs (including a USPS position), 2 homes, and suffered $82,250+ in economic damages as a direct result of the contempt incarceration. These are the consequences of punishment, not of lawful coercion.

The lower court's contempt practice in this case constitutes an abuse of judicial power that exceeds the bounds authorized by law. This Court should exercise superintending control to vacate the contempt orders and prohibit future abuse.

### V. FINANCIAL ACCESS BARRIERS VIOLATE FUNDAMENTAL DUE PROCESS

The United States Supreme Court has held that "a State may not, consistent with the obligations imposed on it by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, pre-empt the right to dissolve [a fundamental relationship] without affording all citizens access to the means it has prescribed for doing so." *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371, 383 (1971).

*Boddie* involved a $60 filing fee that prevented indigent parties from accessing the courts to dissolve a marriage. The Court held that when the state has a monopoly on the means of resolving a fundamental dispute — as it does in custody proceedings — it cannot impose financial barriers that prevent access to those means.

The principle of *Boddie* has been extended beyond divorce filings. In *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102 (1996), the Court held that a state could not condition a parent's right to appeal a termination of parental rights on payment of transcript costs. The Court reasoned that "choices about marriage, family life, and the upbringing of children are among associational rights this Court has ranked as 'of basic importance in our society'" and that financial barriers to exercising those rights violate due process and equal protection. 519 U.S. at 116.

Judge McNeill imposed a $250 bond requirement on Petitioner's motion for judicial disqualification under MCR 2.003. Petitioner is indigent — he has filed for In Forma Pauperis status, having lost three jobs and two homes as a direct result of the lower court's own actions.

The $250 bond operates as a complete bar to Petitioner's disqualification motion. Petitioner cannot afford $250. Therefore, he cannot challenge the bias of the judge who has denied him contact with his child for 200+ days, jailed him for 59+ days, and refused to enforce parenting time despite 305 documented violations.

This is precisely the kind of financial barrier that *Boddie* and *M.L.B.* prohibit. The state has a monopoly on the judicial process. Judicial disqualification is the prescribed mechanism for addressing bias. When a court imposes a financial barrier that prevents an indigent litigant from using that mechanism, the court denies that litigant meaningful access to justice.

The irony is compounding: the judge whose conduct necessitates the disqualification motion is the same judge who imposed the financial barrier that prevents the motion from being heard. This creates a closed loop of bias from which there is no escape within the lower court.

Only this Court's superintending authority can break that loop — by vacating the bond requirement and ordering either the disqualification or reassignment of Judge McNeill.

Superintending control is the only mechanism that can:

4. Monitor the new proceedings to prevent recurrence.

This is not an extraordinary request — it is the ordinary function of superintending control applied to extraordinary circumstances.

### VII. THE CHILD'S FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS DEMAND IMMEDIATE JUDICIAL INTERVENTION

The rights at stake in this case are not solely Petitioner's. L.D.W. — a minor child — has his own, independent constitutional rights that are being violated daily.

The United States Supreme Court has recognized that the parent-child relationship is protected by the Due Process Clause on both sides. In *Troxel v. Granville*, the Court affirmed that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

But the child's interest is equally fundamental. In *Stanley v. Illinois*, the Court recognized that children have a cognizable interest in not being wrongfully separated from their parents: "The private interest here, that of a man in the children he has sired and raised, undeniably warrants deference and, absent a powerful countervailing interest, protection." 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972).

In *Santosky v. Kramer*, the Court held that before the state can sever the parent-child bond, it must provide procedural protections commensurate with the magnitude of the interests at stake. 455 U.S. 745, 753-54 (1982). The Court emphasized that the parent-child relationship is "far more precious than any property right." *Id.* at 758-59.

L.D.W. has been separated from his father for 200+ days. No court has found that this separation serves L.D.W.'s best interests. No court has found that Petitioner is unfit. No court has found that contact with Petitioner would harm L.D.W. The separation exists solely because:

1. Watson refuses to comply with parenting time provisions;
2. Judge McNeill refuses to enforce those provisions; and
3. The disqualification remedy is blocked by a $250 bond.

The documented pattern of systematic parental alienation indicates that L.D.W. is being actively alienated from his father. Developmental research establishes that parental alienation causes severe and lasting psychological harm to children, including anxiety, depression, low self-esteem, difficulty forming relationships, and identity confusion.

Every day without intervention deepens the harm. Unlike property or money, a child's developmental stages cannot be recovered. The months and years of L.D.W.'s childhood that have been lost to this separation are gone permanently.

Michigan law recognizes the child's interest in maintaining a relationship with both parents. MCL 722.23 lists as a best-interest factor "the love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child" and "the willingness and ability of each of the parties and the child care providers to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." *DeRose v. DeRose*, 249 Mich App 388, 397 (2002).

The lower court's inaction — its refusal to enforce parenting time, its tolerance of 305 violations, its use of contempt to punish the non-custodial parent while ignoring the custodial parent's defiance — is antithetical to these statutory values.

This Court has the power and the duty to intervene. The child's fundamental rights demand it.

### VIII. THE PUBLIC INTEREST REQUIRES THIS COURT'S INTERVENTION

Superintending control is particularly appropriate when the issues presented are of significant public interest. The issues in this case transcend the individual dispute between Petitioner and Watson. They implicate systemic concerns affecting Michigan's judicial system:

**A. Pro se litigant access to justice.** A significant and growing percentage of family law litigants are self-represented. If courts can impose financial barriers (like the $250 bond) that prevent pro se litigants from challenging judicial bias, the constitutional guarantee of access to courts becomes illusory for those who cannot afford counsel. This Court should clarify that financial barriers to disqualification motions, imposed on indigent litigants who have filed for IFP status, violate due process.

**B. Enforcement of mandatory statutes.** MCL 552.644's "shall" language creates a mandatory duty. If circuit courts can refuse to enforce parenting time despite documented violations without consequence, the statute is rendered meaningless. This Court's intervention will reaffirm the mandatory nature of MCL 552.644 and establish that refusal to enforce it is an abuse of discretion correctable by superintending control.

**C. Limits on civil contempt power.** The use of civil contempt to incarcerate an indigent litigant for 59+ days on conditions beyond his ability to satisfy raises profound questions about the limits of contempt power in Michigan. This Court's intervention will provide guidance to circuit courts on the constitutional boundaries of civil contempt — particularly regarding ability-to-pay determinations and the duration of coercive sanctions.

**D. Gender neutrality in custody proceedings.** Michigan's custody statute, MCL 722.23, is facially gender-neutral. But if courts systematically favor one parent's violations while punishing the other's, the statute's neutrality is undermined. This case presents an opportunity for this Court to reaffirm that custody determinations must be genuinely gender-neutral.

**E. Parental alienation.** The 305 documented interference incidents in this case reflect a growing crisis in family courts.
