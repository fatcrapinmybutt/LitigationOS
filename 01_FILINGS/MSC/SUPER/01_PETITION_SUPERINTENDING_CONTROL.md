## I. JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT

1. This Court has original and plenary jurisdiction to issue an Order of Superintending Control over any Michigan court. Const 1963, art 6, § 4 provides:

   > "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts; power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs; and appellate jurisdiction as provided by rules of the supreme court."

2. MCR 7.305(A) provides that "[t]he Supreme Court may exercise superintending control over lower courts and tribunals." MCR 7.305(B) establishes the standard: "An order of superintending control may be granted only when the party seeking the order has no adequate legal remedy and the lower court's exercise of power was unauthorized by law."

3. The Supreme Court's superintending control power is constitutionally conferred, plenary in scope, and not dependent on the existence of any other remedy. *Judges of the 74th Judicial District v. Bay County*, 385 Mich 710, 727 (1971). This power is "extraordinary" and extends to "the entire range of supervisory authority necessary to ensure the proper and efficient administration of justice." *Id.*

4. This Court's superintending control authority permits it to "correct abuses of judicial power, to compel the performance of judicial duties, and to ensure the orderly administration of justice" when no other remedy is adequate. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441-42 (1993).

## IV. GROUNDS FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL UNDER MCR 7.305(B)

### A. The Lower Court's Exercise of Power Is Unauthorized by Law

25. MCR 7.305(B) authorizes superintending control when "the lower court's exercise of power was unauthorized by law." The following exercises of power by the 14th Circuit Court are unauthorized:

26. **Refusal to perform a mandatory statutory duty.** MCL 552.644's "shall" language creates a non-discretionary duty to enforce parenting time. The lower court's refusal to enforce parenting time despite 305 documented violations is an exercise of power "unauthorized by law" because no court has authority to refuse a mandatory statutory duty. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 441.

27. **Punitive use of civil contempt.** Civil contempt is coercive, not punitive. When contempt sanctions lose their coercive character and become punitive, the court has exceeded its civil contempt authority. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 96-97 (1987). Jailing Petitioner for 59+ days on conditions he could not satisfy transformed civil contempt into criminal punishment without criminal procedural protections.

28. **Ex parte proceedings on substantive matters.** Conducting proceedings ex parte — at a rate of 44% (24 of 55 orders) — without notice to Petitioner violates the fundamental requirement of MCR 2.119 and due process under U.S. Const, amend XIV, § 1, and Const 1963, art 1, § 17.

29. **Financial barriers to judicial disqualification.** Imposing a $250 bond on a disqualification motion filed by an indigent litigant, where such bond effectively prevents the motion from being heard, is an unauthorized exercise of judicial power that violates due process. *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971).

### B. Petitioner Has No Adequate Legal Remedy

30. As set forth in Section III.C above, no individual remedy — mandamus, habeas corpus, appellate review, or judicial conduct complaint — is adequate to address the totality of the lower court's dysfunction.

31. Mandamus (MCR 7.306) compels specific acts. It cannot restructure an entire case's management, remove a judge, and monitor future compliance.

32. Habeas corpus addresses custody but not the underlying procedural violations, bias, or contempt abuse.

33. The Court of Appeals appeal (No. 366810) is pending but cannot provide emergency relief, cannot remove a judge, and operates on a timeline incompatible with a child's developmental needs.

34. Superintending control is the only remedy with sufficient breadth to address every dimension of the lower court's failure.

### C. Significant Public Interest

35. This case presents issues of significant public interest:
    - The rights of pro se litigants to fair proceedings in Michigan family courts;
    - The enforceability of mandatory parenting time statutes;
    - The limits of civil contempt power when used against indigent litigants;
    - The effect of financial barriers on access to judicial remedies;
    - The duty of courts to protect children's fundamental right to relationships with both parents.

36. This Court's intervention will establish important precedent on the scope of superintending control in family law cases and the obligations of circuit courts to comply with mandatory statutes.
