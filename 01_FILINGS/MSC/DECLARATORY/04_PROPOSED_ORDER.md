### FINDINGS OF FACT

The Court finds the following:

1. The 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County, entered twenty-four (24) orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC and related Case No. 2023-5907-PP that substantively modified custody, parenting time, and child support without prior notice to Plaintiff and without affording Plaintiff an opportunity to be heard before the orders took effect.

2. The twenty-four ex parte orders represent approximately 44% (24 of 55) of all orders entered by the trial court in these cases. This rate exceeds the normal judicial rate of ex parte orders — estimated at less than 2% — by approximately ninefold.

3. The ex parte orders were not confined to narrow, temporary emergency measures. They substantively modified the long-term allocation of custody, parenting time schedules, and child support obligations.

4. As a cumulative result of these ex parte orders, Plaintiff has been denied all parenting time with his minor child, L.D.W., for more than 200+ consecutive days.

5. No evidentiary hearing was held before the entry of the ex parte orders at which the trial court evaluated the best interest factors under MCL 722.23 or made a finding of proper cause or change of circumstances under MCL 722.27(1)(c).

6. The Friend of the Court issued recommendations in these cases that were overridden by the trial court without stated basis on the record.

---

### CONCLUSIONS OF LAW

Based on the foregoing findings, the Court concludes:

**A. Due Process Clause — Fourteenth Amendment**

7. The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972).

8. The Due Process Clause requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a court may enter an order that substantively modifies custody, parenting time, or child support. *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 U.S. 306, 314 (1950); *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 550 (1965).

9. The application of the *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976), to custody modification proceedings confirms that the private interest is of the highest constitutional order, the risk of erroneous deprivation in ex parte proceedings is extreme, and the government has no legitimate interest in avoiding notice and hearing.

10. The trial court's practice of entering substantive custody modifications on an ex parte basis violated the procedural due process rights of Plaintiff under the Fourteenth Amendment.

**B. MCR 2.119(B)**

11. MCR 2.119(B) requires that written motions be served at least nine days before hearing, except for motions that may be heard ex parte. Motions to substantively modify custody, parenting time, or child support are not among those that may be heard ex parte under the general rules.

12. The trial court's routine entry of substantive custody modifications without the notice required by MCR 2.119(B) violated that rule.

**C. MCL 722.27(1)(c)**

13. MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires a threshold showing of "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" before custody may be modified, followed by an evaluation of the child's best interests under MCL 722.23.

14. Both the threshold determination and the best interest evaluation require evidence from both parties. An ex parte proceeding cannot satisfy these requirements because it necessarily excludes one party's evidence and perspective.

15. Ex parte custody modification is permissible only as a narrow, temporary emergency measure — limited to situations of imminent physical danger to the child — and any such emergency order must be followed by a prompt hearing at which both parties are heard.

---

### DECLARATORY RELIEF

### IT IS HEREBY DECLARED:

**Declaration I:**

16. The practice of modifying custody arrangements on an ex parte basis — without notice to the affected parent as required by MCR 2.119(B) and without an evidentiary hearing — violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and Article 1, Section 17 of the Michigan Constitution of 1963.

**Declaration II:**

17. MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires that any modification of custody be preceded by an evidentiary hearing at which both parties have an opportunity to present evidence. The threshold finding of "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" and the best interest analysis under MCL 722.23 cannot be satisfied in an ex parte proceeding.

**Declaration III:**

18. Ex parte orders affecting custody, parenting time, or child support are permissible only when:

    a. There is a demonstrated, imminent risk of serious physical harm to the child that cannot be addressed by scheduling an expedited hearing with notice;

    b. The ex parte order is narrowly tailored to address the specific emergency and does not make permanent or long-term modifications to custody;

    c. A post-deprivation hearing is scheduled and held within seven (7) days of the entry of the ex parte order, at which the affected parent has a full opportunity to be heard and to present evidence; and

    d. The ex parte order expires automatically if a post-deprivation hearing is not held within seven (7) days.

**Declaration IV:**

19. The twenty-four (24) ex parte orders entered by the 14th Circuit Court in Case No. 2024-001507-DC and Case No. 2023-5907-PP — substantively modifying custody, parenting time, and child support without notice and hearing — violated Plaintiff's rights under the Fourteenth Amendment, Const 1963, art 1, § 17, MCR 2.119(B), and MCL 722.27(1)(c).

---

### INJUNCTIVE RELIEF

### IT IS FURTHER ORDERED:

**I. Prospective Injunction Against Ex Parte Custody Modifications**

20. The 14th Circuit Court is hereby ENJOINED from entering any further ex parte orders in Case No. 2024-001507-DC and Case No. 2023-5907-PP that substantively modify custody, parenting time, or child support, except in strict compliance with the conditions set forth in Paragraph 18 above.

21. All motions seeking modification of custody, parenting time, or child support in the above-captioned cases shall be served on all parties at least fourteen (14) days before any hearing, unless the Court determines, based on specific findings on the record, that an emergency necessitating shorter notice exists.

**II. De Novo Hearing Requirement**

22. The substitute judge assigned to these cases (as ordered in any companion order of this Court, or as directed by the State Court Administrator) shall, within sixty (60) days of assignment, conduct a de novo evidentiary hearing on the current custody, parenting time, and child support arrangements.

23. At the de novo hearing, the substitute judge shall:

    a. Evaluate the threshold requirement of proper cause or change of circumstances under MCL 722.27(1)(c);

    b. If the threshold is met, evaluate all twelve best interest factors under MCL 722.23, with testimony and evidence from both parties;

    c. Make specific findings of fact and conclusions of law on the record;

    d. Enter custody, parenting time, and child support orders based on the evidence presented at the de novo hearing.

24. Pending the de novo hearing, the custody, parenting time, and support arrangements shall be as established by this Court's companion Order Granting Emergency Relief, or as the substitute judge may order after a noticed hearing.

**III. Record Production**

25. The Clerk of the 14th Circuit Court is DIRECTED to produce a complete copy of the case file for Case No. 2024-001507-DC and Case No. 2023-5907-PP, including all orders, motions, briefs, transcripts, and other filings, to this Court within thirty (30) days of the entry of this Order.

26. The Clerk shall separately identify and compile all ex parte orders for this Court's review.
