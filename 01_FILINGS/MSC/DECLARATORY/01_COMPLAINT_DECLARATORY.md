## I. NATURE OF THE ACTION

1. This is a Complaint for Declaratory Judgment brought pursuant to MCR 2.605 and this Court's original jurisdiction under Const 1963, art 6, § 4. Plaintiff seeks declarations of law regarding the constitutional validity of the practice of modifying custody, parenting time, and child support orders on an ex parte basis without constitutionally adequate notice and hearing.

2. This action presents a question of first impression for this Court: **Can a circuit court judge modify custody arrangements ex parte — without notice to the affected parent and without an evidentiary hearing — consistent with the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and the Michigan Court Rules?**

3. The answer to this question has statewide significance. While the specific facts arise from a single case before the 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County, the legal principles at issue affect every custody proceeding in Michigan. If a circuit court may modify custody without notice, then no parent's custodial rights are secure. If it may not — as Plaintiff contends — then this Court should say so clearly, both to vindicate Plaintiff's rights and to guide the courts of this State.

4. The trial court in the underlying proceedings has issued twenty-four (24) ex parte orders over the course of this litigation, representing 44% (24 of 55) of all orders entered. These orders were not confined to emergency protective measures; they substantively modified custody arrangements, parenting time schedules, and child support obligations. This rate of ex parte action exceeds normal judicial practice by approximately ninefold.

5. The practical consequence of these ex parte orders is that Plaintiff has been denied all parenting time with his minor child, L.D.W., for 200+ consecutive days. This de facto termination of the parent-child relationship was accomplished without any evidentiary hearing, without any best interest analysis under MCL 722.23, and without any finding that Plaintiff's parenting time posed a risk of harm to L.D.W.

## V. LEGAL FRAMEWORK

31. The legal questions presented by this Complaint implicate four overlapping bodies of law:

    a. **Federal Constitutional Law:** The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires notice and hearing before the State may deprive a person of a protected liberty interest. The parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972).

    b. **Michigan Constitutional Law:** Const 1963, art 1, § 17 ("No person shall... be deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law.").

    c. **Michigan Court Rules:** MCR 2.119(B) requires that motions be served at least nine days before hearing. MCR 2.119(B)(1). Ex parte orders are authorized only in narrow circumstances involving immediate, irreparable injury.

    d. **Michigan Statutory Law:** MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires a showing of "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" before custody modification. MCL 722.23 requires a best interest analysis based on twelve enumerated factors.

## VI. COUNT I — DUE PROCESS VIOLATION: EX PARTE CUSTODY MODIFICATIONS WITHOUT NOTICE OR HEARING

32. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs.

33. **Issue:** Whether the trial court's practice of entering ex parte orders that substantively modify custody, parenting time, and child support — without notice to the affected parent and without an evidentiary hearing — violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.

34. **Rule:** "An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process in any proceeding which is to be accorded finality is notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections." *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 U.S. 306, 314 (1950).

35. In custody proceedings, the Supreme Court has specifically held that a parent must receive notice and an opportunity to be heard: "A fundamental requirement of due process is 'the opportunity to be heard.'... It is an opportunity which must be granted at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 550 (1965) (holding that adoption completed without notice to father violated due process).

36. The *Mathews v. Eldridge* three-factor balancing test governs the adequacy of procedural protections: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional safeguards; (3) the Government's interest, including the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional procedure. 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976).

37. **Application:**

    a. **Factor 1 — Private Interest:** The private interest at stake is the parent-child relationship — "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65. This interest is "far more precious... than property rights." *May v. Anderson*, 345 U.S. 528, 533 (1953). No higher private interest exists in American law. This factor overwhelmingly favors requiring notice and hearing.

    b. **Factor 2 — Risk of Error:** The risk of erroneous deprivation when a court enters custody orders without hearing from the affected parent is extreme. The adversarial system exists precisely because ex parte proceedings produce unreliable outcomes. When only one party presents evidence, the court has no basis for evaluating the credibility of the claims or the adequacy of the proposed arrangement. The twenty-four ex parte orders in this case demonstrate the danger: Plaintiff was progressively stripped of all parental rights without ever having the opportunity to challenge the factual predicates for those orders. The probable value of additional safeguards (i.e., notice and hearing) is self-evident.

    c. **Factor 3 — Government Interest:** The government has no legitimate interest in modifying custody without notice and hearing. The administrative burden of providing nine days' notice under MCR 2.119(B) is negligible. Indeed, the existing court rules already require notice — the trial court simply failed to follow them. There is no cost to the government of compliance with its own rules.

38. **Conclusion:** Under the *Mathews* balancing test, the practice of entering ex parte orders that substantively modify custody arrangements violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Plaintiff requests that this Court so declare.

38A. **Binding MSC Authority:** This Court has repeatedly affirmed the centrality of procedural due process in family law proceedings. In *In re Rood*, 483 Mich 73, 91 (2009), this Court held that "fundamental fairness requires that a party be afforded an opportunity to present evidence and argument before the court takes action affecting the party's rights." In *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 193 (2004), this Court reversed a custody modification where the trial court failed to follow mandated procedures, emphasizing that "compliance with statutory procedures is not optional." See also *Foskett v Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1, 8-9 (2001) (reversal for failure to provide adequate notice before custody modification).

## VII. COUNT II — VIOLATION OF MCR 2.119(B) NOTICE REQUIREMENTS

39. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs.

40. **Issue:** Whether the trial court's issuance of twenty-four ex parte orders violated the notice requirements of MCR 2.119(B).

41. **Rule:** MCR 2.119(B)(1) provides: "Unless a different period is set by these rules or by the court, a written motion other than one that may be heard ex parte, together with all supporting affidavits and a brief or memorandum, must be served by the moving party on all other parties... at least 9 days before the hearing on the motion."

42. Ex parte orders are authorized under the Michigan Court Rules only in narrow circumstances. MCR 3.207 governs ex parte orders in domestic relations cases and requires a showing of immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage. Even emergency ex parte orders under this rule are temporary measures, subject to prompt post-deprivation hearing.

43. **Application:** The twenty-four ex parte orders at issue in this case were not limited to temporary protective measures or emergency preservation of the status quo. They substantively modified custody, parenting time, and support — precisely the types of orders that MCR 2.119(B) requires be preceded by service and notice.

44. The trial court's 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate — compared to a normal rate of less than 2% — demonstrates that ex parte action was not reserved for genuine emergencies. It was employed routinely as a substitute for the adversarial process that MCR 2.119(B) mandates. This systematic departure from the court rules cannot be characterized as isolated judicial error; it constitutes a practice.

45. **Conclusion:** The trial court's routine issuance of ex parte orders modifying custody, parenting time, and support violated MCR 2.119(B). Plaintiff requests that this Court declare that MCR 2.119(B) requires notice before entry of any order substantively modifying custody.

## VIII. COUNT III — VIOLATION OF MCL 722.27(1)(c) PROPER CAUSE REQUIREMENT

46. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs.

47. **Issue:** Whether the trial court's modification of custody through ex parte orders, without findings of "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" on the record, violated MCL 722.27(1)(c).

48. **Rule:** MCL 722.27(1)(c) provides that a court "shall not modify or amend its previous judgments or orders or issue a new order so as to change the established custodial environment of a child unless there is presented clear and convincing evidence that it is in the best interest of the child." Before reaching the best interest analysis, the court must find that "proper cause" or a "change of circumstances" exists to warrant revisiting the custody arrangement. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich. App. 499, 508-09 (2003).

49. The "proper cause" threshold is not a formality. It is a substantive barrier designed to protect children from the instability of frequent custody modifications and to protect parents from litigation harassment. *Id.* at 509. A court that skips this threshold requirement lacks authority to modify custody.

50. **Application:** The trial court entered multiple ex parte orders modifying custody without making threshold findings of proper cause or change of circumstances on the record. An ex parte proceeding — at which only one party is heard — cannot generate the record necessary for a proper cause determination. Proper cause requires factual evaluation that necessarily involves both parties.

51. Furthermore, even where the trial court's ex parte orders purported to be based on emergency circumstances, the proper course under MCL 722.27 was to enter a narrow, temporary protective order and then schedule a prompt hearing at which both parties could be heard on the proper cause threshold. The trial court instead entered substantive, long-duration custody modifications on an ex parte basis, bypassing the statutory framework entirely.

52. **Conclusion:** Plaintiff requests that this Court declare that MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires an evidentiary hearing — at which both parties are heard — before any modification of custody, and that ex parte custody modifications are permissible only as narrow, temporary measures subject to prompt post-deprivation hearing.

52A. **Binding MSC Authority:** In *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92-93 (2010), this Court held that "the threshold finding of proper cause or change of circumstances is a mandatory prerequisite" and that without it "the trial court is without authority to revisit the custody order." In *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 27 (2010), the Court held that the proper cause requirement cannot be satisfied absent testimony and evidence from both parties. See also *Brown v Brown*, 332 Mich App 1, 12 (2020) (reversing where trial court modified custody without making threshold findings on the record).

## IX. COUNT IV — VIOLATION OF FOURTEENTH AMENDMENT PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS (FEDERAL CONSTITUTIONAL CLAIM)

53. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs.

54. **Issue:** Whether the cumulative pattern of twenty-four ex parte orders depriving Plaintiff of parental rights without notice and hearing violates the procedural due process protections of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.

55. **Rule:** The Fourteenth Amendment provides: "No State shall... deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1.

56. The Supreme Court has established that the parent-child relationship constitutes a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65; *Stanley*, 405 U.S. at 651; *Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 753. Deprivation of this interest requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner. *Mathews*, 424 U.S. at 333; *Mullane*, 339 U.S. at 314; *Armstrong*, 380 U.S. at 550.

57. Where a pattern of state action systematically deprives individuals of constitutional rights, declaratory relief is appropriate. *See Steffel v. Thompson*, 415 U.S. 452, 462 (1974) (noting that declaratory judgment is designed to prevent "threatened injury" and to clarify legal rights).

58. **Application:** The trial court's pattern of twenty-four ex parte orders constitutes systematic state action depriving Plaintiff of his fundamental liberty interest in the parent-child relationship without constitutionally adequate process. This is not an isolated procedural error. The 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate, sustained over the duration of the litigation, reflects a practice — a way of doing business — that is incompatible with the Fourteenth Amendment.

59. The harm from this practice is not speculative. Plaintiff has been completely separated from L.D.W. for 200+ days. He has been incarcerated for 59+ days. He has lost three jobs. All of this resulted from orders entered without notice and hearing.

60. The federal constitutional dimension of this case supports this Court's exercise of original jurisdiction. As the highest court of the State, this Court has an independent obligation to ensure that state courts do not violate federal constitutional guarantees. A declaratory judgment that ex parte custody modifications violate the Fourteenth Amendment would provide clear guidance to all Michigan courts and prevent similar violations in other cases.

61. **Conclusion:** Plaintiff requests that this Court declare that the practice of modifying custody, parenting time, and child support on an ex parte basis — without notice and hearing — violates the procedural due process protections of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.

## IX-A. COUNT IV-A — ADVISORY OPINION ON CONSTITUTIONALITY OF 2005 PA 326 AS APPLIED TO EX PARTE CUSTODY MODIFICATIONS

61A. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs.

61B. **Issue:** Whether this Court should issue an advisory opinion on the constitutionality of the ex parte custody modification practice as it interacts with the statutory framework established by **2005 PA 326** (codified at MCL 722.26a et seq.), which amended Michigan's Child Custody Act to establish mandatory procedures for custody dispute resolution.

61C. **Rule:** This Court has authority to issue advisory opinions on questions of law of significant public interest. Const 1963, art 3, § 8. The constitutionality of statutory schemes governing fundamental rights is precisely the type of question warranting this Court's advisory guidance. *See In re Advisory Opinion re Constitutionality of 2005 PA 71*, 479 Mich 1 (2007) (exercising advisory opinion jurisdiction over constitutional questions involving significant public impact).

61D. **2005 PA 326** established a statutory framework designed to protect the stability of custodial environments and impose heightened procedural requirements before courts may modify established custody arrangements. Specifically:

   a. **MCL 722.26a(1)(b)** requires that, absent consent of both parties, a court must hold an evidentiary hearing before modifying a custody order — reinforcing the constitutional requirement of notice and hearing;
   b. **MCL 722.27(1)(c)**, as amended, requires a threshold showing of "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" before the court may even consider modification — a substantive barrier that cannot be satisfied through ex parte proceedings;
   c. The legislative scheme of 2005 PA 326 reflects a deliberate policy judgment by the Legislature that custody stability, procedural protection, and parental rights require heightened safeguards — safeguards that are systematically circumvented when courts resort to ex parte modification.

61E. The trial court's practice of entering twenty-four ex parte custody orders is directly at odds with the procedural protections 2005 PA 326 was enacted to provide. If a circuit court may bypass the evidentiary hearing requirement of MCL 722.26a(1)(b) and the threshold showing requirement of MCL 722.27(1)(c) through the expedient of ex parte action, then the Legislature's 2005 amendments are rendered a nullity.

61F. This Court should exercise its advisory opinion authority to declare that the procedural protections of 2005 PA 326 — including the evidentiary hearing and proper cause requirements — apply with full force to all custody modifications, and that ex parte orders may not be used to circumvent these legislatively mandated protections except in the narrowest emergency circumstances, subject to immediate post-deprivation hearing.

61G. **Binding MSC Authority Supporting This Count:**

   a. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 508-09 (2003) — proper cause threshold is mandatory before any custody modification (MSC denied leave, effectively endorsing);
   b. *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 27-28 (2010) — proper cause or change of circumstances must be established on the record before the best interest analysis begins;
   c. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010) — this Court held that MCL 722.27 requires a factual showing on the record before custody may be modified; the threshold requirement is not a mere formality;
   d. *Rains v Rains*, 301 Mich App 313, 324-25 (2013) — failure to conduct an evidentiary hearing before modifying custody requires reversal;
   e. *Harvey v Harvey*, 470 Mich 186, 192 (2004) — this Court emphasized that custody proceedings must comply with fundamental fairness requirements, and that a court's failure to follow required procedures constitutes reversible error;
   f. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409-10 (2014) — this Court reaffirmed that due process requires meaningful opportunity to be heard before deprivation of parental rights, and that the right to procedural protection is not diminished by the nature of the proceeding.

## X. COUNT V — REQUEST FOR INJUNCTIVE RELIEF

62. Plaintiff incorporates by reference all preceding paragraphs.

63. **Issue:** Whether this Court should enter a prospective injunction prohibiting the trial court from issuing further ex parte custody modifications and requiring fourteen (14) days' notice for all substantive custody motions.

64. **Rule:** Under MCR 2.605(E), a court may grant "supplementary relief based on a declaratory judgment." Injunctive relief is appropriate when declaratory relief alone would be insufficient to prevent ongoing or future violations.

65. Injunctive relief is warranted when: (1) the plaintiff has suffered an irreparable injury; (2) remedies at law are inadequate to compensate for the injury; (3) considering the balance of hardships, a remedy in equity is warranted; and (4) the public interest would not be disserved by an injunction. *eBay Inc. v. MercExchange, L.L.C.*, 547 U.S. 388, 391 (2006).

66. **Application:**

    a. **Irreparable Injury:** Plaintiff has been separated from his child for 200+ days. This injury is categorically irreparable — the lost time cannot be restored.

    b. **Inadequacy of Legal Remedies:** No monetary damages can compensate for the loss of the parent-child relationship. Declaratory relief alone, while valuable, does not prevent the trial court from continuing its current practice pending full resolution of this matter.

    c. **Balance of Hardships:** An injunction requiring fourteen days' notice before substantive custody motions imposes no hardship on any party. It merely requires compliance with procedures similar to those already mandated by MCR 2.119(B). By contrast, the absence of an injunction permits the continued deprivation of Plaintiff's fundamental constitutional rights.

    d. **Public Interest:** The public has a strong interest in ensuring that custody proceedings comply with due process. An injunction requiring notice and hearing protects not only Plaintiff but all parents who appear before the 14th Circuit Court.

67. **Conclusion:** Plaintiff requests that this Court enter a prospective injunction: (a) prohibiting the trial court from issuing ex parte orders modifying custody, parenting time, or child support; and (b) requiring at least fourteen (14) days' notice to all parties before any hearing on a substantive custody motion.
