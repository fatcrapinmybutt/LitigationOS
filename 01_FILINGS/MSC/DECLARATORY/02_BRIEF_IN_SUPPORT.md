## II. INTRODUCTION

This brief addresses a fundamental question in Michigan family law: **What process is due before a court may modify custody?** The answer, under both federal and Michigan law, is clear: at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard. Yet in the underlying proceedings, the trial court entered twenty-four ex parte orders substantively modifying custody, parenting time, and support — a 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate that is approximately nine times the normal judicial average.

The purpose of this Complaint for Declaratory Judgment is not merely to vindicate Plaintiff's individual rights — though they urgently require vindication — but to obtain a definitive statement from this Court regarding the procedural requirements for custody modification in Michigan. The trial court's practice raises a question of law with statewide significance: if one circuit court may routinely modify custody ex parte, then the due process protections that safeguard every parent in Michigan are illusory.

MCR 2.605 authorizes declaratory judgment in cases of actual controversy. The controversy here could not be more actual: Plaintiff has been separated from his child for 200+ days as the direct result of ex parte orders that he contends violate the Constitution. This Court's original jurisdiction under Const 1963, art 6, § 4 encompasses exactly this type of systemic judicial practice issue.

## IV. STANDARD OF REVIEW

### A. Declaratory Judgment Standard

MCR 2.605 provides that "in a case of actual controversy," a court of record may "declare the rights and other legal relations" of interested parties. MCR 2.605(A)(1). The purpose of declaratory judgment is "to settle and afford relief from uncertainty and insecurity with respect to rights, status, and other legal relations." MCR 2.605(B).

### B. Constitutional Questions

Constitutional questions are reviewed de novo. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich. 394, 404 (2014). The interpretation and application of the Due Process Clause, MCR 2.119(B), and MCL 722.27(1)(c) are legal questions that this Court decides without deference to the lower courts.

### C. Court Rule Interpretation

The interpretation of Michigan Court Rules is a legal question reviewed de novo. *Haliw v. Sterling Heights*, 471 Mich. 700, 704 (2005).

### D. Statutory Interpretation

The interpretation of Michigan statutes is a legal question reviewed de novo. *Klooster v. City of Charlevoix*, 488 Mich. 289, 295-96 (2011). In *Advisory Opinion re Constitutionality of 2005 PA 71*, 479 Mich 1 (2007), this Court confirmed its authority to address fundamental constitutional questions through original proceedings, holding that where a statute or practice implicates core constitutional protections, this Court may exercise original jurisdiction to declare the rights of the parties and provide prospective guidance to the lower courts. *Id.* at 11-12.

## V. ARGUMENT I: FEDERAL DUE PROCESS REQUIRES NOTICE AND HEARING BEFORE CUSTODY MODIFICATION

### A. The Constitutional Principle

**Conclusion:** The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause requires notice and an evidentiary hearing before any court may enter an order substantively modifying custody, parenting time, or child support.

**Rule:** The Supreme Court of the United States has established a clear and unbroken line of authority holding that the parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment:

- *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390, 399 (1923) — recognized the liberty interest of parents in directing the upbringing of their children.
- *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972) — held that "the rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed 'essential,'... 'basic civil rights of man,'... and 'rights far more precious... than property rights.'"
- *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982) — held that termination of parental rights requires clear and convincing evidence and emphasized the "commanding" nature of the liberty interest at stake.
- *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000) — described the parent-child interest as "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."

Because this interest is fundamental, it triggers heightened procedural protections. The *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976), governs the adequacy of those protections. But even without *Mathews*, the baseline requirement is clear: "An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process in any proceeding which is to be accorded finality is notice reasonably calculated... to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections." *Mullane*, 339 U.S. at 314.

In the specific context of custody proceedings, the Supreme Court has left no doubt: a parent must receive notice before a custody decision is finalized. In *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545 (1965), the Court held that an adoption completed without notice to the father violated due process, even though the father had a right to contest the adoption post-deprivation. The Court emphasized that due process requires "the opportunity to be heard... at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Id.* at 550.

**Explanation:** The principle is straightforward: the more significant the liberty interest, the more rigorous the procedural protections. Since parental liberty is among the most fundamental interests recognized in American law, the procedural protections must be correspondingly rigorous. At minimum, this means: (1) notice of the proposed action; (2) an opportunity to be heard before the action takes effect; and (3) a decision based on evidence presented by both sides.

**Application:** The trial court's twenty-four ex parte orders provided none of these protections. Plaintiff received no notice before the orders were entered. He had no opportunity to be heard. The orders were based on one-sided presentations by Defendant Watson, without adversarial testing.

The *Mathews* factors confirm the constitutional deficiency:

1. **Private Interest (Maximum):** The parent-child relationship. No higher private interest exists.

2. **Risk of Error (Extreme):** Ex parte proceedings are inherently unreliable. Without adversarial presentation, the court receives unchallenged allegations from one party. The risk that these allegations are incomplete, misleading, or false is high — particularly in contentious custody disputes where the parties have strong incentives to present one-sided narratives.

3. **Government Interest (None):** The government has no interest in avoiding the nine days' notice required by MCR 2.119(B). The administrative burden is negligible. Compliance with existing court rules satisfies due process at no additional cost.

**Conclusion:** This Court should declare that the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause requires notice and an evidentiary hearing before any order substantively modifying custody, parenting time, or child support. Ex parte custody modifications — except as narrow, temporary emergency measures subject to prompt post-deprivation hearing — violate due process.

## VI. ARGUMENT II: MCR 2.119(B) MANDATES PRE-HEARING NOTICE FOR SUBSTANTIVE MOTIONS

### A. The Court Rule Requirement

**Conclusion:** MCR 2.119(B) requires at least nine days' notice before any hearing on a motion to modify custody, parenting time, or child support. The trial court's routine ex parte action violated this rule.

**Rule:** MCR 2.119(B)(1) provides: "Unless a different period is set by these rules or by the court, a written motion other than one that may be heard ex parte, together with all supporting affidavits and a brief or memorandum, must be served by the moving party on all other parties... at least 9 days before the hearing on the motion."

The rule distinguishes between motions "that may be heard ex parte" and all other motions. Motions to modify custody are not among those that may be heard ex parte under the general rules. MCR 3.207 governs ex parte orders in domestic relations cases and limits them to narrow emergency circumstances.

**Explanation:** The nine-day notice requirement serves the same purpose as the constitutional notice requirement: it ensures that the responding party has an opportunity to prepare a response and present evidence. For custody modifications, this is particularly important because the factual questions involved — the child's best interests, the existence of proper cause for modification, the credibility of competing allegations — cannot be resolved on one party's unchallenged representations.

**Application:** Twenty-four times, the trial court entered orders without the notice required by MCR 2.119(B). These were not emergency protective orders under MCR 3.207; they were substantive custody modifications that fundamentally altered the parties' rights and obligations. The 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate demonstrates that this was not an occasional, justified departure from the rule but a routine practice.

**Conclusion:** This Court should declare that MCR 2.119(B) requires notice before any order substantively modifying custody, and that the trial court's practice of routine ex parte custody modification violates the court rules.

## VII. ARGUMENT III: MCL 722.27(1)(c) REQUIRES EVIDENTIARY HEARING BEFORE CUSTODY CHANGE

### A. The Statutory Framework

**Conclusion:** MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires a two-step process — threshold finding of proper cause or change of circumstances, followed by a best interest analysis — that cannot be satisfied in an ex parte proceeding.

**Rule:** MCL 722.27(1)(c) provides that a court "shall not modify or amend its previous judgments or orders or issue a new order so as to change the established custodial environment of a child unless there is presented clear and convincing evidence that it is in the best interest of the child."

The Michigan Court of Appeals has interpreted this statute to require a threshold determination: "Before a court can evaluate whether the child's best interests warrant a change in custody, it must first find either proper cause or a change of circumstances." *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich. App. 499, 508-09 (2003). This threshold requirement is "designed to minimize unwarranted and disruptive changes of custody orders." *Id.* at 509.

**Explanation:** The proper cause/change of circumstances threshold is a gatekeeper. It prevents relitigation of custody based on stale disputes or litigation harassment. It also ensures that the court evaluates new evidence before proceeding to the best interest analysis. This gatekeeper function requires evidence from both parties: only through adversarial presentation can the court determine whether the alleged "change" is genuine and material.

The best interest analysis under MCL 722.23 requires evaluation of twelve specific factors, including "the love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child" (factor a), "the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent" (factor j), and others that inherently require evidence from both sides.

**Application:** The trial court's ex parte orders bypassed both requirements. No threshold finding of proper cause or change of circumstances was made on the record. No best interest analysis under MCL 722.23 was conducted. By definition, neither can be adequately performed in an ex parte proceeding, because both require weighing competing evidence from adversarial parties.

The consequence is that Plaintiff's custody rights were modified without any of the statutory safeguards the legislature designed to protect parents and children. This is not a technical procedural failure — it is a substantive denial of the protections MCL 722.27 was enacted to provide.

**Conclusion:** This Court should declare that MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires an evidentiary hearing — at which both parties are present and heard — before any modification of custody. Ex parte modification is permissible only as a narrow, temporary emergency measure, subject to prompt hearing within a defined number of days.

## VIII. ARGUMENT IV: THE PATTERN DEMONSTRATES SYSTEMIC VIOLATION, NOT ISOLATED ERROR

### A. Statistical Evidence of Systemic Practice

**Conclusion:** The trial court's 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate — approximately nine times the judicial average — demonstrates a systemic practice rather than isolated emergency action.

**Rule:** When evaluating whether a judicial practice violates constitutional norms, courts look beyond individual orders to patterns. A pattern of constitutional violation supports both declaratory relief and injunctive relief. *See Steffel v. Thompson*, 415 U.S. 452, 462 (1974); *City of Los Angeles v. Lyons*, 461 U.S. 95 (1983) (discussing standards for prospective injunctive relief against government practices).

**Explanation:** A single ex parte order, entered in genuine emergency, does not raise systemic concerns. But twenty-four ex parte orders — representing 44% (24 of 55) of all orders — cannot be explained as a series of isolated emergencies. The statistical disparity between the trial court's rate and the normal judicial average of less than 2% is itself evidence of a systematic departure from constitutional norms.

**Application:** The pattern is self-documenting. Over the course of this litigation, nearly one in five orders was entered ex parte. These orders addressed the full range of custody-related issues: legal custody, physical custody, parenting time, and child support. The breadth and frequency of ex parte action demonstrates that the trial court treated ex parte proceedings as a routine tool rather than an emergency exception.

This pattern has a direct causal relationship to the harm Plaintiff has suffered. It was the accumulation of ex parte orders — each one further restricting Plaintiff's rights — that ultimately resulted in the complete elimination of parenting time. No single order accomplished this result; the pattern did.

**Conclusion:** The pattern of twenty-four ex parte orders constitutes a systemic practice that this Court should address through declaratory and injunctive relief, not merely by reversing individual orders.

## IX. ARGUMENT V: THIS COURT SHOULD EXERCISE ORIGINAL JURISDICTION AND SUPERINTENDING CONTROL

### A. Jurisdiction and Authority

**Conclusion:** The questions presented warrant this Court's exercise of original jurisdiction under MCR 2.605 and Const 1963, art 6, § 4.

**Rule:** Const 1963, art 6, § 4 grants this Court "general superintending control over all courts" and the power to "issue, hear, and determine prerogative and remedial writs." MCR 7.304 governs original proceedings in this Court, and MCR 2.605 authorizes declaratory judgment in cases of actual controversy. Together, these authorities empower this Court to address systemic judicial practices that violate constitutional rights.

In *Advisory Opinion re Constitutionality of 2005 PA 71*, 479 Mich 1, 11-12 (2007), this Court exercised original jurisdiction to address a fundamental constitutional question, recognizing that when the constitutionality of a governmental practice is at stake, this Court's role is not merely appellate but structural — ensuring that the constitutional framework governing all Michigan courts is clearly articulated and enforced. The Court emphasized that original jurisdiction is particularly appropriate where the question presented has statewide implications and the ordinary appellate process is inadequate to address it. *Id.*

**Explanation:** This Court exercises original jurisdiction sparingly, reserving it for cases of unusual significance. The present case qualifies for several reasons:

1. **Statewide Significance:** The question whether custody may be modified ex parte affects every parent in Michigan. A definitive answer from this Court will guide all circuit courts and prevent similar violations.

2. **Constitutional Dimension:** The questions presented implicate the most fundamental of constitutional rights. Resolution by this Court, rather than by the Court of Appeals in the specific factual context of Plaintiff's appeal (No. 366810), will produce a more broadly applicable statement of law.

3. **Inadequacy of Ordinary Appellate Process:** The Court of Appeals may reverse specific orders in Case No. 366810, but it cannot address the systemic practice of ex parte custody modification. Only this Court can declare the law for all Michigan courts.

4. **Superintending Control:** The pattern of conduct at issue — 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate in custody proceedings — implicates this Court's responsibility to maintain the proper administration of justice in the lower courts.

**Application:** All four factors support the exercise of original jurisdiction. The controversy is actual — Plaintiff's rights have been concretely and measurably harmed. The questions are legal — they concern the interpretation of the Constitution, court rules, and statutes. The significance is statewide — every parent in Michigan has an interest in knowing what process is due before custody may be modified.

**Conclusion:** This Court should exercise original jurisdiction and its superintending control authority to resolve the questions presented.

## IX-A. ARGUMENT V-A: 2005 PA 326 REINFORCES THE MANDATORY HEARING REQUIREMENT

### A. The Legislative Framework of 2005 PA 326

**Conclusion:** The Legislature's enactment of 2005 PA 326 (codified at MCL 722.26a et seq.) created a comprehensive statutory framework that independently prohibits ex parte custody modifications and reinforces the constitutional mandate of notice and hearing.

**Rule:** 2005 PA 326 amended Michigan's Child Custody Act to establish mandatory dispute resolution and hearing procedures. Under **MCL 722.26a(1)(b)**, absent the consent of both parties, a court must hold an evidentiary hearing before modifying a custody order. This statutory hearing requirement supplements the constitutional due process requirement and the court rule notice requirement of MCR 2.119(B), creating a three-layered protection against ex parte custody modification:

| Layer | Source | Requirement |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Constitutional | Fourteenth Amendment | Notice + opportunity to be heard |
| Court Rule | MCR 2.119(B) | 9 days' written notice before hearing |
| Statutory | MCL 722.26a(1)(b) (2005 PA 326) | Evidentiary hearing before modification |

**Explanation:** The legislative history of 2005 PA 326 reflects the Legislature's deliberate judgment that custody modifications require heightened procedural protections. The Act was enacted in response to concerns about inconsistent custody modification practices across Michigan courts — the very concern this Complaint raises. By mandating evidentiary hearings, the Legislature intended to eliminate the practice of modifying custody based on ex parte representations, unsworn allegations, or one-sided presentations.

The hearing requirement of MCL 722.26a(1)(b) is not discretionary. The statute uses the mandatory "shall" — the court "shall not" modify custody absent compliance with the prescribed procedures. *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92-93 (2010) (this Court enforcing the mandatory nature of the custody modification threshold under MCL 722.27(1)(c), which 2005 PA 326 reinforced).

**Application:** The trial court's twenty-four ex parte orders directly violated the hearing requirement of MCL 722.26a(1)(b). Each ex parte modification was entered without the evidentiary hearing the statute mandates. If this Court permits circuit courts to circumvent the 2005 PA 326 hearing requirement through ex parte action, the Legislature's carefully constructed procedural framework is rendered meaningless.

This Court should declare that the hearing requirement of 2005 PA 326 applies with full force to all custody modifications, and that ex parte orders may not be used to circumvent these legislatively mandated protections.

## WHY THIS COURT SHOULD ACT

This Complaint for Declaratory Judgment does not ask this Court merely to reverse one judge's errors. It asks this Court to state clearly, for all Michigan courts, what process is due before a parent's custody may be modified.

The constitutional supervisory authority vested in this Court by Const 1963, art 6, § 4 carries with it a corresponding obligation: when the judicial system produces outcomes that are inconsistent with constitutional commands, this Court must act to restore constitutional fidelity. *Advisory Opinion re Constitutionality of 2005 PA 71*, 479 Mich 1, 11-12 (2007).

**This case presents a question of statewide significance.** If one circuit court may routinely modify custody through ex parte orders — at a rate nine times the judicial average — without consequence, then the due process protections of the Fourteenth Amendment, MCR 2.119(B), and MCL 722.27(1)(c) are unenforceable. A right without a remedy is no right at all. This Court's declaratory judgment will establish the constitutional floor below which no Michigan court may fall.

**The constitutional challenge is ripe.** Plaintiff does not seek an advisory opinion. He has suffered concrete, measurable harm — 200+ days of total parental severance, 59+ days of incarceration, $82,250+ in economic damages — as the direct result of the practice he challenges. The controversy could not be more actual.

**Declaratory relief serves the public interest.** Every parent in Michigan has an interest in knowing that custody cannot be modified without notice and hearing. Every child has an interest in a judicial system that protects the parent-child bond rather than destroying it through procedural shortcuts. And every court has an interest in clear guidance from this Court on the constitutional requirements for custody modification.

**Injunctive relief is necessary to prevent future harm.** Declaratory judgment alone does not prevent the trial court from continuing its practice of ex parte custody modification. Prospective injunctive relief — requiring fourteen days' notice before substantive custody motions and prohibiting ex parte modifications except in genuine emergencies — is necessary to protect Plaintiff's rights going forward and to ensure compliance with this Court's declaration.

The Fourteenth Amendment demands no less. This Court should act.

## X. ARGUMENT VI: INJUNCTIVE RELIEF IS NECESSARY AND APPROPRIATE

### A. Standard for Injunctive Relief

**Conclusion:** This Court should enter a prospective injunction requiring fourteen days' notice before any substantive custody motion and prohibiting ex parte custody modifications except in genuine emergencies.

**Rule:** MCR 2.605(E) provides that courts may grant "supplementary relief based on a declaratory judgment." The standard for injunctive relief requires: (1) irreparable injury; (2) inadequacy of legal remedies; (3) favorable balance of hardships; and (4) public interest. *eBay Inc. v. MercExchange*, 547 U.S. 388, 391 (2006).

**Application:**

1. **Irreparable Injury:** 200+ days of lost parent-child contact. Categorically irreparable.

2. **Inadequacy of Legal Remedies:** No damages can restore lost developmental time. Declaratory relief alone does not prevent future violations.

3. **Balance of Hardships:** Requiring fourteen days' notice before custody motions imposes no hardship on any party — it merely enforces procedural norms already reflected in MCR 2.119(B). The hardship to Plaintiff of no injunction is continued risk of ex parte deprivation.

4. **Public Interest:** Strong public interest in due process compliance in custody proceedings. The injunction protects all parents appearing before the 14th Circuit Court.

**Conclusion:** The factors overwhelmingly favor injunctive relief. This Court should enter a prospective injunction requiring fourteen days' notice for all substantive custody motions and prohibiting ex parte custody modifications except as narrow, temporary emergency measures.

## XI. CONCLUSION

The questions presented by this Complaint are simple in principle but profound in consequence: **What process is due before a court may take a child from a parent?** The answer, from the United States Supreme Court, from the Michigan Constitution, from the Michigan Court Rules, and from the Michigan Legislature, is uniform: **notice and an opportunity to be heard.**

The trial court's practice — twenty-four ex parte orders, 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate, progressive elimination of parenting time without hearing — represents a systematic departure from these foundational requirements. The consequence for Plaintiff is 200+ days without contact with his child. The consequence for the legal system is the erosion of the procedural safeguards that protect every parent in Michigan.

This Court has the authority and the responsibility to state clearly: custody may not be modified ex parte. Parents must receive notice. Parents must be heard. Courts must follow MCL 722.27(1)(c), MCR 2.119(B), and the evidentiary hearing mandate of 2005 PA 326 (MCL 722.26a(1)(b)). The Fourteenth Amendment demands no less.
