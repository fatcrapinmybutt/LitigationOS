# STATEMENT OF JURISDICTION

This Court has jurisdiction pursuant to Mich Const 1963, art 6, § 4, which grants the Michigan Supreme Court "general superintending control over all courts" and authorizes the Court to "issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs." This provision is self-executing and plenary. *Judges of 74th Judicial Dist v Bay County*, 385 Mich 710, 728 (1971).

This Application is filed under **MCR 7.305(B)**, which authorizes leave to appeal where:

> (1) the issue involves a legal principle of major significance to the state's jurisprudence;
> (2) the issue has significant public interest;
> (3) the lower court's decision is clearly erroneous and will cause material injustice; or
> (4) the decision conflicts with a Supreme Court decision.

This Application further invokes **MCR 7.305(H)(1)**, which authorizes this Court, in its discretion, to treat an application for leave to appeal as an original action:

> *"In lieu of granting leave to appeal, the Court may ... treat the application as an original action and grant such relief as may be appropriate."*

The extraordinary circumstances warranting original-action treatment are set forth in the Argument below: three judges from the same former law firm control the disposition of this case, precluding any adequate remedy at the circuit or intermediate appellate level.

Alternatively, this Court has jurisdiction under **MCR 7.306** to entertain complaints for superintending control. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993) (three-part test for superintending control).

The Court of Appeals appeal (Docket No. 366810) remains pending but **cannot provide an adequate remedy** because: (a) the structural conflict of interest tainting the 14th Circuit cannot be cured on remand to the same circuit; and (b) irreparable harm — complete severance of the father-child relationship for 230+ days and counting — compounds daily.

# ARGUMENT

## I. THIS COURT SHOULD EXERCISE SUPERINTENDING CONTROL BECAUSE THE 14TH CIRCUIT IS STRUCTURALLY COMPROMISED BY A THREE-JUDGE CONFLICT NEXUS

### A. Standard: The Three-Part Test for Superintending Control

This Court exercises superintending control under Mich Const 1963, art 6, § 4 when three conditions are met:

> (1) The lower court has failed to perform a clear legal duty or has exceeded its jurisdiction;
> (2) No other adequate legal remedy exists; and
> (3) The case involves a question of significant public interest or will result in material injustice if not corrected.

*In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993); *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351 (1975). Review is de novo. *Id.*

### B. Rule: Structural Bias Requires Recusal and Cannot Be Self-Corrected

The Due Process Clause requires recusal where "the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable." *Caperton v A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 US 868, 884 (2009) (holding due process required recusal of justice who received $3 million in campaign contributions from party). The Caperton principle applies with even greater force here — the judge's husband is *related to the opposing party's household member*.

Michigan's disqualification standard is codified at MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b): a judge is disqualified where the judge's spouse "is related within the third degree" to a party or person with a substantial interest in the proceeding. This is not a discretionary standard — it is **mandatory**. Canon 3(C) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct independently requires disqualification where "the judge's impartiality might reasonably be questioned."

### C. Application: The Three-Judge Nexus Satisfies All Three Prongs

**Prong 1 — Failure to perform a clear legal duty.** Judge McNeill had a mandatory duty to disclose the Berry familial relationship and to recuse under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b). She did neither. Instead, she imposed a $250 bond on Petitioner's disqualification motion — converting a mandatory duty of recusal into a revenue-gated privilege. This is a clear failure to perform a non-discretionary legal duty.

**Prong 2 — No adequate remedy exists.** This is the critical prong. Normally, a denied disqualification motion can be appealed to the Chief Judge and then to the appellate courts. But here:

- The **Chief Judge** (Hoopes) is McNeill's former law partner and cannot impartially review her conduct.
- The **Court of Appeals** can reverse orders but cannot reassign the case outside the 14th Circuit on remand — the case returns to the same tainted pool.
- The **other judges** in the 14th Circuit would need to be assigned by Hoopes, who has a collegial conflict.

The entire remedial chain is broken. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich at 443 ("superintending control is warranted when no adequate legal remedy exists in the lower courts"). This is precisely the structural scenario *Hatcher* contemplated.

**Prong 3 — Significant public interest and material injustice.** A three-year-old child has been separated from a fit parent for over 230 days based on proceedings presided over by a judge with an undisclosed familial connection to the opposing party's household. The public interest in fair and impartial tribunals — the foundation of the judicial system — could not be higher. *Tumey v Ohio*, 273 US 510, 532 (1927) ("Every procedure which would offer a possible temptation to the average man as a judge ... not to hold the balance nice, clear and true between the state and the accused, denies the latter due process of law.").

### D. The Caperton Framework Compels MSC Intervention

*Caperton* established that the Due Process Clause imposes an independent, constitutional floor for judicial disqualification that cannot be satisfied by state procedural mechanisms where those mechanisms are themselves compromised. 556 US at 883-884.

Here, Michigan's MCR 2.003 procedure has failed because:

1. McNeill did not self-disclose (Canon 3(C) violation);
2. McNeill imposed a financial barrier to the disqualification motion ($250 bond);
3. The Chief Judge who reviews denied disqualification motions (Hoopes) is McNeill's former partner; and
4. No neutral judicial authority exists within the 14th Circuit.

The constitutional minimum of an impartial tribunal requires this Court to remove the case from the 14th Circuit entirely.

### E. Transfer to a Neutral Circuit Is the Only Adequate Remedy

This Court should order transfer to a circuit outside Muskegon County — one with no professional or familial connection to any member of the Ladas-Hoopes-McNeill firm. This is consistent with this Court's inherent authority to "do all things reasonably necessary for proper administration of justice." MCL 600.1701.

## II. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED FUNDAMENTAL DUE PROCESS THROUGH SYSTEMATIC EX PARTE ADJUDICATION

### A. Standard: The Mathews v Eldridge Framework

Due process requires, at minimum: (1) notice; (2) an opportunity to be heard; and (3) a neutral decisionmaker. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 335 (1976). The *Mathews* balancing test weighs: (a) the private interest affected; (b) the risk of erroneous deprivation through current procedures and the value of additional safeguards; and (c) the government's interest.

### B. Rule: Ex Parte Orders in Custody Are Strictly Limited

Michigan law strictly limits ex parte custody orders. MCR 3.207(B) permits ex parte custody orders **only** upon a showing that "irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice" or that "notice itself will precipitate adverse action before an order can be issued." Even then, a hearing must follow promptly.

### C. Application: Systematic Ex Parte Adjudication

The August 8, 2025 order exemplifies the due process breakdown:

1. **No notice.** Petitioner received no advance notice that Watson or any other party had submitted materials to the court.
2. **No hearing.** The order suspending all parenting time was entered the same day as the ex parte submission. No evidentiary hearing occurred.
3. **No finding of imminent harm.** The order contains no finding that L.D.W. was in imminent danger — the only basis on which an ex parte custody order can issue under MCR 3.207(B).
4. **No opportunity to respond to the USB evidence.** A USB drive was submitted to the court outside Petitioner's presence. Its contents were never disclosed to Petitioner before the court relied on it to sever his parental contact.

This was not an isolated event. The docket reflects **181 ex parte events** out of 683 total entries — a rate that bespeaks systematic unilateral adjudication rather than the adversarial process due process demands.

### D. The Mathews Balancing Test Favors Petitioner

Under *Mathews*:

**(a) Private interest:** The parent-child relationship is "far more precious than any property right." *Santosky*, 455 US at 758-59. Petitioner's interest is at its constitutional apex.

**(b) Risk of erroneous deprivation:** The risk is not theoretical — it materialized. A fit father with 438 days of successful shared custody was stripped of all contact based on ex parte submissions he never saw, containing allegations (arsenic, sexual assault, drug use) that are rebutted by police reports and medical records. The value of additional safeguards — a hearing, notice, the opportunity to confront evidence — would have prevented this erroneous deprivation.

**(c) Government interest:** The state has no interest in depriving a child of a fit parent's care. The state's interest in child welfare is served by maintaining, not severing, the parent-child bond absent proven harm.

## III. THE TRIAL COURT VIOLATED CONSTITUTIONALLY PROTECTED PARENTAL RIGHTS WITHOUT ANY FINDING OF HARM OR UNFITNESS

### A. Standard: Parental Rights Are Fundamental

The "liberty interest ... of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). Before the state may sever this bond, it must meet a heightened burden:

- *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 747-48 (1982): at minimum, **clear and convincing evidence** before termination of parental rights.
- *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 658 (1972): a parent is entitled to a **fitness hearing** before being deprived of custody.
- *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 55 (1992): Michigan recognizes the "fundamental nature of a parent's right to direct the care, custody, and upbringing of his or her children."

### B. Rule: MCL 722.23 Best Interest Analysis Is Mandatory

Before modifying custody or parenting time, a Michigan court **must** conduct a best interest analysis under MCL 722.23, evaluating all twelve statutory factors. *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994). This is not discretionary. A change of custody without a best interest analysis is reversible error per se. *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509-10 (2003) (requiring "proper cause" or "change of circumstances" before revisiting custody, followed by full best interest analysis).

Additionally, MCL 722.23(j) specifically requires the court to evaluate each parent's **"willingness and ability ... to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent."** The record shows that Watson — not Petitioner — has systematically obstructed the parent-child relationship, yet it is Petitioner who has been punished.

### C. Application: No Finding, No Analysis, No Due Process

Judge McNeill's orders reveal a total absence of the constitutionally and statutorily required analysis:

1. **No finding of harm, abuse, or unfitness — ever.** In over two years of proceedings, no court has found that Petitioner harmed, abused, or neglected L.D.W. Not once.

2. **No best interest analysis — ever.** No order in the record evaluates the twelve MCL 722.23 factors. The August 8, 2025 ex parte order contains no analysis at all — it simply suspends parenting time.

3. **Favorable evaluation rejected.** HealthWest independently evaluated Petitioner and the evaluation was favorable. Judge McNeill rejected this professional clinical assessment without explanation and without a competing assessment.

4. **Three evaluations demanded without basis.** The court ordered Petitioner to undergo three separate mental health evaluations and drug testing despite zero evidence of substance abuse or mental illness. This is not judicial caution — it is a coercive credentialing gauntlet designed to exhaust an indigent litigant's resources.

5. **438 days of proven success disregarded.** The 438-day period of successful joint custody is powerful evidence that the arrangement worked and that Petitioner is a competent parent. The court never explained why this extended successful track record was insufficient.

Under *Santosky*, the state may not sever parental bonds without at least clear and convincing evidence. Under *Stanley*, it may not do so without a fitness hearing. Under *Fletcher*, it may not modify custody without a best interest analysis. The trial court did none of these things.

### D. Each Day Is Irreparable Harm

L.D.W. was born November 9, 2022. He is approximately three years old. The developmental psychology literature — and common sense — establish that the ages of two through four are a critical period for attachment formation. Each day of complete separation during this window inflicts developmental harm that cannot be undone by a later appellate reversal.

*In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014), recognizes that "a child's need for a normal family home and ... a permanent, stable environment" is a fundamental interest. L.D.W.'s right to a relationship with his father — a father who has never been found unfit — is being extinguished in real time.

MCL 552.644 provides that the court "**shall**" enforce parenting time. This is mandatory language. *Id.* Yet parenting time has not only gone unenforced in Petitioner's favor — it has been eliminated entirely without legal basis.
