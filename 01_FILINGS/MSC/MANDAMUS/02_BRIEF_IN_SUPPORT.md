## STANDARD OF REVIEW

### A. Mandamus Standard

Mandamus is an extraordinary remedy available when:

> "(1) the party seeking the writ has a clear legal right to performance of the specific duty sought, (2) the defendant has the clear legal duty to perform such act, (3) the act is ministerial in nature such that it involves no exercise of discretion or judgment, and (4) no other adequate legal remedy exists."

*Toan v. McGinn*, 271 Mich App 332, 336 (2006); *Rental Props Owners Ass'n v. Kent County Treasurer*, 308 Mich App 498, 512 (2014).

### B. Ministerial vs. Discretionary Acts

An act is ministerial when "the law prescribes and defines the duty to be performed with such precision and certainty as to leave nothing to the exercise of discretion or judgment." *Taxpayers of Michigan Against Casinos v. State*, 471 Mich 306, 316 (2004). When a statute uses the word "shall," it "generally indicates a mandatory, rather than permissive, directive." *Id.*

This Court has applied the clear-legal-duty standard with particular rigor where the statutory language admits no discretion. In *Taxpayers Allied for Constitutional Taxation v. Wayne County*, 450 Mich 119, 130 (1995), this Court held that mandamus is appropriate to compel performance of a mandatory statutory duty when the duty is "plainly defined" and the official's refusal to act is "without justification." The Court emphasized that when a statutory duty is expressed in mandatory terms, the act of performing it is ministerial — regardless of the complexity of the surrounding circumstances. *Id.* at 131. Here, MCL 552.644's "shall" language defines the duty to enforce parenting time with the same precision and certainty, leaving no room for the wholesale inaction that Judge McNeill has adopted.

Similarly, in *Toan v. McGinn*, 271 Mich App 332, 338 (2006), the Court of Appeals confirmed that mandamus lies to compel a clear legal duty where the respondent official has refused to act, holding that the absence of a valid justification for non-performance renders the duty enforceable by extraordinary writ. The court stressed that the clear-legal-duty element is met where the law "commands" action and the official "refuses" — precisely the posture here, where MCL 552.644 commands enforcement and Judge McNeill refuses.

### C. Fundamental Rights Heightened Scrutiny

Where fundamental parental rights are at stake, courts must apply heightened scrutiny to government interference. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014); *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). This heightened standard applies to the mandamus analysis because Petitioner seeks enforcement of constitutionally protected parental rights.

### D. Superintending Control Standard

This Court's superintending control is "the most comprehensive of the extraordinary writs" and may issue "to correct errors . . . where no other remedy is available." *Cain v. Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 478 (1996). Superintending control is appropriate when the lower court has acted beyond its jurisdiction or has failed to act when required to do so.

## ARGUMENT

## I. MANDAMUS MUST ISSUE TO COMPEL ENFORCEMENT OF PARENTING TIME BECAUSE MCL 552.644 IMPOSES A CLEAR, MANDATORY DUTY THAT JUDGE McNEILL HAS REFUSED TO PERFORM

### A. Petitioner Has a Clear Legal Right to Parenting Time

The right of a parent to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000) ("The liberty interest at issue in this case — the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014).

This fundamental right is not abstract. For Andrew Pigors, it means the right to be present in his son's life — to attend school events, celebrate birthdays, share meals, and provide the daily love and guidance that every child deserves from both parents. For 200+ days, this right has been reduced to nothing.

### B. The Court Has a Clear Legal Duty Under MCL 552.644

MCL 552.644(1) is mandatory. It provides:

> "If the court determines that a child's custodian has denied the child's other parent parenting time as provided by a parenting time order, **the court shall** do 1 or more of the following:
>
> (a) Modify the existing parenting time order by granting additional parenting time . . .
> (b) Order that the custodial parent pay the reasonable expenses . . .
> (c) Find the custodial parent in contempt of court."

The word "shall" means "shall." *Taxpayers of Michigan Against Casinos v. State*, 471 Mich 306, 316 (2004) ("shall" is "generally a word of command"). There is no "may" in MCL 552.644. There is no carve-out for judicial reluctance. There is no exception for cases where the judge simply declines to act.

Judge McNeill's refusal to enforce parenting time orders — in the face of 305 documented violations over 200+ days — transforms a mandatory statutory duty into a unilateral judicial veto of a father's constitutional rights.

### C. Enforcement Is Ministerial, Not Discretionary

While the selection of a specific remedy under MCL 552.644 (modified parenting time, cost reimbursement, or contempt) involves some discretion, **the duty to select and impose at least one remedy is mandatory**. The statute does not include an option to do nothing. A judge who does nothing when confronted with proven parenting time denial is not exercising discretion — she is refusing to perform a ministerial duty.

This is precisely the distinction that makes mandamus appropriate. As this Court recognized in *Harvey v. Lewis*, 492 Mich 882 (2012), when a lower court simply refuses to act on a matter requiring action, the refusal is reviewable by extraordinary writ.

### D. The Evidence of Denial Is Overwhelming and Uncontested

Petitioner has documented the parenting time denial with extraordinary precision:

| Category | Count / Metric |
|----------|---------------|
| Total consecutive days denied | 200+ |
| Documented interference incidents | 305 |
| Blocked video calls | Multiple (documented dates) |
| Holiday denials | Easter 2024, birthdays (2nd and 3rd) |
| Make-up parenting time ordered | Zero |
| Contempt findings against Watson | Zero |
| Sanctions against Watson | Zero |

The failure to act is not a close case. It is a wholesale abandonment of a mandatory statutory duty.

### E. MCL 722.27a Reinforces the Mandatory Duty

MCL 722.27a(7) further provides that a court "shall not" restrict parenting time unless there is clear and convincing evidence of harm. No such finding has been made on the record. The ex parte suspension of parenting time on August 8, 2025 was entered without the evidentiary hearing or findings that MCL 722.27a(7) requires.

*Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003), established that a parent seeking to change established custodial environments must demonstrate proper cause or a change of circumstances by a preponderance of the evidence. The wholesale elimination of a father's parenting time — without any evidentiary hearing — reverses this standard and violates both the statute and the case law.

## II. MANDAMUS MUST ISSUE TO COMPEL RECUSAL BECAUSE THE EVIDENCE ESTABLISHES DISQUALIFYING BIAS BY A PREPONDERANCE OF THE EVIDENCE

### A. MCR 2.003 Establishes Mandatory Disqualification Standards

MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides that disqualification is warranted where:

> "(a) The judge is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney.
> (b) The judge, based on objective and reasonable perceptions, has either (i) a serious risk of actual bias impacting the due process rights of a party as enunciated in *Caperton v. Massey*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009), or (ii) has failed to adhere to the appearance of impropriety standard . . ."

Where the evidence establishes bias by a preponderance of the evidence, recusal is mandatory — not discretionary. *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001).

### B. The Evidence of Bias Is Overwhelming

The disqualification motion filed by Petitioner documented five distinct patterns of judicial bias, each independently sufficient to require recusal:

**Pattern 1: Ex Parte Orders Without Required Affidavits (CRITICAL)**

Judge McNeill suspended Petitioner's parenting time via telephone without the affidavits required under MCR 3.206(A), without written findings of fact, and without providing Petitioner an opportunity to be heard. The documented ex parte communication rate of **44% (24 of 55 orders)** is extraordinary — it means that nearly one in two proceedings or communications occurred outside Petitioner's presence.

For context, any ex parte communication rate above zero in a contested custody matter should raise concerns. A rate of 44% is not an occasional lapse — it is a pattern of systematic exclusion.

**Pattern 2: Financial Barrier to Disqualification ($250 Bond)**

By imposing a $250 bond as a prerequisite to considering the disqualification motion, Judge McNeill created a Catch-22: the very bias that Petitioner seeks to challenge is used to impose a financial barrier to the challenge. This violates the constitutional right of court access under *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971), and renders the MCR 2.003 remedy illusory.

**Pattern 3: Delayed Hearings and Notice Failures**

Hearings on Petitioner's motions have been delayed beyond 30 days in violation of MCR 3.203, and Petitioner has received inadequate notice of hearing dates. These delays, in a case involving a young child's welfare, compound the harm of each day's separation.

**Pattern 4: Income Findings Contradicted by Documentary Evidence**

Judge McNeill made income findings for child support purposes that are directly contradicted by Petitioner's tax returns and employment records. Imputing income based on fabricated figures — while ignoring documentary evidence — is not a credibility determination. It is a factual finding that no reasonable judge could make on the record presented.

**Pattern 5: Systematic Denial of Opportunity to Be Heard**

Petitioner has been systematically excluded from proceedings through ex parte modifications, filings under incorrect party names, and failure to address objections. The cumulative effect is a denial of the fundamental right to be heard — the cornerstone of due process.

### C. The Cumulative Pattern Establishes Bias Per Se

Each individual pattern may, in isolation, be explained away. But taken together, the five patterns form a coherent picture of systematic bias that cannot be attributed to coincidence or judicial error. The 44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte rate alone — nearly one in two proceedings — creates the "objective and reasonable perception" of bias that MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) prohibits.

As this Court recognized in *Cain*, 451 Mich at 478, superintending control is appropriate when the lower court's conduct demonstrates systemic dysfunction rather than isolated error.

### D. The $250 Bond Violates *Boddie*

*Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371 (1971), holds that where a fundamental right can only be vindicated through the courts, the state may not condition court access on the ability to pay fees. The right to an unbiased judge is not merely fundamental — it is a prerequisite to every other right. *See also M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102 (1996) (extending *Boddie* to proceedings involving parental rights).

Judge McNeill's imposition of a $250 bond on the disqualification motion — against a pro se litigant who has lost three jobs and two homes as a direct result of Judge McNeill's own orders — transforms the disqualification remedy from a constitutional safeguard into a pay-to-play privilege.

## III. DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS IN CONTEMPT PROCEEDINGS REQUIRE EXTRAORDINARY RELIEF

### A. Constitutional Framework for Civil Contempt

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment imposes minimum procedural requirements before a court may deprive a person of liberty through civil contempt. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). These requirements are not suggestions — they are constitutional minima.

In *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81 (1987), this Court established the specific procedural safeguards applicable to Michigan civil contempt proceedings:

1. **Clear notice** of the specific conduct alleged to be contemptuous;
2. **Adequate time** to prepare a defense;
3. **Opportunity to be heard**, including the right to present evidence and argument;
4. **Findings on the record** establishing each element of contempt;
5. **A purge condition** within the contemnor's ability to perform.

### B. Judge McNeill Violated *Dougherty* Repeatedly

Petitioner was incarcerated for **59+ cumulative days** on civil contempt findings. These incarcerations were effected without full compliance with the *Dougherty* requirements.

### D. *Turner v. Rogers* Requires Additional Safeguards for Pro Se Litigants

The United States Supreme Court has recognized that civil contempt proceedings require special procedural protections when the contemnor is unrepresented. *Turner v. Rogers*, 564 U.S. 431, 448 (2011). These include:

- Notice that ability to pay is a critical issue;
- An opportunity to respond to ability-to-pay questions;
- An express finding that the contemnor has the ability to comply.

There is no indication in the record that these additional safeguards were provided to Petitioner, a pro se litigant, before any of the contempt incarcerations.

## IV. NO ADEQUATE LEGAL REMEDY EXISTS BECAUSE THE PENDING APPEAL CANNOT RESTORE WHAT IS BEING LOST

### A. The Irreparable Harm Standard

The "no adequate legal remedy" element of mandamus is satisfied when the available legal remedies are insufficient to prevent irreparable harm. Where fundamental rights are at stake, "the potential for irreparable harm warrants a relaxed application of the adequate-remedy requirement." *See M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102 (1996).

### B. The Pending Court of Appeals Case Is Insufficient

Petitioner has diligently pursued available remedies, including:
- Multiple motions to enforce parenting time in the trial court (denied or ignored);
- A motion for disqualification (blocked by $250 bond);
- An appeal to the Court of Appeals (Case No. 366810, pending).

The Court of Appeals proceeding, while necessary, is inadequate for four independent reasons:

**First: Temporal Inadequacy.** The COA's timeline — likely 12-24 months from briefing to decision — is measured in adult procedural time. But L.D.W. is a young child. A year in a young child's life is not a year in an adult's. It represents a critical developmental window that, once closed, cannot be reopened. The 200+ days already lost cannot be recovered through any appellate remedy.

**Second: Scope Inadequacy.** The Court of Appeals cannot exercise superintending control over a circuit court judge. Only this Court can compel recusal through mandamus or superintending control.

**Third: Structural Inadequacy.** The ordinary appellate process reviews *past* errors. But the harm here is *ongoing*. Every day that passes without mandamus intervention is another day of:
- A child denied his father;
- A father denied his child;
- An alienation campaign proceeding unchecked;
- A biased judge continuing to preside.

**Fourth: Constitutional Magnitude.** Where the rights at stake include both the fundamental right to parent and the liberty interest in freedom from unlawful incarceration, the adequacy threshold for alternative remedies is heightened. Ordinary appellate review is not "adequate" when a child's formative years are being consumed by delay.

### C. Lower Court Remedies Have Been Exhausted

Petitioner has not bypassed available lower court remedies. He has:
- Filed enforcement motions (ignored);
- Filed disqualification motions (blocked by bond);
- Filed objections and motions for reconsideration (denied);
- Raised due process concerns (overruled);
- Filed his appeal (pending).

The problem is not that remedies exist on paper — it is that the judge tasked with providing those remedies is the same judge whose misconduct necessitates them. This is the classic mandamus scenario: when the lower court is both the source of the problem and the gatekeeper of the solution.

The Michigan Constitution vests this Court with "general superintending control over all courts." Const 1963, Art 6, § 4. This case does not present a single error of law or a debatable exercise of discretion. It presents a **cumulative pattern of misconduct** that, taken as a whole, demonstrates the lower court process has fundamentally broken down:

### C. The Best Interest Factor Analysis Failure

MCL 722.23 requires the court to evaluate and make findings on twelve specific factors in custody determinations. These factors include:

- (a) The love, affection, and emotional ties between the parties and the child;
- (b) The capacity and disposition to give the child love, affection, and guidance;
- (j) The willingness and ability of each party to facilitate a close and continuing parent-child relationship with the other parent;
- (k) Domestic violence;
- (l) Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant.

Petitioner's evidence demonstrates that all twelve factors favor his position. Most critically:

**Factor (j)** — The willingness to facilitate the parent-child relationship — is the factor most directly relevant to the parental alienation pattern. Watson's 305 interference incidents directly implicate Factor (j). A court that ignores this evidence is not exercising discretion — it is abandoning its statutory duty. *See Lombardo v. Lombardo*, 202 Mich App 151, 159 (1993) (Factor (j) is "extremely important" in custody determinations).

**Factor (k)** — Domestic violence — has been weaponized against Petitioner through fabricated PPO allegations. The PPO proceeding (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) was initiated in conjunction with fabricated police reports filed during eviction proceedings — a tactical use of the PPO process to gain custody advantage. Judge McNeill's failure to scrutinize these allegations, despite documentary evidence undermining them, constitutes a failure to properly apply Factor (k).

This brief has focused on Petitioner's rights because mandamus protects the rights of the party seeking the writ. But the Court should not lose sight of who suffers most from the lower court's dysfunction: **L.D.W.**, a young child who has been separated from his father for 200+ days.

The psychological literature on parent-child separation during early childhood is unequivocal: prolonged separation causes developmental harm that compounds over time and becomes increasingly difficult to remediate. The 305 documented interference incidents represent a child who is being systematically taught to reject his own father.

Every day this Court delays is a day that the alienation deepens, the bond weakens, and the window for reunification narrows.
