## IV. ARGUMENT: WHY MANDAMUS IS PROPER

### A. Standard for Mandamus

Mandamus is an extraordinary remedy that issues when:

> "(1) the party seeking the writ has a clear legal right to performance of the specific duty sought, (2) the defendant has the clear legal duty to perform such act, (3) the act is ministerial in nature such that it involves no exercise of discretion or judgment, and (4) no other adequate legal remedy exists." *Toan v. McGinn*, 271 Mich App 291, 296 (2006); *Rental Props Owners Ass'n v. Kent County Treasurer*, 308 Mich App 498, 512 (2014).

Where a judge's duty is defined by mandatory statutory language — particularly the word "shall" — performance of that duty is ministerial and subject to mandamus. *Taxpayers of Michigan Against Casinos v. State*, 471 Mich 306, 316 (2004).

### B. Ground 1: Clear Legal Duty to Enforce Parenting Time

**Clear Legal Right:** Petitioner has a constitutionally protected fundamental right to the care, custody, and companionship of his child. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 409 (2014).

**Clear Legal Duty:** MCL 552.644(1) provides: "If a child's custodian . . . has denied the other parent parenting time . . . the court **shall** do 1 or more of the following: (a) Modify the existing parenting time order . . . (b) Order make-up parenting time . . . (c) Order that the custodial parent pay the costs of the complaint . . . (d) Issue an order of contempt."

The word "shall" imposes a mandatory duty. *Taxpayers of Michigan*, 471 Mich at 316.

**Ministerial Act:** Enforcement of parenting time under MCL 552.644 is ministerial — the statute prescribes mandatory remedial actions and leaves no discretion to simply refuse enforcement.

**No Adequate Remedy:** Petitioner has exhausted lower court remedies by filing multiple enforcement motions, all of which have been ignored. The pending COA appeal cannot restore 200+ lost days with his child.

### C. Ground 2: Clear Legal Duty to Recuse

**Clear Legal Duty:** MCR 2.003(C)(1) establishes mandatory disqualification standards. Where the evidentiary record demonstrates actual bias or the appearance of impropriety, recusal is not discretionary.

A **44% (24 of 55 orders) ex parte communication rate** — nearly one in two court interactions conducted outside Petitioner's presence — constitutes per se evidence of bias under MCR 2.003(C)(1)(b) and a violation of Canon 3(B)(7).

**Ministerial Act:** Once disqualifying conditions are established by a preponderance of the evidence, recusal is mandatory. *Armstrong v. Ypsilanti Charter Twp*, 248 Mich App 573, 597 (2001).

### D. Ground 3: Due Process Violations Require Extraordinary Relief

**Clear Legal Right:** Petitioner has a Fourteenth Amendment right to due process before deprivation of liberty. 59+ days of incarceration without adequate *Dougherty* safeguards violates this right.

**Clear Legal Duty:** *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 103 (1987), establishes mandatory procedural requirements for civil contempt proceedings. These are not guidelines — they are constitutional minima.

**Ministerial Act:** Compliance with constitutional due process requirements is non-discretionary.

### E. Ground 4: No Adequate Legal Remedy Exists

The pending Court of Appeals case (No. 366810) is insufficient because:

1. **Temporal inadequacy:** Every day of delay is an irretrievable day lost in a child's development. L.D.W. has already missed 200+ days with his father during formative developmental years.

2. **Scope inadequacy:** The COA cannot exercise superintending control to compel judicial recusal — that power belongs exclusively to this Court.

3. **Systemic inadequacy:** The pattern of misconduct demonstrated here — ex parte proceedings, refusal to enforce statutory mandates, denial of due process — represents a systemic breakdown of the lower court process that requires the extraordinary remedy this Court alone can provide.

4. **Constitutional magnitude:** Where fundamental parental rights and liberty interests are at stake, the ordinary appellate process does not constitute an "adequate legal remedy" sufficient to bar mandamus. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich at 409.

### F. Ground 5: Pattern of Misconduct Warrants Superintending Control

This Court's superintending control authority exists precisely for circumstances like these — where a lower court's cumulative pattern of misconduct demonstrates that the judicial process itself has broken down:

| Misconduct Category | Evidence |
|---------------------|----------|
| Ex parte proceedings | 44% (24 of 55 orders) communication rate |
| Parenting time denial | 200+ days, 305 interference incidents |
| Due process violations | 59+ days incarceration without *Dougherty* safeguards |
| Best interest factors | 12/12 factors ignored |
| Parental alienation tolerated | 305 interference incidents, no sanctions |
| Court access barriers | $250 bond on disqualification motion (*Boddie* violation) |

This is not a single error of discretion — it is a systematic deprivation of a litigant's fundamental rights by a judge who has demonstrated actual bias through her pattern of rulings.
