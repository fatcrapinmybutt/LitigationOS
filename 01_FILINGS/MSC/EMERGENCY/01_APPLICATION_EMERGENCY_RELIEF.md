## I. INTRODUCTION AND STATEMENT OF EMERGENCY

1. Plaintiff-Appellant Andrew J. Pigors ("Father") respectfully applies to this Honorable Court for emergency relief pursuant to MCR 7.311(D), requesting immediate intervention to prevent continuing and irreparable harm to his constitutionally protected parental rights and to the welfare of his minor child, L.D.W.

2. Father has been denied all parenting time with his son, L.D.W., for more than 200 consecutive days as of the filing of this Application. This extraordinary deprivation constitutes an ongoing, daily violation of the fundamental liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution. *See Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000) ("The liberty interest at issue in this case — the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court.").

3. This emergency arises from a pattern of ex parte orders issued by the Honorable Judge McNeill of the 14th Circuit Court, Muskegon County, which have progressively stripped Father of all custodial rights without constitutionally adequate notice and hearing. The trial court has issued twenty-four (24) ex parte orders affecting custody, parenting time, and support — an ex parte rate of 44% of all orders issued (24 of 55), a figure that vastly exceeds normal judicial practice.

4. The pendency of Father's appeal in the Court of Appeals (No. 366810) does not provide adequate or timely relief. The developmental harm to L.D.W. from prolonged parental deprivation compounds daily and cannot be remedied by eventual appellate reversal. Only this Court's immediate exercise of superintending control can halt the irreparable injury now occurring.

5. Father has exhausted or is pursuing all available lower-court remedies. The circuit court has demonstrated a pattern of issuing adverse orders without hearing; the Court of Appeals cannot provide the immediate emergency relief necessary to prevent further harm to the parent-child bond.

## IV. STANDARD FOR EMERGENCY RELIEF

28. Under MCR 7.311(D), this Court may grant emergency relief when: (1) the relief sought is not available by other adequate means, and (2) the need for relief is immediate. MCR 7.311(D)(1)-(2).

29. **Issue:** Whether Father's 200+ day denial of parenting time, resulting from a pattern of ex parte orders issued without constitutionally adequate process, constitutes an emergency warranting this Court's immediate intervention.

30. **Rule:** The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children" is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). Deprivation of this fundamental right without due process violates the Fourteenth Amendment. *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972) ("The rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed 'essential'... 'basic civil rights of man'... and 'rights far more precious... than property rights.'").

31. Michigan courts have recognized that emergency appellate relief is appropriate when "the harm alleged is real and imminent, rather than speculative." *Eldred v. Ziny*, 246 Mich. App. 142, 150 (2001). The Michigan Supreme Court's superintending control authority under Const 1963, art 6, § 4 extends to correcting systemic judicial practices that violate constitutional rights.

32. **Application:** Here, the harm is neither speculative nor remote. Father has been separated from his child for over 200 consecutive days. Child development research uniformly establishes that prolonged parental deprivation during formative years causes measurable psychological harm that becomes increasingly difficult to remediate with the passage of time. Every additional day of separation compounds the injury to both Father and L.D.W.

33. The lower courts cannot provide timely relief. The circuit court has demonstrated through its pattern of 24 ex parte orders that it will not voluntarily restore Father's rights. The Court of Appeals appeal (No. 366810) may take months or years to resolve. Only this Court's immediate intervention can halt the ongoing constitutional violation.

34. **Conclusion:** The standard for emergency relief under MCR 7.311(D) is satisfied. The harm is real, ongoing, and irreparable; no other adequate remedy is available on a timeline that can prevent further damage to L.D.W.'s developmental wellbeing and the parent-child bond.

## V. ARGUMENTS FOR EMERGENCY RELIEF

### A. Argument I: The 200+ Day Parenting Time Deprivation Constitutes Irreparable Harm to a Fundamental Constitutional Right

35. **Issue:** Whether the complete denial of parenting time for over 200 consecutive days constitutes irreparable harm to a fundamental constitutional right warranting emergency intervention.

36. **Rule:** The Fourteenth Amendment protects the fundamental liberty interest of parents in the "care, custody, and control of their children." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). This right is "far more precious... than property rights." *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972) (quoting *May v. Anderson*, 345 U.S. 528, 533 (1953)). The complete termination of a parent-child relationship, whether de jure or de facto, triggers the highest level of constitutional protection and demands the most rigorous procedural safeguards. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

37. Under Michigan law, MCL 722.27a establishes a strong presumption that parenting time serves the best interests of the child. Parenting time may be restricted only upon a showing of harm to the child, and even then, the trial court must fashion the least restrictive arrangement consistent with the child's welfare. MCL 722.27a(3)-(7).

38. **Application:** Father has been denied all parenting time for 200+ consecutive days. This is not a temporary suspension pending investigation; it is a de facto termination of the parent-child relationship accomplished through a series of ex parte orders. No court has found that Father's parenting time would harm L.D.W. No evidentiary hearing has been held at which Father could present evidence, cross-examine witnesses, or challenge the basis for the deprivation.

39. The developmental science is unambiguous: children require regular, meaningful contact with both parents for healthy psychosocial development. At L.D.W.'s age, 200+ days represents a significant portion of the child's conscious memory and developmental window. The longer the separation continues, the more difficult it becomes to restore the parent-child bond. This harm cannot be remedied by monetary damages or retroactive schedule adjustments — it is, by definition, irreparable.

40. **Conclusion:** The 200+ day deprivation of parenting time constitutes irreparable harm to Father's fundamental constitutional right and to L.D.W.'s developmental welfare, satisfying the emergency relief standard of MCR 7.311(D).

### B. Argument II: The Pattern of Ex Parte Orders Violates Procedural Due Process

41. **Issue:** Whether the trial court's issuance of 24 ex parte orders — representing 44% of all orders — violates the procedural due process requirements of the Fourteenth Amendment.

42. **Rule:** Procedural due process requires, at minimum, "notice and opportunity for hearing appropriate to the nature of the case." *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 US 306, 313 (1950). In custody proceedings specifically, the Supreme Court has held that a parent must receive notice and an opportunity to be heard before the State may deprive them of custody rights. *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 550 (1965) ("A fundamental requirement of due process is 'the opportunity to be heard.'... It is an opportunity which must be granted at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner.").

43. The *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976), requires courts to weigh: (1) the private interest affected; (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation through the procedures used, and the probable value of additional safeguards; and (3) the Government's interest, including the fiscal and administrative burdens of additional procedures.

44. Under Michigan court rules, MCR 2.119(B) requires that "[a] written motion... and any supporting brief or affidavits must be served and filed... at least 9 days before the hearing." Ex parte orders are reserved for narrow circumstances involving immediate, irreparable injury. MCR 2.119(B)(1).

45. **Application:** Applying the *Mathews* factors:

    a. **Private Interest:** The private interest at stake — the parent-child relationship — is "far more precious than property rights" and constitutes one of the most fundamental liberty interests recognized in American law. *Stanley*, 405 U.S. at 651. This factor weighs overwhelmingly in favor of rigorous procedural protections.

    b. **Risk of Erroneous Deprivation:** The risk of error when a court modifies custody without hearing from the affected parent is extraordinarily high. The trial court heard only one side — Defendant Watson's — before entering orders that progressively eliminated Father's parenting time. The 24 ex parte orders demonstrate that the existing procedures (or lack thereof) are systematically producing outcomes untested by adversarial presentation. The probable value of requiring notice and hearing before custody modifications is self-evident: it is the foundational mechanism by which courts distinguish truth from allegation.

    c. **Government Interest:** The government has no legitimate interest in denying a parent notice and hearing before custody modification. The administrative burden of providing nine days' notice under MCR 2.119(B) is negligible. Indeed, compliance with existing court rules would satisfy due process at no additional cost.

46. The trial court's 44% ex parte rate — compared to a normal judicial average of less than 5% — demonstrates not isolated emergency action but a systematic practice of deciding contested custody matters without affording Father the opportunity to be heard. This practice violates the Fourteenth Amendment.

47. **Conclusion:** The pattern of 24 ex parte orders, issued without notice or hearing on substantive custody matters, violates the procedural due process protections of the Fourteenth Amendment and MCR 2.119(B). This Court should exercise its superintending control to halt this practice.

### C. Argument III: L.D.W. Suffers Ongoing Developmental Harm

48. **Issue:** Whether the ongoing separation of L.D.W. from Father for 200+ consecutive days causes developmental harm requiring emergency intervention in the child's best interest.

49. **Rule:** Michigan's Child Custody Act, MCL 722.23, establishes twelve best interest factors that courts must consider in custody determinations. Among these, factor (j) — "the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent" — is directly relevant. MCL 722.23(j). Factor (a) — "the love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child" — requires consideration of the bond between L.D.W. and Father. MCL 722.23(a).

50. This Court has recognized that children have an independent interest in maintaining relationships with both parents. *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich. App. 499, 512 (2003) (establishing framework for parenting time disputes).

51. **Application:** No court has made any finding under MCL 722.23 that Father's relationship with L.D.W. is harmful or contrary to the child's best interests. The 200+ day separation was not ordered based on an evidentiary hearing evaluating best interest factors; it evolved through a series of ex parte orders entered without Father's participation.

52. The passage of 200+ consecutive days without contact between Father and L.D.W. has predictable developmental consequences: deterioration of the parent-child bond, confusion about parental attachment, and potential for parental alienation — a phenomenon supported by the 305 documented interference incidents by Defendant Watson. 53. **Conclusion:** L.D.W.'s best interests require emergency restoration of at least minimum parenting time with Father. Every additional day of complete separation deepens the developmental harm and makes eventual restoration of the parent-child bond more difficult.

### D. Argument IV: The Trial Court's Conduct Warrants Disqualification and Reassignment

55. **Rule:** MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification of a judge when the judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." Under *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich. 347, 351 (1975), disqualification is warranted when "the judge's conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception of bias." This Court's superintending control authority under Const 1963, art 6, § 4 includes the power to direct reassignment of cases.

56. **Application:** The record demonstrates the following pattern:

    a. Twenty-four (24) ex parte orders — 44% of all orders — entered without notice to Father, substantively modifying custody, parenting time, and support;

    b. Systematic overriding of FOC recommendations without stated basis in the record;

    c. Incarceration of Father for contempt for a total of 59+ days, resulting in the loss of three jobs and creating a cycle of economic devastation that makes compliance with support orders increasingly impossible;

    d. Imposition of $1,056.60/month support without proper hearing;

    e. [ANDREW_REQUIRED: Any specific instances of demonstrated bias or prejudice, with dates and descriptions].

57. A reasonable person, viewing this pattern in its totality, would conclude that the trial court's procedures and outcomes reflect a systematic predisposition against Father. The frequency of ex parte orders alone — at more than nine times the normal judicial average — creates a perception of bias that undermines public confidence in the judiciary.

58. **Conclusion:** Judge McNeill should be disqualified and a substitute judge assigned for all further proceedings.

### E. Argument V: Federal Constitutional Rights Compel This Court's Intervention

59. **Issue:** Whether the cumulative violations of Father's federal constitutional rights — including substantive due process, procedural due process, and equal protection — compel this Court to exercise its superintending control authority.

60. **Rule:** The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that no State shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1. This guarantee encompasses both procedural protections (*Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976)) and the substantive liberty interest in parenting (*Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65). The Supreme Court has held that a parent's right to the "companionship, care, custody, and management" of children is an interest "far more precious than any property right." *Stanley*, 405 U.S. at 651.

61. The Michigan Supreme Court has an independent obligation to ensure that state courts do not violate federal constitutional guarantees. Const 1963, art 6, § 4 grants this Court "general superintending control over all courts," which includes the authority and duty to correct lower-court practices that systematically deprive litigants of constitutional rights.

62. **Application:** The cumulative effect of the trial court's actions constitutes a multi-faceted constitutional violation:

    a. **Substantive Due Process:** The de facto termination of Father's parent-child relationship — accomplished through progressive ex parte orders rather than through the termination procedures prescribed by law — infringes the substantive liberty interest recognized in *Troxel* and *Stanley*. The State may not accomplish indirectly (through serial ex parte orders) what it may only accomplish directly with the most rigorous procedural safeguards (*Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 753).

    b. **Procedural Due Process:** As detailed in Argument II, the 24 ex parte orders violate the notice and hearing requirements of *Mullane*, *Armstrong*, and *Mathews*.

    c. **Equal Protection:** Father, as a pro se litigant and as a father, has been subjected to procedures that a similarly situated party represented by counsel, or a similarly situated mother, would not face. [ANDREW_REQUIRED: Any specific instances where Watson received procedural advantages, such as ex parte access or favorable scheduling].

63. These constitutional violations are not isolated incidents; they represent a pattern of practice by the trial court. This Court's superintending control authority exists precisely for situations where systemic lower-court practices violate constitutional rights and where ordinary appellate processes are inadequate to provide timely relief.

64. **Conclusion:** The cumulative federal constitutional violations compel this Court to exercise its superintending control, stay the trial court's orders, restore minimum parenting time, and reassign the case to a substitute judge.
