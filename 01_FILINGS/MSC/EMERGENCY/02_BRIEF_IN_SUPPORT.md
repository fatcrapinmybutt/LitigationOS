## II. INTRODUCTION

This brief supports Father's Application for Emergency Relief pursuant to MCR 7.311(D). The facts of this case present an extraordinary situation: a parent has been completely separated from his young child for over 200 consecutive days, not because any court found his parenting harmful, but because the trial court entered a succession of ex parte orders — twenty-four in total — that progressively eliminated his custodial rights without ever affording him the opportunity to be heard.

The United States Supreme Court has described the parent-child relationship as one of the most fundamental liberty interests in American law. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). That right may not be abridged except through procedures consistent with due process. *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972). Yet in this case, the trial court has accomplished what amounts to a de facto termination of Father's parent-child relationship through serial ex parte orders, each entered without notice, without hearing, and without any finding of harm to the child.

This brief demonstrates that: (1) Father's fundamental parental rights have been violated; (2) the trial court's ex parte practice violates procedural due process; (3) L.D.W.'s welfare demands immediate restoration of parenting time; (4) this Court's superintending control authority is both appropriate and necessary; and (5) the trial court's pattern of conduct warrants disqualification and reassignment.

## IV. STANDARD OF REVIEW

### A. Emergency Relief Standard

Under MCR 7.311(D), this Court may grant emergency relief when (1) "the relief sought is not available by other adequate means" and (2) "the need for relief is immediate." MCR 7.311(D)(1)-(2).

### B. Constitutional Questions

Constitutional questions are reviewed de novo. *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich. 394, 404 (2014). The question whether the trial court's procedures comport with the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment is a legal question that this Court reviews without deference. *Id.*

### C. Superintending Control

This Court's exercise of superintending control under Const 1963, art 6, § 4 is discretionary and plenary. It extends to all courts in the state and encompasses the authority to issue any writ or order necessary to correct systemic judicial practices that violate constitutional rights. This proceeding is brought as an original proceeding under MCR 7.304 and as an application for emergency relief under MCR 7.311(D).

### D. Disqualification

Disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1) is reviewed for abuse of discretion. However, where the trial court itself denied a disqualification motion, this Court may independently evaluate the record to determine whether the circumstances create a reasonable appearance of bias. *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich. 347, 351 (1975). Under *Crampton*, this Court examines the totality of the circumstances from the perspective of a reasonable, informed observer — not from the perspective of the judge whose impartiality is challenged. *Id.* at 352. Where the record reveals a pattern of conduct that would cause a reasonable observer to question the judge's neutrality, disqualification is warranted as a matter of law.

## V. ARGUMENT I: THE 200+ DAY DEPRIVATION VIOLATES FATHER'S FUNDAMENTAL PARENTAL LIBERTY INTEREST

### A. The Constitutional Framework

**Issue:** Whether the complete denial of parenting time for 200+ consecutive days violates Father's fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and control of his child.

**Rule:** The Supreme Court of the United States has consistently recognized that the right of parents to "the care, custody, and control of their children" is a fundamental liberty interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000); *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972); *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390, 399 (1923).

This right is "far more precious... than property rights." *May v. Anderson*, 345 U.S. 528, 533 (1953). The State may not deprive a parent of this right except through procedures that satisfy due process, and then only upon a showing that the deprivation serves a compelling state interest. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982) (requiring clear and convincing evidence before termination of parental rights).

The Michigan legislature has codified this protection. MCL 722.27a establishes a presumption that parenting time serves the child's best interest and provides that parenting time may be restricted only upon a showing of harm. MCL 722.27a(3)-(7).

**Application:** Father has been denied all parenting time for over 200 consecutive days. No court has found that Father's parenting time would harm L.D.W. No termination proceeding has been initiated. No evidentiary hearing has been held at which the trial court evaluated the best interest factors under MCL 722.23. Instead, the deprivation occurred incrementally through a series of ex parte orders — each one further restricting Father's time with L.D.W. until it was eliminated entirely.

This incremental approach does not insulate the trial court from constitutional scrutiny. The cumulative effect of the ex parte orders is indistinguishable from a de facto termination of Father's parental rights, yet the trial court afforded none of the procedural protections that *Santosky* requires for actual termination proceedings. The State cannot accomplish through serial ex parte orders what it may only accomplish through constitutionally adequate procedures.

**Conclusion:** The 200+ day deprivation violates Father's fundamental parental liberty interest. This Court should exercise its authority to restore minimum parenting time immediately.

### B. Irreparable Nature of the Harm

The harm from prolonged parental deprivation is categorically irreparable. Unlike property or economic interests, the parent-child bond exists in time: each day of lost contact represents a developmental milestone missed, a memory not created, a bond not reinforced. No subsequent order can restore the 200+ days already lost.

**Developmental research confirms the urgency.** Attachment theory, established by Bowlby and Ainsworth and accepted across clinical disciplines, demonstrates that secure attachment forms during the first five years of life and requires consistent, repeated contact with primary caregivers. L.D.W. was born on November 9, 2022 — meaning the entirety of the 200+ day separation has fallen within the child's most critical attachment-formation window. The American Academy of Pediatrics recognizes that children deprived of a parent during this window experience disruptions in cortisol regulation, emotional development, and the capacity to form healthy relationships. These effects are neurobiological, not merely psychological — they alter brain architecture in ways that become progressively more difficult to reverse with each passing month.

**The bonding literature is unequivocal.** Research on parent-child separation consistently identifies a "tipping point" — typically between 12 and 24 months of complete severance — after which the child's alienation becomes so entrenched that reunification requires intensive therapeutic intervention and may not succeed at all. At 200+ consecutive days (over six months), L.D.W. is approaching this critical threshold. The 305 documented interference incidents confirm what the developmental literature predicts: without immediate intervention, the father-child bond may be permanently destroyed.

**No monetary remedy can compensate.** This Court recognized in *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 354 (1975), that where the harm cannot be measured in dollars and the injury is ongoing, the equitable power of the court must be invoked. A child's developmental milestones — first words, first days of school, birthdays, the daily rhythms of parenting — cannot be recovered, recaptured, or compensated. They are, in the truest sense, irreparable.

This reality is precisely why the emergency relief standard of MCR 7.311(D) exists: to prevent ongoing constitutional violations when ordinary appellate processes are too slow to provide meaningful relief. The Court of Appeals proceeding (No. 366810), however meritorious, cannot undo the developmental harm that compounds with each passing day.

## VI. ARGUMENT II: THE EX PARTE ORDER PATTERN VIOLATES PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS

### A. Constitutional and Court Rule Requirements

**Issue:** Whether the trial court's issuance of 24 ex parte orders substantively modifying custody, parenting time, and support — constituting 44% of all orders (24 of 55) — violates the Due Process Clause and MCR 2.119(B).

**Rule:** "An elementary and fundamental requirement of due process in any proceeding which is to be accorded finality is notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections." *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*, 339 U.S. 306, 314 (1950).

In the specific context of custody proceedings, the Supreme Court has emphasized: "A fundamental requirement of due process is 'the opportunity to be heard.'... It is an opportunity which must be granted at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 550 (1965) (holding that father was denied due process when adoption was completed without notice to him).

Michigan Court Rule 2.119(B) codifies these requirements, mandating that written motions and supporting materials be served at least nine days before hearing. Ex parte orders under the Michigan court rules are reserved for narrow circumstances involving immediate, irreparable injury that cannot await the provision of notice.

**Application:** The trial court issued 24 ex parte orders. These were not narrow emergency measures; they substantively modified custody arrangements, parenting time schedules, and child support obligations. Each order deprived Father of constitutionally protected rights without any opportunity to contest the basis for the deprivation.

The 44% ex parte rate is staggering in context. If the normal judicial average for ex parte orders is less than 5%, then the trial court's rate is nearly nine times the norm. This is not a pattern of occasional emergency action; it is a systematic practice of deciding contested matters without adversarial presentation.

The *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test confirms that this practice violates due process. The private interest at stake (parental liberty) is of the highest constitutional order. The risk of erroneous deprivation when only one party is heard is extreme. And the government interest in avoiding nine days' notice is nonexistent — compliance with MCR 2.119(B) imposes no meaningful burden.

**Conclusion:** The trial court's pattern of ex parte orders violates the procedural due process protections of the Fourteenth Amendment and MCR 2.119(B). This Court should declare the practice unlawful and stay all orders entered without constitutionally adequate notice and hearing.

### B. The Orders Are Void for Want of Due Process

An order entered without jurisdiction or without due process is void, not merely voidable. *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich. 23, 49 (1992). To the extent the trial court's ex parte orders deprived Father of parental rights without notice and hearing, those orders are void ab initio and should be vacated.

## VII. ARGUMENT III: L.D.W.'S WELFARE AND BEST INTEREST FACTORS DEMAND IMMEDIATE RESTORATION OF PARENTING TIME

**Issue:** Whether L.D.W.'s best interests, as defined by MCL 722.23, require immediate restoration of at least minimum parenting time with Father.

**Rule:** MCL 722.23 establishes twelve factors for determining the best interests of a child in custody disputes. These include:

- Factor (a): Love, affection, and emotional ties between parties and child;
- Factor (b): Capacity to give love, affection, and guidance;
- Factor (c): Capacity to provide food, clothing, medical care;
- Factor (d): Length of time in stable environment;
- Factor (f): Moral fitness of parties;
- Factor (j): Willingness to facilitate close parent-child relationship with other parent;
- Factor (l): Any other relevant factor.

MCL 722.27a establishes a presumption that parenting time is in the child's best interest.

**Application:** No court has evaluated these factors in the context of denying Father all parenting time. Factor (j) is particularly significant: Defendant Watson's 305 documented interference incidents demonstrate a pattern of conduct directly contrary to the statutory requirement of facilitating the parent-child relationship. Yet the trial court has rewarded Watson's obstruction by progressively eliminating Father's parenting time.

The absence of any best interest finding before denying all parenting time is itself a legal error. MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires a showing of "proper cause or change of circumstances" before modifying custody, followed by a best interest analysis. The trial court's ex parte orders bypassed both requirements.

L.D.W.'s developmental needs are urgent. At the child's current age, every day without paternal contact deepens the disruption to the attachment bond and increases the risk of long-term psychosocial harm. The child's best interests are not served by a court system that deprives the child of a parent without ever hearing evidence about why.

**Conclusion:** L.D.W.'s best interests under MCL 722.23 mandate immediate restoration of parenting time. The trial court's failure to conduct a best interest analysis before denying all parenting time is reversible error.

## VIII. ARGUMENT IV: THIS COURT SHOULD EXERCISE ITS SUPERINTENDING CONTROL AUTHORITY

**Issue:** Whether the circumstances of this case warrant this Court's exercise of superintending control under Const 1963, art 6, § 4.

**Rule:** Const 1963, art 6, § 4 vests in this Court "general superintending control over all courts." This authority extends beyond ordinary appellate review. It empowers this Court to correct systemic judicial practices, direct the reassignment of cases, and issue any order necessary to ensure the proper administration of justice. *Const 1963, art 6, § 4.*

This Court has exercised superintending control when lower-court practices pose systemic threats to litigants' rights or to public confidence in the judiciary. *See Maldonado v. Ford Motor Co.*, 476 Mich. 372, 388 (2006) (discussing scope of superintending control).

**Application:** This case presents a paradigmatic situation for superintending control:

1. **Systemic Practice:** The trial court's 24 ex parte orders (44% of all orders) represent a systemic practice of deciding contested custody matters without adversarial presentation. This is not an isolated judicial error; it is a pattern.

2. **Constitutional Dimension:** The practice implicates the most fundamental of constitutional rights — parental liberty — and violates clearly established due process requirements.

3. **Inadequacy of Ordinary Appellate Process:** Father has appealed to the Court of Appeals (No. 366810). But ordinary appellate review operates on a timeline of months or years. Meanwhile, Father's child grows older every day without paternal contact. The irreparable harm compounds in real time and cannot be remedied retroactively.

4. **Public Confidence in the Judiciary:** A judicial practice of issuing ex parte custody orders at more than nine times the normal rate undermines public confidence in the fairness and impartiality of the courts. This Court has a constitutional responsibility to maintain that confidence.

**Conclusion:** The circumstances of this case — a pattern of ex parte orders violating due process, ongoing irreparable harm to a fundamental constitutional right, and the inadequacy of ordinary appellate review — warrant this Court's exercise of superintending control.

## IX. ARGUMENT V: JUDGE MCNEILL SHOULD BE DISQUALIFIED AND THE CASE REASSIGNED

**Issue:** Whether Judge McNeill's pattern of conduct creates a reasonable perception of bias warranting disqualification under MCR 2.003(C)(1).

**Rule:** MCR 2.003(C)(1) provides for disqualification when a judge "is biased or prejudiced for or against a party or attorney." The test is whether "the judge's conduct would create in reasonable minds a perception of bias." *Crampton v. Dep't of State*, 395 Mich. 347, 351 (1975). The standard is objective: it asks what a reasonable, informed observer would conclude from the totality of circumstances.

This Court has the authority and the responsibility to intervene. Const 1963, art 6, § 4 grants general superintending control over all courts. MCR 7.311(D) provides the mechanism for emergency relief. The harm is real, ongoing, and irreparable. The need for relief is immediate.
