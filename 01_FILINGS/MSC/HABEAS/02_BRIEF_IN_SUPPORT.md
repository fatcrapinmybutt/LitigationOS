**Whether the Michigan Supreme Court should issue a writ of habeas corpus where a father has been completely denied all contact with his minor child for 200+ consecutive days, no valid court order authorizes such denial, the lower court has refused to enforce mandatory statutory protections, and the child's fundamental right to a relationship with both parents is being destroyed daily?**

## II. INTRODUCTION

This case presents one of the most fundamental questions a court can face: **May a child be permanently separated from a fit parent without due process of law?**

The answer, under two centuries of American constitutional jurisprudence, is an unequivocal **no**. Yet that is precisely what has occurred here. Andrew J. Pigors has been separated from his son L.D.W. for 200+ consecutive days. Not a single phone call. Not a single visit. Not a single letter. The complete and total severing of a parent-child bond — accomplished not by a court order finding unfitness, not by a termination proceeding with its attendant safeguards, but by the simple refusal of one parent to comply with the law and the failure of the lower court to enforce it.

The Great Writ of habeas corpus exists for precisely this moment. When a human being is restrained without lawful authority and the ordinary machinery of justice has failed, habeas corpus is the constitutional backstop — "the best and only sufficient defence of personal freedom." *Boumediene v. Bush*, 553 U.S. 723, 739 (2008), quoting 1 W. Blackstone, *Commentaries on the Laws of England* 131 (1765).

This petition is not about visitation schedules or custody percentages. It is about whether a father — never adjudged unfit, never afforded a termination hearing — can be permanently erased from his child's life through a combination of one parent's willful defiance and the judiciary's inaction. If that is permissible, then parental rights in Michigan are not rights at all — they are privileges revocable at the whim of whichever parent happens to have physical possession of the child.

## IV. STANDARD OF REVIEW

A petition for writ of habeas corpus raises questions of constitutional law, statutory interpretation, and fundamental rights, which this Court reviews **de novo**. *People v. Maxson*, 482 Mich 385, 392 (2008); *In re Sanders*, 495 Mich 394, 404 (2014).

Whether habeas corpus relief is appropriate in a given case is a question of law subject to de novo review. The factual circumstances underlying the petition — the duration and completeness of the deprivation — are not in genuine dispute.

## V. ARGUMENT

### A. ARGUMENT I — A Parent's Fundamental Right to Custody and Access Cannot Be Extinguished Without Due Process

The United States Supreme Court has recognized for over a century that the right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of their child is among the oldest and most fundamental liberty interests recognized in American law:

> "The child is not the mere creature of the State; those who nurture him and direct his destiny have the right, coupled with the high duty, to recognize and prepare him for additional obligations."

*Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390, 401 (1923).

This principle has been reaffirmed in an unbroken line of cases:

- *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972): "The rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed essential … basic civil rights of man … far more precious than property rights."

- *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982): A parent's "fundamental liberty interest" in the care of their child "does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody."

- *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000): "[T]he interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children … is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."

Because parental rights are **fundamental**, they cannot be terminated or effectively nullified without due process. *Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 747–48. The minimum requirements include:

1. Notice and an opportunity to be heard
2. A clear and convincing evidence standard
3. A determination of unfitness or danger to the child
4. Consideration of less restrictive alternatives

**None of these protections have been provided to Petitioner.** There has been no termination proceeding, no finding of unfitness, no evidentiary hearing on whether complete severance of contact is warranted. The result — 200+ days without any contact — was achieved through Respondent's unilateral action and the lower court's abdication of its duty.

The United States Supreme Court underscored the gravity of these requirements in *Dusenbery v. United States*, 534 U.S. 161, 167 (2002), holding that due process requires "notice reasonably calculated, under all the circumstances, to apprise interested parties of the pendency of the action and afford them an opportunity to present their objections." Where the government deprives a person of a constitutionally protected interest, the adequacy of notice is measured not by the government's convenience but by the magnitude of the interest at stake. *Id.* at 168. Here, the interest at stake — the parent-child relationship — is of the highest constitutional order, yet Petitioner received no notice whatsoever before twenty-four ex parte orders progressively eliminated his parental rights.

This Court's own precedent in *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993), confirms that when a lower court fails to perform a clear legal duty and there is no adequate remedy, this Court's extraordinary writ power — including habeas corpus — must be exercised. The failure to enforce MCL 552.644's mandatory parenting time provisions despite 305 documented violations constitutes precisely the "abuse of judicial power" that *Hatcher* identified as the trigger for this Court's intervention.

When the practical effect of a state's action (or inaction) is indistinguishable from a termination of parental rights, the same constitutional protections must apply. *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102, 116–17 (1996).

### B. ARGUMENT II — Habeas Corpus Is a Proper Remedy in Michigan Child Custody Cases

Respondent may argue that habeas corpus is not available in a child custody context — that it is a "prisoner's writ" limited to criminal or immigration detention. This argument is historically and legally wrong.

**1. The statutory text is unambiguous.**

MCL 600.4304 provides:

> "Any person committed, detained, confined or restrained of his or her liberty … for any cause or under any pretense whatsoever, may prosecute a writ of habeas corpus to inquire into the cause of the commitment, detainer, confinement or restraint."

The phrase "any cause or under any pretense whatsoever" is among the broadest grants of jurisdiction in Michigan law. It is not limited to incarceration.

**2. Michigan courts have applied habeas to custody disputes.**

In *In re Dougherty*, 36 Mich App 14 (1971), the Michigan Court of Appeals recognized habeas corpus as proper where custody arrangements violated due process. The court examined the underlying restraint — a parent's denial of access — through the lens of fundamental rights.

In *Hatcher v. Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993), the Michigan Supreme Court addressed the intersection of parental rights and judicial authority, recognizing that courts must protect the fundamental nature of the parent-child relationship.

**3. The historical pedigree is unassailable.**

Habeas corpus has been used in child custody cases since **before Michigan statehood**. The English courts of equity employed habeas corpus to determine custody of children as early as the 18th century. American courts adopted this practice at the founding. *See* Barry v. Mercein, 46 U.S. (5 How.) 103 (1847) (habeas corpus petition by father for custody of child).

**4. The child is "restrained" within the meaning of the statute.**

L.D.W. is not in prison. But he is confined — confined to a household where his relationship with his father has been systematically destroyed. He is restrained — restrained from exercising his own right to a relationship with both parents. The restraint is psychological, not physical, but it is no less real and no less damaging.

### C. ARGUMENT III — 200+ Days Without Contact Constitutes Constructive Termination Without Due Process

The 200+ day period of complete severance is not a temporary disruption. It is, in practical effect, **a termination of the parent-child relationship**.

**1. The practical effect test.**

Courts evaluate constitutional claims based on practical effect, not labels. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 335 (1976). A complete denial of all parent-child contact for 200+ days has the **identical practical effect** as a formal order terminating parental rights:

| Effect | Formal Termination | 200+ Day Severance |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Parent-child contact | None | None |
| Parent's role in child's life | Eliminated | Eliminated |
| Child's knowledge of parent | Fades | Fades |
| Psychological bond | Severed | Severed |
| Legal status of parent | Terminated | *Nominally intact but meaningless* |

The only difference is that a formal termination comes with due process. The 200+ day severance came with **none**.

**2. Duration matters.**

At some point, a temporary suspension of contact becomes a permanent severance. That point has long since passed. A child's sense of time differs fundamentally from an adult's. For L.D.W., 200+ days without his father is not a pause — it is a **lifetime**. Research in developmental psychology consistently demonstrates that prolonged separation from a parent causes:

- Attachment disruption
- Anxiety and depression
- Identity confusion
- Long-term difficulty forming healthy relationships
- Cognitive and academic decline

These harms are **cumulative and accelerating**. The longer the separation continues, the harder reunification becomes, and the greater the permanent damage to the child.

**3. The due process floor.**

If the state wishes to sever the parent-child bond permanently, it must satisfy *Santosky*'s requirements: clear and convincing evidence, procedural safeguards, and a determination of unfitness. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 769–70 (1982).

Here, the state has permitted the permanent severance of the parent-child bond through **inaction** — with none of *Santosky*'s protections. This is constitutionally impermissible. A state cannot accomplish through neglect what it is forbidden from accomplishing through adjudication.

### D. ARGUMENT IV — The Lower Court's Refusal to Enforce Statutory Mandates Makes Habeas Necessary

MCL 552.644 is Michigan's parenting time enforcement statute. Its language is mandatory:

> "(1) If the court determines that a custodial parent has not reasonably complied with a parenting time order, the court **shall** do 1 or more of the following: (a) Order additional parenting time to compensate … (b) Find the custodial parent in contempt … (c) Modify the custody order …"

The word **"shall"** is not advisory. It is mandatory. *Browder v. International Fidelity Ins Co*, 413 Mich 603, 612 (1982) ("The word 'shall' is generally interpreted as being mandatory."). When a court determines interference has occurred, it has **no discretion** to do nothing.

Yet that is precisely what Judge McNeill has done: **nothing**. Despite 305 documented incidents of interference, the lower court has:

- Not ordered makeup parenting time
- Not found Watson in contempt
- Not modified the custody arrangement
- Not taken any enforcement action whatsoever

When the very court responsible for enforcing a parent's rights refuses to do so, the parent is left without remedy. That is the definition of the circumstances in which habeas corpus is necessary. The writ exists to provide relief when the ordinary judicial process fails.

As the Supreme Court recognized in *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 552 (1965), "a fundamental requirement of due process is the opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner." Petitioner has been heard — and ignored. The hearing was therefore not meaningful. Due process requires not just a forum, but a forum that functions.

### E. ARGUMENT V — L.D.W.'s Own Liberty Interest in Maintaining the Parental Relationship

This petition is not only about Petitioner's rights. **L.D.W. has his own, independent constitutional interest** in maintaining a relationship with both parents.

In *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65–66 (2000), the Supreme Court recognized the fundamental nature of the parent-child relationship from **both sides**. The child's interest in knowing, being cared for by, and maintaining a bond with their parent is itself constitutionally protected.

L.D.W. did not choose this separation. He did not consent to the erasure of his father from his life. He is a child — powerless to assert his own rights, dependent on the courts to protect them.

For 200+ days, no court has protected L.D.W.'s right to his father. The lower court has failed him. The appellate process is too slow. This Court is his last hope.

*Prince v. Massachusetts*, 321 U.S. 158, 170 (1944), recognized that "the family itself is not beyond regulation in the public interest." But regulation must serve the child's interest — not destroy it. The current state of affairs serves no one's interest. It does not protect L.D.W. It harms him.

### F. ARGUMENT VI — Parental Alienation Constitutes Psychological Restraint of a Child

The 305 documented incidents of interference constitute **parental alienation** — the systematic destruction of a child's relationship with one parent by the other.

While Michigan courts have not adopted a specific statutory framework for parental alienation, the behaviors described constitute a form of **psychological restraint**:

1. **The child's autonomy is suppressed** — L.D.W. cannot choose to contact his father because Respondent controls all means of communication.

2. **The child's perception is manipulated** — Alienating behaviors are designed to turn the child against the absent parent, replacing the child's own memories and feelings with implanted hostility.

3. **The child's freedom of association is eliminated** — L.D.W. has no ability to associate with his father, his father's family, or anyone who might counter the alienating narrative.

This is not mere parental conflict. It is the **systematic psychological confinement** of a child within a single parent's worldview, with the deliberate exclusion of the other parent. It is restraint in every meaningful sense.

Habeas corpus inquires into whether a person is restrained without lawful authority. L.D.W. is restrained — not by chains, but by the psychological architecture of alienation. The restraint is no less real for being invisible.

## WHY THIS COURT SHOULD ACT

The Michigan Constitution declares that "the privilege of the writ of habeas corpus shall not be suspended." Const 1963, art 1, § 10. This is not hortatory language. It is a command — the strongest in Michigan's constitutional architecture.

This Court should exercise its constitutional supervisory authority under Const 1963, art 6, § 4 and issue the Great Writ for four independently sufficient reasons:

**First, the deprivation is without lawful authority.** No valid court order authorizes 200+ days of complete parental severance. The ex parte orders that progressively eliminated Petitioner's rights were entered without the notice required by MCR 2.119(B) and without the due process protections mandated by the Fourteenth Amendment and *Dusenbery v. United States*, 534 U.S. 161, 167 (2002). Void orders cannot authorize restraint. *Bowie v. Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 49 (1992).

**Second, the child is unlawfully restrained.** L.D.W. is confined to a household where his relationship with his father is being systematically destroyed. MCL 600.4304 extends habeas to "any person committed, detained, confined or restrained of his or her liberty ... for any cause or under any pretense whatsoever." The restraint is psychological, not physical, but it is no less real — and it is no less unlawful for being accomplished through one parent's defiance and the other's exclusion from the judicial process.

**Third, the ordinary judicial machinery has failed.** In *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441 (1993), this Court recognized that extraordinary writ power exists for precisely this moment: when a lower court has committed an abuse of judicial power tantamount to a failure to perform a clear legal duty, and there is no other adequate remedy. Petitioner has exhausted every available remedy — motions denied, disqualification blocked by $250 bond, appeal pending but too slow. The lower court is both the source of the dysfunction and the gatekeeper of the solution. Only this Court can break the impasse.

**Fourth, every day of delay deepens irreversible harm.** The developmental psychology literature is unequivocal: prolonged parental separation during early childhood causes cumulative, accelerating damage to attachment, identity, and psychological well-being. L.D.W. is past the 200+ day mark. The alienation tipping point — after which reunification becomes extraordinarily difficult — may have already been reached. No retroactive order can undo this harm. Only immediate action can prevent it from becoming permanent.

The Great Writ exists for precisely this moment. This Court should issue it.
