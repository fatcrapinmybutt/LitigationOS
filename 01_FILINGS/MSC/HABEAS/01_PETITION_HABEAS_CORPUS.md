## I. INTRODUCTION

Andrew J. Pigors, a father, has not seen, spoken to, or had any form of contact with his son, L.D.W., for **more than two hundred (200) consecutive days**. This is not a gap in visitation. This is not a scheduling dispute. This is the **total severance of a parent-child bond** — accomplished without due process, without a termination order, and without any lawful justification.

Petitioner invokes the Great Writ — habeas corpus — because every other remedy has failed. The lower court will not act. The Court of Appeals is too slow. And every single day that passes, the psychological damage to a child separated from his father compounds irreversibly.

The Michigan Constitution provides that **"[t]he privilege of the writ of habeas corpus shall not be suspended unless in case of rebellion or invasion the public safety may require it."** Const 1963, art 1, § 10. This writ exists precisely for moments like this — when a human being is restrained without lawful authority and no other remedy is adequate.

---

## II. JURISDICTION AND AUTHORITY

1. This Court has **original jurisdiction** over habeas corpus proceedings. Const 1963, art 6, § 4; MCR 3.303(A).

2. The Michigan Constitution guarantees: "The privilege of the writ of habeas corpus shall not be suspended unless in case of rebellion or invasion the public safety may require it." Const 1963, art 1, § 10.

3. The Michigan habeas corpus statutes, MCL 600.4301 through MCL 600.4365, authorize any person restrained of their liberty to petition for the writ.

4. MCR 3.303 establishes the procedure for habeas corpus proceedings in Michigan courts.

5. MCL 600.4304 provides: "Any person committed, detained, confined or restrained of his or her liberty … for any cause or under any pretense whatsoever, may prosecute a writ of habeas corpus to inquire into the cause of the commitment, detainer, confinement or restraint."

6. Michigan courts have long recognized that habeas corpus is available in **child custody cases** where a child is being unlawfully restrained from a parent. *In re Dougherty*, 36 Mich App 14 (1971); *Armstrong v. Manzo*, 380 U.S. 545, 550 (1965).

7. This Court exercises **superintending control** over all state courts (Const 1963, art 6, § 4), and this petition arises from the 14th Judicial Circuit Court's failure to enforce the parental rights of the Petitioner.

## IV. THE RESTRAINT IS UNLAWFUL

### A. No Lawful Authority for Complete Denial

16. Michigan law is clear: a parent's right to the custody and companionship of their child is a **fundamental constitutional right** that cannot be abridged without due process. *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972); *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

17. No court has entered an order terminating Petitioner's parental rights under MCL 712A.19b.

18. No court has entered a formal order suspending all parenting time following the procedural safeguards required by *In re Dougherty* and Michigan court rules.

19. The restraint of L.D.W. from his father is therefore **without lawful authority**.

### A-1. The August 8, 2025 Ex Parte Order Is Void Ab Initio (SG-3)

**Issue:** The August 8, 2025 ex parte order that suspended all of Father's parenting time was issued without the required supporting affidavit and without compliance with MCR 3.207(B)(1). **Rule:** MCR 3.207(B)(1) requires that an ex parte order in a domestic relations case be supported by an affidavit setting forth specific facts showing that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result from the delay required to effect notice. An order entered in violation of mandatory procedural prerequisites is **void ab initio** — void from inception — not merely voidable. *See Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 38 (1992) (orders entered without jurisdiction are void and without legal effect). **Application:** No affidavit complying with MCR 3.207(B)(1) was filed in support of the August 8, 2025 ex parte order. The order was issued based solely on a police report that was — by Albert Watson's own admission to Officer Ella Randall (NSPD Case NS2505044) — filed as a litigation tactic, not because of any genuine safety concern. An ex parte order lacking its mandatory jurisdictional prerequisite is void ab initio and cannot serve as lawful authority for the restraint of L.D.W. from his father. **Conclusion:** Because the foundational order is void, every subsequent extension of the parenting time suspension is equally void, and the entire chain of custody deprivation from August 8, 2025 forward lacks lawful authority.

### A-2. Respondent's Poisoning Allegation Disproven by Medical Records (SG-1)

**Issue:** Respondent Watson alleged in her ex parte filings that Petitioner engaged in conduct suggesting drug use or poisoning, citing Officer Ella Randall's speculation about "meth use." **Rule:** Medical evidence is the gold standard for determining the factual basis of health-related allegations. Unsubstantiated speculation by a non-medical professional cannot substitute for clinical findings. **Application:** Emergency room medical records establish that Petitioner presented with **hypertension** — a common cardiovascular condition — not any indication of drug use, poisoning, or substance abuse. The HealthWest clinical evaluation further confirmed: Psychosis = 0, Substance Abuse = 0, Danger to Self/Others = 0, LOCUS Level One (routine outpatient — the lowest acuity level). The poisoning/drug-use narrative that undergirded the ex parte order is affirmatively **disproven** by the medical record. **Conclusion:** The factual predicate for the restraint of L.D.W. from his father — that Petitioner posed a danger due to substance use — has been demolished by objective medical evidence. Continued restraint on a disproven basis violates due process.

### B. Constructive Termination of Parental Rights

20. The complete severance of all parent-child contact for more than 200 days constitutes a **constructive termination** of parental rights. The practical effect is identical to a termination order, but without:
    - Clear and convincing evidence of unfitness
    - Appointment of counsel (where required)
    - A best-interest determination under proper standards
    - Any of the procedural safeguards mandated by *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982)

21. A state may not accomplish through inaction what it cannot accomplish through action without due process.

### C. The Child's Own Liberty Interest

22. L.D.W. has his own **independent constitutional right** to maintain a relationship with both parents. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65–66 (2000) (recognizing the fundamental nature of the parent-child relationship from both directions). As the Supreme Court held in *Troxel*, "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Id.* at 65. This liberty interest runs in both directions: L.D.W. has a constitutionally protected interest in not being severed from his father without due process, and Petitioner has a constitutionally protected interest in not being severed from his son.

23. L.D.W. is not a possession to be withheld. He is a person with his own rights, including the right not to be psychologically restrained from his father through alienation and interference.

23A. L.D.W. has been separated from his father for **more than 200 consecutive days** without any due process determination that such separation serves his best interests. No best-interest analysis under MCL 722.23 has been conducted. No finding of parental unfitness has been made. No termination proceeding has been initiated. The child's fundamental right to a relationship with both parents has been extinguished through administrative inaction, not adjudicative process. This Court should intervene to protect L.D.W.'s independent constitutional interests.

## V. HABEAS CORPUS IS PROPER IN CHILD CUSTODY CASES

24. Michigan has long recognized that habeas corpus lies in **child custody disputes**. The writ is not limited to prisoners or criminal detainees.

25. MCL 600.4304 broadly authorizes the writ for any person "restrained of his or her liberty … for any cause or under any pretense whatsoever."

26. In *In re Dougherty*, 36 Mich App 14 (1971), the Michigan Court of Appeals recognized habeas corpus as a proper vehicle for challenging custody arrangements that violate due process.

27. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that a parent's interest in the "companionship, care, custody, and management" of their children is "an interest far more precious than any property right." *Lassiter v. Department of Social Services*, 452 U.S. 18, 27 (1981); *May v. Anderson*, 345 U.S. 528, 533 (1953).

28. Where a parent is completely denied access to their child without due process, and the court system has failed to provide a remedy, **habeas corpus is not merely appropriate — it is the only adequate remedy.**

## VI. NO ADEQUATE ALTERNATIVE REMEDY EXISTS

29. Petitioner has exhausted or is pursuing every available remedy:

    a. **Lower Court Motions** — Filed and ignored or denied by Judge McNeill. The court refuses to enforce its own orders and the mandatory provisions of MCL 552.644.

    b. **Court of Appeals (No. 366810)** — Pending, but appellate timelines extend months or years. L.D.W. cannot wait. Every day of separation causes further psychological damage that no appellate order can undo.

    c. **Mandamus (MSC)** — Filed with this Court to compel the lower court to act. But mandamus compels a judge; habeas compels the custodian. Both are needed.

    d. **Contempt** — Requires the lower court to act, which it has refused to do.

30. The inadequacy of these remedies is itself grounds for habeas relief. When the judicial system fails to protect a fundamental right, the Great Writ stands as the last safeguard of liberty.

31. **Habeas corpus is the proper remedy.** Unlike mandamus (which compels a judge to act) or contempt (which punishes disobedience), habeas corpus directly commands the custodian to produce the child. Where a parent is unlawfully deprived of custody and the lower courts have failed to act, the Michigan Supreme Court has original jurisdiction to issue the writ. Const 1963, art 4, § 13; MCR 3.303(A). This is precisely the circumstance the Great Writ was designed to address: a person is restrained of their liberty (here, the parent-child relationship) without lawful authority, and no other remedy has proven adequate.
