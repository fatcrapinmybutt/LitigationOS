## III. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. MCR 2.002 — Waiver of Fees and Costs

MCR 2.002(B) provides that a court "shall" waive fees and costs for a party who demonstrates by affidavit that he or she is "unable to pay fees and costs because of indigency." The rule establishes objective criteria based on income, assets, and expenses, and contemplates both absolute indigency and practical inability to pay without deprivation of necessities.

### B. MCR 7.302(D)(2) — Supreme Court IFP

MCR 7.302(D)(2) provides that a party "who is unable to pay required fees" may apply for leave to proceed in forma pauperis. The Court evaluates the application based on the financial showing and the apparent merit of the claims.

### C. Constitutional Standard

The United States Supreme Court has held that "a State may not, consistent with the obligations imposed on it by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, pre-empt the right to dissolve [a legal] relationship without affording all citizens access to its courts." *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 U.S. 371, 374 (1971). This principle extends to all proceedings in which the State holds a monopoly on the resolution of disputes. *See also M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102, 116 (1996) (extending *Boddie* to parental rights cases).

## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. The Constitutional Right of Access to Courts Requires Fee Waiver

The right of access to courts is a fundamental right protected by both the United States and Michigan Constitutions. U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1; Mich. Const. 1963, art. 1, § 13 ("A suitor in any court of this state has the right to have all matters promptly and without delay decided").

In *Boddie v. Connecticut*, the Supreme Court held that filing fees cannot bar an indigent party's access to the only forum capable of granting relief. 401 U.S. at 380-81. The Court reasoned:

> "[A] cost requirement, valid on its face, may offend due process because it operates to foreclose a particular party's opportunity to be heard." *Id.* at 380.

This principle is especially compelling where, as here, parental rights are at stake. In *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 U.S. 102 (1996), the Supreme Court held that Mississippi could not condition an indigent mother's right to appeal the termination of her parental rights on prepayment of record preparation fees. The Court recognized that "choices about marriage, family life, and the upbringing of children are among associational rights this Court has ranked as 'of basic importance in our society.'" *Id.* at 116 (quoting *Boddie*, 401 U.S. at 376).

Michigan courts likewise recognize the right of access. *In re Contempt of Dorsey*, 306 Mich App 571, 585 (2014), affirmed the applicability of IFP protections in custody and family law proceedings. The Michigan Court of Appeals held that courts must conduct a meaningful inquiry into a party's ability to pay before imposing financial barriers to court access.

The Michigan Supreme Court itself, in Administrative Order 2005-6, emphasized that courts must ensure that filing fees do not effectively deny access to justice for indigent parties.

These are precisely the types of claims that the IFP statute was designed to protect — substantial constitutional challenges that would be forever barred if filing fees were allowed to block the courthouse door.

### E. Denial of IFP Status Would Effectively Deny Access to the Only Forum Capable of Providing Relief

The Michigan Supreme Court is the court of last resort for Petitioner's claims. If IFP status is denied:

1. **No alternative forum exists.** The trial court is the source of the violations. The Court of Appeals has limited original jurisdiction over extraordinary writs. Only this Court possesses plenary superintending control authority. *See* Const. 1963, Art. 6, § 4.

2. **The harm is ongoing.** Every day that passes, Petitioner remains separated from his child, subject to an oppressive support order, and barred from meaningful court access. Delay caused by inability to pay effectively becomes permanent denial.

3. **The filing fee is the only barrier.** Petitioner has prepared comprehensive filings, assembled a massive evidentiary record, and demonstrated the capacity to prosecute his claims. The sole obstacle is a financial barrier created by the very misconduct he seeks to challenge.

Under these circumstances, denial of IFP status would violate both the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and Article 1, § 13 of the Michigan Constitution. *See Boddie*, 401 U.S. at 380-81; *M.L.B.*, 519 U.S. at 116-17.
