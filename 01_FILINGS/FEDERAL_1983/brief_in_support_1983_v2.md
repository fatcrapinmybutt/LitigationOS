## IV. ARGUMENT

### A. Judicial Immunity Should Not Bar Plaintiff's Claims

#### 1. The Framework: *Mireles v. Waco* and Its Two Exceptions

The Supreme Court established in *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11-12 (1991), that judicial immunity does not apply when a judge: (1) takes action "not taken in the judge's judicial capacity," or (2) acts "in the complete absence of all jurisdiction." Both exceptions are met here.

#### 2. Exception One: Non-Judicial Acts

Judicial immunity protects "truly judicial acts." *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 227 (1988). The Supreme Court in *Forrester* distinguished between judicial acts (adjudicating cases) and administrative or executive acts (managing court operations). Non-judicial acts receive no immunity.

The following actions by Judge McNeill are **non-judicial in character**:

**(a) Self-Ruling on Disqualification.** MCR 2.003(D) explicitly assigns the determination of disqualification motions to the **Chief Judge** when the challenged judge does not voluntarily step aside. McNeill's decision to rule on her own disqualification motion was an administrative act that the court rules assign to a different judicial officer. This is precisely the type of "administrative" action that *Forrester* held is unprotected. The function of deciding whether a judge should be disqualified is not a "judicial act" performed by the challenged judge — it is, by rule, a function assigned to the Chief Judge.

**(b) Ex Parte Communications.** Canon 3A(4) of the Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct and MCR 2.003 prohibit ex parte communications. When Judge McNeill engaged in ex parte communications with Watson or Berry — evidenced by Berry's voicemail (Item #6), the temporal coordination pattern, and the same-day ruling pattern — she was not performing a judicial function. She was engaging in private, off-record coordination that is the antithesis of adjudication. *See Bright v. Gallia County*, 753 F.3d 639, 650 (6th Cir. 2014) (discussing ex parte contacts as relevant to immunity analysis).

**(c) Cross-Case Coordination (PPO Weaponization).** The 27 documented incidents where PPO and custody actions occurred in coordinated lockstep — all with the same 10/10 weaponization score — constitute administrative case management decisions, not adjudicative acts. Using one case's proceedings to generate evidence or orders for use in another is a strategic, administrative function, not the resolution of a controversy.

#### 3. Exception Two: Clear Absence of All Jurisdiction

The Supreme Court has held that judicial immunity does not apply to acts taken "in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356-57 (1978). While the *Stump* Court construed jurisdiction broadly, the bar is not insurmountable.

**(a) The MCR 3.207(B) Jurisdictional Defect.** Michigan law imposes specific, mandatory prerequisites before a court may enter an ex parte order affecting custody or parenting time. MCR 3.207(B) requires a **verified motion** supported by an **affidavit** setting forth specific facts showing imminent and irreparable harm. MCR 3.207(C)(2) further requires that the motion contain "specific facts that satisfy the court that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result."

On August 8, 2025, Judge McNeill entered five ex parte orders suspending ALL of Plaintiff's parenting time. The motion:
- Was not supported by an affidavit;
- Did not contain specific sworn facts showing imminent danger;
- Was not served on Plaintiff;
- Was not followed by an expedited hearing.

These are not procedural niceties — they are **jurisdictional prerequisites** that confer the authority to act ex parte. Without compliance, the court lacked jurisdiction to enter the ex parte orders. *See King v. Love*, 766 F.2d 962, 967 (6th Cir. 1985) (discussing jurisdictional requirements that condition judicial authority).

**(b) PPO Cross-Boundary Actions.** When Judge McNeill used findings, allegations, or orders from the PPO case (No. 2023-5907-PP) to affect the custody case (No. 2024-001507-DC) — or vice versa — without independent jurisdictional basis in each case, she acted outside the jurisdiction of the individual case. Each case has its own jurisdictional foundation; importing actions across case boundaries without proper procedure constitutes action beyond the court's jurisdiction in the receiving case.

### B. The Volume of Evidence Negates Any "Judicial Discretion" Defense

Defendants will inevitably argue that Judge McNeill was exercising her judicial discretion in a contentious family law case. This argument fails for three independent reasons:

#### 1. Discretion Requires Adherence to Rules

Judicial discretion is the authority to make choices *within the bounds of the law*. *See People v. Babcock*, 469 Mich. 247, 269 (2003) ("An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision falls outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes."). When a judge violates MCR 3.207(B) (no affidavit), MCR 2.003(D) (self-rules on disqualification), and Canon 3A(4) (ex parte communications), she is not exercising discretion — she is violating the rules that define the boundaries of discretion.

The evidence establishes **167 MCR 2.003 violations**, **150 ex parte violations**, and **105 fraud-on-court indicators**. These are not close calls within the zone of discretion. They are rule violations, documented and cataloged with specificity.

#### 2. Discretion Cannot Explain One-Directional Outcomes

Even assuming each individual ruling was within the judge's discretion, the **statistical pattern** eliminates any innocent explanation:
- **100%** of ex parte orders entered against Father, **0%** against Mother;
- **100%** of contempt sanctions against Father, **0%** against Mother;
- **100%** of financial barriers imposed on Father, **0%** on Mother;
- **567+ days** of total separation for Father, **0 days** of restricted access for Mother.

A coin that lands the same way 150 times in a row is not a fair coin. A judge whose discretion always favors one party and always disfavors the other is not exercising discretion — she is exercising bias. The 4,195 bias signal entries in the evidence matrix quantify this pattern.

#### 3. Discretion Does Not Encompass Separation Without Due Process

No exercise of judicial discretion permits the separation of a parent from his child for 567+ consecutive days without a hearing, without findings of fact, and without any mechanism for challenge. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982), requires "clear and convincing evidence" before the state may sever a parental relationship. Here, no evidence was presented, let alone clear and convincing evidence. The deprivation is absolute, prolonged, and procedurally void.

### C. Declaratory and Injunctive Relief Survive Immunity

Even if this Court were to conclude that judicial immunity bars damages claims against Judge McNeill individually, Plaintiff's claims for declaratory and injunctive relief remain fully viable.

#### 1. *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984)

The Supreme Court held in *Pulliam* that judicial immunity does not bar actions for declaratory or injunctive relief against a judicial officer. While Congress later amended 42 U.S.C. § 1983 through the Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1996 to limit *injunctive* relief against judges to situations where declaratory relief is inadequate or a declaratory decree has been violated (42 U.S.C. § 1983, as amended), **declaratory relief remains fully available** against judges without any such limitation.

Here, Plaintiff seeks declarations that:
- The ex parte orders violated due process;
- The $250 bond violates the First Amendment;
- The pattern of conduct constitutes a conspiracy;
- Muskegon County policies facilitated the violations.

These declaratory claims survive any immunity defense.

#### 2. *Ex parte Young*, 209 U.S. 123 (1908)

Official-capacity claims for prospective relief are not barred by sovereign immunity under the *Ex parte Young* doctrine. McNeill's official-capacity designation is for precisely this purpose — to obtain forward-looking injunctive relief ordering the cessation of unconstitutional conduct, reassignment of the case, and restoration of Plaintiff's parenting time.

#### 3. Post-1996 Injunctive Relief

Under the 1996 amendment, injunctive relief against a judicial officer is available when "a declaratory decree was violated or declaratory relief was unavailable." 42 U.S.C. § 1983. Given the ongoing nature of the violations and the $250 bond that prevents Plaintiff from seeking state-court relief, declaratory relief alone is arguably inadequate, satisfying the statutory standard for injunctive relief.

### D. The Monell Claim Is Viable

#### 1. Municipal Liability Framework

*Monell v. Department of Social Services*, 436 U.S. 658, 694 (1978), established that municipalities are liable under § 1983 when an official policy or established custom causes a constitutional violation. Municipalities **cannot assert qualified immunity**. *Owen v. City of Independence*, 445 U.S. 622, 638 (1980).

#### 2. Application to Muskegon County

Muskegon County's liability rests on:

**(a) Final Policymaker Ratification.** Judge McNeill is arguably a final policymaker for purposes of the specific decisions challenged here. *Pembaur v. City of Cincinnati*, 475 U.S. 469, 483 (1986) ("municipal liability under § 1983 attaches where — and only where — a deliberate choice to follow a course of action is made from among various alternatives by the official or officials responsible for establishing final policy").

**(b) Failure to Supervise.** The Chief Judge's failure to exercise supervisory authority — particularly after McNeill self-ruled on her own disqualification in violation of MCR 2.003(D) — constitutes deliberate indifference to the known risk of constitutional violations. *City of Canton v. Harris*, 489 U.S. 378, 388 (1989).

**(c) Custom of Permitting Ex Parte Abuse.** The 150 documented ex parte violations, 1,202 service/notice defects, and zero corrective actions establish a custom so persistent and widespread as to constitute de facto policy. *See Doe v. Claiborne County*, 103 F.3d 495, 508 (6th Cir. 1996).

### E. The Conspiracy Exception Applies

Under *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980), a judge who conspires with private parties to deprive a person of constitutional rights may be held liable under § 1983. The conspiracy exception operates independently of the non-judicial-act and absence-of-jurisdiction exceptions.

The evidence of conspiracy here is both circumstantial and direct:

- **Temporal coordination**: Watson files → McNeill rules same day → Berry files within 48 hours (repeated pattern);
- **Direct evidence**: Berry's voicemail to Plaintiff (Item #6);
- **One-directional benefit**: Every coordinated action exclusively benefited Watson;
- **PPO-custody cross-weaponization**: 27 incidents, all scoring 10/10;
- **30+ same-day events** across the two case numbers.

Critically, the private co-conspirators — Watson and Berry — have **no judicial immunity whatsoever**. *Dennis*, 449 U.S. at 28-29. Even if the Court were to hold McNeill immune, Watson and Berry remain fully liable for their participation in the conspiracy.

### F. Younger Abstention Is Inappropriate

*Younger v. Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971), requires federal courts to abstain from interfering with pending state judicial proceedings. However, the Supreme Court has recognized three exceptions:

1. **Bad faith or harassment** by state officials;
2. **Extraordinary circumstances** rendering the state forum inadequate;
3. **Flagrantly unconstitutional** state procedures.

*Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n*, 457 U.S. 423, 432 (1982); *Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124 (1975).

All three exceptions are met:

**(1) Bad Faith/Harassment.** 24 of 55 orders entered ex parte (43.6%); 150 documented ex parte violations; 2,722 contempt sanction atoms with jail threats; $250 bond imposed to prevent Plaintiff from filing motions. This is textbook bad faith and harassment.

**(2) Extraordinarily Inadequate Forum.** The $250 bond blocks ALL filings. Plaintiff cannot access the state court to challenge the very orders that deprive him of his rights. The judge has self-ruled on disqualification. The appellate process is compromised by Berry's connections. This creates an "extraordinarily and unconstitutionally inadequate" state forum.

**(3) Flagrantly Unconstitutional Procedures.** Entry of five ex parte custody orders on a single day, without affidavit, without notice, without hearing, affecting a fundamental liberty interest, is flagrantly unconstitutional by any measure.

### G. Rooker-Feldman Does Not Apply

The Rooker-Feldman doctrine bars federal district courts from hearing "de facto appeals" of state court judgments. *Rooker v. Fidelity Trust Co.*, 263 U.S. 413 (1923); *D.C. Court of Appeals v. Feldman*, 460 U.S. 462 (1983).

The doctrine is "confined to cases ... brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments rendered before the district court proceedings commenced and inviting district court review and rejection of those judgments." *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp.*, 544 U.S. 280, 284 (2005).

Plaintiff does not ask this Court to review or reverse any state court custody determination. Plaintiff challenges the **process** — the systematic denial of notice, hearing, and a neutral decision-maker — by which those determinations were reached. This is a challenge to the procedures, not the outcomes. *See McCormick v. Braverman*, 451 F.3d 382, 393 (6th Cir. 2006) (distinguishing process challenges from appeals of judgments).
