## IV. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY DOES NOT BAR THESE CLAIMS

40\. Judicial immunity is not absolute. The Supreme Court has recognized two exceptions: (1) actions taken in the **clear absence of all jurisdiction**, and (2) actions that are **not judicial in nature**. *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11–12 (1991); *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356–57 (1978).

41\. Judge McNeill's actions fall within both exceptions:

&emsp;(a) **Clear absence of jurisdiction**: A judge who conducts proceedings and enters orders without providing constitutionally required notice and opportunity to be heard acts in the clear absence of jurisdiction. Entering 68 ex parte orders — 44% of all orders — without notice is not the exercise of jurisdiction; it is the exercise of raw power without jurisdiction;

&emsp;(b) **Non-judicial acts**: Incarcerating a parent for 45 days for sending birthday messages to his child is not a judicial act — it is punitive retaliation against protected speech. Refusing to review exculpatory evidence is not adjudication — it is a refusal to adjudicate. Muting a litigant three times during a hearing is not presiding — it is silencing;

&emsp;(c) **Conspiracy exception**: When a judge conspires with private parties to deprive a litigant of rights, the conspiratorial acts fall outside the scope of judicial immunity because they are not performed in the judge's judicial capacity.

42\. Therefore, Defendant McNeill is not entitled to judicial immunity for the acts alleged in this Complaint.

Plaintiff was forced to attend a hearing remotely while simultaneously required to appear at a different courthouse. These procedures virtually guaranteed erroneous deprivation;

&emsp;(c) **The government's interest, including the burden of additional safeguards**: The government's interest in summary adjudication is **minimal**. No imminent danger was demonstrated. 47\. Defendant McNeill deprived Plaintiff of his fundamental liberty interest in his child by:

&emsp;(a) Issuing **68 ex parte communications and orders** (44% ex parte rate) without notice to Plaintiff;

&emsp;(g) Failing to appoint counsel before imposing incarceration, in violation of the right to counsel recognized in *Mead v. Batchlor*, 435 Mich. 480 (1990), and *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich. App. 656 (2009).

The contempt finding was predicated on the content of Plaintiff's birthday messages.
