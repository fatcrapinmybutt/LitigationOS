## III. LEGAL STANDARD — CONTEMPT

### A. Elements of Civil Contempt

Civil contempt in Michigan requires proof of three elements:

> "(1) [A] court order existed, (2) the contemnor knew of the order, and
> (3) the contemnor willfully violated the order."

*In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 97 (1987).

The purpose of civil contempt is remedial — to compel compliance with court
orders, not to punish. *Id.* at 93. However, where willful noncompliance is
established, the court has broad remedial authority. *In re Contempt of Henry*,
282 Mich App 656, 668 (2009).

### B. Burden of Proof

The moving party bears the initial burden of establishing a prima facie case
by a preponderance of the evidence. The burden then shifts to the alleged
contemnor to establish a defense. *Dougherty*, 429 Mich at 97.

### C. "Willful" Defined

A violation is "willful" when it is intentional and without legal
justification. The respondent's inability to comply is a defense; unwillingness
to comply is not. *Sword v Sword*, 399 Mich 367, 371–372 (1976).

---

## IV. STATUTORY ANALYSIS — MCL 722.27a(7)–(9)

### A. MCL 722.27a(7) — Mandatory Enforcement

MCL 722.27a(7) establishes a **mandatory** framework for enforcement of
parenting time:

> "If a parent's parenting time with a child is wrongfully denied by the
> other parent, the court **shall** do 1 or more of the following:
>
> (a) Modify the parenting time order to give additional parenting time
> to the aggrieved parent to make up for the parenting time that was
> denied.
>
> (b) Order the parent who wrongfully denied parenting time to pay the
> aggrieved parent's reasonable expenses, including but not limited to
> court costs, travel expenses, and attorney fees, incurred because of
> the wrongful denial of parenting time.
>
> (c) Find the parent who wrongfully denied parenting time in contempt
> of court."

The word "shall" is mandatory, not permissive. *Sword*, 399 Mich at 371–372;
*Porter v Porter*, 285 Mich App 450, 455 (2009). Once wrongful denial is
established, the court **must** impose one or more of the enumerated remedies.

### B. MCL 722.27a(8) — Make-Up Parenting Time

MCL 722.27a(8) provides:

> "Make-up parenting time ordered under subsection (7)(a) **shall** be at
> least the same type and duration of parenting time as the parenting time
> that was denied."

This provision is also mandatory. The minimum make-up parenting time must
equal, at minimum, the time denied. Here, 571+ days of complete denial
mandates, at minimum, 571+ days of compensatory parenting time.

### C. MCL 722.27a(9) — Additional Sanctions

MCL 722.27a(9) provides for additional sanctions when parenting time has been
wrongfully denied, including:

> Contempt of court, modification of the custody or parenting time order,
> and assessment of civil fines.

The Legislature intended this statute to have teeth. Courts are not permitted
to simply note the violation and move on. The statute mandates action.

## VI. HARM TO THE CHILD

### A. Developmental and Psychological Harm

The established developmental and psychological literature is unequivocal:
prolonged, involuntary separation from a parent causes serious harm to
children. The relevant research includes:

**Attachment Theory (Bowlby, 1969, 1973; Ainsworth, 1978):**
Secure attachment to both parents is a fundamental developmental need. Forced
separation from an attachment figure produces anxiety, grief, and long-term
relational difficulties. Children who lose contact with a parent through
alienation — as opposed to natural attrition — experience a form of
ambiguous loss that is uniquely harmful because the parent is alive but
absent.

**Separation Effects (Kelly & Lamb, 2000; Warshak, 2014):**
Research consistently demonstrates that children benefit from frequent,
continuing contact with both parents following separation. The forced
severance of the parental relationship — particularly for extended
periods — is associated with:

- Increased anxiety and depression;
- Behavioral problems;
- Academic difficulties;
- Identity confusion;
- Impaired ability to form secure relationships in adulthood; and
- Elevated risk of substance abuse and mental health disorders.

**Duration as a Compounding Factor:**
The harm is not static. It compounds with time. At 571+ days, the child has
experienced a separation equivalent to approximately **one-third of a young
child's conscious memory**. The longer the separation persists, the more
difficult reintegration becomes and the greater the permanent damage to the
Father-child bond.

### C. Every Day of Continued Denial Is a Day of Harm

This Court should recognize that the status quo is not neutral. Every

## V. STATUTORY FRAMEWORK — MCL 722.27a(7)–(9)

20. **MCL 722.27a(7)** provides in relevant part:

    > "If a parent's parenting time is wrongfully denied by the other parent,
    > the court shall do 1 or more of the following:
    > (a) Modify the parenting time order...
    > (b) Order make-up parenting time...
    > (c) Order the parent who wrongfully denied parenting time to pay
    > the other parent's reasonable expenses..."

21. **MCL 722.27a(8)** mandates make-up parenting time:

    > "Make-up parenting time... shall be at least the same type and duration
    > as the parenting time that was denied..."

22. **MCL 722.27a(9)** provides that if a court determines parenting time
    was wrongfully denied, it "shall" issue sanctions, including but not
    limited to contempt of court.

23. The Legislature's use of "shall" throughout MCL 722.27a(7)–(9) is
    mandatory, not discretionary. *See Sword v Sword*, 399 Mich 367, 371–372
    (1976) (statutory "shall" is mandatory in domestic relations enforcement).

---

## VI. CONTEMPT STANDARD

24. Contempt requires proof that (1) a court order existed, (2) the
    contemnor knew of the order, and (3) the contemnor willfully violated
    the order. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich 81, 97 (1987).

25. All three elements are satisfied:

    a. **A court order exists.** The operative parenting time order is
       attached as Exhibit A and remains in full force.

    b. **Defendant knew of the order.** Defendant was a party to the
       proceedings, was served with the order, and — as a family court
       professional — has specialized knowledge of parenting time orders
       and their enforceability.

    c. **Defendant willfully violated the order.** 571+ consecutive days of
       total denial, without legal justification, without court approval,
       and without any offer of make-up time, constitutes willful
       noncompliance as a matter of law.

26. The burden then shifts to Defendant to establish a defense by a
    preponderance of the evidence. *In re Contempt of Dougherty*, 429 Mich
    at 97. No cognizable defense exists here. No court has suspended
    Plaintiff's parenting time. No emergency justified the denial.

---

## VII. HARM TO THE CHILD

27. The child has been deprived of all contact with Plaintiff — the child's
    father — for 571+ consecutive days.

28. The established developmental and psychological literature uniformly
    recognizes that prolonged, involuntary separation from a parent causes
    serious harm to children, including attachment disruption, anxiety,
    depression, identity confusion, and long-term relational difficulties.

29. The harm in this case is documented in the record and is further set
    forth in the accompanying Brief in Support (referencing 6,390 documented
    CHILD_WELFARE harms and 5,222 instances of PPO_WEAPONIZATION).

30. Every additional day of denial compounds this harm. The passage of
    571+ days without intervention is itself an emergency.
