## IV. FACTUAL ALLEGATIONS

### A. The Underlying State-Court Proceedings

14. On or about April 2024, custody and parenting-time proceedings were initiated in the 14th Circuit Court for the County of Muskegon, Case No. 2024-001507-DC, before Defendant Judge McNeill.

15. On **April 11, 2024**, Defendant Judge McNeill entered an amended Personal Protection Order ("PPO") on an *ex parte* basis in related Case No. 2023-5907-PP, without affording Plaintiff notice or an opportunity to be heard.

16. On **April 29, 2024**, an *ex parte* Order for Custody, Parenting Time, and Child Support was entered. Plaintiff was not afforded notice of the application for this order, was not present, and was not given an opportunity to present evidence or argument before the order was entered.

17. On **July 1, 2024**, Defendant Judge McNeill entered an emergency *ex parte* custody order granting sole custody to Defendant Watson without a full evidentiary hearing, stripping Plaintiff of his custodial rights and parenting time.

### B. The August 2025 Ex Parte Parenting-Time Suspension

18. On **August 6, 2025**, Defendant Judge McNeill signed an *ex parte* "emergency" order **suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time** with his three-year-old child. This order was entered without prior notice to Plaintiff, without a hearing, and without any finding of imminent danger supported by specific facts in a verified pleading as required by MCR 3.207(B)(1) and (B)(5)–(6).

19. The August 6, 2025 *ex parte* order operated as a **total bar** to all parenting time between Plaintiff and the Child. The order effectively terminated the parent-child relationship without satisfying the procedural safeguards mandated by the Due Process Clause.

20. Plaintiff attempted to present exculpatory evidence — including photographs from a July 29 exchange — but Defendant Judge McNeill refused to consider this evidence while simultaneously accepting and relying upon *ex parte* submissions from Defendant Watson and/or her counsel.

### C. Off-Record, Ex Parte Handling of Evidence

21. During the pendency of the proceedings, evidence was received and considered off the record and *ex parte*, including but not limited to a USB recording and a mental-health evaluation. This conduct violated the prohibition on *ex parte* communications regarding pending matters. See Michigan Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3(A)(4); MCR 2.003.

22. The combination of (a) *ex parte* submissions accepted from Defendant Watson, (b) refusal to review Plaintiff's exculpatory evidence, and (c) willingness to rely on one-sided communications demonstrates a pattern of bias and prejudgment that deprived Plaintiff of a fair and impartial tribunal.

### D. The Pattern of Ex Parte Orders

23. Of the fifty-five (55) orders entered in the state-court proceedings, **twenty-four (24) were entered ex parte** — a rate of 44%. Every one of these *ex parte* orders was signed by Defendant Judge McNeill.

24. This pattern is not the product of isolated emergency circumstances but rather reflects a systematic practice of adjudicating custody and parenting-time matters without affording Plaintiff constitutionally adequate notice and opportunity to be heard.

25. Multiple *ex parte* orders had the cumulative and compounding effect of progressively stripping Plaintiff of all parental rights and contact with the Child, ultimately resulting in the total severance of the parent-child relationship for 329+ continuous days and counting.

### E. Retaliatory Filing Sanction

26. Defendant Judge McNeill imposed a **$250 filing sanction** against Plaintiff, a *pro se* litigant, as a direct consequence of Plaintiff's exercise of his First Amendment right to petition the court for redress of grievances.

27. The $250 filing sanction was imposed without adequate procedural safeguards, without a finding that Plaintiff's filings were frivolous or filed in bad faith, and served as a financial barrier to Plaintiff's continued access to the courts.

28. The filing sanction had a **chilling effect** on Plaintiff's ability and willingness to file meritorious motions, including motions to modify the *ex parte* custody and parenting-time orders that were destroying his relationship with the Child.

### F. Contempt, Incarceration, and Cascading Consequences

29. Plaintiff was subjected to contempt hearings and **incarceration** which, in turn, caused job loss and housing instability that were later used against Plaintiff in custody proceedings — creating a self-reinforcing cycle of deprivation.

30. The cascading consequences of the unconstitutional *ex parte* orders — including loss of employment, loss of housing, and weaponization of those losses in custody evaluations — compounded the constitutional injury and rendered meaningful remedy in state court impossible.

### G. The Judicial Violation Record

31. An analysis of the state-court proceedings has identified **1,127 judicial violations** attributable to Defendant Judge McNeill's conduct, including:

   a. **377 violations classified as critical severity**, including repeated entry of *ex parte* orders without required factual findings, off-record consideration of evidence, and refusal to consider exculpatory evidence;

   b. **243 violations classified as high severity**, including *ex parte* communications in violation of the Code of Judicial Conduct;

### COUNT I: DEPRIVATION OF SUBSTANTIVE DUE PROCESS — 42 U.S.C. § 1983
**(Against All Defendants)**

33. Plaintiff realleges and incorporates by reference each and every allegation contained in Paragraphs 1 through 32 as though fully set forth herein.

34. The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that no State shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1.

35. The liberty interest of a parent in the care, custody, and control of his or her child is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

36. The right of a parent to the companionship, care, and custody of his child is a fundamental right protected by the Due Process Clause. *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972) ("The rights to conceive and to raise one's children have been deemed 'essential'... 'far more precious... than property rights.'").

37. Because the parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest, governmental interference with that right is subject to **strict scrutiny**: the State must demonstrate that the interference is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. *Washington v. Glucksberg*, 521 U.S. 702, 720 (1997).

38. Defendants' conduct — including the entry of *ex parte* custody orders, the total suspension of parenting time without a hearing, and the progressive elimination of Plaintiff's parental rights over 329+ days — constitutes an arbitrary and conscience-shocking deprivation of Plaintiff's fundamental liberty interest in the parent-child relationship.

39. No compelling governmental interest justified the total termination of Plaintiff's parenting time. The *ex parte* orders were not narrowly tailored and were entered without the factual findings required by Michigan law to support emergency modifications.

40. Defendants' conduct shocks the conscience of the court and is not rationally related to any legitimate governmental purpose. *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833, 846 (1998).

### COUNT II: DEPRIVATION OF PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS — 42 U.S.C. § 1983
**(Against All Defendants)**

41. Plaintiff realleges and incorporates by reference each and every allegation contained in Paragraphs 1 through 40 as though fully set forth herein.

42. Procedural due process requires, at a minimum, that a person be afforded notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard before being deprived of a protected liberty or property interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976).

43. Under the three-factor balancing test of *Mathews v. Eldridge*, the procedures afforded to Plaintiff were constitutionally deficient:

   a. **The private interest affected**: Plaintiff's interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his child is a fundamental liberty interest of the highest order. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 758–59 (1982) ("A parent's interest in the accuracy and justice of the decision to terminate his or her parental status is... a commanding one.").

   b. **The risk of erroneous deprivation**: The risk of error from *ex parte* proceedings is extreme. By definition, *ex parte* orders are entered without the participation of the absent party. When 44% of all orders in a case are entered *ex parte*, the risk of erroneous deprivation is not merely elevated — it is systemic. The refusal to consider Plaintiff's exculpatory evidence while accepting Defendant Watson's one-sided submissions further compounded this risk.

   c. **The governmental interest**: While the State has a *parens patriae* interest in protecting children, that interest does not justify the wholesale abandonment of adversarial process. The State's interest in administrative convenience cannot outweigh a parent's fundamental right to be heard.

44. Plaintiff was deprived of his fundamental parental rights through *ex parte* orders entered on April 11, 2024; April 29, 2024; July 1, 2024; and August 6, 2025 — none of which afforded Plaintiff constitutionally adequate notice or opportunity to be heard.

45. The standard of proof applied in the proceedings failed to satisfy the requirement of *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982), that the State must support allegations of parental unfitness by at least **clear and convincing evidence** before fundamentally altering the parent-child relationship.

## VI. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY — INAPPLICABILITY

70. Plaintiff acknowledges the doctrine of absolute judicial immunity as set forth in *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349 (1978), and *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9 (1991).

71. Judicial immunity does not apply to the claims asserted herein against Defendant Judge McNeill because:

   a. **Non-judicial acts**: The off-record, *ex parte* receipt and consideration of evidence (including a USB recording and mental-health evaluation) outside the context of any judicial proceeding constitutes an administrative or executive function, not a judicial act. *Mireles*, 502 U.S. at 11 ("judicial immunity is not overcome by allegations of bad faith or malice" but is overcome when the act is "not taken in the judge's judicial capacity" or is "taken in the complete absence of all jurisdiction").

   b. **Absence of jurisdiction**: To the extent that *ex parte* orders were entered without compliance with mandatory procedural prerequisites — including the verified-pleading and specific-factual-finding requirements of MCR 3.207(B)(1) and (B)(5)–(6) — Defendant Judge McNeill acted in the **clear absence of all jurisdiction**. *Stump*, 435 U.S. at 356–57.

   c. **Prospective injunctive relief**: To the extent judicial immunity bars damages claims, Plaintiff seeks prospective injunctive and declaratory relief, which is not barred by judicial immunity. *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522, 541–42 (1984); 42 U.S.C. § 1983 (as amended by the Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1996).

## VII. ROOKER-FELDMAN DOCTRINE — INAPPLICABILITY

72. This action does not seek appellate review of the state-court judgments. Rather, Plaintiff challenges the **process by which** the state-court orders were entered — specifically, the deprivation of constitutional rights through *ex parte* proceedings, retaliatory sanctions, and systematic bias.

73. The Rooker-Feldman doctrine is limited to "cases brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments rendered before the district court proceedings commenced and inviting district court review and rejection of those judgments." *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp.*, 544 U.S. 280, 284 (2005).

74. Plaintiff's § 1983 claims are independent of the state-court judgments. They challenge the constitutionality of the procedures employed, not the merits of the custody determination. *See Skinner v. Switzer*, 562 U.S. 521, 532 (2011); *Bolden v. City of Topeka*, 441 F.3d 1129, 1143 (10th Cir. 2006).

## VIII. YOUNGER ABSTENTION — INAPPLICABILITY

75. To the extent Defendants argue that *Younger v. Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971), requires abstention, Plaintiff respectfully submits that the *Younger* doctrine does not apply because:

   a. The state-court proceedings do not afford Plaintiff an adequate opportunity to raise his federal constitutional claims, as demonstrated by the systematic denial of due process and access to courts;

   b. The state proceedings are tainted by **bad faith, harassment, and flagrant unconstitutional conduct** sufficient to invoke the recognized exceptions to *Younger* abstention. *Younger*, 401 U.S. at 53–54; *Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124–25 (1975);

   c. Plaintiff's claims challenge systemic procedural deficiencies, not the merits of ongoing state proceedings.
