## II. LEGAL STANDARD

### A. The IFP Statute

28 U.S.C. § 1915(a)(1) provides that "any court of the United States may authorize the commencement, prosecution or defense of any suit, action or proceeding, civil or criminal, or appeal therein, without prepayment of fees or security therefor, by a person who submits an affidavit that includes a statement of all assets such person possesses [and] that the person is unable to pay such fees or give security therefor."

### B. The Standard for Granting IFP Status

The standard for IFP status does not require **destitution**. A plaintiff need only demonstrate that payment of the filing fee would "constitute a serious hardship on the plaintiff, not necessarily that plaintiff's financial position is combletely combletely combletely combletely combletely combletely combletely devoid of funds." *See Adkins v. E.I. DuPont de Nemours & Co.*, 335 U.S. 331, 339 (1948) (the statute "is designed to ensure that indigent litigants have meaningful access to the federal courts").

The inquiry is whether, "in view of all of the financial circumstances of the applicant," requiring payment of fees would be unduly burdensome. *See Foster v. Ludsin*, 2 F. Supp. 2d 1117, 1120 (N.D. Ind. 1998).

### C. The Frivolity Screen

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1915(e)(2), a court may dismiss an IFP action if it finds the action is frivolous, malicious, fails to state a claim, or seeks monetary relief from a defendant who is immune. This provision protects the court from abuse but should not be used to deny meritorious claims brought by indigent plaintiffs.

### B. Plaintiff's Claims Are Non-Frivolous and Meritorious

The Court must assess whether the claims asserted clear the threshold of non-frivolousness. *See Neitzke v. Williams*, 490 U.S. 319, 325 (1989) (a claim is frivolous only if it "lacks an arguable basis either in law or in fact").

Plaintiff's claims easily clear this threshold:

#### 1. Legal Basis Is Well-Established

Plaintiff's claims rest on firmly established constitutional law:

- **The fundamental right to parent** is among the oldest and most firmly established liberty interests recognized by the Supreme Court. *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923); *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000).

- **42 U.S.C. § 1983** provides a cause of action for deprivation of constitutional rights under color of state law. *Monroe v. Pape*, 365 U.S. 167 (1961).

- **42 U.S.C. § 1985** provides a cause of action for conspiracies to deprive constitutional rights. *Griffin v. Breckenridge*, 403 U.S. 88 (1971).

- **First Amendment retaliation claims** are cognizable under § 1983 when adverse action is taken in response to the exercise of constitutionally protected activity. *Thaddeus-X v. Blatter*, 175 F.3d 378 (6th Cir. 1999).

#### 2. Factual Basis Is Documented and Substantial

Unlike cases where IFP applicants present speculative or conclusory allegations, Plaintiff's claims are supported by an extraordinary evidentiary record:

- **1,127 documented violations** — not allegations of a single incident, but a systematically cataloged pattern of constitutional deprivations;
- **571+ days of forced separation** — a concrete, measurable, ongoing harm;
- **Pattern evidence** demonstrating uniformly adverse directionality, temporal correlation with protected activity, and coordination among Defendants;
- **Bates-numbered exhibits** preserving the documentary record.

The scope and specificity of the factual record distinguishes this case from the vast majority of *pro se* civil rights filings and demonstrates a well-grounded, meritorious claim.

#### 3. Claims Are Not Barred by Immunity

While judicial immunity presents a potential defense, Plaintiff has carefully structured this action to address immunity concerns:

- Claims against Defendant McNeill are brought in **official capacity for prospective injunctive relief**, which is not barred by judicial immunity. *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984).
- Claims against Defendants Watson and Barnes are brought against **private actors** who are not entitled to judicial immunity.
- The § 1985 conspiracy claims reach all Defendants without implicating judicial immunity. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980).

### C. Access to Federal Courts Is Essential

This case presents a paradigm example of why the IFP statute exists:

1. **Federal jurisdiction is the only available forum.** Plaintiff's claims arise under the federal Constitution and federal civil rights statutes. State courts — the very tribunals that generated the constitutional violations — cannot serve as adequate forums for their adjudication.

2. **The constitutional violations are ongoing.** Every day that passes without federal intervention is a day of continuing constitutional deprivation. Financial barriers to filing prolong the violation.

3. **The Civil Rights Act of 1871 was enacted precisely for this situation.** Section 1983 was designed to provide a federal remedy when state systems fail to protect constitutional rights. Denying IFP status to a plaintiff alleging systematic state-actor deprivation of fundamental rights would frustrate the core purpose of the statute.

4. **The right at stake is fundamental.** The Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized the parent-child relationship as among the most sacred of constitutional protections. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982). Financial inability to pay a filing fee should not foreclose vindication of such a right.

### D. Plaintiff Has Not Abused the IFP Privilege

Plaintiff has not previously filed three or more federal actions dismissed as frivolous, malicious, or for failure to state a claim. The "three strikes" provision of 28 U.S.C. § 1915(g) is therefore inapplicable.
