## I. INTRODUCTION

1\. This is a civil rights action brought pursuant to 42 U.S.C. § 1983, 42 U.S.C. § 1985(3), and 42 U.S.C. § 1988 to redress the deprivation under color of state law of Plaintiff Andrew J. Pigors's rights secured by the Due Process Clause, Equal Protection Clause, and First Amendment of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, and to redress a conspiracy to deprive Plaintiff of equal protection and equal privileges and immunities under the law.

2\. For more than 329 consecutive days and counting, Plaintiff — a fit, loving father with no finding of abuse, neglect, or unfitness — has been completely separated from his child, L.D.W. (born November 9, 2022), now in a critical developmental window. This separation was imposed through five ex parte orders entered on a single day — August 8, 2025 — without notice, without a hearing, without written findings, and without any process whatsoever. These orders effectively terminated Plaintiff's fundamental liberty interest in the care, custody, and companionship of his child without the constitutionally required clear-and-convincing evidentiary standard mandated by *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982), and without any of the procedural safeguards required by the Fourteenth Amendment.

3\. The deprivations alleged herein were not the product of isolated error. They arose from a pattern of coordinated action between a state-court judge acting outside her judicial capacity and a private litigant exploiting ex parte access, sustained by a county that maintained policies and customs permitting systematic denial of due process in its family-court division. Defendant McNeill is sued solely for administrative and non-judicial acts falling outside the protection of judicial immunity, and for acts taken in the complete absence of all jurisdiction, consistent with *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9 (1991), and *Pulliam v Allen*, 466 US 522 (1984).

8A\. This action is timely. ## V-A. PREEMPTIVE RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED DEFENSES

### 1. Judicial Immunity Does Not Apply

Defendants will argue that Judge McNeill is entitled to absolute judicial immunity. This defense fails for two independent reasons:

**First**, judicial immunity does not extend to **non-judicial acts** — acts that are administrative, executive, or ministerial in nature. *Forrester v White*, 484 US 219, 229 (1988); *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9, 11 (1991) (per curiam). The following acts by Defendant McNeill were non-judicial:

- Directing evidence submission through a personal email to her secretary rather than through the court clerk (*MCR 8.119 violation*) — an administrative act;
- The secretary's ex parte communications with Defendant Watson regarding PPO/jail status on November 29, 2024 — an administrative act;
- Pamela Rusco's ex parte telephone call to HealthWest on October 29, 2025 to arrange testimony — an administrative act directed or ratified by the court; and
- Imposing a $250 bond on future filings without statutory authority — an administrative act outside the judicial function.

**Second**, judicial immunity does not apply where a judge acts "in the clear absence of all jurisdiction." *Stump v Sparkman*, 435 US 349, 357 (1978). Suspending all parenting time through ex parte orders without any emergency finding, without notice, without a hearing, and without written best-interest findings under MCL 722.23 — when no statute or court rule authorizes such action — constitutes action in the clear absence of jurisdiction.

### 2. Qualified Immunity Does Not Apply

Defendants may invoke qualified immunity. Qualified immunity shields officials from liability unless their conduct violates "clearly established" statutory or constitutional rights. *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800, 818 (1982). The defense fails here because:

**(a)** The right of a parent to the care and custody of his child was clearly established at the time of the violations. *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982).

**(b)** The right to notice and a hearing before deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest was clearly established. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976); *Fuentes v Shevin*, 407 US 67 (1972).

**(c)** No reasonable official could believe that suspending all parenting time for 329+ days, without any hearing, any evidence of abuse or neglect, any emergency finding, or any written best-interest analysis, was lawful. *See Hope v Pelzer*, 536 US 730, 741 (2002) (officials can be on notice that their conduct violates established law "even in novel factual circumstances").

### 3. Rooker-Feldman Doctrine Does Not Apply

The Rooker-Feldman doctrine bars federal review of claims that are "inextricably intertwined" with state-court judgments. *Exxon Mobil Corp. v Saudi Basic Indus. Corp.*, 544 US 280, 284 (2005). This doctrine does not apply here because:

**(a)** Plaintiff does not seek reversal of a final state-court judgment. The August 8, 2025 ex parte orders were entered without any adjudication and remain subject to ongoing proceedings. *See Bolden v City of Topeka*, 441 F.3d 1129, 1143 (10th Cir. 2006) (Rooker-Feldman inapplicable to interlocutory orders).

**(b)** Plaintiff challenges the **process** by which his rights were taken — the absence of notice, hearing, and findings — not the **merits** of any custody determination. Claims challenging the constitutionality of judicial procedures are independent of the underlying judgment. *Plyler v Moore*, 129 F.3d 728, 731 (4th Cir. 1997).

**(c)** Plaintiff challenges **non-judicial administrative acts** (evidence routing through secretary, ex parte contacts, $250 bond) that are not "judgments" subject to Rooker-Feldman at all.

### 4. Younger Abstention Does Not Apply

Younger v. Harris, 401 US 37 (1971), counsels federal courts to abstain from interfering with ongoing state proceedings that implicate important state interests. Younger does not apply here because:

**(a)** The "bad faith" exception applies. Where state proceedings "are brought to harass," Younger abstention is inapplicable. *Younger*, 401 U.S. at 53-54. The coordinated campaign — filing a police report on August 7 specifically to obtain an ex parte order on August 8 (per Albert Watson's admission), combined with the 44% ex parte rate — constitutes bad faith.

**(b)** The "patently unconstitutional" exception applies. Where state proceedings are "flagrantly and patently violative of express constitutional prohibitions," Younger does not apply. *Younger*, 401 U.S. at 53. Five ex parte orders eliminating all parental rights with zero process is patently unconstitutional.

**(c)** There is no adequate state remedy. Plaintiff was told "Do not file any more. I will not look at it." The $250 bond and filing ban effectively close the state court to Plaintiff, rendering state remedies inadequate. *See Gibson v Berryhill*, 411 US 564, 577 (1973) (abstention inappropriate where state proceedings are "inadequate to protect federal rights").
