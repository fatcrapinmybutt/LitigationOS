## I. THE ROOKER-FELDMAN DOCTRINE DOES NOT BAR THIS ACTION

### A. The Doctrine Is Narrow

4\. The Rooker-Feldman doctrine, derived from *Rooker v. Fidelity Trust Co.*, 263 U.S. 413 (1923), and *District of Columbia Court of Appeals v. Feldman*, 460 U.S. 462 (1983), divests federal district courts of jurisdiction only over cases "brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments rendered before the district court proceedings commenced and inviting district court review and rejection of those judgments." *Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp.*, 544 U.S. 280, 284 (2005).

5\. The Supreme Court has repeatedly emphasized the doctrine's narrow scope. It is "confined to cases . . . brought by state-court losers complaining of injuries *caused by* state-court judgments." *Id.* at 284 (emphasis added). It does not bar federal claims that are merely "related to" or "intertwined with" a state court proceeding. The doctrine "is not simply preclusion by another name." *Lance v. Dennis*, 546 U.S. 459, 466 (2006).

### B. The Sixth Circuit Applies Rooker-Feldman Narrowly

6\. The Sixth Circuit in *McCormick v. Braverman*, 451 F.3d 382, 393 (6th Cir. 2006), adopted the Supreme Court's narrow reading and held that Rooker-Feldman applies only where (1) the federal plaintiff lost in state court; (2) the plaintiff complains of injuries caused by the state court judgment itself; (3) the plaintiff invites federal review and rejection of that judgment; and (4) the state court judgment was rendered before the federal suit commenced. All four elements must be satisfied.

7\. The Sixth Circuit has further clarified that "the pertinent inquiry is the 'source of the injury' the plaintiff alleges." *Id.* at 393 (quoting *Exxon Mobil*, 544 U.S. at 284). If the source is conduct by state actors rather than the judgment itself, the doctrine does not apply.

### C. Plaintiff's Claims Are Independent Federal Claims

8\. This Complaint does not ask the Court to reverse, vacate, modify, or set aside any state court custody determination. Plaintiff does not argue that the state court reached the wrong substantive result on custody. Rather, Plaintiff asserts that the *process* by which he was deprived of his fundamental parental rights, his liberty, and his First Amendment rights was unconstitutionally deficient — regardless of the outcome of any state court proceeding.

9\. The "source of injury" in each of Plaintiff's five counts is *conduct*, not a *judgment*:

&emsp;(a) **Count I (Due Process)**: The injury is the denial of notice and hearing before deprivation — not the custody ruling itself. Even if the state court had reached identical custody conclusions after providing constitutionally adequate process, this claim would not exist. The injury is the *absence of process*;

&emsp;(b) **Count II (First Amendment Retaliation)**: The injury is a 45-day incarceration imposed as punishment for constitutionally protected speech (birthday messages to a child). No state court judgment is implicated — the claim is that the *punishment* violates the First Amendment;

&emsp;(c) **Count III (Fourth Amendment)**: The injury is 59 days of incarceration without probable cause. This is a seizure claim, not a challenge to any judgment;

&emsp;(d) **Count IV (§ 1985(3) Conspiracy)**: The injury is the coordinated deprivation of rights through a concerted pattern of ex parte adjudication, filing screening, and extrajudicial collaboration;

&emsp;(e) **Count V (Monell)**: The injury is the County's deliberate indifference to a pattern of unconstitutional conduct.

10\. None of these claims would be "rendered moot" or "made meaningless" by any particular custody outcome. They are independent constitutional violations actionable under § 1983 regardless of the state court's substantive determination. *See Catz v. Chalker*, 142 F.3d 279, 293 (6th Cir. 1998) (§ 1983 due process claim is "separate and distinct from any challenge to the underlying state court order").

11\. Rooker-Feldman therefore poses no jurisdictional bar.

## II. YOUNGER ABSTENTION DOES NOT APPLY

### A. The Doctrine's Scope After Sprint Communications

12\. In *Younger v. Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971), the Supreme Court held that principles of equity, comity, and federalism counsel federal courts to abstain from interfering with certain ongoing state proceedings. However, in *Sprint Communications, Inc. v. Jacobs*, 571 U.S. 69 (2013), the Court dramatically narrowed *Younger* to only three "exceptional" categories:

&emsp;(a) Ongoing state criminal prosecutions;

&emsp;(b) Civil enforcement proceedings akin to criminal prosecutions; and

&emsp;(c) Civil proceedings uniquely in furtherance of the state courts' ability to perform their judicial functions.

*Id.* at 78. The Court held that *Younger* "extends to the three 'exceptional circumstances' identified in *Middlesex*'s trilogy" and no further. *Id.* at 82.

13\. A private custody proceeding between two parents does not fall within any of these three exceptional categories. It is not a criminal prosecution, not a civil enforcement action akin to a criminal prosecution, and not a proceeding in furtherance of state judicial functions. *See Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n*, 457 U.S. 423, 432 (1982) (third category limited to proceedings like attorney disciplinary hearings and contempt of court actions *integral to the administration of judicial authority*).

### B. The Sixth Circuit Applies Younger Narrowly Post-Sprint

14\. In *Fieger v. Cox*, 524 F.3d 770, 776 (6th Cir. 2008), the Sixth Circuit held that *Younger* abstention is reserved for "extraordinary circumstances" and "should not be applied reflexively." The court must analyze whether the state proceeding actually implicates the *Sprint* categories.

15\. Moreover, even where a state proceeding arguably fits a *Sprint* category, the Sixth Circuit recognizes that the "bad faith" and "patently unconstitutional" exceptions apply. *Fieger*, 524 F.3d at 776; *see also Kugler v. Helfant*, 421 U.S. 117, 124–25 (1975) (federal court need not abstain "where the danger of irreparable loss is both great and immediate" or where "proceedings are brought . . . in bad faith").

### C. The Bad Faith Exception Applies

16\. Even if *Younger* were otherwise applicable, the "bad faith prosecution" exception squarely applies here. The following facts, alleged in the Complaint and supported by state court records, demonstrate bad faith:

&emsp;(a) **68 ex parte orders — a 44% ex parte rate** — constitute 3.65 times the rate that would be expected if ex parte proceedings were reserved for genuine emergencies. This is not judicial discretion; it is a systematic pattern of depriving one party of notice and hearing;

&emsp;(b) **Muting Plaintiff three times** during the November 15, 2024 Show Cause hearing, physically preventing him from speaking in his own defense;

&emsp;(c) **Refusing to review the exculpatory police report** (Report No. NS2505044) at the hearing, then imposing 14 days of incarceration based on allegations the report contradicted;

&emsp;(d) **Imposing 45 days of incarceration** for sending birthday messages to a child — messages sent through the court-approved AppClose platform — without a finding that any harm occurred;

&emsp;(e) **Screening Plaintiff's filings** through judicial staff (Defendant Rusco) to prevent judicial review, effectively closing the courthouse doors to one party while leaving them open to the other.

17\. These facts, taken together, "demonstrate bad faith, harassment, or any other unusual circumstance that would call for equitable relief." *Younger*, 401 U.S. at 54.

### D. The State Court Cannot Adjudicate § 1983 Claims Against Its Own Judge

18\. A fundamental premise of *Younger* is that the state forum provides an "adequate opportunity" to raise federal claims. *Middlesex*, 457 U.S. at 432. But where the federal claim is *against the judge presiding over the state proceeding*, no adequate state remedy exists. A litigant cannot raise a § 1983 claim against the very judge presiding over his case. The inherent conflict of interest renders the state forum structurally inadequate, and *Younger* abstention cannot apply where the state forum cannot provide relief. *See Gibson v. Berryhill*, 411 U.S. 564, 577 (1973) (abstention inappropriate where state forum is biased or lacks capacity to hear the claim).

19\. Younger abstention therefore does not apply.

## III. HECK v. HUMPHREY DOES NOT BAR THESE CLAIMS

### A. The Heck Favorable-Termination Requirement

20\. In *Heck v. Humphrey*, 512 U.S. 477, 486–87 (1994), the Supreme Court held that a § 1983 damages claim is not cognizable if "a judgment in favor of the plaintiff would *necessarily imply* the invalidity of his conviction or sentence," unless the plaintiff demonstrates that the conviction or sentence has already been invalidated.

21\. The operative word is "*necessarily*." *Heck* bars only those claims where success would *necessarily* impugn the validity of a conviction or sentence. It does not bar claims where success would merely *call into question* the procedures used. *Id.* at 487 n.7.

### B. Plaintiff Does Not Challenge the PPO Violation Conviction

22\. Plaintiff does not assert in this action that he was innocent of the conduct underlying the contempt findings. Plaintiff does not seek to invalidate the contempt adjudications. Instead, Plaintiff challenges the *process* by which those adjudications were reached — specifically:

&emsp;(a) The denial of the right to be heard (muting three times);

&emsp;(b) The denial of the right to present exculpatory evidence (refusal to review police report);

&emsp;(c) The denial of the right to counsel before incarceration;

&emsp;(d) The imposition of incarceration as punishment for protected speech (birthday messages).

23\. A judgment in Plaintiff's favor on these claims would not *necessarily* imply that Plaintiff was innocent of the underlying conduct. It would establish only that the *process* by which he was adjudicated and incarcerated was constitutionally deficient. Due process claims and the underlying merits are distinct inquiries. One can be guilty of the underlying conduct and still have been deprived of due process.

### C. The Sixth Circuit Distinguishes Process from Outcome

24\. The Sixth Circuit has consistently held that *Heck* does not bar § 1983 claims challenging the procedures used in a proceeding — as opposed to the outcome. In *Schreiber v. Moe*, 596 F.3d 323, 334 (6th Cir. 2010), the court recognized that due process claims based on deficient procedures do not necessarily imply the invalidity of the resulting judgment, because a constitutional hearing might have produced the same result.

25\. Similarly, in *Powers v. Hamilton County Public Defender Commission*, 501 F.3d 592, 603 (6th Cir. 2007), the Sixth Circuit held that a § 1983 claim based on deprivation of the right to counsel does not *necessarily* imply the invalidity of the conviction, because competent counsel might have reached the same outcome.

### D. The First Amendment Retaliation Claim Is Categorically Outside Heck

26\. Count II — First Amendment Retaliation — asserts that the 45-day incarceration was imposed *as punishment for* constitutionally protected speech. A judgment in Plaintiff's favor on this claim would not imply that Plaintiff did not send the birthday messages; it would establish that punishing a parent for sending birthday messages to his child violates the First Amendment. The conduct occurred. The question is whether the *government's response* was constitutional. *Heck* is simply inapplicable to this inquiry.

### E. Spencer v. Kemna Preserves § 1983 Claims After Release

27\. To the extent Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claims are moot because the contempt sentences have been served, the Supreme Court has held that the expiration of a sentence does not moot a § 1983 claim for damages arising from the unconstitutional deprivation. *Spencer v. Kemna*, 523 U.S. 1, 17 (1998) (Souter, J., concurring) (recognizing that § 1983 damages claims survive completion of sentence); *see also Wilson v. Johnson*, 535 F.3d 262, 265–66 (4th Cir. 2008) (applying same principle).

28\. *Heck* therefore poses no bar to any of Plaintiff's five counts.

## IV. QUALIFIED IMMUNITY DOES NOT SHIELD DEFENDANTS

### A. The Qualified Immunity Framework

29\. Under *Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800, 818 (1982), government officials performing discretionary functions are entitled to qualified immunity unless their conduct violates "clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known."

30\. The Supreme Court in *Saucier v. Katz*, 533 U.S. 194, 201 (2001), established a two-prong analysis: (1) whether the facts alleged show the defendant violated a constitutional right; and (2) whether the right was "clearly established" at the time of the violation. *See also Pearson v. Callahan*, 555 U.S. 223, 236 (2009) (courts may address either prong first).

31\. Defendants McNeill, Rusco, Watson, and Barnes cannot satisfy either prong.

### B. Prong One: Constitutional Violations Occurred

32\. **Due Process**: The right to notice and an opportunity to be heard before the government deprives a person of a fundamental liberty interest has been settled law since at least *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). The right of a parent to the care, custody, and companionship of his child is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000). Issuing 68 ex parte orders, muting Plaintiff, refusing to review exculpatory evidence, and incarcerating Plaintiff for 59 days without constitutionally adequate hearings violated this right.

33\. **First Amendment**: A parent's right to communicate with his child is a core liberty protected by the First Amendment. *See Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65. Imposing 45 days of incarceration for birthday messages sent through a court-approved communication platform constitutes content-based punishment of protected speech that would chill a person of ordinary firmness from engaging in such speech. *See Pickering v. Board of Education*, 391 U.S. 563, 568 (1968).

34\. **Fourth Amendment**: Incarceration without probable cause and without adequate process constitutes an unreasonable seizure. *See Baker v. McCollan*, 443 U.S. 137, 142 (1979).

35\. **Right of Access to Courts**: Defendant Rusco's screening of Plaintiff's filings deprived Plaintiff of his constitutional right of access to the courts. *See Christopher v. Harbury*, 536 U.S. 403, 415 (2002). A judicial employee who prevents a litigant's filings from reaching the judge has effectively closed the courthouse doors.

### C. Prong Two: Every Right Was Clearly Established

36\. Every right at issue in this case was clearly established decades before the conduct alleged in the Complaint:

| Constitutional Right | Clearly Established Since |
|---|---|
| Right to notice and hearing before deprivation of fundamental liberty | *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) |
| Fundamental right to parent | *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923); *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) |
| Right against incarceration without probable cause | *Gerstein v. Pugh*, 420 U.S. 103 (1975) |
| Right to counsel before incarceration for contempt | *Mead v. Batchlor*, 435 Mich. 480 (1990) |
| First Amendment protection of parent-child speech | *Troxel*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) |
| Right of access to courts | *Bounds v. Smith*, 430 U.S. 817 (1977) |

37\. No reasonable official could have believed that issuing 68 ex parte orders, incarcerating a father for 45 days for birthday messages, muting a litigant three times during a hearing, or screening a litigant's filings to prevent judicial review was lawful. These are not close calls on the margins of constitutional law. They are fundamental violations of bedrock constitutional protections established over the course of a century.

### D. Watson and Barnes Cannot Invoke Qualified Immunity

38\. Qualified immunity is available only to government officials performing *discretionary functions*. *Harlow*, 457 U.S. at 818. Defendants Watson and Barnes are private parties, not government officials. Private parties cannot invoke qualified immunity as a defense. *Wyatt v. Cole*, 504 U.S. 158, 168–69 (1992).

39\. To the extent Watson and Barnes acted in concert with state actors to deprive Plaintiff of his rights, they are liable under § 1983 and § 1985(3) without the shield of qualified immunity. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 27–28 (1980).

40\. Qualified immunity therefore does not shield any Defendant from liability.

## V. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY DOES NOT PROTECT JUDGE McNEILL

### A. The General Rule and Its Exceptions

41\. Judges enjoy absolute immunity from damages for acts performed in their judicial capacity. *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349, 356 (1978). However, this immunity is subject to two well-established exceptions: (1) acts taken in the "clear absence of all jurisdiction," and (2) acts that are not "judicial in nature." *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11–12 (1991).

42\. Additionally, judicial immunity does not bar prospective injunctive and declaratory relief. *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522, 541–42 (1984); 42 U.S.C. § 1983 (as amended by the Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1996, providing that "injunctive relief shall not be granted" against a judicial officer "unless a declaratory decree was violated or declaratory relief was unavailable").

### B. Exception One: Clear Absence of All Jurisdiction

43\. A judge acts in the "clear absence of all jurisdiction" when the judge's actions are so far beyond the scope of judicial authority that no reasonable judge could have believed the actions were within the court's power. *Stump*, 435 U.S. at 356–57.

44\. Judge McNeill's conduct falls within this exception. The Due Process Clause — which operates as a *jurisdictional prerequisite* to the valid exercise of judicial power — requires notice and an opportunity to be heard before deprivation of a fundamental liberty interest. When a court enters orders depriving a parent of all contact with his child without any notice and without any hearing, the court is not exercising jurisdiction — it is exercising raw power without constitutional authority.

45\. The systematic nature of these deprivations — 68 ex parte orders constituting a 44% ex parte rate — distinguishes this case from the isolated ex parte emergency order that might be justified by exigent circumstances. No exigent circumstances were found. No emergency was declared. No sworn facts demonstrating imminent danger were presented. Instead, the court simply adopted a practice of routinely adjudicating one party's rights without his knowledge.

46\. A court that routinely adjudicates a party's fundamental rights without notice operates in the "clear absence of all jurisdiction" with respect to each such order.

### C. Exception Two: Non-Judicial Acts

47\. Judicial immunity protects only "judicial" acts — acts that are "normally performed by a judge" and undertaken in the judge's "judicial capacity." *Stump*, 435 U.S. at 362. The Supreme Court in *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 229 (1988), held that administrative, legislative, and executive acts by judges are not protected by judicial immunity, because "the factors which support immunity for [judicial] acts are not present."

48\. Several categories of Judge McNeill's conduct are non-judicial:

&emsp;(a) **Directing staff to screen filings**: Defendant Rusco screened Plaintiff's filings and prevented them from reaching judicial review. If Judge McNeill directed this screening, the directive was an *administrative* act — the assignment and supervision of staff duties — not a judicial act. *See Forrester*, 484 U.S. at 229 (demoting a probation officer was an administrative act not protected by immunity);

&emsp;(b) **Refusing to perform judicial functions**: A judge who states that she will not review a party's filings — effectively refusing to adjudicate — is not performing a judicial act. A judicial act requires, at minimum, the exercise of judicial judgment. Categorical refusal to consider one party's submissions is the *absence* of judicial action, not the *exercise* of it;

&emsp;(c) **Conspiratorial conduct**: When a judge conspires with private parties to deprive a litigant of constitutional rights, the conspiratorial conduct falls outside the scope of judicial immunity because conspiracy is not a function normally performed by a judge. *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24, 31 (1980) ("immunity . . . would not be available for a judge who conspired with one party");

&emsp;(d) **Systematic pattern as administrative policy**: The issuance of 68 ex parte orders in a single case — 44% of all court actions — constitutes a systemic *administrative policy* of adjudication rather than individual case-by-case *judicial decisions*. When conduct reflects a policy rather than a case-specific judgment, it is administrative in nature. *Forrester*, 484 U.S. at 229.

### D. Injunctive and Declaratory Relief Are Available Regardless

49\. Even if judicial immunity barred damages (which it does not on these facts), it would not bar the injunctive and declaratory relief sought in the Complaint. Under *Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522, 541–42 (1984), judicial immunity does not extend to claims for prospective injunctive relief.

50\. Plaintiff seeks, inter alia, an injunction prohibiting Judge McNeill from presiding over any further proceedings involving Plaintiff or L.D.W., and a declaratory judgment that the conduct alleged violated Plaintiff's constitutional rights. These forms of relief are available against judicial officers regardless of the immunity analysis.

51\. The Sixth Circuit has confirmed this principle. In *Barnes v. Winchell*, 105 F.3d 1111, 1115 (6th Cir. 1997), the court held that judicial immunity does not protect acts taken in the "clear absence of all jurisdiction" or non-judicial acts, and that prospective relief remains available.

52\. Judicial immunity therefore does not protect Judge McNeill from the claims asserted in this Complaint.
