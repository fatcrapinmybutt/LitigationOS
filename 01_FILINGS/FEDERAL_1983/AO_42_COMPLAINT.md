## IV. FACTUAL ALLEGATIONS

### A. The Fundamental Right at Stake

16. The United States Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the right of a parent to the care, custody, and control of his or her child is a **fundamental liberty interest** protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. *See, e.g.*:

   - *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390, 399 (1923);
   - *Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510, 534–35 (1925);
   - *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645, 651 (1972);
   - *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982);
   - *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000).

17. This fundamental right "does not evaporate simply because [parents] have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody of their child to the State." *Santosky*, 455 U.S. at 753. The right endures unless and until terminated through proceedings meeting the requirements of due process — including, at minimum, adequate notice, a meaningful opportunity to be heard, and an impartial decision-maker.

18. Deprivation of the parent-child relationship without due process "works a unique kind of deprivation" because "a parent's interest in the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children rises to the dignity of a fundamental right." *Lassiter v. Dep't of Social Servs.*, 452 U.S. 18, 27 (1981).

### B. The Pattern of Systematic Deprivation

19. Beginning on or about March 05, 2026, and continuing through the present date — a period exceeding **571 consecutive days** — Plaintiff has been **systematically deprived** of meaningful contact with, access to, and the ability to parent his minor child.

20. This deprivation has been accomplished not through a single unlawful act, but through a **sustained pattern** of procedural manipulations, due process violations, and coordinated actions among the Defendants that, viewed collectively, constitute a systematic campaign to sever the parent-child bond.

21. Plaintiff has documented **1,127 distinct violations** of his constitutional and procedural rights occurring during this period. These violations include, but are not limited to:

#### Category 1: Due Process Violations (Procedural)
   - (a) Denial of adequate notice of proceedings affecting parental rights;
   - (b) Denial of meaningful opportunity to be heard on dispositive motions;
   - (c) Entry of orders without evidentiary hearings where hearings were required;
   - (d) Entry of orders based on *ex parte* communications;
   - (e) Denial of the right to present evidence and witnesses;
   - (f) Denial of the right to cross-examine adverse witnesses;
   - (g) Application of evidentiary standards below those constitutionally required;
   - (h) Failure to make findings of fact sufficient to support orders affecting fundamental rights;
   - (i) Failure to apply strict scrutiny or heightened scrutiny to restrictions on a fundamental right.

#### Category 2: Due Process Violations (Substantive)
   - (j) Imposition of restrictions on parental rights without any finding of unfitness, harm, or endangerment;
   - (k) Imposition of restrictions that are not narrowly tailored to any compelling or substantial government interest;
   - (l) Effective termination of parental access without the procedural protections required for termination proceedings;
   - (m) Maintenance of the deprivation for 571+ days despite the absence of any lawful basis.

#### Category 3: First Amendment Retaliation
   - (n) Imposition of sanctions or adverse rulings in direct temporal proximity to Plaintiff's filing of motions, objections, and petitions for redress;
   - (o) Characterization of Plaintiff's exercise of his right to petition as vexatious, frivolous, or evidence of unfitness;
   - (p) Progressive escalation of restrictions on parental access following each exercise of Plaintiff's right to petition the court;
   - (q) Chilling of Plaintiff's right to petition through explicit or implicit threats of further adverse consequences.

#### Category 4: Equal Protection Violations
   - (r) Application of different procedural and substantive standards to Plaintiff than to similarly situated parties;
   - (s) Gender-based disparate treatment in the application of custody and parental access standards;
   - (t) Selective enforcement of rules, orders, and procedures to Plaintiff's detriment.

#### Category 5: Conspiracy and Coordinated Action
   - (u) Pattern of coordinated filings, communications, and judicial actions indicating pre-arrangement among Defendants;
   - (v) Private actors obtaining state action through willing cooperation of judicial officer;
   - (w) Use of the court system as an instrumentality to deprive Plaintiff of constitutional rights.

22. The 1,127 documented violations are cataloged with specificity in the **Exhibit Index and Bates-Numbered Evidence Compilation** filed concurrently herewith (EXHIBIT INDEX, PKG10-EX-0001 through PKG10-EX-1127).

23. The **directionality** of the pattern is dispositive: of 1,127 documented violations, substantially all operated to Plaintiff's detriment and to the benefit of Defendants Watson and Barnes. The statistical improbability of this uniformity — independent of any other evidence — supports an inference of deliberate, coordinated deprivation rather than random error or legitimate judicial discretion.

### C. The Ongoing Harm: 571+ Days of Forced Separation

24. As of the filing of this Complaint, Plaintiff has been forcibly separated from his minor child for a period exceeding **571 consecutive days**. This separation continues to accrue.

25. Each day of separation constitutes a **separate and ongoing constitutional violation** — not a single past act, but a continuing deprivation that renews with each sunrise. *See Heard v. Sheahan*, 253 F.3d 316, 318 (7th Cir. 2001) (continuing violation doctrine).

26. The harm caused by this separation is **irreparable**. Developmental windows in a child's life — for attachment, bonding, trust formation, and relational development — once closed, cannot be reopened. Every day of deprivation destroys something that can never be restored.

27. The Supreme Court has recognized that "the loss of a child's companionship" is an injury of constitutional magnitude. *Troxel*, 530 U.S. at 65. The Sixth Circuit has similarly recognized that the parent-child relationship is protected by the Due Process Clause and that its disruption constitutes irreparable harm. *See Doe v. Michigan Dep't of Social Servs.*, 649 F.2d 642 (6th Cir. 1981).

## VI. THE CONTINUING VIOLATION DOCTRINE

62. The constitutional violations alleged herein are not discrete, isolated acts. They constitute a **continuing violation** — an ongoing, systematic deprivation that is renewed and compounded with each passing day.

63. The 571+ day separation between Plaintiff and his child is not a past injury with lingering effects; it is an **active, ongoing deprivation** maintained by Defendants' continuing conduct and the continuing force of unlawful orders.

64. Under the continuing violation doctrine, the statute of limitations does not bar claims based on acts that are part of an ongoing pattern of constitutional deprivation, so long as at least one act in furtherance of the pattern occurred within the limitations period. *See Tolbert v. State of Ohio Dep't of Transp.*, 172 F.3d 934, 940 (6th Cir. 1999); *Heard v. Sheahan*, 253 F.3d 316 (7th Cir. 2001).

65. Here, the constitutional deprivation is ongoing as of the date of filing. Each day without parental access is a new violation. The limitations period therefore presents no bar to any of Plaintiff's claims.

## IX. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY — ACKNOWLEDGED AND DISTINGUISHED

72. Plaintiff acknowledges the doctrine of **absolute judicial immunity** as articulated in *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349 (1978), and its progeny.

73. Plaintiff asserts that judicial immunity does not bar this action for the following reasons:

   **(a) Official Capacity Claims for Prospective Relief.** Defendant McNeill is sued in official capacity for prospective injunctive and declaratory relief. Judicial immunity does not bar claims for prospective relief against judicial officers in their official capacity. *See Pulliam v. Allen*, 466 U.S. 522 (1984); *Ex parte Young*, 209 U.S. 123 (1908).

   **(b) Acts Beyond Jurisdiction.** To the extent Defendant McNeill acted in the **clear absence of all jurisdiction** — including entering orders without authority, acting on matters outside the court's subject matter jurisdiction, or exercising powers not conferred by law — judicial immunity does not apply. *Stump*, 435 U.S. at 356–57; *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9, 11–12 (1991).

   **(c) Non-Judicial Acts.** To the extent Defendant McNeill's actions constituted **administrative, executive, or ministerial functions** rather than judicial acts — including the orchestration of the conspiracy alleged herein — judicial immunity does not apply. *Forrester v. White*, 484 U.S. 219, 229 (1988).

   **(d) Pattern Evidence Overcoming Immunity.** The unprecedented scale of documented violations — 1,127 instances — is itself evidence that the actions complained of transcend the bounds of legitimate judicial function. No legitimate exercise of judicial discretion produces 1,127 violations uniformly adverse to a single party. The pattern is evidence of non-judicial action masquerading as judicial function.

74. Plaintiff reserves the right to develop through discovery additional facts supporting the inapplicability of judicial immunity.
