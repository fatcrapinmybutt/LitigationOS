## III. CONSTITUTIONAL VIOLATIONS

### A. Substantive Due Process (14th Amendment)
**Fundamental Right:** Parent-child relationship is a fundamental liberty interest. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982).
- *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000)
- *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972)
- *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982)

**Violation:** Complete severance of parental relationship for 571+ days without constitutionally adequate process.

### B. Procedural Due Process (14th Amendment)
**Requirements:** Notice, opportunity to be heard, impartial decision-maker.
- *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976) -- Balancing test
- *Cleveland Board of Education v Loudermill*, 470 US 532 (1985)

**Violations:**
- Ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s entered without notice or hearing
- Orders based on unsworn, unverified allegations
- Denial of opportunity to present evidence/witnesses
- Biased decision-maker (McNeill)

### C. Equal Protection (14th Amendment)
**Claim:** Disparate treatment of pro se father vs. represented mother.
- Different procedural standards applied
- Different evidentiary burdens
- Pattern of ruling asymmetry

## V. CRITICAL LEGAL HURDLES

See 02_IMMUNITY_ANALYSIS.md for detailed analysis of:
1. Judicial immunity (nearly absolute barrier)
2. Rooker-Feldman doctrine (cannot appeal state court decisions in federal court)
3. Younger abstention (federal court defers to ongoing state proceedings)
4. Domestic relations exception
5. Qualified immunity (see *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800; 102 S Ct 2727 (1982)) for non-judicial actors

---

## VI. STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

**Recommendation:** EVALUATE CAREFULLY before filing.
- Judicial immunity makes claims against McNeill extremely difficult
- Rooker-Feldman may bar claims that effectively appeal state court orders
- Younger may require state proceedings to conclude first
- Focus on PROCEDURAL violations (lack of notice/hearing) rather than disagreement with rulings
- Consider timing: file AFTER state proceedings conclude to avoid abstention

**Best path:** File after COA resolves; focus on systemic due process failures, not individual rulings.
