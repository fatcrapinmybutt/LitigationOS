# IMMUNITY AND ABSTENTION ANALYSIS
## Federal 1983 Action -- Pigors v. McNeill et al.

---

## I. JUDICIAL IMMUNITY

### General Rule
Judges are ABSOLUTELY immune from Section 1983 (42 USC 1983) liability for actions taken in their judicial capacity.
- *Stump v Sparkman*, 435 US 349 (1978) -- Broad judicial immunity
- *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9 (1991) -- Immunity even for "grave procedural errors"
- *Pierson v Ray*, 386 US 547 (1967) -- Original immunity doctrine

### Exceptions (NARROW)
1. **Non-judicial acts:** Acts not taken in judicial capacity
   - Administrative actions
   - Actions outside all jurisdiction
2. **Complete absence of jurisdiction:** Judge acted wholly outside subject matter jurisdiction
   - *Stump*: Very high bar -- jurisdiction construed broadly

### Application to McNeill
- Family court orders ARE judicial acts = **immunity likely applies**
- Ex parte order (MCR 3.207(B))s, while potentially improper, are still judicial acts
- Exception analysis: McNeill had jurisdiction over custody case -- no absence argument
- **Assessment: Judicial immunity is a MAJOR barrier to claims against McNeill individually**

### Workaround Strategies
- Focus on injunctive relief (prospective) rather than damages (past)
- Target non-judicial actors (FOC, county) instead
- Frame claims as systemic policy failures (Monell) rather than individual judge conduct

---

## II. ROOKER-FELDMAN DOCTRINE

### Rule
Federal district courts cannot hear cases that are "de facto appeals" of state court judgments.
- *Rooker v Fidelity Trust Co*, 263 US 413 (1923)
- *DC Court of Appeals v Feldman*, 460 US 462 (1983)

### Application
- Claims that custody orders were wrong = BARRED (would effectively reverse state court)
- Claims that PROCESS was unconstitutional = POTENTIALLY NOT BARRED
- Key distinction: Challenging the process, not the outcome

### Strategy
Frame all claims as PROCEDURAL violations:
- "I was denied notice and hearing" (procedural) vs. "The custody order was wrong" (substantive/barred)

---

## III. YOUNGER ABSTENTION

### Rule
Federal courts abstain from interfering with ongoing state proceedings.
- *Younger v Harris*, 401 US 37 (1971)

### Application
- If custody case is STILL PENDING: Federal court will likely abstain
- If custody case is CONCLUDED: Younger does not apply
- **Strategy:** Wait until state proceedings conclude, then file federal action

---

## IV. DOMESTIC RELATIONS EXCEPTION

### Rule
Federal courts generally decline jurisdiction over domestic relations matters.
- *Ankenbrandt v Richards*, 504 US 689 (1992) -- Exception is narrow

### Application
- 1983 claims are NOT domestic relations claims -- they are constitutional claims
- Exception should NOT bar filing, but court may be reluctant
- Frame as civil rights case, not custody dispute

---

## V. QUALIFIED IMMUNITY (see *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800; 102 S Ct 2727 (1982)) (Non-Judicial Actors)

### Standard
Government officials are immune unless they violated "clearly established" constitutional rights.
- *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800 (1982)
- *Ashcroft v al-Kidd*, 563 US 731 (2011)

### Application to FOC/County Employees
- Right to parenting IS clearly established (Troxel)
- Right to procedural due process IS clearly established
- But: Specific conduct must violate specific clearly established right
- **Assessment:** Qualified immunity (see *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800; 102 S Ct 2727 (1982)) is a significant but not insurmountable barrier

---

## VI. OVERALL FEASIBILITY ASSESSMENT

| Barrier | Severity | Workaround |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Judicial Immunity | **CRITICAL** | Target non-judicial actors, seek injunctive relief |
| Rooker-Feldman | **HIGH** | Frame as process challenges, not outcome challenges |
| Younger Abstention | **HIGH** | Wait until state proceedings conclude |
| Domestic Relations | **MEDIUM** | Frame as civil rights, not custody |
| Qualified Immunity (see *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800; 102 S Ct 2727 (1982)) | **MEDIUM** | Cite clearly established parental rights |

### RECOMMENDATION
**Filing Score: 55/100 -- PROCEED WITH CAUTION**
- Best filed AFTER COA appeal concludes
- Focus on procedural due process violations
- Target county/systemic policies (Monell) rather than individual judge
- Consider as leverage/pressure tool alongside JTC complaint
- Consult with civil rights attorney if possible before filing
