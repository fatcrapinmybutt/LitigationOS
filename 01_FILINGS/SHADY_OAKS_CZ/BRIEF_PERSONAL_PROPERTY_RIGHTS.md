### I. INTRODUCTION

This Brief is filed in support of Plaintiff's Emergency Motion for Temporary Restraining Order and Preliminary Injunction to prevent Defendants from seizing, removing, or destroying Plaintiff's mobile home — a 1970 Marlette, VIN 1646732, titled in Plaintiff's name through the Michigan Secretary of State.

The central legal question is straightforward: **Can a landlord seize a tenant's titled personal property without a court order?** Michigan law answers this question with an emphatic **no**.

Under Michigan's comprehensive manufactured housing regulatory framework, a mobile home titled with the Secretary of State is **personal property** belonging to the titleholder. MCL 125.2330i. The landlord owns the land; the tenant owns the home. These are separate legal interests, and the landlord's authority over the land does not extend to the tenant's titled chattel.

Defendants have not merely threatened to violate this principle — they have already done so. On July 17, 2025, Defendants' agents drilled Plaintiff's locks, removed his belongings, and posted a "FREE" sign on his titled property, all without judicial authorization. This Brief establishes that Defendants' conduct violates multiple Michigan statutes, constitutional protections, and well-settled common law principles — and that this Court has both the authority and the obligation to prevent it from happening again.

### II. STATEMENT OF ISSUES PRESENTED

**Issue 1:** Whether a mobile home for which a certificate of title has been issued by the Michigan Secretary of State is personal property under Michigan law, such that the manufactured housing community operator has no ownership interest in it.

> *Plaintiff's Answer:* **Yes.** MCL 125.2330i.

**Issue 2:** Whether a landlord may seize, remove, or dispose of a tenant's titled personal property — including a mobile home — without a valid court order and completion of statutory eviction procedures.

> *Plaintiff's Answer:* **No.** MCL 600.2918; MCL 600.5714; MCL 125.2312f.

**Issue 3:** Whether a landlord's lien, if any exists, extends to a mobile home titled as personal property with the Secretary of State.

> *Plaintiff's Answer:* **No.** MCL 125.2330i; MCL 440.9109.

**Issue 4:** Whether an administratively dissolved Michigan LLC may initiate eviction proceedings or other legal actions beyond winding up its affairs.

> *Plaintiff's Answer:* **No.** MCL 450.4802.

**Issue 5:** Whether the use of four or more separate and undisclosed entity names in dealings with a single tenant creates standing deficiencies and constitutes unfair and deceptive practices.

> *Plaintiff's Answer:* **Yes.** MCL 445.903; MCL 450.4802.

**Issue 6:** Whether the Due Process Clauses of the Michigan and United States Constitutions require notice and an opportunity to be heard before a tenant may be deprived of titled personal property.

> *Plaintiff's Answer:* **Yes.** U.S. Const. Amend. XIV; Mich. Const. 1963, Art. I, § 17.

### IV. ARGUMENT

#### A. A Mobile Home Titled with the Secretary of State Is Personal Property Under Michigan Law — Period.

The Michigan Legislature has spoken clearly and unambiguously on the classification of mobile homes. **MCL 125.2330i** provides:

> *"A manufactured home for which a certificate of title has been issued under the manufactured housing commission act . . . shall be subject to the same laws as those that apply to titled personal property."*

This is not a close question. The statute does not say "may be treated as personal property." It does not say "is presumed to be personal property." It says a titled manufactured home **"is personal property"** and **"shall be subject to the same laws"** as other personal property.

Plaintiff's 1970 Marlette mobile home, VIN 1646732, is titled in Plaintiff's name through the Michigan Secretary of State. It is therefore, as a matter of law, **Plaintiff's personal property** — no different in legal classification than a titled automobile, boat, or other chattel.

The distinction between real property and personal property is critical here because it determines whose property is whose:

| Property Interest | Owner | Legal Basis |
|------------------|-------|-------------|
| **The land** (Lot 17, 1977 Whitehall Rd) | Shady Oaks Park MHP LLC | Deed recorded with Muskegon County Register of Deeds (Parcel No. 09-001-200-0064-00) |
| **The mobile home** (1970 Marlette, VIN 1646732) | Andrew James Pigors | Certificate of Title, Michigan Secretary of State |
| **The lot lease** | Contractual interest | Lease agreement (disputed — multiple entities, multiple versions) |

The lot lease governs Plaintiff's right to keep his home on Defendants' land. Even if Defendants had validly terminated the lease — which Plaintiff vigorously disputes — termination of a lot lease **does not transfer ownership of the mobile home to the landlord.** The mobile home remains Plaintiff's personal property regardless of the status of the underlying tenancy.

**MCL 125.1305** reinforces this framework by defining "manufactured home" and establishing the regulatory regime under which mobile homes are titled, sold, and transported — all as personal property, not real property.

This distinction is not merely academic. It means that when Defendants drilled Plaintiff's locks, removed his belongings, and posted a "FREE" sign on his titled property, they were not merely engaging in an unlawful eviction — they were **converting personal property that belongs to someone else.** This is theft by another name.

#### B. A Landlord Cannot Seize a Tenant's Personal Property Even During Eviction

Michigan has **abolished self-help eviction.** A landlord who wishes to recover possession of premises must proceed through the summary proceedings provided in **MCL 600.5714** and the manufactured housing community procedures established in **MCL 125.2312f**. There is no shortcut, no exception, and no equitable workaround.

**MCL 600.2918** makes this principle enforceable with teeth:

> *"Any person who is ejected or put out of any lands or tenements in a forcible and unlawful manner, or being out is held and kept from the possession thereof by force . . . may recover 3 times the amount of his or her actual damages or $200.00, whichever is greater."*

The statute imposes **treble damages** because the Legislature recognized that self-help eviction is not merely tortious — it is a form of vigilante justice that the legal system cannot tolerate.

In **Stokes v Millen Roofing Co, 466 Mich 660 (2002)**, the Michigan Supreme Court confirmed that landlords must use judicial process to recover possession. Self-help remedies — including changing locks, removing personal property, and shutting off utilities — are unlawful regardless of whether the tenant is in default.

The principle extends with even greater force to the seizure of a tenant's **titled personal property**. Even if Defendants had obtained a valid writ of restitution — which they did not — that writ would authorize the sheriff to restore possession of the **premises** (the lot) to the landlord. It would **not** authorize the landlord to seize, destroy, or dispose of the tenant's mobile home. The home is not part of the premises — it is a separate, titled asset belonging to the tenant.

**MCL 125.2312f** establishes the specific procedures for eviction from manufactured housing communities. These procedures include:

1. Written notice specifying the grounds for eviction;
2. A minimum notice period before proceedings may be commenced;
3. Filing of a complaint in the appropriate court;
4. Service of process upon the tenant;
5. A hearing before a judicial officer;
6. Entry of a judgment of possession;
7. Issuance of a writ of restitution;
8. Execution of the writ by the sheriff or court officer.

**None of these steps authorize the landlord to seize the tenant's mobile home.** The writ of restitution, if properly obtained, authorizes removal of the *person* from the *premises* — not seizure of the person's *titled property.*

Defendants did not follow any of these procedures before drilling Plaintiff's locks and posting a "FREE" sign on his home on July 17, 2025. They engaged in naked self-help, which Michigan law categorically forbids.

#### C. No Landlord's Lien Extends to Titled Vehicles or Personal Property

Defendants may argue that they hold a "landlord's lien" on Plaintiff's mobile home for unpaid rent. This argument fails for multiple independent reasons.

**First**, Michigan does not recognize a common-law landlord's lien on personal property. The Michigan Legislature has not enacted a statute creating such a lien for manufactured housing community operators. Any lien claim must be based on a specific statutory grant — and no such grant exists here.

**Second**, even if a contractual lien were created by the lease agreement, **MCL 125.2330i** classifies the mobile home as titled personal property subject to the same laws as other titled chattels. Under Michigan's Uniform Commercial Code (**MCL 440.9109** et seq.), a security interest in titled personal property must be perfected by notation on the certificate of title. There is no indication that Defendants have perfected any security interest on the title to Plaintiff's mobile home. An unperfected lien is unenforceable against the titleholder.

**Third**, the lease agreements in this case are of highly questionable validity. The original lease was with "Homes of America" (now dissolved). The coerced replacement lease was with "Cricklewood MHP LLC" (a different entity). The rent amounts are irreconcilable across three ledger versions. A lien purportedly arising from a contract tainted by entity fraud, coercion, and irreconcilable accounting is not a lien that equity should enforce.

**Fourth**, even a valid lien would not authorize self-help seizure. A lienholder must proceed through judicial process to enforce its lien — typically through a foreclosure action or a motion in the pending case. Drilling locks and posting "FREE" signs is not a recognized form of lien enforcement under any Michigan statute or court rule.

#### D. A Dissolved LLC Has No Authority to Initiate Legal Proceedings Beyond Winding Up

**Homes of America, LLC** is administratively dissolved under Michigan law. This fact is verifiable through the Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) entity search. Despite its dissolved status, Homes of America continued to operate the mobile home park, collect rent, issue lease modifications, and — most critically — **initiate eviction proceedings** against Plaintiff.

**MCL 450.4802** governs the authority of a dissolved LLC:

> *"A dissolved limited liability company shall do only those things that are appropriate to wind up and liquidate its business and affairs, including . . . discharging or making provision for discharging its liabilities [and] distributing its remaining property among its members."*

The statute is clear: a dissolved LLC may **only** take actions appropriate to "wind up and liquidate." The phrase "wind up" has a specific legal meaning — it refers to the orderly cessation of business activities, satisfaction of existing obligations, and distribution of remaining assets.

**Initiating new eviction proceedings is not "winding up."** It is the commencement of new legal action — the opposite of cessation. A dissolved LLC cannot expand its legal exposure by initiating new claims against tenants. It can only resolve existing obligations.

The consequences of this statutory bar are significant:

1. **Any eviction proceeding initiated by Homes of America, LLC after its dissolution is void or voidable.** The entity lacked the legal capacity to file the action.

2. **Any judgment obtained in such proceedings is subject to challenge.** A judgment entered in favor of an entity that lacked standing to bring the action is not entitled to preclusive effect.

3. **Any agent claiming to act on behalf of Homes of America, LLC is acting without authority.** Nicole Browley's claim, at the July 14, 2025 proceeding, that she represented "Homes of America" was a representation on behalf of a legally nonexistent principal. An agent cannot have greater authority than the principal — and a dissolved principal has no authority to prosecute new claims.

4. **The eviction dispositions entered in File Nos. 2405891BLT and 250616264T** must be examined to determine whether they were obtained by or on behalf of Homes of America, LLC. If so, they are subject to collateral attack.

This is not a technicality. The Michigan Legislature dissolved Homes of America, LLC for a reason — presumably failure to comply with annual filing requirements or other regulatory obligations. The dissolution statutes exist to protect the public from entities that operate outside the regulatory framework. Allowing a dissolved LLC to continue prosecuting eviction actions defeats the purpose of the dissolution regime entirely.

#### E. Entity-Switching Fraud Creates Standing Deficiencies for All Defendants

Plaintiff has been subjected to a bewildering succession of entity names throughout his tenancy at Lot 17:

| Entity | Relationship to Plaintiff | Time Period |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------|
| **Homes of America, LLC** (Dissolved Michigan LLC) | Original lease counterparty | November 2023 |
| **Cricklewood MHP LLC** | Coerced lease counterparty | March 26, 2024 |
| **Shady Oaks Park MHP LLC** (New Jersey LLC) | Actual landowner | July 30, 2021 – present |
| **Partridge Security LLC / Partridge Equity Group** | Cashed Plaintiff's payment | 2004 |

Each entity transition occurred without clear disclosure to Plaintiff. At no point was Plaintiff provided with a formal assignment of lease, a notice of change in ownership or management, or an explanation of the relationship among these entities. Plaintiff was simply expected to continue paying rent — though to whom, at what amount, and under what legal authority was never made clear.

This entity-switching creates **standing deficiencies** that infect every legal action Defendants have taken against Plaintiff:

**Standing requires a concrete, particularized injury traceable to the defendant.** *Lujan v Defenders of Wildlife*, 504 U.S. 555, 560 (1992). When four different entities have variously claimed to be Plaintiff's landlord, collected his rent, issued his lease modifications, and initiated eviction proceedings against him, the question of **which entity has standing to do what** becomes inescapable.

- Can Homes of America (dissolved) initiate eviction? **No** — it lacks legal capacity.
- Can Cricklewood MHP LLC claim a lease relationship? **Only if the March 2024 lease is valid** — but that lease was obtained through coercion by Kim Davis.
- Can Shady Oaks Park MHP LLC (the actual landowner) enforce a lease to which it is not a named party? **Only through proper assignment** — but no assignment has been disclosed.
- Can any of these entities claim rental arrearages when they maintain three irreconcilable ledger versions? **Not credibly.**

This entity chaos also constitutes a violation of the **Michigan Consumer Protection Act, MCL 445.903**, which prohibits unfair, unconscionable, and deceptive methods, acts, and practices in trade or commerce. Operating a business under multiple undisclosed entity names, collecting payments through entities that are not parties to the governing lease, and coercing lease modifications with different entity counterparties are precisely the kinds of deceptive practices the MCPA was designed to address.

#### F. Due Process Requires Proper Notice and a Hearing Before Any Deprivation of Property

The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides:

> *"No State shall . . . deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."*

The Michigan Constitution contains a parallel guarantee. **Mich. Const. 1963, Art. I, § 17:**

> *"No person shall . . . be deprived of life, liberty or property, without due process of law."*

Due process requires, at minimum, **notice and an opportunity to be heard** before the government — or a private party acting under color of legal process — may deprive a person of a property interest. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976).

Plaintiff's mobile home is his **property** — both in the colloquial sense (it is where he lives) and in the constitutional sense (it is a titled asset in which he holds a legally protected ownership interest). Deprivation of this property without due process violates both the federal and state constitutions.

**Defendants provided no notice before the July 17, 2025 seizure.** There was no court order. There was no writ of restitution. There was no statutory notice of intent to remove the home. Defendants simply drilled the locks, removed Plaintiff's belongings, and posted a "FREE" sign. This is the antithesis of due process.

Even in cases where eviction proceedings have been properly completed, the evicted tenant retains a **property interest in the mobile home** that cannot be extinguished without separate, constitutionally adequate process. MCL 125.2312f contemplates that even after eviction, the mobile home owner has a right to remove the home within a reasonable time. The home does not become the landlord's property merely because the lot lease is terminated.

The $750 purchase offer — conditioned on Plaintiff's waiver of all legal rights — further demonstrates that Defendants recognized Plaintiff's ownership interest. One does not offer to purchase property one already owns. The offer itself is an admission that Plaintiff holds title.

**The due process violation here is compounded by the involvement of minor child L.D.W.** Courts apply heightened scrutiny when government action — or private action enabled by legal process — threatens the welfare of children. Rendering a three-year-old child homeless through extrajudicial property seizure is an outcome no court should countenance.

### VI. CONCLUSION

Michigan law is clear:

1. **A titled mobile home is personal property** belonging to the titleholder, not the landlord. MCL 125.2330i.

2. **A landlord cannot seize a tenant's personal property** through self-help. MCL 600.2918; MCL 600.5714.

3. **No landlord's lien extends to titled personal property** without perfection on the certificate of title. MCL 440.9109.

4. **A dissolved LLC cannot initiate new legal proceedings.** MCL 450.4802.

5. **Operating under four undisclosed entity names** constitutes deceptive practices and creates fatal standing deficiencies. MCL 445.903.

6. **Due process requires notice and a hearing** before any deprivation of property. U.S. Const. Amend. XIV; Mich. Const. 1963, Art. I, § 17.

Defendants have violated every one of these principles. They have already seized Plaintiff's property once. They will do so again unless restrained by this Court.
