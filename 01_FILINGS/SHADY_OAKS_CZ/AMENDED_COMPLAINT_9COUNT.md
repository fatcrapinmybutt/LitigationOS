## III. COMMON ALLEGATIONS OF FACT

### A. Plaintiff's Tenancy and Mobile Home Ownership

9. In approximately 2021, Plaintiff became a resident of Shady Oaks Mobile Home Community at Lot 17, 1977 Whitehall Road, Laketon Township, Muskegon County. Plaintiff submitted a rental application to "Homes of America," identified as the Owner/Manager, and was approved. Plaintiff's original monthly lot rent was $325 per month under a November 2023 lease.

10. Plaintiff is the titled owner of a 1970 Marlette mobile home, VIN 1646732, located on Lot 17. Under Michigan law, a mobile home is **personal property** belonging to the titled owner, distinct from the real property on which it sits. MCL 125.2330i; MCL 125.1305.

11. Plaintiff resided at Shady Oaks with his minor child, **L.D.W.**, who was approximately three years old at the time of tenancy. Lot 17 was L.D.W.'s primary residence during Plaintiff's custodial periods.

### B. Rent Escalation and Lease Coercion

12. On or about March 26, 2024, Defendants coerced Plaintiff into signing a new lease agreement with a different entity — Cricklewood MHP LLC — under express threat of immediate eviction. The lease was signed off-site, not at the community office. This constituted a coerced lease modification executed under duress.

13. Through this coerced modification and subsequent unnoticed increases, Plaintiff's monthly lot rent escalated as follows:
   - Original rent (November 2023 lease): **$325/month**
   - First increase: **$395/month**
   - Second increase: **$695/month**
   - Third increase (by February 2025): **$720/month**

   This represents a total increase of **121% in approximately 14 months** ($325 → $720). No evidence of proper 60-day statutory notice under MCL 554.632 has been produced by Defendants for any of these increases.

14. The timing of the rent escalation correlates directly with Plaintiff's complaints about habitability conditions, establishing a retaliatory motive.

### C. Ledger Manipulation and Payment Fraud

15. Defendants systematically manipulated Plaintiff's payment ledger to fabricate rental arrears and justify eviction proceedings. Forensic analysis reveals **three irreconcilable versions** of the same RentManager ledger (Account #33700, SO LOT 17):

   a. **Ledger Version A** (printed August 22, 2024, at 11:01 AM, software v12.240702): Shows a **credit balance of negative $61.64** — meaning Plaintiff owed nothing and was in fact overpaid.

   b. **Ledger Version B** (printed January 8, 2025, at 1:50 PM, software v12.241104): Shows a **balance due of $2,180.18** — printed three days before Defendants filed the second eviction action.

   c. **Ledger Version C** (submitted to Court): Shows yet different amounts from both Versions A and B.

16. The **$2,241.82 discrepancy** between Ledger Versions A and B cannot be explained by normal rent accrual. The software version changed between printings (enabling retroactive editing), and the page count expanded (proving transactions were added or removed). Version A was emailed by Park Manager Kim Davis at 11:32 AM on the same day it was printed, creating a 39-minute contemporaneous evidence window proving its authenticity.

17. Defendants omitted at least **$6,085.99** in documented payments from Plaintiff's ledger:

   a. **MDHHS Housing Assistance: $1,962.45** (April 2024) — Defendants' bank rejected this government assistance payment. Defendants admitted in their own Answer (filed January 9, 2025, Affirmative Defense #14) that they have a policy of refusing government housing assistance;

   b. **MDHHS Housing Assistance: $768.48** (April 12, 2024) — omitted entirely from all ledger versions;

   c. **TrueNorth CERA Grant: $2,054.80** (check #428979, August 14, 2024) — appears in Ledger Version A but vanishes from Version B;

   d. **Personal Check: $1,300.26** (August 14, 2024) — fully credited in Version A, completely omitted from Version B.

18. When these documented payments totaling $6,085.99 are credited against Defendants' counter-complaint claim of $3,805.44, **the actual account balance is negative $2,280.55** — meaning Defendants owe Plaintiff, not the reverse.

19. Defendants further inflated Plaintiff's account through unauthorized charges including: an NSF fee of $35.00 originating from another tenant's account (Jamie Semelbauer); legal fees of $178.00 (Defendants' own attorney costs charged to Plaintiff's ledger before any judgment); and late fee overcharges exceeding contractual amounts by a total of $53.00.

### D. Sewage Crisis and Habitability Failures

20. Beginning in approximately late 2024, **raw sewage began overflowing from an interceptor tank** onto the ground surface near Lot 17 and adjacent lots, creating a severe public health hazard.

21. On March 7, 2025, Plaintiff sent a formal written complaint to Defendants at shadyoaks@ourhomesofamerica.com demanding remediation within 48 hours. On March 17, 2025, Plaintiff sent an urgent follow-up citing MCL 554.139 and threatening complaints to Muskegon County Health, City of Muskegon Water & Sewer, EGLE, and Housing Law Enforcement. Defendants did not respond to either communication.

22. On March 18, 2025, the Michigan Department of Environment, Great Lakes, and Energy ("EGLE") received a complaint from Muskegon County Public Health regarding the sewage conditions at Shady Oaks.

23. On March 20, 2025, EGLE Environmental Quality Analysts Connor O'Brien and Amanda St. Amour inspected the property. The inspection confirmed **raw sewage discharging from an interceptor tank onto the ground surface** near occupied homes. The inspectors found residual pooled sewage, staining, sewage odor, and an unsecured septic/holding tank lid. Neighboring residents confirmed persistent odors and plumbing backups. EGLE rated the facility **"NOT IN COMPLIANCE"** for both Facility Site Review and Operation & Maintenance.

24. On March 26, 2025, EGLE left a voicemail on Shady Oaks' posted office telephone number. As of April 4, 2025 — seventeen days after the inspection — no one from Shady Oaks had contacted EGLE.

25. On April 15, 2025, EGLE issued **Violation Notice VN-017235**, citing violations of Part 31 of the Natural Resources and Environmental Protection Act (MCL 324.3101 et seq.) and the Mobile Home Commission Act (MCL 125.2301 et seq.), including illegal sewage discharge onto the ground, failure to notify EGLE or health authorities within 24 hours, and failure to submit required written reports.

26. In late June 2025, sewage was again leaking during heavy rains, killing vegetation near occupied homes, demonstrating that Defendants' minimal remediation efforts were inadequate.

### E. Utility Meter Fraud

27. Beginning approximately May 2025, Defendants imposed unauthorized water and sewer submeter charges of approximately $87 per month. The water meter was installed without Plaintiff's notice or consent, in violation of MCL 125.2328(1)(b).

28. Analysis of Defendants' Zego billing portal records reveals that **water gallons consumed and sewer gallons discharged are reported as identical every month**: April 2025 (4,245 = 4,245); May 2025 (3,233 = 3,233); June 2025 (3,100 = 3,100). This is a **physical impossibility**. Plaintiff had installed an outdoor spigot connected to the main water line for outdoor use (sprinkler), which drained into the ground and never entered the sewer system. Water consumed must always exceed sewer discharged. Identical readings prove the metering was fabricated or fraudulently calculated.

### MCL 554.139

46. Plaintiff re-alleges and incorporates by reference paragraphs 1 through 38 as if fully set forth herein.

   (b) The premises are kept in reasonable repair during the term of the lease, and the premises comply with applicable health and safety laws of the state and local governmental units.

Sewage resumed leaking in late June 2025;

   d. **Unauthorized and defective utility service**: Installation of water submeters without notice or consent in violation of MCL 125.2328(1)(b), with demonstrably fabricated meter readings;

51. As a direct and proximate result of Defendants' breach, Plaintiff has suffered damages including but not limited to: diminished use and enjoyment of the premises; health risks from raw sewage exposure near his home where his minor child L.D.W. resided; costs of temporary remediation measures; and constructive eviction from conditions rendering the premises uninhabitable.

## COUNT VIII — UNJUST ENRICHMENT
### Common Law

80. Plaintiff re-alleges and incorporates by reference paragraphs 1 through 38 as if fully set forth herein.

81. Under Michigan law, a claim for unjust enrichment requires: (1) the defendant received a benefit from the plaintiff; (2) the benefit was at the plaintiff's expense; and (3) it would be inequitable for the defendant to retain the benefit without paying for it. *Karaus v. Bank of New York Mellon*, 300 Mich. App. 9, 22 (2012).

82. Defendants received the following benefits at Plaintiff's expense:

   a. **Coerced rent overpayments**: Defendants collected rent at rates ranging from $395 to $720/month — increases of up to 121% — through coercion and without proper statutory notice, while simultaneously failing to maintain habitability. The differential between the original $325/month rent and the coerced rates constitutes unjust enrichment;

   b. **Omitted payment retention**: Defendants received $6,085.99 in payments (including $2,054.80 from TrueNorth CERA, $1,300.26 personal check, and $2,730.93 from MDHHS) that were either rejected, omitted from ledgers, or diverted to other entities, while simultaneously suing Plaintiff for non-payment;

   c. **$750 buyout conditioned on rights waiver**: Defendants offered only $750 in exchange for Plaintiff's waiver of all legal claims — claims conservatively valued at hundreds of thousands of dollars — attempting to obtain an unconscionable release;

   d. **Post-destruction billing**: After destroying Plaintiff's home on July 17, 2025, Defendants billed $7,633.13 for lot rent on an empty, destroyed lot;

   e. **Property conversion**: Defendants took possession of Plaintiff's titled mobile home and personal property (including appliances, furniture, and belongings) without lawful authority and without compensation;

   f. **Blocked sale proceeds**: By interfering with at least three potential sales of Plaintiff's mobile home through false statements to buyers, Defendants effectively retained the economic value of Plaintiff's property.

83. It would be inequitable and unjust for Defendants to retain these benefits without paying fair compensation to Plaintiff.

**WHEREFORE**, Plaintiff demands judgment against Defendants, jointly and severally, for restitution of all rent overpayments, return of omitted/diverted payments, fair market value of converted property, and disgorgement of all unjust enrichment, plus such other relief as this Court deems just.

## COUNT IX — VIOLATION OF TRUTH IN RENTING ACT
### MCL 554.631 et seq.

84. Plaintiff re-alleges and incorporates by reference paragraphs 1 through 38 as if fully set forth herein.

85. The Michigan Truth in Renting Act, MCL 554.631 through MCL 554.641, protects residential tenants from unfair lease provisions. MCL 554.633 provides that a rental agreement shall not include a clause which:

   a. Waives any of the tenant's rights under the Act;
   b. Requires the tenant to waive any legal rights or remedies;
   c. Contains provisions that are contrary to state law or are against public policy.

86. Any lease provision that violates MCL 554.633 is **void and unenforceable**. MCL 554.634.

87. Defendants violated the Truth in Renting Act in the following respects:

   a. **$750 buyout with complete rights waiver**: Defendants offered Plaintiff $750 conditioned upon execution of a complete waiver of all legal rights and claims — a provision that is unenforceable under MCL 554.633(1) because it requires waiver of rights guaranteed by Michigan law;

   b. **Lease waterfall clause**: The lease contains a payment application clause directing all payments first to attorney fees, then to late fees, then to other charges, and only last to rent — guaranteeing perpetual "unpaid rent" status regardless of payments made, which is unconscionable and against public policy;

   c. **Coerced entity substitution**: The March 26, 2024 lease transferred Plaintiff's tenancy to a different entity (Cricklewood MHP LLC) under threat of eviction, without proper disclosure of the entity change or its implications;

   d. **Rent increase without proper notice**: Rent increases exceeding 10% without the statutorily required 60-day notice under MCL 554.632 constitute a violation of Plaintiff's rights under the Act;

   e. **Unauthorized fee provisions**: Lease provisions purporting to authorize charges for Defendants' attorney fees to be billed directly to the tenant's account before any court judgment.

88. Under MCL 554.636, a tenant may recover actual damages sustained, plus a statutory penalty of **$250 for each violation**, plus reasonable attorney fees and costs.

89. Plaintiff has identified at least five separate violations of the Truth in Renting Act, entitling Plaintiff to actual damages plus statutory penalties of at least $1,250.

**WHEREFORE**, Plaintiff demands judgment against Defendants, jointly and severally, for actual damages, statutory penalties of $250 per violation, costs and fees under MCL 554.636, and such other relief as this Court deems just.
