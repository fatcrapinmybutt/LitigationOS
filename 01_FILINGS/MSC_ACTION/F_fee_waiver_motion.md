## IV. LEGAL BASIS FOR FEE WAIVER

15. **MCR 2.002** provides that a court shall waive fees and costs for a party who, by reason of indigency, is unable to pay them. The rule applies to all Michigan courts, including the Supreme Court.

16. **MCR 2.002(B)** establishes that a party is presumed eligible for a fee waiver if the party's income is at or below 125% of the federal poverty guidelines, or if the party receives means-tested public assistance.

17. **MCR 2.002(C)** provides that even if a party does not meet the presumptive eligibility standard, the court may still waive fees if the party demonstrates inability to pay.

18. **MCR 7.319** governs motions practice in the Michigan Supreme Court. This Motion is filed in compliance with the applicable rules governing motion practice before this Court.

19. SCAO Form MC 20 is the approved form for requesting fee waivers under MCR 2.002, and Movant has completed and filed said form with this Motion.

## V. CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHT OF ACCESS

20. The Michigan Constitution, Article 1, § 13, guarantees that "[a] suitor in any court of this state has the right to prosecute or defend his suit, either in his own proper person or by an attorney."

21. The United States Supreme Court held in *Boddie v. Connecticut*, 401 US 371, 374 (1971), that a state may not condition access to the courts on ability to pay when the state holds a monopoly over the resolution of fundamental legal relationships.

22. Parental rights are among the most fundamental rights recognized under the Constitution. *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). *M.L.B. v. S.L.J.*, 519 US 102, 116–117 (1996), held that states may not condition appeals involving parental rights on ability to pay.

23. Conditioning Movant's access to this Court's superintending control (MCR 7.304; Const. 1963, art. 6, Sec. 4) jurisdiction on fees he cannot afford would deny him meaningful access to justice at a time when his fundamental constitutional rights are being violated daily. See *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57; 120 S Ct 2054 (2000); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745; 102 S Ct 1388 (1982).
