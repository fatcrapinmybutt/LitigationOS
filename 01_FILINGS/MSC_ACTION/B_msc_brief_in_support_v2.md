## I. JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

### A. This Court's Jurisdiction

This Court possesses original jurisdiction over this Petition under Const 1963, art 6, § 4, which vests this Court with "general superintending control over all courts" and "power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs." MCR 7.304(A) authorizes original proceedings in this Court, and MCR 7.306(A) specifically authorizes complaints for superintending control.

MCL 600.1701 provides complementary statutory authority for superintending control proceedings, confirming the broad scope of this Court's power to correct lower court action.

### B. Standard for Superintending Control

Superintending control is an extraordinary remedy. This Court has established a three-part test:

**First**, the party must demonstrate that the lower court has failed to perform a clear legal duty or has exceeded its jurisdiction. *Crampton v Dep't of State*, 395 Mich 347, 351–352 (1975); *Hinkle v Wayne Co Clerk*, 467 Mich 337, 340 (2002).

**Second**, the party must show there is no other adequate legal remedy available. *Lapeer County Clerk v Lapeer Circuit Court*, 469 Mich 146, 155 (2003).

**Third**, the party must demonstrate that the extraordinary writ is necessary to prevent material injustice. *In re Hatcher*, 443 Mich 426, 441–442 (1993); *In re Hague*, 412 Mich 532 (1982).

This standard is informed by the historical scope of mandamus and related prerogative writs, but is broader. *Chevalier v Barnard*, 290 Mich 202, 206 (1939); *Eldred v Ziny*, 246 Mich 478, 480 (1929). The Court exercises superintending control "to prevent a failure of justice" and to ensure that courts within its supervision comply with the law. *Teasel v Dep't of Mental Health*, 419 Mich 390, 408–409 (1984).

This brief demonstrates that all three elements are met — and that the comprehensive adverse evidence now available makes the case for this Court's intervention overwhelming.

## II. THE THREE-PART TEST FOR SUPERINTENDING CONTROL IS SATISFIED

### A. The Trial Court Failed to Perform Clear Legal Duties

#### 1. Failure to Conduct Best Interest Analysis (MCL 722.23)

Michigan law is unequivocal: before any court may modify custody or parenting time, it must evaluate and make findings on all 12 statutory best interest factors set forth in MCL 722.23(a)–(l). *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 882 (1994) ("[T]he trial court is required to consider all the statutory best interest factors."); *DeRose v DeRose*, 469 Mich 320, 333 (2003); *Ireland v Smith*, 451 Mich 457, 465 (1996).

This is not a discretionary act — it is a mandatory legal duty. A trial court that modifies custody without conducting this analysis has, by definition, failed to perform a clear legal duty.

The trial court in this case has never conducted the MCL 722.23 analysis. It suspended all parenting time — the functional equivalent of awarding sole physical custody to the mother — without any findings on any of the 12 factors. This is a textbook case of a trial court failing to perform a mandatory duty.

#### 2. Failure to Hold Evidentiary Hearings

MCR 3.210(C) requires the trial court to hold a hearing before entering a custody or parenting time order when a party has requested one. MCR 3.207(B) governs interim orders and limits the circumstances under which a court may act without a full hearing.

Petitioner repeatedly requested evidentiary hearings. The trial court denied these requests and entered dispositive orders without any hearing. This constitutes a failure to perform the duty imposed by the court rules.

#### 3. Failure to Protect Due Process Rights

Under both the Michigan Constitution (art 1, § 17) and the United States Constitution (Amend XIV), a parent is entitled to notice and an opportunity to be heard before the state may interfere with the fundamental right to parent. *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319, 333 (1976); *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753–754 (1982); *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645, 649 (1972); *Lassiter v Dep't of Social Services*, 452 US 18, 27 (1981).

The right to parent is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). When a fundamental right is at stake, due process demands rigorous procedural protections.

The trial court afforded Petitioner none of these protections. It:
- Suspended parenting time without notice or hearing;
- Denied motions without conducting hearings;
- Directed that evidence be submitted outside the official record;
- Suppressed Petitioner's filings.

**The adverse evidence matrix quantifies this failure: 2,008 documented due process violations are relevant to MSC superintending control.** (Source: Adverse Evidence Summary, Filing Target × Type Cross-Tab, MSC row, "due process" column.)

Each of these failures independently constitutes a failure to perform a clear legal duty. Taken together, they represent a systemic denial of due process.

#### 4. Failure to Apply Parenting Time Protections

MCL 722.27a creates a statutory presumption that parenting time is in the best interest of the child. MCL 722.27a(7) provides that parenting time "shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child." MCL 722.27a(9) provides specific protections against wholesale denial of parenting time. *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 49–51 (1992); *Pierron v Pierron*, 486 Mich 81, 92 (2010).

The trial court disregarded these mandatory protections entirely, eliminating all parenting time without the findings required by statute. *Vodvarka v Grasher*, 259 Mich App 499, 512 (2003); *Kaeb v Kaeb*, 309 Mich App 556, 572 (2015).

**The Adverse Rebuttal Packet Summary documents 146 adverse parenting time restriction entries across both proceedings** — 66 in the PPO case (2023-005907-PP) and 63 in the custody case (2024-001507-DC). (Source: Adverse_Rebuttal_Packet_Summary.csv, topic: PARENTING_TIME_RESTRICTION.)

### B. No Adequate Legal Remedy Exists

The second prong of the superintending control test requires a showing that no adequate legal remedy exists. *Lapeer County Clerk*, 469 Mich at 155. This element is clearly met.

Every additional day that passes without contact permanently alters the parent-child bond. No appellate reversal, no matter how complete, can restore this lost developmental time.

*Hinkle*, 467 Mich at 340, recognizes that a remedy is "adequate" only if it can effectively redress the injury. A remedy that arrives too late to prevent irreparable harm is not adequate.

The trial court's practice of routing evidence outside the official record means that the Court of Appeals will be reviewing an incomplete record. ## VII. THE $250 BOND BARRIER CONSTITUTES UNCONSTITUTIONAL ACCESS-TO-JUSTICE DENIAL *(New Section)*

### A. The Bond Requirement

The trial court imposed a requirement that Petitioner must pay **a $250 bond for each motion** before it will be considered. This requirement is documented in the JTC Conduct Pattern Table: "It is further ordered that the Plaintiff must pay a bond of $250 (for each motion) before he [can file]." (Source: JTC_Conduct_Pattern_Table.csv, UNASSIGNED / GENERAL_CONDUCT_EPISODE, priority 2,393; Adverse Evidence Matrix AEM_000044.)

### B. Constitutional Analysis

The $250 bond per motion constitutes an unconstitutional barrier to court access under multiple authorities:

**1. Michigan Constitution, Art 1, § 13 — Access to Courts**

"Every person for an injury done to him in his person or property, shall have remedy by due course of law, and shall have justice administered without sale, denial or delay." (Emphasis added.) The $250-per-motion bond is, by definition, a "sale" of justice — conditioning access to the court on payment of a fee.

**2. *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971)**

The Supreme Court held that where the state holds a monopoly over the resolution of fundamental disputes, it may not condition access on payment of a fee that a party cannot afford. Custody is precisely such a fundamental dispute — indeed, it involves the same parental rights at issue in *Boddie*.

**3. *M.L.B. v S.L.J.*, 519 US 102 (1996)**

In *M.L.B.*, the Supreme Court held that a state may not deny appellate review of a parental-rights termination based on inability to pay transcript costs. The Court specifically recognized that "choices about marriage, family life, and the upbringing of children are among associational rights this Court has ranked as of basic importance in our society, rights sheltered by the Fourteenth Amendment against the State's unwarranted usurpation, disregard, or disrespect." 519 US at 116.

The $250-per-motion bond operates identically to the fee barrier struck down in *M.L.B.* — it denies a parent access to the judicial process in a proceeding affecting his fundamental parental rights.

**4. *Griffin v Illinois*, 351 US 12 (1956)**

"There can be no equal justice where the kind of trial a man gets depends on the amount of money he has." 351 US at 19. The $250 bond requirement ensures that Petitioner's ability to challenge unconstitutional orders depends entirely on his financial resources — violating the equal protection principles articulated in *Griffin*.

### C. The Bond Barrier Compounds Every Other Violation

The $250 bond requirement does not operate in isolation. It functions as the final link in a chain of coercion:

1. The trial court enters ex parte orders denying Petitioner's rights (3,495 documented rows);
2. The trial court denies hearings to challenge those orders (2,008 due process violations);
3. The trial court imposes a $250-per-motion bond to prevent further challenges;
4. The trial court uses contempt power (3,986 documented rows) to enforce compliance with the very orders Petitioner seeks to challenge.

This cycle — documented across 44,974 evidentiary rows — ensures that Petitioner cannot meaningfully access the court system. Only this Court's intervention can break it.
