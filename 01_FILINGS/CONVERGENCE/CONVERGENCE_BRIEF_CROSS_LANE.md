## III. THE CONVERGENCE PATTERN

### A. The Housing-Custody Nexus (Lanes A ↔ B)

**Conclusion:** The uninhabitable conditions at the Shady Oaks residential complex were weaponized in custody proceedings to deprive Andrew Pigors of parenting time, in violation of the best interest analysis required by MCL 722.23.

**Rule:** The Child Custody Act requires that custody and parenting time determinations be based on the twelve best interest factors enumerated in MCL 722.23. Factor (d) addresses "the length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment." Factor (j) addresses "the willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent." Under *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871, 889 (1994), each factor must be independently evaluated and weighed; no single factor is dispositive.

**Explanation:** The housing-custody nexus operates through a mechanism of manufactured instability:

1. **The Housing Failure:** Shady Oaks and Alden Management maintained residential conditions that violated Michigan habitability standards. The 22-count complaint in Case No. 2025-002760-CZ documents conditions including but not limited to structural deficiencies, failure to maintain common areas, and failure to address documented maintenance requests. These conditions rendered Andrew Pigors's residence unsuitable for overnight parenting time with a child born November 9, 2022. Critically, the Shady Oaks eviction timeline coincides with the custody proceeding timeline — the housing deterioration accelerated during the precise period when custody was being adjudicated, creating maximum prejudice.

2. **The Custody Exploitation:** Emily A. Watson's filings in Case No. 2024-001507-DC cited Andrew Pigors's housing conditions as a basis for restricting or denying parenting time. Rather than recognizing the housing conditions as a remediable circumstance — or as the fault of third-party landlords — the court treated the conditions as reflecting on Andrew Pigors's fitness as a parent.

3. **The Feedback Loop:** The PPO restrictions in Case No. 2023-5907-PP limited Andrew Pigors's ability to relocate freely. The financial burden of simultaneous litigation across multiple proceedings (custody, PPO defense, housing remediation) consumed resources that could have been directed toward securing alternative housing. The 59 days of incarceration for contempt further destroyed any housing stability that remained.

**Application:** When the housing and custody proceedings are viewed together, the MCL 722.23 analysis is fundamentally contaminated:

- **Factor (d) — Stable Environment:** The "instability" in Andrew Pigors's environment was not volitional. It was created by third-party landlord misconduct (Shady Oaks / Alden Management defendants) and compounded by judicial action (contempt incarceration). Using this manufactured instability against Andrew Pigors reverses the statutory purpose: the Child Custody Act is designed to protect the child's best interest, not to reward the party who benefits from the other party's externally imposed hardship.

- **Factor (j) — Facilitation of Relationship:** Emily A. Watson's 305 documented interference incidents demonstrate that the party pressing the "unstable housing" argument is the same party actively obstructing the parent-child relationship. The convergence of the housing weapon with the interference pattern reveals that the housing argument was not made in good faith concern for the child's welfare, but as one prong of a multi-lane strategy to exclude Andrew Pigors from L.D.W.'s life.

**Conclusion Restated:** The housing conditions documented in Case No. 2025-002760-CZ cannot be evaluated in isolation when determining custody in Case No. 2024-001507-DC. The interconnection between the landlord misconduct, the PPO restrictions, and the financial burden of multi-proceeding litigation created a manufactured cycle of instability that the court treated as a voluntary parental deficiency. This constituted an abuse of the MCL 722.23 framework and resulted in a custody determination untethered from the child's actual best interest.

When issued routinely, they become instruments of one-sided adjudication.

The private interest here — a parent's fundamental liberty interest in the care and custody of his child, *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000) — is among the most significant interests recognized in American law. ### C. The PPO-Custody Nexus (Lanes D ↔ A)

**Conclusion:** The Personal Protection Order in Case No. 2023-5907-PP was weaponized to achieve through the PPO process what Emily A. Watson could not achieve through the custody process: the complete exclusion of Andrew Pigors from his son's life.

**Rule:** MCL 600.2950 authorizes personal protection orders to protect individuals from domestic violence, stalking, and other enumerated harms. The statute was enacted to provide emergency protection to genuine victims of domestic violence — not to serve as a de facto custody order. *TM v MZ*, 501 Mich 312 (2018), establishes the standards for issuance and the scope of PPO relief. A PPO is not a custody determination and does not substitute for the best interest analysis required by MCL 722.23. When a PPO is deployed to achieve custody objectives that could not be obtained through the custody process — with its higher evidentiary standards and procedural protections — the resulting misuse of MCL 600.2950 constitutes an abuse of the domestic violence protection framework that harms genuine victims and distorts the custody proceedings it was never designed to influence.

**Explanation:** The PPO-custody nexus operates through the following convergence mechanism:

1. **PPO as Custody Substitute:** The PPO in Case No. 2023-5907-PP effectively imposed a custody regime — zero contact between Andrew Pigors and L.D.W. — without the procedural protections required in custody proceedings. Where MCL 722.23 requires evaluation of twelve best interest factors, MCL 722.27 requires findings of proper cause or change of circumstances, and *Fletcher v Fletcher* requires independent weighing of each factor, the PPO accomplished the same result — total parental exclusion — under a lower standard and with fewer procedural safeguards.

2. **The Scale of Weaponization:** The record documents 5,222 instances of PPO weaponization. This figure is not an estimate; it is derived from documented incidents across the record. These instances include:
   - Filing PPO violation allegations in response to Andrew Pigors's attempts to exercise statutory parenting rights;
   - Using the PPO to prevent communication regarding the child's welfare, health, and education;
   - Leveraging PPO enforcement to generate contempt proceedings that resulted in 59 days of incarceration;
   - Citing the PPO's existence in custody proceedings as evidence that Andrew Pigors poses a danger to the child.

3. **The Circular Logic:** The convergence creates a logical trap: (a) the PPO restricts all contact; (b) any attempt at contact violates the PPO; (c) PPO violations are cited as evidence of unfitness in custody proceedings; (d) custody denial is justified by the PPO violations. The father is placed in an impossible position: comply with the PPO and lose his child through absence, or attempt to maintain a relationship and lose his child through contempt. Either path leads to the same result — permanent exclusion.

**Application:** The PPO-custody nexus demonstrates why convergence analysis is essential. Viewed in isolation, the PPO appears to be a standard protective order. Viewed in isolation, the custody denial appears to be based on the father's "lack of involvement" and "instability." Only when both proceedings are viewed together does the weaponization pattern emerge: the PPO *created* the lack of involvement, and the custody court then *punished* the father for it.

This is precisely the abuse of process that the Legislature did not intend when enacting MCL 600.2950. Domestic violence protection statutes serve a critical function. Their credibility is undermined — and genuine victims are harmed — when they are deployed as tactical weapons in custody disputes.

**Conclusion Restated:** The 5,222 documented instances of PPO weaponization constitute the operational mechanism through which the 300+ day custody deprivation was achieved. The PPO and custody proceedings cannot be evaluated independently without concealing the pattern.

### D. The Attorney-Judicial Nexus (Barnes/McNeill)

**Conclusion:** Attorney Jennifer Barnes's violations of MRPC 3.5, in coordination with Judge McNeill's ex parte order practice, created a two-party system of one-sided adjudication that deprived Andrew Pigors of the adversarial process guaranteed by due process.

**Rule:** MRPC 3.5 prohibits a lawyer from seeking "to influence a judge, juror, prospective juror, or other official by means prohibited by law" and from engaging in "ex parte communication" with a judge "during the proceeding unless authorized to do so by law or court order." MRPC 8.4(a) provides that it is professional misconduct for a lawyer to "violate or attempt to violate the Rules of Professional Conduct, knowingly assist or induce another to do so, or do so through the acts of another."

**Explanation:** The attorney-judicial nexus is documented through:

1. **The 48-Hour Pattern:** Across the custody and PPO proceedings, a recurring sequence is documented: Attorney Jennifer Barnes files a motion → a ruling favorable to Emily A. Watson issues within 48 hours → Andrew Pigors receives notice only after the ruling. This pattern is inconsistent with the normal motion practice timeline under MCR 2.119(C), which requires service of a motion at least 9 days before the hearing date (or 7 days for certain motions). The 48-hour turnaround, recurring across multiple filings, raises a reasonable inference that the motions were coordinated with the court outside the adversarial process.

2. **Ex Parte Communications:** The combination of the 48-hour pattern with the 44% ex parte order rate creates a composite picture: motions filed by Barnes were resolved by McNeill on an ex parte basis at a rate far exceeding normal practice, with a consistency of favorable outcomes that is difficult to attribute to the merits alone.

3. **The Dennis v Sparks Framework:** Under *Dennis v Sparks*, 449 US 24, 27-28 (1980), the United States Supreme Court held that "private persons, jointly engaged with state officials in [a] challenged action, are acting 'under color' of law for purposes of § 1983." A private attorney who conspires with a judge to deprive a litigant of constitutional rights is subject to § 1983 liability. The 48-hour pattern, combined with the ex parte order rate and the systematic one-sidedness of the outcomes, establishes facts from which a conspiracy may be inferred.

**Application:** The attorney-judicial nexus is the delivery mechanism for the judicial misconduct. Judge McNeill's 24 ex parte orders did not originate spontaneously — they were requested by Attorney Jennifer Barnes through a filing practice designed to circumvent the adversarial process. The AGC complaint against Barnes and the JTC complaint against McNeill are not separate matters; they document two sides of the same coordination.

Under *Lugar v Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 US 922, 941 (1982), the deprivation of rights "is caused by the exercise of some right or privilege created by the State or by a rule of conduct imposed by the State or by a person for whom the State is responsible." When a judge acts in concert with a private attorney to issue orders without notice or hearing, the resulting deprivation is state action — actionable under 42 USC § 1983 and attributable to both the judicial actor and the private conspirator.

**Conclusion Restated:** The Barnes-McNeill coordination is the engine that powered the ex parte order system. Without Barnes's filings, McNeill's ex parte orders would have no vehicle. Without McNeill's orders, Barnes's strategy of unilateral adjudication would have no effect. Together, they operated a system that deprived Andrew Pigors of notice, hearing, and the adversarial process across 300+ days of custody deprivation.

### E. The Federal Constitutional Nexus (Federal Action)

**Conclusion:** The coordinated pattern of abuse documented across all six proceedings constitutes a deprivation of rights under color of state law, actionable under 42 USC § 1983 and 42 USC § 1985(3), with municipal liability under *Monell v Dep't of Social Servs*, 436 US 658 (1978). As the Supreme Court recognized in *Monroe v Pape*, 365 US 167, 172 (1961), § 1983 was designed to provide a federal remedy where state remedies are "inadequate in practice" — a standard manifestly satisfied here where the state court itself is the instrument of deprivation.

**Rule:** Section 1983 provides that "[e]very person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State … subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen … to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured." 42 USC § 1983. Municipal liability attaches when the deprivation results from an official "policy or custom." *Monell*, 436 US at 694. The Fourteenth Amendment protects the fundamental liberty interest of a parent in "the care, custody, and management of their child." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57, 65 (2000). *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745, 753 (1982), recognizes that "a natural parent's desire for and right to the companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children is an interest far more precious than any property right."

**Explanation:** The federal constitutional nexus transforms six state proceedings into a single federal cause of action:

1. **State Action (Judge McNeill):** Every order issued by Judge McNeill constitutes state action. The 24 ex parte orders, the contempt incarceration, and the custody denial — each an exercise of state judicial power — directly deprived Andrew Pigors of his fundamental liberty interest in his relationship with his son.

2. **Private Conspiracy (Watson, Barnes, Rusco):** Under *Dennis v Sparks*, private persons who conspire with a judge to deprive a citizen of constitutional rights act under color of law. The record supports the inference that Emily A. Watson, Attorney Jennifer Barnes, and other private actors coordinated with Judge McNeill to achieve outcomes that no adversarial process would have produced. The 48-hour filing-to-ruling pattern, the 44% ex parte rate (24 of 55 orders), and the 5,222 PPO weaponization instances are evidence from which a jury could infer conspiracy.

3. **Monell Municipal Liability:** Muskegon County, through its circuit court and Friend of the Court, maintained customs and practices that enabled the pattern:
   - A Friend of the Court system that failed to investigate interference allegations despite 305 documented incidents;
   - A court system that tolerated a 44% ex parte order rate without supervision or correction;
   - An administrative structure that permitted the 48-hour filing-to-ruling pattern to persist across years of litigation.
   These are not isolated failures. They are systemic customs within the meaning of *Monell*, and the county is liable for the constitutional deprivations they enabled.

## IV. LEGAL FRAMEWORK FOR CONVERGENCE

The procedural question underlying this brief is: *How does a court consider evidence and findings from other proceedings?* The answer lies in four established legal doctrines.

### A. Judicial Notice Across Proceedings (MRE 201)

MRE 201 permits a court to take judicial notice of facts "not subject to reasonable dispute" because they are "capable of accurate and ready determination by resort to sources whose accuracy cannot reasonably be questioned." Court records from other proceedings satisfy this standard.

**Application:** Each court in this litigation may take judicial notice of the records in the other proceedings:

- The custody court may take judicial notice of the PPO proceeding record — not merely the existence of the PPO, but the pattern of filings, the number of alleged violations, and the outcomes.
- The housing court may take judicial notice of the custody and contempt proceedings to establish the impact of housing conditions on Andrew Pigors's legal position.
- The appellate court may take judicial notice of the JTC and AGC complaint filings as evidence of the judicial and attorney misconduct patterns.
- The federal court may take judicial notice of all state proceedings.

Judicial notice does not require that one court adopt the findings of another. It requires only that the records be considered. The convergence pattern becomes visible only when those records are placed side by side.

### B. Collateral Estoppel and Issue Preclusion

Where an issue has been actually litigated and necessarily determined in one proceeding, collateral estoppel may prevent relitigation in another. This doctrine operates across the lanes:

- If the appellate court determines that Judge McNeill's ex parte orders violated MCR 2.119(B), that determination may preclude relitigation of the same issue in the federal § 1983 action — to Andrew Pigors's advantage.
- If the housing court determines that Shady Oaks maintained uninhabitable conditions, that determination may be relevant to the custody court's evaluation of Factor (d) under MCL 722.23.
- If the JTC finds that Judge McNeill violated Canon 2 or Canon 3, that finding bears directly on the § 1983 claims regarding judicial misconduct.

Collateral estoppel does not merely simplify litigation — it prevents defendants from maintaining contradictory positions across lanes.

### C. Pattern Evidence Admissibility (MRE 404(b) Analogy)

MRE 404(b) provides that evidence of "other crimes, wrongs, or acts" is admissible to prove "motive, opportunity, intent, preparation, scheme, plan, or system in doing an act." While MRE 404(b) is typically applied in criminal proceedings, the underlying principle — that pattern evidence is admissible to prove a scheme or plan — applies with equal force in civil litigation.

**Application:** The convergence pattern is precisely the type of evidence contemplated by the "scheme or plan" exception:

- The PPO weaponization (Case No. 2023-5907-PP) is admissible in the custody proceeding (Case No. 2024-001507-DC) to demonstrate a scheme to exclude Andrew Pigors from the child's life.

- The housing exploitation (Case No. 2025-002760-CZ) is admissible in the custody proceeding to demonstrate an intentional strategy of manufacturing evidence of unfitness.

Each lane, standing alone, appears to present an isolated dispute. The pattern evidence doctrine permits the court to see what isolation conceals.

### D. RICO Enterprise Theory (18 USC § 1962)

The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act provides civil remedies where a "pattern of racketeering activity" is conducted through an "enterprise." 18 USC § 1962(c). While RICO is typically associated with organized crime, its provisions apply to any enterprise — including an informal association of individuals — engaged in a pattern of activity that includes predicate acts such as obstruction of justice, extortion, or mail and wire fraud.

**Application:** The convergence pattern presents facts that warrant analysis under RICO's civil provisions:
