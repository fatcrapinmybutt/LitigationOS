## VII. CONSTITUTIONAL FOUNDATION

### A. Substantive Due Process — Fundamental Parental Rights

34. The Fourteenth Amendment protects against governmental deprivation of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." In *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000), the Supreme Court reaffirmed that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."

35. In *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982), the Court held that "the fundamental liberty interest of natural parents in the care, custody, and management of their child does not evaporate simply because they have not been model parents or have lost temporary custody." A deprivation of all parenting time exceeding 363 days, in the face of overwhelming exculpatory evidence, constitutes a de facto severance of parental rights without the constitutional protections required by *Santosky*.

### B. Procedural Due Process

36. Before the government may deprive a person of a fundamental constitutional right, procedural due process requires: (a) adequate notice; (b) an opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time; (c) the right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses; and (d) a decision by a neutral and detached adjudicator. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).

37. None of these protections were afforded before Plaintiff's parenting time was suspended. The suspension was entered ex parte without notice, hearing, or opportunity to respond. Given the pending disqualification motion, the neutrality of the adjudicator is also in question.

### C. Equal Protection

38. The Equal Protection Clause requires that similarly situated individuals be treated alike. The record demonstrates a pattern of disparate treatment in the entry of ex parte orders against Plaintiff.

## VIII. MICHIGAN STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

### A. MCL 722.27a — Parenting Time Presumption

39. MCL 722.27a(1) establishes a strong presumption in favor of parenting time: "[P]arenting time shall be granted in accordance with the best interests of the child."

40. MCL 722.27a(3) provides: "A child has a right to parenting time with a parent unless it is shown on the record by clear and convincing evidence that it would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health."

41. No such showing has been made. Every objective metric confirms that parenting time with Plaintiff poses no danger to L.D.W. The statutory presumption has not been rebutted.

### B. MCL 722.27a(7) — Parenting Time Factors

42. Application of the parenting time factors uniformly supports restoration:

   (a) **Special circumstances or danger:** None established. Multiple investigations yielded zero findings.

   (b) **Reasonable likelihood of abuse or neglect:** None demonstrated. Drug screen negative. Evaluation clean.

   (c) **Reasonable likelihood of abuse of court process:** Evidence shows Defendant, not Plaintiff, has engaged in abuse of court process through serial ex parte motions and weaponization of PPO proceedings.

   (d) **Reasonable likelihood of compliance:** Plaintiff has consistently demonstrated willingness and ability to comply with all court orders.

   (e) **Child's reasonable preference:** L.D.W. is too young to express a meaningful preference. The presumption favors parenting time with both parents.

   (f) **Conviction of child abuse:** Plaintiff has no history of child abuse convictions, charges, or substantiated findings.

### C. Makeup Parenting Time — *Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17 (2010)

43. In *Shade v. Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 25-27 (2010), the Michigan Court of Appeals held that when parenting time has been wrongfully denied, the trial court has authority — and may have a duty — to award **makeup parenting time** to remedy the deprivation.

44. The *Shade* court recognized that the purpose of makeup parenting time is twofold: (1) to partially compensate the deprived parent for lost time with the child; and (2) to serve the child's best interest by rebuilding and strengthening the parent-child bond that was disrupted by the denial.

45. MCL 722.27a(8) provides that the court "shall order makeup parenting time for the parenting time that was wrongfully denied" when a parent demonstrates the denial. Given the 363+ days of denied parenting time, a substantial makeup award is warranted to begin rebuilding the father-child bond.
