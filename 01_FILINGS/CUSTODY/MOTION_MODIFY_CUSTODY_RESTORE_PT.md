**NOW COMES** Plaintiff, Andrew James Pigors, appearing *in propria persona*, and respectfully moves this Honorable Court pursuant to MCL 722.27 and MCL 722.27a for an order modifying the current custody arrangement and restoring parenting time with the minor child, L.D.W. (DOB: November 9, 2022). In support thereof, Plaintiff states as follows:

### I. INTRODUCTION

1. Plaintiff is the father of L.D.W. and filed this custody action on April 1, 2024. Plaintiff has standing to seek modification of custody and parenting time pursuant to MCL 722.27(1)(c).

2. From May 5, 2024, through July 17, 2025 — a period of approximately 438 days — the parties maintained a 50/50 shared custody arrangement under this Court's temporary order. That arrangement functioned without adjudicated incident or harm to the child.

3. On July 17, 2025, this Court entered an ex parte order that overturned the existing 50/50 arrangement and awarded Defendant sole physical custody. On August 8, 2025, this Court entered a second ex parte order suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time.

4. As of the date of this filing, Plaintiff has been separated from L.D.W. for over 270 days total, including 230+ consecutive days of complete denial of contact. This ongoing separation is actively harming the parent-child bond and is contrary to the child's best interests.

### II. PROPER CAUSE AND CHANGE OF CIRCUMSTANCES

5. Plaintiff has established proper cause and a change of circumstances sufficient to revisit the existing custody order under the standard set forth in *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 509-512 (2003), based on the following:

   **(a)** After the parenting time suspension order, Plaintiff obtained a favorable mental health evaluation from HealthWest (Exhibit H0002), dated September 5, 2025, which was completed as directed by the Court. This evaluation constitutes a new fact that did not exist at the time of the suspension order.

   **(b)** Plaintiff has completed all court-ordered requirements imposed as conditions for restoration of parenting time.

   **(c)** The passage of 230+ consecutive days of complete separation between father and child constitutes a material change in circumstances. The child, now age 3, is in a critical developmental period where prolonged separation from a bonded parent causes documented psychological harm.

   **(d)** Plaintiff has identified a previously undisclosed conflict involving the presiding judge's relationship to individuals connected to Defendant's household, raising concerns about the impartiality of prior orders.

   **(e)** Evidence of Defendant's sustained pattern of withholding the child from Plaintiff and documented Watson family violence constitute changed circumstances warranting review.

   **(f)** The original suspension was based on allegations that have not been substantiated through adjudication. No finding of harm to L.D.W. has been entered by this Court.

### III. RELIEF REQUESTED

6. Plaintiff respectfully requests this Court:

   **(a)** Find that proper cause and/or a change of circumstances exists to revisit the current custody arrangement;

   **(b)** Conduct an evidentiary hearing on the best interest factors under MCL 722.23;

   **(c)** Restore Plaintiff's parenting time with L.D.W. on an immediate basis, either:
   - (i) Reinstating the prior 50/50 shared custody schedule that functioned for 438 days; or
   - (ii) Entering a graduated reunification schedule commencing within 14 days;

   **(d)** Enter such other and further relief as this Court deems just and equitable.

**WHEREFORE**, Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court grant this Motion and enter an order consistent with the relief requested above.

## BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF MOTION TO MODIFY CUSTODY AND RESTORE PARENTING TIME

### I. STATEMENT OF ISSUES PRESENTED

1. Whether proper cause and/or a change of circumstances exists to revisit the current custody order where Plaintiff obtained a favorable court-ordered mental health evaluation after entry of the suspension order, completed all court-ordered requirements, and has been separated from the child for 270+ total days.

2. Whether the best interest factors under MCL 722.23 favor modifying custody to restore Plaintiff's parenting time with L.D.W.

### II. CONTROLLING AUTHORITY

This motion is governed by the Child Custody Act of 1970, MCL 722.21 *et seq.*, and the following authorities:

- **MCL 722.23** — Best interest factors (a) through (l).
- **MCL 722.27(1)(c)** — Court authority to modify custody orders upon proper cause or change of circumstances.
- **MCL 722.27a** — Parenting time rights; parenting time shall be granted in frequency, duration, and type reasonably calculated to promote a strong relationship between the child and the parent.
- ***Vodvarka v Grasmeyer***, 259 Mich App 499 (2003) — Defines "proper cause" and "change of circumstances" as the threshold showing required before a court may revisit custody.
- ***Shade v Wright***, 291 Mich App 17 (2010) — Established custodial environment analysis.
- ***Pierron v Pierron***, 486 Mich 81 (2010) — The child's best interest is the paramount consideration.
- ***Troxel v Granville***, 530 U.S. 57 (2000) — The liberty interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is a fundamental right.
- ***Rains v Rains***, 301 Mich App 313 (2013) — Parenting time rights and the standard for restriction.
- ***Lieberman v Orr***, 319 Mich App 68 (2017) — Modification standard and evidentiary requirements.

### III. STATEMENT OF FACTS

7. On April 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed this custody action. L.D.W. was then approximately 17 months old.

8. On or about May 5, 2024, this Court entered a temporary order establishing a 50/50 shared custody arrangement. That arrangement remained in effect for approximately 438 days, through July 17, 2025.

9. During the 438-day period of shared custody, no adjudicated finding of harm or abuse involving Plaintiff was entered. The arrangement functioned and provided stability for L.D.W.

10. On July 17, 2025, this Court entered an ex parte order — without prior notice to Plaintiff and without an evidentiary hearing — that overturned the existing 50/50 arrangement.

11. On August 8, 2025, this Court entered a second ex parte order on the same day it was requested, suspending all of Plaintiff's parenting time.

12. On September 5, 2025, Plaintiff obtained a favorable evaluation from HealthWest (Exhibit H0002), which Plaintiff had completed as directed by the Court. This evaluation found no contraindication to Plaintiff's parenting time. Despite this favorable result, the Court has not restored parenting time and did not convene a hearing to consider the evaluation.

13. Plaintiff has satisfied all court-ordered conditions imposed in connection with the suspension of parenting time.

14. As of the date of this filing, Plaintiff has been denied all contact with L.D.W. for 230+ consecutive days and 270+ total days. L.D.W. is now 3 years old and in a critical period of social and emotional development.

### IV. ARGUMENT

#### A. Proper Cause and Change of Circumstances Have Been Established

15. Under *Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 509, "proper cause" means one or more appropriate grounds that have or could have a significant effect on the child's life, and "change of circumstances" refers to conditions that have changed since the last custody order to the extent that modification is warranted. The movant's burden is to demonstrate facts that were not previously presented to the court.

16. Plaintiff satisfies this threshold on multiple independent grounds:

**First**, the favorable HealthWest evaluation (H0002) is a new fact that postdates the suspension order. The Court ordered Plaintiff to undergo evaluation; Plaintiff complied; and the evaluation was favorable. This is classic "proper cause" — a new fact that did not exist when the prior order was entered and that bears directly on the child's best interest. *See Vodvarka*, 259 Mich App at 512.

**Second**, Plaintiff's completion of all court-ordered requirements is itself a changed circumstance. The suspension order was premised, at least in part, on conditions Plaintiff was to satisfy. Those conditions have now been met.

**Third**, the passage of 230+ consecutive days of complete separation from a bonded parent is a material change in circumstances. The Michigan Court of Appeals has recognized that the passage of significant time — particularly during a young child's formative years — can constitute a change of circumstances warranting review. *See Lieberman v Orr*, 319 Mich App at 82-83.

**Fourth**, evidence of Defendant's sustained pattern of withholding the child from Plaintiff, documented across the record, constitutes a changed circumstance. This is not a single isolated event but a continuing course of conduct that has deepened since the suspension order.

#### B. The Best Interest Factors Favor Modification

17. Once proper cause or a change of circumstances is established, the Court must evaluate the statutory best interest factors under MCL 722.23. *Pierron*, 486 Mich at 92. The factors are analyzed below.

---

**(a) Love, Affection, and Other Emotional Ties — MCL 722.23(a)**

18. Plaintiff maintained a close, bonded relationship with L.D.W. throughout the 438-day period of shared custody. Plaintiff's bond with the child is documented through records of consistent caregiving during that period.

19. Since the suspension, Plaintiff has attempted to maintain connection with L.D.W. through birthday messages and other communications. Plaintiff's separation from L.D.W. — now exceeding 270 total days — is eroding the very bond this factor is designed to protect. More than 80 days of lost parenting time have been specifically documented.

20. This factor supports restoration of parenting time to preserve the parent-child bond before further damage occurs.

**Factor (a): Favors Plaintiff.**

---

**(b) Capacity and Disposition to Give Love, Affection, and Guidance — MCL 722.23(b)**

21. Plaintiff has demonstrated consistent capacity to provide love, affection, and guidance to L.D.W. Plaintiff maintained regular communication through the AppClose co-parenting application and maintained stable employment at Shape Corp to provide financial support.

22. Defendant's disposition is called into question by 270+ days of withholding the child from Plaintiff. A parent who facilitates the child's relationship with the other parent demonstrates the disposition this factor measures; a parent who systematically blocks all contact does not.

**Factor (b): Favors Plaintiff.**

---

**(c) Capacity and Disposition to Provide Food, Clothing, Medical Care — MCL 722.23(c)**

23. Plaintiff has made 9 documented insurance payments totaling approximately $855 to maintain health insurance coverage for L.D.W. Plaintiff has also made child support payments totaling approximately $792, despite being denied all contact with the child.

24. Plaintiff has demonstrated both the capacity and the disposition to provide for L.D.W.'s material needs, even while separated from the child.

**Factor (c): Favors Plaintiff.**

---

**(d) Length of Time the Child Has Lived in a Stable, Satisfactory Environment — MCL 722.23(d)**

25. L.D.W. lived in a stable environment under the 50/50 shared arrangement for 438 days. The ex parte orders disrupted that stability.

26. Plaintiff's own housing stability has been disrupted as a collateral consequence of incarceration arising from PPO enforcement proceedings. This factor is acknowledged but must be weighed against the reality that the instability was caused by the very legal proceedings initiated by or on behalf of Defendant.

**Factor (d): Neutral to slightly favors Defendant for current housing; however, Plaintiff's prior 438-day track record of providing a stable home weighs in Plaintiff's favor.**

---

**(e) Permanence as a Family Unit of the Existing or Proposed Custodial Home — MCL 722.23(e)**

27. The existing arrangement — in which Plaintiff has zero contact with L.D.W. — is not a "family unit" at all with respect to Plaintiff. The 50/50 arrangement that functioned for 438 days provided L.D.W. with two stable family units.

28. This factor supports restoration of the arrangement that provided the child with the permanence of two involved parents.

**Factor (e): Favors Plaintiff (restoration of dual-home stability).**

---

**(f) Moral Fitness of the Parties — MCL 722.23(f)**

29. Evidence in the record raises concerns about Defendant's moral fitness, including: documented HIPAA violations related to Plaintiff's confidential information; proxy harassment conducted through third parties; and documented incidents of violence within the Watson family household.

30. These are not allegations made lightly. They are supported by documentary evidence that Plaintiff is prepared to present at an evidentiary hearing.

**Factor (f): Favors Plaintiff.**

---

**(g) Mental and Physical Health of the Parties — MCL 722.23(g)**

31. On September 5, 2025, Plaintiff obtained a favorable mental health evaluation from HealthWest (Exhibit H0002). This evaluation was conducted at the direction of the Court and found no contraindication to Plaintiff's parenting time.

32. Despite this favorable evaluation, the Court has not convened a hearing to consider it and has not restored any parenting time. The favorable evaluation is the single most significant piece of evidence under this factor, and it supports restoration.

**Factor (g): Favors Plaintiff based on favorable HealthWest evaluation.**

---

**(h) Home, School, and Community Record of the Child — MCL 722.23(h)**

33. L.D.W. is 3 years old. Available records indicate the child was thriving during the 438-day shared custody period. Plaintiff requests discovery of the child's current records to fully evaluate this factor.

**Factor (h): Insufficient information; discovery requested.**

---

**(i) Reasonable Preference of the Child — MCL 722.23(i)**

34. L.D.W. is approximately 3 years old and is not of an age to express a reasonable preference. This factor is inapplicable.

**Factor (i): Inapplicable due to child's age.**

---

**(j) Willingness and Ability to Facilitate a Close and Continuing Parent-Child Relationship — MCL 722.23(j)**

35. **This is the most dispositive factor in this case.**

36. Defendant has withheld L.D.W. from Plaintiff for 270+ total days, including 230+ consecutive days of complete denial of all contact. This is not a minor scheduling dispute. It is a sustained, deliberate campaign to sever the father-child relationship.

37. The Michigan Legislature specifically identified this factor because research consistently demonstrates that children benefit from close relationships with both parents. A parent who blocks all contact for 230+ consecutive days is demonstrating the opposite of the willingness this factor demands.

38. Defendant's use of PPO proceedings as a mechanism to prevent contact further evidences an unwillingness to facilitate Plaintiff's relationship with L.D.W. The weaponization of protective orders to gain tactical advantage in custody proceedings is well-documented in family law literature and is precisely the conduct this factor is designed to evaluate.

39. Plaintiff, by contrast, has continued to pay child support, maintain insurance coverage, and seek restoration of contact through proper legal channels — demonstrating both willingness and ability to maintain the parent-child bond despite extraordinary obstacles.

**Factor (j): Strongly favors Plaintiff.**

---

**(k) Domestic Violence — MCL 722.23(k)**

40. Plaintiff denies any adjudicated finding of domestic violence against L.D.W. or Defendant. Defendant's allegations in this regard have not been substantiated at an evidentiary hearing.

41. Plaintiff is prepared to present evidence at hearing regarding incidents of violence within the Watson family household that bear on L.D.W.'s safety in Defendant's home.

**Factor (k): Disputed; evidentiary hearing required. Available evidence favors Plaintiff.**

---

**(l) Any Other Factor Considered by the Court to Be Relevant — MCL 722.23(l)**

42. Several additional factors are relevant:

**(i) Pattern of ex parte orders.** Two ex parte orders entered in rapid succession (July 17 and August 8, 2025) overturned a 438-day arrangement without an evidentiary hearing. The use of ex parte procedures to effect such a dramatic change — from 50/50 shared custody to zero contact — raises due process concerns. *See Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. at 65-66 (recognizing fundamental parental rights under the Fourteenth Amendment).

**(ii) Favorable evaluation disregarded.** The Court ordered Plaintiff to undergo a mental health evaluation. Plaintiff complied. The evaluation was favorable. Yet no hearing has been convened to consider the result, and no parenting time has been restored. Plaintiff is entitled to meaningful consideration of evidence that he obtained in compliance with the Court's own directives.

**(iii) Conflict of interest concerns.** Plaintiff has identified a conflict involving relationships between the presiding judge and individuals connected to Defendant's household. This raises questions about the impartiality of prior rulings adverse to Plaintiff.

**(iv) Length of separation.** At 230+ consecutive days and counting, this separation constitutes one of the longest documented complete parenting time denials in a case where no adjudicated harm to the child has been found. Every additional day of separation deepens the harm to L.D.W.'s bond with Plaintiff.

**Factor (l): Strongly favors Plaintiff.**

---

#### C. The Established Custodial Environment and Burden of Proof

43. Under *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App at 26-27, an established custodial environment exists where the child naturally looks to the custodian for guidance, discipline, the necessities of life, and parental comfort over an appreciable period of time.

44. For 438 days, L.D.W. had an established custodial environment with *both* parents. The ex parte orders disrupted that dual environment. Although the passage of 230+ days with Defendant as sole custodian may have created a new unilateral custodial environment, this Court should recognize that the prior dual environment was forcibly dismantled — it did not naturally evolve.

45. Even if a clear-and-convincing standard applies, the weight of the evidence presented above — particularly the favorable HealthWest evaluation, 270+ days of withholding, and the absence of any adjudicated harm — satisfies that burden.

#### D. The Child's Fundamental Right to a Relationship with Both Parents

46. The United States Supreme Court has recognized that "the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." *Troxel v Granville*, 530 U.S. at 65. This right belongs to the parent *and* to the child.

47. Under MCL 722.27a(1), "[p]arenting time shall be granted in frequency, duration, and type reasonably calculated to promote a strong relationship between the child and the parent granted parenting time." The Michigan Legislature has thus created a statutory presumption in favor of parenting time. Restrictions on parenting time require a showing that parenting time "would endanger the child's physical, mental, or emotional health." MCL 722.27a(3).

48. No such showing has been made here. The HealthWest evaluation affirmatively found no contraindication. Plaintiff has completed all court-ordered requirements. The continued denial of all parenting time is inconsistent with the statutory mandate and with L.D.W.'s developmental needs.

### V. CONCLUSION

49. The evidence overwhelmingly supports modification. A favorable court-ordered evaluation exists. All conditions have been met. No adjudicated harm has been found. The child has been separated from her father for 270+ days. Ten of twelve best interest factors favor Plaintiff or are neutral. Factor (j) — the most telling factor in alienation cases — strongly favors Plaintiff.

50. The child's best interests require restoration of parenting time. Every additional day of separation deepens the harm. Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court schedule an evidentiary hearing at the earliest available date and, in the interim, enter an order restoring supervised parenting time pending that hearing.
