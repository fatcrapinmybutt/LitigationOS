# EXHIBIT B: Ex Parte Orders — August 8–9, 2025

**Bates Range:** PIGORS-A-B
**Filing:** Petition for Superintending Control, MCR 7.306
**Court:** Michigan Supreme Court
**Case:** Pigors v. Watson, No. 2024-001507-DC (14th Circuit Court)

---

## Description

This exhibit comprises five (5) ex parte orders issued by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill on August 8–9, 2025, in a single day, without prior notice to Plaintiff Andrew Pigors and without any hearing. These orders suspended all of Father's parenting time, modified custody from joint 50/50 to sole custody with Mother, and imposed additional restrictions — all entered ex parte. Father received no notice of the proceedings, no opportunity to respond, and no hearing before his parental rights were extinguished.

## Legal Significance

MCR 3.207(A) restricts ex parte orders in domestic relations cases to situations involving "immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage." The record demonstrates no such emergency existed:

1. **Father had exercised custody for 15+ months** under the Consent Order (Exhibit A) without any finding of harm, abuse, or unfitness.
2. **The timing reveals premeditation, not emergency.** On August 5, 2025, a recording was made at the Watson residence. On August 7, 2025, Albert Watson called police (NSPD Report No. NS2505044) and told officers: *"They want this documented so Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son."* The next day — August 8 — five ex parte orders issued.
3. **No independent judicial assessment occurred.** The police report itself shows no imminent danger to L.D.W. Officer Ella Randall's report documented no arrests, no charges, and no evidence of harm to the child.

This Court's superintending control is necessary because the 14th Circuit Court issued the most extreme remedy available in family law — complete termination of parental contact — without any of the procedural safeguards required by the Michigan Court Rules or the Due Process Clause.

## Key Evidence

- **August 5, 2025:** Recording made at Watson residence (Albert Watson intimidation incident).
- **August 7, 2025:** Albert Watson called Norton Shores Police. NSPD Report No. NS2505044 documents his statement that the call was made so *"Emily can go tomorrow to get an Ex Parte order for full custody of her son."* This proves premeditation — the ex parte orders were planned at least one day in advance.
- **August 8, 2025:** Five (5) ex parte orders issued by Hon. Jenny L. McNeill in a single day. Father received no notice of any pending motion, no service of any papers, and no opportunity to respond.
- **No emergency existed.** Father had been exercising custody peacefully for over fifteen months. The police report from August 7 documents no arrests, no charges, and no evidence of imminent danger.
- **Emily A. Watson's ex parte motion** cited a voicemail Father left on Ronald Berry's business phone and alleged erratic behavior. Watson's own filing references Officer Randall's report, which noted that the officer *"believed this was a result of meth use"* — a finding that pointed to Watson, not Father.

## Constitutional Implications

The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires, at minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a state may deprive a person of a protected liberty interest. *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319, 333 (1976). Parental rights are among the most fundamental liberty interests recognized in law. *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745, 753 (1982).

Five ex parte orders in a single day — eliminating a fit father's custody and all parenting time without notice or hearing — represents a wholesale abandonment of due process. When the orders were the product of a premeditated plan (documented in a police report the day before), the court's failure to conduct any independent inquiry before signing them constitutes an abuse of judicial authority requiring this Court's intervention.
