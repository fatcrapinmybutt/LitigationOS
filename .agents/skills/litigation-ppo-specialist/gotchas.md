# Gotchas — litigation-ppo-specialist

> Critical failure modes for PPO modification, termination, and violation
> defense in Michigan courts. Every row represents a trap that has harmed
> pro se respondents in PPO proceedings.

## Anti-Rationalization Table: PPO Modification/Termination

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "The PPO is clearly being used as a custody weapon, so the court will see through it" | The timing and motive are obvious | Courts treat PPOs and custody as separate proceedings—you must make the legal argument that the PPO is a de facto custody modification, not just complain about motive | File a formal motion with legal argument; the court won't connect the dots sua sponte |
| "I haven't done anything wrong, so the PPO should be terminated" | Innocence should be enough | Absence of new conduct doesn't automatically terminate a PPO—the petitioner's fear may be deemed ongoing based on past allegations; you must show CHANGED CIRCUMSTANCES | MCL 600.2950(12); the burden shifts at the hearing but you must file the motion and present your case |
| "The PPO was based on lies, so it's void" | False allegations should invalidate the order | A PPO issued by a court is VALID until modified or terminated—you cannot simply ignore it because the basis was false; you must challenge through proper motion | Even a wrongly-issued PPO must be obeyed until legally modified; violation while challenging = criminal charge |
| "I can modify the PPO myself by agreement with the petitioner" | We both agree on changes | Only the COURT can modify a PPO—informal agreements between parties to change PPO terms are not enforceable and may create confusion about what is legally required | Must file formal motion to modify; even if petitioner consents, the court order must be changed |
| "The PPO will expire on its own" | It's been a long time | Michigan domestic PPOs have NO automatic expiration—they continue indefinitely until terminated by court order or motion | MCL 600.2950(11); PPOs are effective until modified or terminated by the court |
| "I'll just avoid the petitioner and the PPO doesn't matter" | Compliance makes it harmless | PPO restrictions may affect custody, parenting time, firearms possession, employment, and housing—passive compliance doesn't eliminate the collateral consequences | PPO on record affects custody proceedings, background checks, firearm rights, and more |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: PPO Violation Defense

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I didn't mean to violate the PPO—it was accidental contact" | I genuinely didn't intend contact | While specific intent isn't always required, Michigan courts require that the violation be "willful"—genuinely accidental contact (e.g., same grocery store) may be defensible, but you must prove it | MCL 600.2950(23); willfulness is an element; but burden is on you to show the contact was truly accidental |
| "The petitioner contacted me first, so I can respond" | They initiated contact | Petitioner-initiated contact does NOT waive the PPO—responding to their contact is STILL a violation; only the court can modify the PPO | The PPO restricts YOU, not the petitioner; petitioner's contact doesn't authorize your response |
| "The custody order says I have parenting time, so the PPO can't stop me" | The custody order is valid and current | If the PPO and custody order conflict, the MOST RECENT ORDER generally controls—and until the conflict is resolved by the court, violating the PPO is criminal even if the custody order seems to authorize contact | File emergency motion to reconcile the orders; do NOT self-help by relying on the custody order alone |
| "My attorney said I could [contact / go there / etc.]" | Attorney advice should protect me | Advice of counsel is NOT a defense to PPO violation—only a court order modifying the PPO authorizes previously prohibited conduct | Only the court can change PPO terms; attorney advice provides no legal protection for violation |
| "The PPO terms are so vague I didn't know I was violating them" | The order is genuinely unclear | Vagueness can be a defense, but you must raise it through a modification motion or at the violation hearing—you cannot simply interpret ambiguous terms in your favor and hope for the best | Challenge vagueness proactively through modification motion; don't wait until charged with violation |
| "The petitioner won't pursue the violation because they don't want me arrested" | They've let things slide before | Law enforcement can arrest for PPO violation WITHOUT the petitioner's request—once the violation is reported, the petitioner cannot "drop charges" because it's a criminal matter handled by the prosecutor | MCL 600.2950(23); arrest without warrant; prosecution decision is the prosecutor's, not petitioner's |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Due Process Challenges

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "Ex parte PPO issuance violates my due process rights" | I had no notice or hearing | Ex parte PPOs are constitutional because they're temporary and you have the right to an immediate hearing to challenge them—the post-issuance hearing satisfies due process | Constitutionality upheld: immediate post-deprivation hearing satisfies Mathews v. Eldridge balancing |
| "The judge who issued the PPO is biased and should be disqualified" | The same judge handled the ex parte petition | Issuing an ex parte PPO does not create disqualifying bias—the judge evaluated a one-sided presentation and is expected to reconsider at the contested hearing | Ex parte proceedings are inherently one-sided; the contested hearing is the opportunity for full presentation |
| "The PPO violates my Second Amendment rights" | I can't possess firearms with a PPO | Federal law (18 USC § 922(g)(8)) prohibits firearm possession during certain PPOs—this has been challenged under the Second Amendment but UPHELD in United States v. Rahimi (2024) | United States v. Rahimi, 602 U.S. ___ (2024); domestic violence PPO firearm restriction is constitutional |
| "I can appeal the PPO to a higher court" | Appellate courts can review trial court errors | PPO decisions ARE appealable, but the standard of review is abuse of discretion—very deferential; and the appeal process takes months, during which the PPO remains in effect | MCR 7.203; appeal as of right; but abuse of discretion standard makes reversal difficult; PPO stays in effect during appeal |
| "The petitioner has to prove domestic violence to get a PPO" | A PPO should require proof of actual violence | Domestic PPOs under MCL 600.2950 can be based on THREATS, harassment, and conduct that makes petitioner reasonably fearful—actual physical violence is NOT required | MCL 600.2950(1); PPO can issue for threats, stalking, harassment, interference; no violence required |
| "My constitutional right to parent trumps the PPO" | Parental rights are fundamental | While parental rights are fundamental, they can be restricted by court order based on safety concerns—the PPO represents the court's finding that restrictions are necessary | Balance test: safety vs. parental rights; court must consider both; file motion making the constitutional argument |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Strategic Errors

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I should fight the PPO aggressively and prove she's a liar" | Truth is the best defense | Aggressive PPO defense often backfires in custody context—judge may interpret aggressive behavior as confirming the need for the PPO; measured, factual presentation is far more effective | Demeanor matters enormously in PPO hearings; calm, factual presentation beats aggressive cross-examination |
| "I should bring up everything she's done wrong at the PPO hearing" | Her bad conduct is relevant | PPO hearings are narrowly focused on whether the PPO is warranted—broader custody disputes, her alienation behavior, and unrelated misconduct are not relevant and waste your limited hearing time | Stay focused on PPO criteria; save broader issues for custody proceedings |
| "The PPO hearing is my chance to argue the whole custody case" | It's finally a hearing before the judge | PPO hearings have a SPECIFIC and LIMITED scope—whether the PPO criteria are met; the judge will cut you off if you try to relitigate custody; use this hearing ONLY for PPO issues | PPO hearing ≠ custody hearing; different standards, different scope, different outcome |
| "I should refuse the PPO hearing and challenge it in federal court instead" | Federal court is better for constitutional challenges | State court is the proper venue for PPO modification/termination; federal court will likely abstain under Younger doctrine (ongoing state proceedings involving important state interests) | Exhaust state remedies first; federal court is a supplemental path, not a replacement |
| "Getting the PPO terminated will win me custody" | Without the PPO, there's no barrier | PPO termination removes ONE obstacle but doesn't change custody—custody requires separate modification motion with best-interest analysis under MCL 722.23 | Coordinate PPO strategy with custody strategy; PPO termination is a step, not the destination |
| "I can ignore the PPO because I'll win the modification motion eventually" | The motion is pending | PPO is enforceable FROM THE MOMENT OF SERVICE until the court modifies or terminates it—pending motions do NOT suspend the PPO's effect | Full compliance is mandatory until the court rules; any violation during pending motion is criminal |

## Critical Timing Traps

- **14-day hearing requirement:** Court must hold hearing within 14 days of motion to modify/terminate
- **No automatic expiration:** Michigan domestic PPOs are indefinite—must be actively terminated
- **Violation prosecution window:** Misdemeanor statute of limitations (6 years under MCL 767.24)
- **Custody impact:** PPO existence is relevant to factor (k) (domestic violence) in custody proceedings—every day PPO exists, it builds the other party's custody case
- **Bond conditions:** Imposed immediately upon violation charge—may be MORE restrictive than PPO
- **Federal firearms prohibition:** Attaches upon issuance—violation is a federal felony (18 USC § 922(g)(8))

## Pro Se PPO Hearing Tips

1. **Prepare a written outline** — Don't rely on memory during a stressful hearing
2. **Bring documentary evidence** — Text messages, communications, timeline of events
3. **Have witnesses ready** — Character witnesses AND fact witnesses about the specific incidents
4. **Stay calm and factual** — The judge is watching your demeanor as evidence of whether the PPO is needed
5. **Address the criteria directly** — Show why EACH PPO restriction is no longer warranted
6. **Propose alternatives** — Instead of full termination, propose modifications (custody exchange exceptions, reduced restrictions)
7. **Don't attack the petitioner** — Focus on why the PPO is not needed, not on why the petitioner is wrong
