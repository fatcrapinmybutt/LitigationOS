## PPO Statutory Framework

### MCL 600.2950 — Domestic PPO
Applies when petitioner and respondent have a domestic relationship:
- Spouse or former spouse
- Person with whom respondent has a child in common
- Person who resides or has resided in the same household
- Person in a dating relationship

### MCL 600.2950a — Non-Domestic (Stalking) PPO
Applies when there is NO domestic relationship:
- Stalking behavior (MCL 750.411h, MCL 750.411i)
- Requires at least 2 or more unconsented contacts
- Higher threshold for issuance than domestic PPO

### Key Distinction for Pigors
As father of Amy Watson's child, the domestic PPO statute (MCL 600.2950) applies. This is important because:
- Lower threshold for issuance (domestic PPO)
- Broader range of prohibited conduct
- Different modification/termination standards
- Intersection with custody proceedings is governed by specific provisions

## PPO Issuance — Elements and Process

### Ex Parte Issuance — MCL 600.2950(9)
Court may issue a domestic PPO **ex parte** (without notice to respondent) if:
1. Petition alleges specific facts showing immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage
2. Petitioner's affidavit supports the allegations
3. **No bond is required** from petitioner

### What a Domestic PPO Can Prohibit — MCL 600.2950(1)
- Entering onto property where petitioner resides
- Assaulting, attacking, beating, molesting, or wounding petitioner
- Threatening to kill or physically injure petitioner
- Removing minor children (except as authorized by custody order)
- Purchasing or possessing firearms
- Interfering with petitioner at place of employment or education
- Engaging in conduct specifically prohibited by the court
- Having access to information in records concerning minor child that would reveal petitioner's location
- Engaging in any other specific act or conduct that imposes upon or interferes with personal liberty or that causes a reasonable apprehension of violence

### Respondent's Rights After Ex Parte Issuance
1. **Service:** PPO must be served on respondent
2. **Motion to modify/terminate:** Respondent may file at any time
3. **Hearing:** Court must schedule hearing within 14 days of motion to modify/terminate
4. **Burden shifts:** At hearing, petitioner bears burden of showing PPO should continue

## PPO Modification and Termination

### Grounds for Modification — MCL 600.2950(12)

#### Changed Circumstances
- Original basis for PPO no longer exists
- Threat has diminished or been eliminated
- Parties' circumstances have materially changed
- Custody order has been entered providing structured contact
- Time has passed without incident

#### Due Process Challenges
- PPO issued without adequate factual basis
- Petitioner's affidavit contained false statements
- No reasonable person would feel threatened by the alleged conduct
- PPO restrictions are broader than necessary to protect petitioner

#### Overbreadth
- PPO restricts conduct protected by the Constitution (speech, parenting rights)
- Restrictions are not narrowly tailored to address the specific threat
- PPO effectively terminates parenting time without best-interest analysis

#### Vagueness
- PPO terms are so vague that respondent cannot know what is prohibited
- "Stay away" without specifying distance or exceptions
- "No contact" without exception for custody exchanges

### Modification Motion Framework

```
MOTION TO MODIFY / TERMINATE PERSONAL PROTECTION ORDER

I. PROCEDURAL HISTORY
   - Date PPO issued, terms, basis alleged

II. CHANGED CIRCUMSTANCES
   - Material changes since issuance
   - No incidents during compliance period
   - Custody order now governs contact

III. DUE PROCESS CHALLENGE
   - Inadequate factual basis for specific restrictions
   - False statements in petitioner's affidavit
   - Restrictions exceed what is necessary

IV. OVERBREADTH / VAGUENESS
   - Constitutional rights impacted (parenting, speech)
   - Restrictions not narrowly tailored
   - Terms too vague for compliance

V. IMPACT ON PARENTING RIGHTS
   - PPO effectively modifies custody without best-interest analysis
   - MCL 722.23 factors not considered in PPO issuance
   - Parenting time rights under MCL 722.27a impaired

VI. PROPOSED MODIFICATION
   - Specific modifications requested (e.g., exception for custody exchanges)
   - Alternatively: Full termination with supporting basis

VII. PRAYER FOR RELIEF
```

## PPO and Custody Intersection

### The Custody-PPO Conflict
PPOs can effectively change custody arrangements WITHOUT the best-interest analysis required by MCL 722.23:
- "No contact" PPO prevents parenting time
- "Stay away from residence" prevents custody exchanges
- "No contact with children" modifies custody order without modification hearing

**This is a due process argument:** Using a PPO to accomplish what requires a custody modification hearing violates the respondent's fundamental right to parent.

### Reconciling PPO with Custody Order
| Scenario | Resolution |
|----------|-----------|
| PPO says "no contact" + Custody order says "parenting time" | Custody order generally controls for child contact; PPO should be modified to include custody exchange exception |
| PPO entered AFTER custody order | PPO must be read in harmony with existing custody order; motion to modify PPO to add custody exception |
| PPO entered BEFORE custody order | Custody order should address PPO and include appropriate provisions |
| PPO includes "no contact with children" | Challenge as de facto custody modification without MCL 722.23 analysis |

### Constitutional Argument
- Parental rights are FUNDAMENTAL (Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000))
- Depriving a parent of custody/parenting time requires due process
- PPO ex parte procedure provides LESS process than custody modification
- Therefore: PPO cannot be used to accomplish custody change without custody hearing

## PPO Violation — MCL 600.2950(23)

### Criminal Penalties
Violation of a PPO is a **misdemeanor** punishable by:
- Up to 93 days imprisonment
- Fine up to $500
- Or both

### Contempt Penalties
In addition to criminal prosecution, violation may be punished as contempt:
- Civil contempt (coercive)
- Criminal contempt (punitive)
- These are SEPARATE from criminal prosecution (no double jeopardy if contempt is civil)

### Bond Conditions
After PPO violation:
- Court may impose bond conditions pending hearing
- Bond conditions may include: no contact, GPS monitoring, substance abuse testing
- Violation of bond conditions → bond revocation and potential incarceration

## Emergency Relief Procedures

### Motion for Emergency Modification
When PPO creates immediate crisis (e.g., preventing scheduled parenting time):
1. File emergency motion to modify PPO
2. Request ex parte relief or expedited hearing
3. Argue irreparable harm to parent-child relationship
4. Propose specific, narrow modification (custody exchange exception)
5. Offer safeguards (neutral exchange location, third-party present)

### Timing
- Court MUST hold hearing within 14 days of motion to modify/terminate
- For true emergencies: Request same-day or next-day hearing
- Judge has authority to modify ex parte in emergency circumstances
