# PPO Violation Defense — Strategies, Defenses, and Procedures

> Complete guide to defending against PPO violation charges in Michigan,
> including substantive defenses, procedural protections, and strategic guidance.

---

## I. PPO Violation — Legal Framework

### Criminal Violation — MCL 600.2950(23)
Violation of a domestic PPO is a misdemeanor punishable by:
- **Imprisonment:** Up to 93 days
- **Fine:** Up to $500
- **Both:** Court's discretion

### Enhanced Penalties — MCL 600.2950(25)
Second or subsequent violation within certain timeframe:
- Misdemeanor with enhanced penalties
- Up to 1 year imprisonment
- Fine up to $1,000
- Court may order counseling, electronic monitoring

### Contempt — Separate Track
PPO violation may ALSO be prosecuted as contempt of court:
- Civil contempt (coercive — "comply and be released")
- Criminal contempt (punitive — definite sentence)
- Not double jeopardy if contempt is civil (different purpose)

### Arrest Authority — MCL 600.2950(22)
- Law enforcement may arrest without warrant upon probable cause
- Do not need petitioner's complaint — can act on own observation
- Must verify PPO exists in LEIN (Law Enforcement Information Network)
- Respondent typically held for arraignment within 24-48 hours

## II. Elements the Prosecution Must Prove

For a PPO violation conviction, the prosecution must establish:

| Element | Definition | Burden |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| 1. Valid PPO exists | PPO properly issued by court with jurisdiction | Beyond reasonable doubt |
| 2. PPO was served or respondent had actual knowledge | Proof of service or evidence of knowledge | Beyond reasonable doubt |
| 3. Respondent committed a prohibited act | Specific conduct matching specific PPO provision | Beyond reasonable doubt |
| 4. Violation was willful | Intentional, not accidental | Beyond reasonable doubt |

**Each element must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt.** Attack the weakest element.

## III. Substantive Defenses

### Defense 1: Ambiguity of PPO Terms
**When to use:** PPO language is vague or susceptible to multiple interpretations.

**Argument:** "The PPO provision stating '[vague term]' does not clearly prohibit the conduct alleged. Respondent interpreted the provision in a reasonable manner and did not willfully violate the order. Where an order is ambiguous, it must be construed in favor of the alleged contemnor."

**Supporting authority:**
- *In re Contempt of Auto Club Ins. Ass'n*, 243 Mich App 697 (2001): Ambiguous orders construed in favor of alleged contemnor
- Due process requires fair notice of what conduct is prohibited

**Application scenarios:**
| PPO Says | Alleged Violation | Ambiguity Argument |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "No contact" | Responded to petitioner's text | "Contact" could mean initiated contact; responding to petitioner-initiated communication is ambiguous |
| "Stay away from residence" | Was at child's school which is near residence | Distance not specified; school attendance is lawful |
| "No threatening behavior" | Sent firm text about custody exchange | "Threatening" is subjective; reasonable persons could disagree on whether the communication was threatening |

### Defense 2: Impossibility of Compliance
**When to use:** Compliance with PPO was literally impossible under the circumstances.

**Examples:**
- Court-ordered parenting time requires contact that PPO prohibits
- Employment requires presence at location PPO restricts
- Medical emergency involving children required contact
- Respondent's residence is within restricted area

**Argument:** "Compliance with the PPO was impossible because [conflicting obligation/circumstance]. Respondent acted in the least restrictive manner necessary to fulfill the competing legal obligation."

### Defense 3: No Willful Violation
**When to use:** Contact was genuinely accidental or unintentional.

**Willfulness requires intentional conduct.** Situations lacking willfulness:
- Encountering petitioner at public location by coincidence
- Third party communicated without respondent's knowledge or authorization
- Respondent was unaware of petitioner's new location
- Automated systems (mail forwarding, social media) created contact

**Evidence to present:**
- Pattern of compliance showing good faith
- Circumstances showing contact was unavoidable
- Lack of threatening or harassing intent in the contact
- Immediate withdrawal once contact was recognized

### Defense 4: Petitioner-Initiated Contact
**When to use:** Petitioner initiated or invited the contact that is now alleged as violation.

**Important:** This is NOT a complete defense — the PPO restricts respondent regardless of petitioner's conduct. BUT petitioner-initiated contact is relevant to:
- Showing respondent lacked intent to violate
- Demonstrating petitioner is not in genuine fear
- Supporting motion to terminate PPO
- Mitigating sentence if convicted

**Evidence:** Save ALL communications showing petitioner initiated contact. Do NOT delete. Screenshot with metadata.

### Defense 5: Compliance with Custody Order
**When to use:** The alleged violation was respondent exercising court-ordered parenting time.

**MCL 600.2950(1)(d):** PPO may prohibit removing children "except as provided by court order granting custody or parenting time."

**Argument:** "Respondent's conduct was authorized by the custody/parenting time order dated [date], which grants respondent [describe parenting time]. The PPO does not prohibit conduct authorized by a valid custody order."

### Defense 6: Constitutional Defense
**When to use:** The PPO or the prosecution violates respondent's constitutional rights.

**Arguments:**
- PPO is overbroad (restricts protected conduct)
- PPO is unconstitutionally vague (fair notice problem)
- Prosecution targets protected speech or petitioning activity
- PPO effectively punishes exercise of parental rights

## IV. Procedural Defenses

### Improper Service
- PPO was not properly served under MCL 600.2950(16)
- Respondent did not have actual knowledge of PPO at time of alleged violation
- Service was to wrong address or wrong person

### Defective PPO
- PPO was not properly issued (jurisdictional defect)
- PPO was issued without required findings
- PPO had expired or been terminated before alleged violation

### Statute of Limitations
- PPO violation is a misdemeanor
- General misdemeanor statute of limitations in Michigan: **6 years** (MCL 767.24)
- But prosecution delay may raise due process concerns

### Speedy Trial
- Constitutional right to speedy trial (6th Amendment)
- Michigan: MCR 6.004 — trial within specified time limits
- Delay in prosecution may warrant dismissal

## V. Violation Hearing / Trial Preparation

### Pre-Hearing Checklist
```
□ Obtain copy of PPO order (exact terms)
□ Obtain proof of service (verify proper service)
□ Review police report for alleged violation
□ Identify specific PPO provision allegedly violated
□ Determine which defense(s) apply
□ Gather evidence supporting defense
□ Identify and prepare witnesses
□ Research applicable case law
□ Prepare cross-examination questions for prosecution witnesses
□ Consider whether to testify (5th Amendment right)
□ Prepare written motion to dismiss if procedural defenses exist
```

### Cross-Examination of Petitioner

**If petitioner testifies, explore:**
1. Did petitioner initiate any contact with respondent? (texts, calls, emails)
2. Has petitioner voluntarily been in respondent's presence since PPO?
3. Did petitioner report the alleged violation immediately or wait?
4. Has petitioner made false allegations before?
5. Is the PPO hearing scheduled near a custody hearing date? (motive)
6. What specifically made petitioner feel threatened/harassed?
7. Was there any physical contact or just communication?

### Cross-Examination of Law Enforcement

**If arresting officer testifies, explore:**
1. How was PPO existence verified? (LEIN check)
2. Was PPO properly entered into LEIN?
3. What specifically did the officer observe?
4. Was respondent cooperative at time of arrest?
5. Did officer interview both parties at scene?
6. Did petitioner's story change between complaint and written statement?

## VI. Sentencing Mitigation

If convicted, mitigate the sentence by presenting:

| Mitigating Factor | Evidence |
|-------------------|----------|
| First-time violation | Criminal history (or lack thereof) |
| Compliance history | Record of compliance before and after incident |
| Minor nature of violation | Compared to what PPO was designed to prevent |
| Petitioner-initiated contact | Communications showing petitioner invited contact |
| Custody conflict | Competing court orders created confusion |
| Counseling/treatment | Enrollment or completion of anger management, counseling |
| Employment and community ties | Employment records, community involvement |
| Impact on children | Children's need for relationship with respondent |

### Sentencing Recommendations
- Request probation rather than jail time
- Offer to complete counseling or anger management
- Request modification of PPO rather than additional criminal penalties
- Emphasize rehabilitation and compliance going forward

## VII. Post-Conviction Considerations

### Appeal
- Right to appeal misdemeanor conviction
- Must file claim of appeal within 21 days of sentencing
- Appellate review: Sufficient evidence? Legal errors? Due process?

### Expungement
- Michigan expungement laws (MCL 780.621 et seq.) may apply
- Waiting period after completion of sentence
- Clean Record Act (2020) expanded expungement eligibility

### Collateral Consequences
| Consequence | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| Criminal record | Background checks, employment, housing |
| Custody proceedings | MCL 722.23(k) domestic violence factor |
| Firearm prohibition | Federal 18 USC § 922(g)(8) remains during PPO |
| Immigration | Potential deportability for domestic violence crime |
| Professional licensing | May affect certain professional licenses |

### Strategy: Avoid Conviction
Given the severe collateral consequences, explore:
1. Diversion programs (if available in Muskegon County)
2. Plea to lesser charge without domestic violence component
3. Deferred sentence / delayed sentencing
4. Dismissal based on procedural defenses
5. Acquittal based on substantive defenses
