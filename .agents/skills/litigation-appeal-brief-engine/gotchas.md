# Gotchas — litigation-appeal-brief-engine

## Anti-Rationalization Table

| # | Excuse (The Lie) | Reality (The Truth) | Consequence |
|---|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| 1 | "The standard of review section is boilerplate — just copy it from the last brief." | Standard of review is case-specific and issue-specific. Custody findings get great weight under MCL 722.28. Legal questions are de novo. Discretionary decisions use abuse of discretion. A single brief may require THREE different standards if it raises multiple issues. Copying boilerplate = wrong standard = waived argument. | The Court of Appeals will apply the WRONG standard and rule against you, or worse, find your argument waived because you failed to articulate the correct standard. MCR 7.212(C)(7) requires identification of the standard of review for each issue. |
| 2 | "We can raise this issue on appeal even though it wasn't raised at trial — it's important enough." | Michigan follows strict issue preservation requirements. Unpreserved issues are reviewed only for plain error affecting substantial rights. MCR 7.203, MCR 7.205(E). "Important enough" is not a legal standard. You must demonstrate plain error that was clear, affected the outcome, AND seriously affected the fairness/integrity of proceedings. | The COA will refuse to consider unpreserved issues or apply plain-error review, which almost never succeeds. In COA 366810 (Pigors v Watson), every unpreserved issue is a wasted page in the 50-page brief limit (MCR 7.212(B)). |
| 3 | "The 50-page limit gives us plenty of room — include every possible argument." | Brief quality inversely correlates with argument quantity. Michigan COA judges read hundreds of briefs. A focused brief with 3-4 strong arguments is more persuasive than a scattered brief with 10 weak ones. MCR 7.212(B) sets 50 pages as a MAXIMUM, not a target. | Argument dilution: strong arguments get buried among weak ones. The court may address only the weakest argument and dismiss the appeal. Focused briefs with clear issue statements win. |
| 4 | "The appendix requirements aren't strictly enforced — we can skip some exhibits." | MCR 7.212(C)(8) requires specific appendix contents including the judgment/order being appealed, relevant trial court opinions, and register of actions. Missing appendix items can result in the brief being struck or the appeal dismissed. The COA clerk's office checks appendix compliance. | Brief struck for non-compliance, requiring re-filing with corrected appendix. This wastes one of your limited filings and creates a negative impression with the panel. In pro se cases, clerks may be less forgiving of technical defects. |
| 5 | "An unpublished opinion that perfectly supports our position is just as good as a published one." | MCR 7.215(C)(1): unpublished opinions are NOT precedentially binding. You may cite them but must identify them as unpublished and cannot rely on them as primary authority. The panel is not required to follow unpublished opinions, even their own. | Relying on unpublished authority as your primary support means the court can distinguish or ignore it entirely. Always lead with published Michigan Supreme Court and Court of Appeals authority. Unpublished opinions are supplementary only. |
| 6 | "The statement of facts is just background — the legal argument is what matters." | The statement of facts is your first opportunity to frame the case. MCR 7.212(C)(6) requires a clear, concise factual statement with record citations. An effective statement of facts tells a persuasive story using the trial court record. Judges form impressions during the facts section that color their reading of arguments. | A weak statement of facts forces the court to rely on the opposing party's version. In custody cases, how factual disputes are framed (especially regarding L.D.W.'s best interests) determines whether the court sees a basis for reversal. |

## Common Failure Modes

### 1. Wrong Standard of Review
- **What happens**: Brief applies de novo review to discretionary custody findings, or abuse-of-discretion to legal questions
- **How to prevent**: Map each issue to its specific standard; MCL 722.28 for custody findings (great weight), de novo for legal questions, abuse of discretion for procedural rulings
- **Lane risk**: CRITICAL for Lane F — wrong standard = automatic loss on that issue

### 2. Record Citation Failures
- **What happens**: Factual assertions in the brief lack record citations (transcript page, exhibit number, lower court filing)
- **How to prevent**: Every factual statement must include a parenthetical record citation per MCR 7.212(C)(6)-(7); build citation map before drafting
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — uncited facts are treated as unsupported; COA will not search the record for you

### 3. Issue Preservation Gaps
- **What happens**: Brief raises issues that were never raised before the trial court, and fails to argue plain error
- **How to prevent**: Cross-reference each appellate issue against trial court record; for unpreserved issues, explicitly argue plain error under the three-prong test
- **Lane risk**: HIGH for COA 366810 — pro se litigants frequently fail to preserve issues at trial level

### 4. Page Limit Violation
- **What happens**: Brief exceeds MCR 7.212(B) 50-page limit (or applicable word count for certain filings)
- **How to prevent**: Monitor page count during drafting; prioritize strongest arguments; use appendix for supporting material that doesn't require argument space
- **Lane risk**: MEDIUM — brief can be rejected at filing, requiring emergency revision under deadline pressure

### 5. Appendix Deficiency
- **What happens**: Required appendix items missing per MCR 7.212(C)(8) — judgment, opinion, register of actions, relevant orders
- **How to prevent**: Use MCR 7.212(C)(8) checklist before filing; verify every required item is included and correctly paginated
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — deficient appendix triggers clerk rejection or court order to supplement, delaying consideration

## Integration Gotchas

- **litigation-appellate-record-specialist** must complete record compilation BEFORE brief drafting begins — you cannot cite what isn't in the record
- **litigation-authority-validator** must verify every citation in the brief — phantom citations in appellate briefs are sanctionable
- **litigation-filing-packager** handles the physical assembly — brief engine produces content, packager produces the filing
- COA 366810 is the active appeal for Pigors v Watson — all brief content must reference this docket number
- Pro se brief filings get extra scrutiny from clerk's office — technical compliance is MORE important, not less
