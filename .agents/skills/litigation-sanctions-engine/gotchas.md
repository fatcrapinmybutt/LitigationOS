# Gotchas — litigation-sanctions-engine

> Critical failure modes when seeking sanctions, fees, contempt, and default
> in Michigan courts. Every row represents a real-world trap.

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Sanctions Motions

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "Their conduct is so outrageous the judge must sanction them" | The abuse is obvious and documented | Judges have broad discretion on sanctions and often prefer graduated responses—expect warnings before nuclear options | MCR 2.313; judicial discretion is near-absolute on sanctions; appellate review is abuse-of-discretion |
| "I don't need to meet-and-confer because they'll never cooperate" | Past experience suggests futility | Courts require good-faith meet-and-confer regardless of predicted outcome—skipping it gets your motion denied | MCR 2.313(A)(5); certification is mandatory; judges deny motions lacking it even when merits are strong |
| "Filing a frivolous-action motion under MCL 600.2591 is free money" | The statute says fees are mandatory once frivolousness is found | Finding of frivolousness is HARD to obtain—courts apply a high bar; the action must be "devoid of arguable legal merit" and you must file within 56 days of final order | MCL 600.2591(3)(a); "arguable legal merit" is a generous standard; 56-day deadline is jurisdictional |
| "The court will award me the same fees an attorney would get" | Fee-shifting statutes reference attorney fees | Pro se fee equivalents are accepted in Michigan but face skepticism—you must document hours contemporaneously and justify your hourly rate with market evidence | Courts often reduce pro se fee requests; $150-$250/hour range in Muskegon County |
| "Default judgment is automatic when they miss the deadline" | The rules say defendant must respond in 21 days | Entry of default is ministerial, but default JUDGMENT requires proving damages—and courts freely set aside defaults when defendant shows any colorable excuse | MCR 2.603(D); "good cause" for setting aside is interpreted broadly; courts prefer merits resolution |
| "I can get sanctions for everything they've done across all cases" | The pattern is clear across lanes | Sanctions must be case-specific—you can reference patterns for context but each motion must address specific conduct in that specific case | Each case requires its own sanctions motion; cross-case evidence is context, not basis |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Contempt Proceedings

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "She violated the parenting time order, so contempt is guaranteed" | The order is clear and she didn't comply | Contempt requires showing the violation was WILLFUL—if she has any excuse (child's illness, misunderstanding, transportation), court may deny contempt | MCR 3.606; willfulness is an element; "unable to comply" is a complete defense |
| "Civil contempt means I can put her in jail until she complies" | Civil contempt allows coercive incarceration | The purge condition must be something the contemnor CAN PRESENTLY DO—if compliance is no longer possible (past parenting time), you can't use civil contempt | Turner v. Rogers, 564 U.S. 431 (2011); purge condition must be achievable; impossible conditions convert to criminal contempt |
| "I can file contempt myself without the prosecutor" | Contempt motions don't need the prosecutor | For CRIMINAL contempt (punishing past conduct), due process may require appointment of prosecutor or impartial prosecutor; for CIVIL contempt you can proceed directly | Criminal contempt requires greater procedural protections; Young v. U.S. ex rel. Vuitton (1987) |
| "Contempt findings carry over between cases" | A pattern of contempt should accumulate | Each contempt finding is case-specific—contempt in custody case doesn't automatically create contempt in housing case, though it may be relevant evidence | Separate proceedings required for each alleged contempt |
| "The judge must hold a contempt hearing if I file a motion" | I have a right to be heard | Courts have discretion to decline contempt proceedings if they find the violation is minor, excusable, or if other remedies are more appropriate | Judicial discretion; courts may suggest mediation, FOC intervention, or modified orders instead |
| "I don't need evidence beyond my word that the order was violated" | I was there and witnessed the violation | Contempt must be proven—civil by preponderance, criminal beyond reasonable doubt—uncorroborated testimony may be insufficient | Burden of proof is on the moving party; corroborating evidence (texts, witnesses, documents) is essential |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Default Judgment

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "Once default is entered, I've won" | Default means they lost | Default establishes LIABILITY but not DAMAGES—you still must prove damages at a prove-up hearing (for unliquidated claims) | MCR 2.603(B)(2); prove-up hearing required for unliquidated damages; judge can deny default judgment entirely |
| "The corporation can't appear because they didn't hire a lawyer in time" | Corporations must appear through counsel | Courts often grant corporations additional time to retain counsel rather than defaulting them—expect a liberal approach | Courts strongly disfavor default; corporate defendant will likely get extension if they're trying to retain counsel |
| "They can't set aside the default because they have no excuse" | They just ignored the lawsuit | MCR 2.603(D) good cause is interpreted liberally—excusable neglect, mistake, attorney error, and "interests of justice" all count | MCR 2.603(D); three factors: good cause, meritorious defense, no prejudice; courts lean toward setting aside |
| "I should wait and stockpile their failures before seeking default" | More failures = stronger case | Delay in seeking default undermines your argument for prejudice and may suggest you weren't actually harmed by the delay | Seek default promptly after deadline passes; delay weakens your position |
| "Default judgment against one defendant doesn't affect the others" | Each defendant is separate | Default against one defendant may create inconsistent findings—court must manage this carefully, especially with joint defendants | Frow v. De La Vega (1872); potential for inconsistent judgments requires careful coordination |
| "I can get default for any amount I claim" | Default admits all allegations | Court can independently assess damages even after default—unreasonable or unsupported amounts will be reduced | MCR 2.603(B); court has discretion on damages amount; must provide evidence of actual damages |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Fee Quantification

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I should bill every minute I spent on this case" | All my time was necessary | Courts examine reasonableness and will reduce for excessive time, duplicative work, and time spent on unsuccessful issues | Lodestar method: reasonable hours × reasonable rate; courts regularly cut 20-40% |
| "I can charge $400/hour because that's what big-firm lawyers charge" | I'm doing lawyer-quality work | Pro se rate must reflect LOCAL Muskegon County rates for attorney of comparable experience—$150-$250/hour is typical range | Market rate survey required; state-specific; courts reject inflated rates |
| "I don't need to document my hours because the work is obvious" | The filings prove I worked | Contemporaneous time records are expected—reconstructed records face deep skepticism and substantial reduction | Document time as you go; task descriptions must be specific; block billing gets cut |
| "I can recover fees for ALL time spent, including learning the law" | I had to research everything as a pro se litigant | Courts may reduce time for legal research that an attorney would not need—pro se learning curve is not fully compensable | Reasonable attorney standard applies; excessive research time gets cut |
| "Filing costs are trivial and don't need documentation" | Costs are small amounts | Every cost must be documented with receipts—filing fees, copies, postage, mileage—undocumented costs are denied | Keep all receipts; IRS mileage rate for travel; itemize everything |
| "I can recover emotional distress damages through sanctions" | The process caused me enormous stress | Sanctions under MCR 2.313 and MCL 600.2591 are limited to COSTS AND FEES—emotional distress requires a separate tort claim | Sanctions = costs + fees only; emotional distress damages require independent cause of action |

## Critical Timing Traps

- **MCL 600.2591 deadline:** 56 days from final judgment/order — this is JURISDICTIONAL, not discretionary
- **Default request timing:** Must be filed after 21-day answer period (or 28 days if stipulated)
- **Contempt motion timing:** File promptly after violation—stale contempt motions suggest the violation wasn't that important
- **Discovery sanctions escalation:** Each tier requires opportunity to comply before escalating
- **Fee petition documentation:** Start tracking time from DAY ONE—you cannot effectively reconstruct months later
- **MCR 2.114 timing:** Court can impose sanctions at any time, but motion practice should be prompt

## Sanctions Strategy Principles

1. **Document everything first:** Never file a sanctions motion without comprehensive documentation
2. **Escalate gradually:** Warnings → meet-and-confer → motion to compel → sanctions → contempt
3. **Be specific, not emotional:** Courts respond to specific, provable conduct, not general grievances
4. **Quantify the harm:** Always include a specific dollar amount with supporting documentation
5. **Anticipate defenses:** Address "substantially justified" and "inability to comply" in your motion
6. **Cross-reference lanes:** Use patterns across Lane A/B/C to show willfulness, not one-off mistakes
