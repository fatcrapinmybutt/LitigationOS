# Gotchas — Voir Dire Specialist

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Using voir dire as argument | Asking leading questions designed to persuade rather than uncover bias | Voir dire is for identifying bias and selecting impartial jurors, not for arguing the case; courts will stop argumentative questioning | Ask open-ended, non-leading questions designed to reveal relevant biases, experiences, and attitudes |
| Exhausting peremptory challenges on hunches | Using peremptory challenges based on appearance or demographics rather than revealed bias | MCR 2.511(E) provides limited peremptory challenges (3 per side in civil); wasting them leaves you stuck with genuinely problematic jurors later | Save peremptory challenges for jurors who reveal specific concerning attitudes but do not meet the for-cause standard |
| Missing Batson challenges | Allowing opposing counsel to use peremptory challenges in a discriminatory pattern | Batson v Kentucky protections apply in Michigan civil cases per MCR 2.511(E)(3); racially motivated strikes are prohibited | Monitor opposing party's strike pattern; raise a Batson challenge immediately if a discriminatory pattern emerges |
| Not challenging for cause when warranted | Failing to move to excuse jurors who express actual bias or conflict | For-cause challenges are unlimited (MCR 2.511(D)); failing to use them wastes peremptory challenges on jurors who should be excused for cause | Identify and challenge for cause first; use peremptory challenges only for jurors who survive the for-cause analysis |
| Ignoring jury questionnaires | Not requesting or adequately using written jury questionnaires before oral voir dire | Questionnaires save time and reveal information jurors may not volunteer orally; courts may permit them under MCR 2.511 | Request written questionnaires for complex cases; use responses to target oral voir dire efficiently |

## Failure Modes
1. **Biased Juror Seated**: Juror with undisclosed bias is seated and influences verdict. Recovery: Move for new trial under MCR 2.611(A)(1)(b) (misconduct of juror); investigate juror's background for undisclosed conflicts; this is extremely difficult to prove post-verdict.
2. **Batson Challenge Sustained Against You**: Court finds your peremptory strike was discriminatory. Recovery: Provide race-neutral justification for the strike immediately; if the court is not satisfied, the struck juror is reseated; ensure all strikes have documented non-discriminatory rationale.
3. **Insufficient Voir Dire Time**: Court limits voir dire time severely, preventing adequate questioning. Recovery: Prioritize the most critical bias areas; use written questionnaires to cover ground before oral voir dire; request additional time with specific justification.
4. **Juror Non-Disclosure**: Juror fails to disclose relevant relationship or experience during voir dire. Recovery: If discovered during trial, raise immediately; move for mistrial; if discovered post-verdict, move for new trial under MCR 2.611.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-jury-instruction-engine**, voir dire themes should align with the jury instructions you plan to request — if you will instruct on a specific legal concept, explore juror understanding of and attitudes toward that concept during voir dire.
- Data flow from **litigation-trial-preparation-specialist** determines your juror profile — identify the characteristics of favorable and unfavorable jurors BEFORE voir dire begins based on your case theory.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: Most custody determinations in Michigan are bench trials (no jury). Voir dire skills apply when tort claims, contract claims, or other jury-eligible issues are joined with or related to the family action. Verify that your case actually has a jury trial right before investing in voir dire preparation.
