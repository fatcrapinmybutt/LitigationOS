# Gotchas — Jury Instruction Engine

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Using outdated instructions | Pulling jury instructions from old form books without checking current Michigan Model Civil Jury Instructions (M Civ JI) | Instructions are periodically revised; using outdated versions creates reversible error | Always verify instructions against the current M Civ JI on the Michigan courts website; note effective dates |
| Failing to request special instructions | Relying solely on standard instructions when the case has unique factual or legal issues | Standard instructions may not cover case-specific theories; failure to request preserves nothing for appeal | Draft and submit proposed special instructions before the charge conference per MCR 2.512(B) |
| Missing the charge conference deadline | Submitting proposed instructions at trial instead of before the charge conference | MCR 2.512(B) requires instructions to be submitted before the charge conference; late submissions may be refused | Submit proposed instructions per the scheduling order; confirm deadline with clerk |
| Not objecting on the record | Disagreeing with jury instructions but failing to state specific objections after the charge | MCR 2.512(C) requires specific objections before jury retires; failure to object waives appellate review | State each objection specifically, identifying the instruction number and the precise legal error |
| Confusing civil and criminal standards | Applying criminal jury instruction standards or burdens to a civil family law matter | Family law uses preponderance of the evidence (or clear and convincing for some issues), not beyond a reasonable doubt | Verify the correct burden of proof for each claim; use M Civ JI, not M Crim JI |

## Failure Modes
1. **Waived Objection**: Failing to object to erroneous instruction before jury retires. Recovery: Argue plain error on appeal under MCR 2.613(A), but success rate is very low — prevention is the only reliable strategy.
2. **Rejected Special Instruction**: Court refuses proposed special instruction. Recovery: Place the proposed instruction and the court's denial on the record; file offer of proof showing why the instruction was necessary; preserve for appeal.
3. **Inconsistent Verdict**: Jury returns verdict inconsistent with instructions given. Recovery: Move for new trial under MCR 2.611(A)(1)(e) before entry of judgment; argue the instructions confused or misled the jury.
4. **Burden of Proof Error**: Wrong burden of proof instruction given for the specific claim. Recovery: Object specifically to the burden instruction before jury retires; if overruled, this is strong grounds for reversal on appeal.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-trial-preparation-specialist**, ensure proposed jury instructions are drafted BEFORE witness outlines — the instructions define the elements you must prove, which drives your examination strategy.
- Data flow from **litigation-motion-practice**: If a motion in limine excludes evidence, the corresponding jury instruction must be modified or withdrawn — stale instructions referencing excluded evidence confuse the jury.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: Michigan family law cases are typically bench trials (no jury), but jury demands are possible under MCL 600.1301 for certain issues. Know when jury trial rights attach — custody determinations under MCL 722.23 are inherently equitable (no jury right), but related tort claims may carry jury rights.
