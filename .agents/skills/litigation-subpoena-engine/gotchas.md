# Gotchas — Subpoena Engine

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Serving subpoena without proper form | Using a homemade subpoena form instead of an official court-issued subpoena | MCR 2.506 requires subpoenas to be issued by the court clerk or an attorney; improperly issued subpoenas are unenforceable | Use SCAO form MC 11 (subpoena) or obtain from the court clerk; pro se litigants must request issuance from the clerk |
| Insufficient witness/mileage fees | Serving a subpoena without tendering the required witness fee and mileage | MCR 2.506(D) requires tender of witness fee ($12/day) and mileage ($0.655/mile) at time of service; failure renders subpoena unenforceable | Calculate and tender fees at time of service; retain receipt showing fee tender |
| Subpoenaing privileged records | Issuing subpoena duces tecum for records protected by attorney-client, doctor-patient, or other privilege | Privileged records are not discoverable; subpoena will be quashed; may invite sanctions for abuse of process | Research applicable privileges before issuing; use proper authorization forms for medical/mental health records |
| Late service of trial subpoena | Serving subpoena too close to trial date for the witness to comply | MCR 2.506(C) requires "reasonable time" for compliance; serving days before trial gives grounds for quashing | Serve trial subpoenas at least 14 days before the hearing; serve document subpoenas earlier |
| Ignoring HIPAA requirements for medical records | Subpoenaing medical records without HIPAA-compliant authorization or proper notice | MCL 600.2157 and federal HIPAA regulations require specific procedures for medical record subpoenas including patient notification | Follow HIPAA subpoena procedures: proper authorization, notice to patient, or qualified protective order |

## Failure Modes
1. **Subpoena Quashed**: Court quashes subpoena on motion for undue burden, privilege, or improper service. Recovery: Re-serve with corrected deficiency; if burden is the issue, narrow the scope of documents or offer to pay copying costs; if privilege, withdraw or seek in camera review.
2. **Witness No-Show**: Properly subpoenaed witness fails to appear. Recovery: Move for contempt under MCR 2.506(E); request bench warrant if criminal contempt; seek adjournment to secure witness attendance.
3. **Deficient Document Production**: Records custodian produces incomplete or redacted records in response to subpoena duces tecum. Recovery: File motion to compel full production; if redactions are privilege-based, request privilege log; seek in camera review of disputed records.
4. **Third-Party Challenge**: Non-party moves to quash subpoena on grounds of burden or privacy. Recovery: Demonstrate relevance and necessity; offer to narrow scope; agree to protective order for sensitive information per MCR 2.302(C).

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-interrogatory-specialist**, use subpoenas to verify interrogatory answers — if a party claims certain facts, subpoena the records that prove or disprove those claims from third parties.
- Data flow from **litigation-timeline-forensics** identifies which third-party records are needed — phone records, bank records, social media records — issue subpoenas early in discovery to avoid trial delays.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: Pro se litigants in the 14th Circuit cannot self-issue subpoenas like attorneys can under MCR 2.506(A). You must request the clerk to issue subpoenas. Plan for this additional step — the clerk's office may need 24-48 hours to process requests.
