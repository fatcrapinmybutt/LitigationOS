# CONSTITUTIONAL CLAIMS — COMPLETE ELEMENTS

## CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHTS VIOLATIONS VIA 42 USC 1983 AND DIRECT ACTION

These claims protect the most fundamental rights. They are the backbone of Lane C (convergence) and any claim involving state actors.

---

## SUBSTANTIVE DUE PROCESS (14TH AMENDMENT)

**Standard:** Preponderance (via § 1983)
**SOL:** 3 years (borrows Michigan personal injury SOL via § 1983)
**Authority:** Washington v Glucksberg, 521 US 702 (1997); County of Sacramento v Lewis, 523 US 833 (1998)

### ELEMENTS (3)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Fundamental right | Plaintiff possesses a fundamental right or liberty interest protected by the Due Process Clause (life, liberty, property, parental rights, bodily integrity, privacy) |
| 2 | Government deprivation | A state actor deprived plaintiff of this right through executive, legislative, or judicial action |
| 3 | No legitimate justification / Shocks the conscience | The deprivation was arbitrary, conscience-shocking, or not rationally related to a legitimate government interest |

### LEVELS OF SCRUTINY

| SCRUTINY LEVEL | APPLIES WHEN | GOVERNMENT MUST SHOW | EXAMPLES |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|----------|
| Strict | Fundamental right at stake | Action is narrowly tailored to a compelling government interest | Parental rights, marriage, procreation, interstate travel |
| Intermediate | Important right (quasi-fundamental) | Action is substantially related to an important government interest | Gender-based classifications |
| Rational basis | No fundamental right | Action is rationally related to a legitimate government interest | Economic regulation, general welfare |
| Shocks the conscience | Executive action (non-legislative) | Action does not shock the conscience | Police conduct, administrative decisions |

### FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS RECOGNIZED

| RIGHT | KEY AUTHORITY | APPLICATION |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| Parental rights | Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57 (2000) | Custody interference, visitation denial |
| Family integrity | Moore v City of East Cleveland, 431 US 494 (1977) | Family living arrangements |
| Bodily integrity | Rochin v California, 342 US 165 (1952) | Physical abuse by state actors |
| Property interest | Board of Regents v Roth, 408 US 564 (1972) | Government taking without process |
| Privacy | Griswold v Connecticut, 381 US 479 (1965) | Intrusion into private affairs |

### PARENTAL RIGHTS — DEEP DIVE

**This is the crown jewel of Lane A claims.**

**Troxel v Granville, 530 US 57 (2000):**
- Parental right to make decisions regarding care, custody, and control of children is a fundamental liberty interest
- Presumption that fit parents act in best interests of children
- Government interference with parental decisions must survive strict scrutiny
- Third-party visitation statutes that don't give special weight to parent's determination are unconstitutional

**Stanley v Illinois, 405 US 645 (1972):**
- Due process prohibits taking children from fit parents without individualized hearing
- Blanket presumptions against parents violate due process
- State cannot categorically deny parental rights based on status (unmarried, etc.)

**Santosky v Kramer, 455 US 745 (1982):**
- Clear and convincing evidence standard required before state can terminate parental rights
- Preponderance standard is constitutionally insufficient for termination
- The private interest at stake (parent-child relationship) is "far more precious than any property right"

**Michigan Application:**
- Michigan Child Custody Act (MCL 722.21+) must be applied consistent with these constitutional standards
- Any custody determination that does not respect the fit parent presumption is constitutionally suspect
- Denial of parenting time without due process hearing = § 1983 claim
- Ex parte custody orders without emergency justification = due process violation

## PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS (14TH AMENDMENT)

**Standard:** Preponderance (via § 1983)
**SOL:** 3 years
**Authority:** Mathews v Eldridge, 424 US 319 (1976)

### ELEMENTS (3)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Protected interest | Plaintiff has a constitutionally protected liberty or property interest |
| 2 | Government deprivation | A state actor deprived plaintiff of this interest |
| 3 | Inadequate process | The deprivation occurred without constitutionally adequate process (notice + opportunity to be heard) |

### MATHEWS v ELDRIDGE BALANCING TEST

To determine what process is due, courts balance three factors:

| FACTOR | ANALYSIS |
|--------|----------|
| (1) Private interest | The nature and weight of the private interest that will be affected by the government action. Parental rights = extremely weighty. Property rights = significant. |
| (2) Risk of erroneous deprivation | The risk that the current procedures will lead to an erroneous deprivation, and the probable value of additional or substitute procedural safeguards |
| (3) Government interest | The government's interest, including the function involved and the fiscal/administrative burdens of additional procedural requirements |

### WHAT PROCESS IS DUE

At minimum, due process requires:
1. **Notice:** Adequate and timely notice of the action being taken
2. **Hearing:** Opportunity to be heard at a meaningful time and in a meaningful manner
3. **Neutral decision-maker:** An impartial decision-maker

Additional protections depending on the interest at stake:
- Right to counsel (in some contexts)
- Right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses
- Written findings and reasons for decision
- Right to appeal

### COMMON PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS
- Ex parte orders affecting parental rights without notice or hearing
- Custody changes without proper hearing
- Property seizure (eviction) without legal process
- Denial of access to courts
- Biased or predetermined decision-making
- Failure to provide meaningful notice of proceedings

## EQUAL PROTECTION (14TH AMENDMENT)

**Standard:** Preponderance (via § 1983)
**SOL:** 3 years
**Authority:** City of Cleburne v Cleburne Living Ctr, 473 US 432 (1985)

### ELEMENTS (3)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Differential treatment | Plaintiff was treated differently from similarly situated persons |
| 2 | No rational basis (or higher scrutiny) | The differential treatment was not rationally related to a legitimate government interest (rational basis) — or, if a suspect/quasi-suspect class is involved, fails intermediate or strict scrutiny |
| 3 | State action | The differential treatment was by a state actor or under color of state law |

### SCRUTINY LEVELS FOR EQUAL PROTECTION

| CLASSIFICATION | SCRUTINY | GOVERNMENT BURDEN | EXAMPLES |
|---------------|----------|-------------------|----------|
| Race, national origin, alienage | Strict | Narrowly tailored to compelling interest | Racial discrimination in housing, services |
| Gender, illegitimacy | Intermediate | Substantially related to important interest | Sex-based differential treatment |
| All other classifications | Rational basis | Rationally related to legitimate interest | Economic classifications, status-based |

### "CLASS OF ONE" EQUAL PROTECTION
Village of Willowbrook v Olech, 528 US 562 (2000): A plaintiff need not be a member of a protected class. An equal protection claim can be brought as a "class of one" where the plaintiff alleges being intentionally treated differently from others similarly situated with no rational basis for the difference.

**Application to Pigors:** If government officials (courts, agencies, officers) treated Pigors differently from similarly situated persons without rational justification, an equal protection claim exists regardless of whether Pigors is in a traditional protected class.

## FIRST AMENDMENT — ACCESS TO COURTS AND PETITION

**Standard:** Preponderance (via § 1983)
**SOL:** 3 years
**Authority:** BE&K Construction Co v NLRB, 536 US 516 (2002)

### ELEMENTS (3)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Protected activity | Plaintiff engaged in First Amendment-protected activity (petition for redress of grievances, access to courts, speech) |
| 2 | Adverse action | A state actor took adverse action against plaintiff |
| 3 | Causal connection | The adverse action was motivated by or caused by plaintiff's exercise of First Amendment rights |

### ACCESS TO COURTS
The right of access to courts is a fundamental right under the First Amendment and Due Process Clause:
- Interference with filing court actions
- Retaliation for filing complaints or lawsuits
- Denial of access to legal materials or assistance
- Obstruction of court processes

## FOURTH AMENDMENT — UNREASONABLE SEIZURE OF PROPERTY

**Standard:** Preponderance (via § 1983)
**SOL:** 3 years
**Authority:** Soldal v Cook County, 506 US 56 (1992)

### ELEMENTS (3)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Seizure | Government meaningfully interfered with plaintiff's possessory interest in property |
| 2 | Unreasonable | The seizure was unreasonable — lacking probable cause, warrant, exigent circumstances, or other justification |
| 3 | State action | The seizure was conducted by a state actor or at the direction of a state actor |

### APPLICATION TO HOUSING
**Soldal v Cook County, 506 US 56 (1992):** The Supreme Court held that a government-assisted eviction (removing a mobile home) constituted a "seizure" under the Fourth Amendment. This means:
- Government-involved evictions must comply with Fourth Amendment reasonableness
- Police assisting in extrajudicial evictions = Fourth Amendment violation
- Any government seizure of property (personal belongings during eviction, etc.) must be reasonable

## PARENTAL LIBERTY INTEREST — COMPREHENSIVE FRAMEWORK

This synthesizes multiple constitutional doctrines protecting the parent-child relationship.

### THE DOCTRINAL FRAMEWORK

**Tier 1: Fundamental Right Status**
- Parental right to care, custody, and control is a fundamental liberty interest (Troxel)
- Among the "oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court" (Troxel, 530 US at 65)
- Protected by the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause

**Tier 2: Presumptions**
- Fit parents presumptively act in best interests of children (Troxel)
- State must overcome this presumption before intervening
- Mere disagreement with parental decisions is insufficient basis for state intervention

**Tier 3: Procedural Requirements**
- Individualized hearing required before any deprivation (Stanley v Illinois)
- Clear and convincing evidence required for termination (Santosky v Kramer)
- Meaningful opportunity to be heard at every critical stage

**Tier 4: Limits on State Power**
- State cannot sever parent-child relationship based on blanket presumptions
- "Best interests of the child" alone is not sufficient to override parental rights — the parent must be found unfit or the child in danger
- Third parties have no constitutional standing to override parental decisions absent unfitness finding

### MICHIGAN CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTIONS
- Michigan Constitution Art 1, § 17 (due process)
- Michigan Child Custody Act must be construed consistent with federal constitutional requirements
- Michigan courts recognize parental rights as fundamental: Heltzel v Heltzel, 248 Mich App 1 (2001)

### CAUSES OF ACTION FOR PARENTAL RIGHTS VIOLATIONS
1. § 1983 — substantive due process (fundamental right)
2. § 1983 — procedural due process (inadequate process)
3. § 1983 — equal protection (differential treatment of parents)
4. State tort — IIED (extreme and outrageous interference with parental relationship)
5. State tort — interference with custody (MCL 750.350a informs civil duty)
6. State constitutional claim (Michigan Art 1, § 17)
