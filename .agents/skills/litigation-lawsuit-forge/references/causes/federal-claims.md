# FEDERAL CLAIMS — COMPLETE ELEMENTS

## FEDERAL STATUTORY AND CONSTITUTIONAL CLAIMS

These claims provide federal jurisdiction, fee-shifting, and powerful remedies. They are the cornerstone of Lane C (convergence) litigation and any action against state actors.

---

## 42 USC § 1983 — CIVIL RIGHTS ACTION

**SOL:** 3 years (borrows Michigan personal injury SOL — Owens v Okure, 488 US 235 (1989))
**Standard:** Preponderance
**Authority:** 42 USC 1983; Monroe v Pape, 365 US 167 (1961)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, punitive damages (individuals only), declaratory/injunctive relief, attorney fees (42 USC 1988)

### ELEMENTS (4)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Person | Defendant is a "person" within the meaning of § 1983 (individuals, municipalities — NOT states or state agencies in their own right per Will v Michigan) |
| 2 | Under color of state law | Defendant acted under color of statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage of a state or territory |
| 3 | Deprivation of rights | Defendant's conduct deprived plaintiff of rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution or laws of the United States |
| 4 | Causation and damages | The deprivation caused plaintiff actual, compensable damages (or nominal damages for constitutional violations even absent tangible harm) |

### UNDER COLOR OF STATE LAW — ANALYSIS

**Who qualifies as acting "under color of state law":**

| ACTOR | COLOR OF LAW? | ANALYSIS |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| Police officers | Yes | Acting in official capacity, exercising state authority |
| Judges | Yes (but absolute immunity) | Judicial acts are under color of law |
| Court clerks | Yes | Ministerial acts of the court |
| Social workers | Yes | Government employees exercising state authority |
| Prison/jail officials | Yes | State custodial authority |
| Private parties | Maybe | If acting jointly with state actors or performing traditional state function |
| Landlords | Generally no | Private action — unless conspiring with state actors |
| Contractors of government | Maybe | Depends on degree of state involvement and control |

**Joint Action / Conspiracy Test:** A private party acts under color of state law when they are "a willful participant in joint activity with the State or its agents." Lugar v Edmondson Oil Co, 457 US 922 (1982).

### INDIVIDUAL CAPACITY vs. OFFICIAL CAPACITY

| CAPACITY | LIABILITY TARGET | DEFENSES | DAMAGES |
|----------|----------------|----------|---------|
| Individual | The person, personally | Qualified immunity | Compensatory + punitive |
| Official | The governmental entity | Sovereign immunity (states); Monell requirements (municipalities) | Compensatory only (no punitive against entities) |

**ALWAYS SUE IN BOTH CAPACITIES** when suing government officials.

### QUALIFIED IMMUNITY ANALYSIS

Government officials sued in individual capacity may assert qualified immunity:

**Two-Part Test (Saucier v Katz, 533 US 194 (2001), modified by Pearson v Callahan, 555 US 223 (2009)):**

| PRONG | QUESTION | ANALYSIS |
|-------|----------|----------|
| 1 | Did defendant violate a constitutional right? | Was there an actual deprivation of a protected right? |
| 2 | Was the right "clearly established" at the time? | Would a reasonable official have known the conduct violated that right? Existing precedent must place the question "beyond debate." |

**Overcoming qualified immunity:**
- Cite specific, analogous case law showing the right was clearly established
- Show the violation was so obvious that no reasonable official could have believed the conduct was lawful
- Demonstrate a pattern of similar violations with prior notice

## 42 USC § 1983 — MONELL LIABILITY (MUNICIPAL)

**Authority:** Monell v Department of Social Services, 436 US 658 (1978)
**Applies to:** Municipalities, counties, school districts, and other local government entities
**Does NOT apply to:** States and state agencies (11th Amendment immunity)

### ELEMENTS (6)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Constitutional violation | An underlying constitutional violation occurred |
| 2 | Person under § 1983 | The municipality is a "person" under § 1983 |
| 3 | Policy | The violation was caused by an official policy adopted by the municipality's governing body |
| 4 | OR Custom | The violation was caused by a widespread, persistent practice so common as to constitute a custom with the force of law |
| 5 | OR Failure to train/supervise | The municipality's failure to train or supervise employees amounts to deliberate indifference to constitutional rights (City of Canton v Harris, 489 US 378 (1989)) |
| 6 | Moving force | The policy, custom, or failure was the "moving force" behind the constitutional violation — direct causal link |

### PROVING MONELL LIABILITY

| THEORY | WHAT TO SHOW | EVIDENCE |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Official policy | Written policy, ordinance, regulation, or official decision | Policy documents, meeting minutes, official statements |
| Custom/practice | Pattern of similar violations tolerated by municipality | Prior complaints, similar incidents, lack of discipline |
| Failure to train | Inadequate training + deliberate indifference | Training records (or lack thereof), known risks, failure to respond to pattern |
| Ratification | Final policymaker approved or ratified the unconstitutional act | Policymaker's statements, failure to discipline, explicit approval |

**NO RESPONDEAT SUPERIOR:** A municipality cannot be held liable under § 1983 on a respondeat superior theory. The municipality must have its OWN culpable conduct (policy/custom/failure to train).

## 42 USC § 1985(3) — CONSPIRACY TO DEPRIVE CIVIL RIGHTS

**SOL:** 3 years (borrows state personal injury SOL)
**Standard:** Preponderance
**Authority:** Griffin v Breckenridge, 403 US 88 (1971)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, punitive damages

### ELEMENTS (5)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Conspiracy | Two or more persons conspired together |
| 2 | Purpose | The conspiracy was for the purpose of depriving any person or class of persons of equal protection of the laws, or of equal privileges and immunities under the laws |
| 3 | Act in furtherance | One or more of the conspirators committed an act in furtherance of the conspiracy |
| 4 | Class-based animus | The conspiracy was motivated by some racial or otherwise class-based, invidiously discriminatory animus |
| 5 | Injury | Plaintiff was injured in person or property, or was deprived of exercising any right or privilege of a citizen |

### CLASS-BASED ANIMUS REQUIREMENT
§ 1985(3) requires discriminatory animus directed at a protected class. This is a significant limitation:
- Race: Always qualifies
- National origin: Qualifies
- Sex/gender: Courts are split but trend toward inclusion
- Religion: Qualifies in most circuits
- Other classes: Uncertain — argue by analogy to recognized classes
- Political affiliation: Generally does NOT qualify
- "Class of one": Generally does NOT qualify for § 1985(3) (unlike equal protection)

### INTRACORPORATE CONSPIRACY DOCTRINE
Employees of the same entity generally cannot conspire with each other for § 1985 purposes (intracorporate conspiracy doctrine). BUT: Exception exists when employees act outside scope of employment for personal reasons.

## 42 USC § 1986 — NEGLECT TO PREVENT CONSPIRACY

**SOL:** 1 year from the date the cause of action accrued (VERY SHORT — 42 USC 1986)
**Standard:** Preponderance
**Authority:** 42 USC 1986
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages

### ELEMENTS (4)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Knowledge | Defendant had knowledge that a § 1985 conspiracy was being planned or was in progress |
| 2 | Power to prevent | Defendant had the power to prevent or aid in preventing the conspiracy |
| 3 | Neglect or refusal | Defendant neglected or refused to prevent the conspiracy |
| 4 | Actionable § 1985 violation | There is a viable underlying § 1985 claim (§ 1986 is derivative — cannot exist without § 1985) |

### KEY LIMITATIONS
- **Derivative claim:** § 1986 REQUIRES a valid § 1985 claim. If § 1985 fails, § 1986 necessarily fails.
- **1-year SOL:** This is one of the shortest limitation periods in federal civil rights law. Calendar carefully.
- **Typical defendants:** Supervisors who knew of subordinates' conspiracy; officials who ignored complaints of conspiratorial conduct.

## FAIR HOUSING ACT (42 USC 3601+)

**SOL:** 2 years for civil action (42 USC 3613(a)(1)(A)); 1 year for HUD administrative complaint (42 USC 3610(a)(1)(A))
**Standard:** Preponderance
**Authority:** 42 USC 3601–3631
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, punitive damages, injunctive relief, attorney fees (42 USC 3613(c))

### ELEMENTS (4)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Dwelling | The property at issue qualifies as a "dwelling" under the FHA (broadly defined — includes rental housing) |
| 2 | Protected class | Plaintiff is a member of a protected class: race, color, religion, sex, familial status, national origin, or disability |
| 3 | Discriminatory action | Defendant engaged in a prohibited discriminatory act (refusal to rent, discriminatory terms, harassment, retaliation, etc.) |
| 4 | Causation and damages | The discriminatory action caused plaintiff damages (actual damages, including emotional distress, are recoverable) |

### PROHIBITED ACTS (42 USC 3604)

| SUBSECTION | PROHIBITION |
|-----------|-------------|
| 3604(a) | Refusal to sell or rent based on protected class |
| 3604(b) | Discriminatory terms, conditions, privileges, or services |
| 3604(c) | Discriminatory statements, notices, or advertisements |
| 3604(d) | Representing that dwelling is unavailable when it is (steering) |
| 3604(f) | Disability-based discrimination (includes failure to make reasonable accommodations) |

### RETALIATION (42 USC 3617)
It is unlawful to coerce, intimidate, threaten, or interfere with any person exercising FHA rights. This includes retaliation for filing complaints.

### DISPARATE IMPACT
FHA claims can be based on discriminatory intent OR disparate impact. Texas Dept of Housing v Inclusive Communities Project, 576 US 519 (2015). A facially neutral policy that disproportionately affects a protected class may violate the FHA.

## ADA TITLE II / SECTION 504 OF THE REHABILITATION ACT

**SOL:** 2 years (borrows state personal injury SOL in most circuits)
**Authority:** 42 USC 12131+ (ADA); 29 USC 794 (Section 504)
**Remedies:** Injunctive relief, compensatory damages, attorney fees

### ADA TITLE II ELEMENTS (4)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Qualified individual with disability | Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability as defined by the ADA |
| 2 | Public entity | Defendant is a public entity or receives federal financial assistance (Section 504) |
| 3 | Exclusion or discrimination | Plaintiff was excluded from participation in, or denied the benefits of, services, programs, or activities of the public entity, by reason of disability |
| 4 | Damages | Plaintiff suffered damages from the discrimination |

### APPLICATION TO HOUSING/GOVERNMENT
- Housing authorities that receive federal funds must comply with both ADA Title II and Section 504
- Failure to provide reasonable accommodations in government-operated or -funded housing = violation
- Inaccessible government buildings/courthouses = potential violation of access to courts

## 14TH AMENDMENT — DIRECT ACTION

**Authority:** 14th Amendment, § 1 (self-executing as to state action)
**Vehicle:** Typically brought via 42 USC 1983, but can be pled as direct constitutional claim

### ELEMENTS (3)

| # | ELEMENT | DESCRIPTION |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | State action | Defendant is a state actor or acting under color of state law |
| 2 | Constitutional violation | Defendant violated plaintiff's rights under the 14th Amendment (due process, equal protection, privileges and immunities) |
| 3 | Damages | Plaintiff suffered injury from the violation |

### WHEN TO PLEAD DIRECT 14TH AMENDMENT CLAIM
- When § 1983 might not reach the defendant (state agencies in certain contexts)
- As an alternative theory alongside § 1983
- When seeking declaratory or injunctive relief against state officials (Ex parte Young doctrine, 209 US 123 (1908))

### ATTORNEY FEES
42 USC 1988 provides for attorney fees in § 1983 and constitutional claims. While pro se litigants generally cannot recover attorney fees (Kay v Ehrler, 499 US 432 (1991)), preserve the argument in case counsel is later retained.

## FEDERAL CLAIMS STRATEGIC NOTES

### FILING IN FEDERAL COURT
- Federal question jurisdiction: 28 USC 1331 — no minimum amount in controversy
- Supplemental jurisdiction for state claims: 28 USC 1367
- Venue: 28 USC 1391 — district where any defendant resides or where the claim arose
- Western District of Michigan covers Muskegon County

### ADVANTAGES OF FEDERAL COURT
- Federal judges may be more receptive to constitutional claims
- Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (broader discovery tools)
- Jury trial right for § 1983 damages claims
- Fee-shifting under 42 USC 1988
- Published opinions create binding precedent

### DISADVANTAGES OF FEDERAL COURT
- Stricter pleading standards (Twombly/Iqbal)
- No punitive damages against municipal entities
- Supplemental state claims can be declined (28 USC 1367(c))
- Higher filing fee ($405 vs ~$175 state)
- Distance to courthouse (Grand Rapids vs Muskegon)
