# Gotchas — litigation-emergency-motion-engine

## Anti-Rationalization Table

| # | Excuse (The Lie) | Reality (The Truth) | Consequence |
|---|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| 1 | "Everything feels like an emergency — we should file emergency motions frequently for maximum impact." | Courts reserve emergency procedures for GENUINE emergencies. MCR 2.119(F) requires showing that "immediate, significant, and irreparable harm" will result without emergency relief. Filing non-emergencies as emergencies burns judicial goodwill. Judge McNeill will remember the boy who cried wolf. | Judicial skepticism of ALL future filings, including real emergencies. Possible sanctions for frivolous emergency filings. The court may impose a requirement that future emergency motions be pre-screened, adding delay when you actually need emergency relief. |
| 2 | "An ex parte order doesn't need notice to the other side — that's the whole point." | Ex parte orders under MCR 3.207 have STRICT requirements: (1) irreparable injury will result from delay, (2) the moving party certifies efforts to give notice OR reasons why notice should not be required, and (3) the order is temporary pending a hearing with both parties. MCR 3.207(B) requires a hearing within 14 days. | Filing ex parte without proper certification of notice efforts = motion denied. Filing ex parte without genuinely qualifying circumstances = sanctions. The 14-day hearing requirement means ex parte is NOT a shortcut — it's a faster path to a two-party hearing. |
| 3 | "The emergency motion just needs to say things are urgent — the details can wait for the hearing." | Emergency motions require MORE specificity, not less. The court is being asked to act quickly with limited information. MCR 2.119(F)(1) requires: specific facts showing immediate harm, why normal motion procedures are inadequate, and the specific relief requested. Vague urgency claims get denied. | A denied emergency motion means waiting for the regular motion calendar, which could be weeks away. Worse, a denied emergency motion on record makes it harder to file a second emergency motion on the same issue. Get it right the first time or lose the emergency pathway. |
| 4 | "We filed the emergency motion — our job is done until the hearing." | Emergency motions create downstream obligations: (1) serve on opposing party immediately, (2) prepare for the 14-day hearing (MCR 3.207(B)), (3) comply with any conditions the court attaches to emergency relief, and (4) be available for telephone conference if the court wants to hear from both sides before ruling. | Missing the 14-day hearing after obtaining ex parte relief = the order lapses. Failing to serve the opposing party = due process violation that voids the order. Emergency motions require MORE follow-up, not less. |
| 5 | "Emergency custody motions and emergency PPO motions use the same procedure." | Different emergency types use different procedures: custody emergencies use MCR 3.207 (ex parte custody orders); PPO emergencies use MCL 600.2950 and MCR 3.310; TROs use MCR 3.310(A)-(C). Each has different standards, different forms, and different follow-up requirements. | Filing the wrong type of emergency motion for the situation = procedural defect and likely denial. A custody emergency isn't a PPO; a PPO isn't a TRO. Using the wrong vehicle means the court applies the wrong standard and probably denies relief. |

## Common Failure Modes

### 1. Insufficient Irreparable Harm Showing
- **What happens**: Motion claims emergency but facts show the harm is economic, compensable, or not truly immediate — court denies for failure to show irreparable harm
- **How to prevent**: Document specific, concrete, IMMINENT harm that cannot be undone with money; include declarations with specific facts; attach supporting evidence (police reports, medical records, threatening communications)
- **Lane risk**: CRITICAL — denied emergency motion means no relief during the emergency

### 2. Failure to Serve After Ex Parte Order
- **What happens**: Ex parte order obtained but not promptly served on opposing party, creating due process issues
- **How to prevent**: Serve the ex parte order AND the motion papers on the same day; use personal service for immediate effect; file proof of service with the court
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — unserved ex parte orders may be vacated and create judicial skepticism

### 3. Missing the 14-Day Hearing
- **What happens**: Ex parte order expires because the required follow-up hearing under MCR 3.207(B) was not scheduled or attended
- **How to prevent**: Calendar the 14-day deadline IMMEDIATELY upon obtaining the ex parte order; prepare for the hearing as soon as the order is entered; file notice of hearing
- **Lane risk**: CRITICAL — expired emergency order means back to square one

### 4. Wrong Emergency Vehicle
- **What happens**: Filing a MCR 3.207 custody emergency motion when the situation calls for a MCR 3.310 TRO, or vice versa
- **How to prevent**: Analyze the situation: Is the threat to the child (MCR 3.207)? Is the threat to a party requiring restraint (MCR 3.310)? Is it a PPO matter (MCL 600.2950)? Match the relief to the correct procedure
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — wrong procedure = wrong standard = likely denial

### 5. Emotional Rather Than Factual Presentation
- **What happens**: Emergency motion relies on emotion and conclusory statements rather than specific, provable facts
- **How to prevent**: Every claim of emergency must be backed by evidence: dates, times, witnesses, documents. Replace "Respondent is a danger" with "On January 15, 2025, Respondent [specific conduct] as documented in [Exhibit A]"
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — judges rule on facts, not feelings
