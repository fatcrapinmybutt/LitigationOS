# Gotchas — litigation-deposition-strategist

## Anti-Rationalization Table

| # | Excuse (The Lie) | Reality (The Truth) | Consequence |
|---|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| 1 | "We can wing the deposition — we know the case well enough to ask good questions on the fly." | Depositions are the MOST expensive discovery tool (reporter fees, transcript costs, time). Every question must serve a strategic purpose: lock in testimony, expose contradictions, preserve testimony for trial, or create impeachment material. Unplanned questions waste money and may open harmful lines of inquiry. | Rambling depositions that cost hundreds in transcript fees but produce nothing usable at trial. Worse, unplanned questions may elicit harmful testimony that you've now preserved on the record. In Pigors v Watson, deposition costs are significant for a pro se litigant. |
| 2 | "The deposition is about getting information — we'll worry about impeachment later." | Information-gathering and impeachment preparation happen SIMULTANEOUSLY. When a witness gives testimony that contradicts prior statements, documents, or other testimony, you must be prepared to follow up immediately. Having the contradicting evidence organized and accessible during the deposition is essential. | Missing impeachment opportunities in real-time. Once the deposition ends, you can't go back and ask the follow-up question that would have locked in the contradiction. Under MRE 613, prior inconsistent statements from depositions are powerful impeachment tools at trial. |
| 3 | "We don't need to depose friendly witnesses — they'll testify for us at trial." | Friendly witnesses become unavailable (move, illness, schedule conflicts). MCR 2.308 allows deposition testimony to be used at trial when the witness is unavailable. Additionally, deposing your own witnesses preserves their testimony and prepares them for cross-examination at trial. | Key testimony lost because the friendly witness moved out of state or became ill before trial. Without a deposition, their testimony is simply gone. MCR 2.308(A)(2) specifically allows deposition use when the witness is more than 100 miles from the courthouse. |
| 4 | "The 7-hour limit isn't important — we'll get everything we need in a few hours." | MCR 2.306(D)(3) limits depositions to one day of seven hours. This limit applies unless the court orders otherwise or parties stipulate. With breaks, objections, and lawyer delays, you have approximately 5-6 hours of actual questioning. Poor time management means critical topics get rushed or skipped at the end. | Running out of time before covering critical topics. The last hour of a deposition is often the most important (the witness is tired and less guarded), but if you spent 4 hours on background, you'll never reach the important questions. Budget time per topic before the deposition starts. |
| 5 | "Objections at depositions work like trial objections — we should object frequently." | MCR 2.306(D)(4) limits deposition objections to form and privilege. Substantive objections are preserved for trial. Excessive objections at depositions (especially coaching objections like "if you know" or "don't speculate") can result in sanctions under MCR 2.306(D)(4) and MCR 2.313. | Sanctions for obstructive conduct during depositions. Judge McNeill has authority to impose sanctions including fees, adverse inferences, or striking the deposition. Keep objections to form only and let the witness answer — you can address admissibility at trial. |

## Common Failure Modes

### 1. No Question Outline
- **What happens**: Examiner walks into deposition without a structured question plan, resulting in disorganized, repetitive, and inefficient questioning
- **How to prevent**: Prepare topic-based question outline with time allocations; organize exhibits in order of use; have impeachment documents pre-tabbed and accessible
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — wasted deposition = wasted money and missed evidence

### 2. Failure to Lock In Testimony
- **What happens**: Witness gives favorable testimony but examiner doesn't get specific, quotable commitments that can be used at trial
- **How to prevent**: When a witness says something helpful, follow up with "So to be clear, your testimony is that [specific statement]?" Get the admission nailed down in clear, unambiguous language
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — vague testimony is easy to walk back at trial

### 3. Tipping Strategic Hand
- **What happens**: Questions reveal the examiner's litigation strategy and key evidence to opposing counsel
- **How to prevent**: Be strategic about WHAT you ask — not every question reveals your theory; save some impeachment material for trial; don't show all your documents
- **Lane risk**: MEDIUM — revealing strategy allows opponent to prepare defenses

### 4. Missing Document Foundation
- **What happens**: Showing a document to a witness during deposition without proper foundation, making the exchange usable for impeachment but the document inadmissible
- **How to prevent**: Authenticate each document before asking about its contents: "I'm showing you what's been marked as Exhibit [X]. Do you recognize this document?" Establish foundation per MRE 901/902
- **Lane risk**: MEDIUM — unauthenticated documents can't be admitted at trial

### 5. Inadequate Deposition Notice
- **What happens**: Deposition notice doesn't comply with MCR 2.306(B) requirements, giving opponent grounds to quash or decline to appear
- **How to prevent**: Verify notice includes: date, time, location, officer name, and documents requested (if duces tecum); serve with reasonable notice (usually 14+ days); check for scheduling conflicts
- **Lane risk**: MEDIUM — procedural defects delay the deposition and waste time
