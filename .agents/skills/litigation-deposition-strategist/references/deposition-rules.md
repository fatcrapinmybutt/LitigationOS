# Deposition Rules — Michigan Court Rules Reference

## MCR 2.306 — Depositions Upon Oral Examination

### MCR 2.306(A) — When Depositions May Be Taken
- After commencement of action
- By leave of court if before responsive pleading filed
- Without leave after responsive pleading filed

### MCR 2.306(B) — Notice Requirements
Deposition notice must state:
1. Time and date of deposition
2. Place of deposition
3. Name and address of deponent (or description if name unknown)
4. Officer before whom deposition will be taken
5. If duces tecum: specific documents/materials to produce

**Service timing**: Reasonable notice required (Michigan practice: minimum 14 days)

**Sample Notice Format:**
```
NOTICE OF TAKING DEPOSITION

TO: [Party/Witness name and address]

PLEASE TAKE NOTICE that on [date] at [time], at [location],
the undersigned will take the deposition of [deponent name]
upon oral examination before a notary public or other officer
authorized to administer oaths, pursuant to MCR 2.306.

[If duces tecum:]
The deponent is requested to bring and produce the following
documents and materials:
1. [Specific description]
2. [Specific description]
3. [Specific description]

This deposition may be recorded by [video/audio/stenographic means].

Date: _______________
[Party name]
[Address]
[Phone]
```

### MCR 2.306(D) — Conduct During Depositions

#### MCR 2.306(D)(3) — Duration Limits
- **One day of seven hours** unless court orders otherwise
- Parties may stipulate to different duration
- Breaks don't count toward the seven hours
- Court reporter setup/breakdown time excluded

#### MCR 2.306(D)(4) — Objections
**Permitted objections during deposition:**
- Objections to form (leading, compound, vague, assumes facts)
- Objections to privilege (attorney-client, work product)
- Motions to terminate for bad faith conduct

**NOT permitted:**
- Substantive objections (relevance, hearsay) — preserved for trial
- Speaking objections that coach the witness
- Instructions not to answer (except privilege or court order)

**Sanctions for obstruction:**
Per MCR 2.306(D)(4) and MCR 2.313, obstructive conduct may result in:
- Costs and attorney fees
- Order to answer
- Adverse inference
- Striking testimony

### MCR 2.307 — Subpoenas for Depositions
For non-party witnesses:
- Subpoena required under MCR 2.305
- Must be served personally
- Witness fees and mileage must be tendered
- Failure to comply → contempt
- Geographic limitation: generally within Michigan

### MCR 2.308 — Use of Depositions at Trial

#### When Deposition May Be Used
| Situation | Use | Authority |
|-----------|-----|-----------|
| Impeachment | Always available | MCR 2.308(A)(1) |
| Party admission | Against the party deponent | MCR 2.308(A)(2) |
| Witness unavailable | In lieu of live testimony | MCR 2.308(A)(3) |
| Witness >100 miles away | In lieu of live testimony | MCR 2.308(A)(3)(c) |
| Witness unable to attend | Illness, age, infirmity | MCR 2.308(A)(3)(d) |
| Exceptional circumstances | Court discretion | MCR 2.308(A)(3)(e) |

#### Reading Deposition at Trial
- May read any portion of a party's deposition
- Adverse party may require reading of additional portions for context
- Video depositions may be played
- Court may limit cumulative testimony

## MRE Rules Relevant to Depositions

### MRE 613 — Prior Inconsistent Statements
- Witness must be given opportunity to explain or deny
- Extrinsic evidence admissible if witness doesn't admit
- Foundation: identify the statement, give opportunity to explain
- Deposition testimony is a prime source of prior inconsistent statements

### MRE 801(d)(1) — Prior Statements of Witnesses
- Prior inconsistent statements given under oath at a proceeding: NOT hearsay
- Depositions are proceedings under oath
- Statement may be used substantively (not just for impeachment)

### MRE 801(d)(2) — Admissions of Party Opponent
- A party's deposition statement is admissible against them
- Not hearsay — admission by party opponent
- No foundation requirements beyond identification of speaker

### MRE 611(b) — Scope of Cross-Examination
At deposition, scope is broad:
- Not limited to direct examination topics
- Any relevant matter may be explored
- Work product and privilege still protect certain areas

## Deposition Logistics

### Location Selection
| Option | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Attorney's office | Familiar, professional | Travel for witness |
| Court reporter's office | Neutral ground | Less comfortable |
| Court facility | Authoritative setting | Availability issues |
| Remote (video) | Convenient, cost-effective | Technical issues |

### Recording Methods
| Method | MCR Authority | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Stenographic | MCR 2.306(E)(1) | Standard — produces written transcript |
| Audio | MCR 2.306(E)(2) | Cheaper — less useful for trial |
| Video | MCR 2.306(E)(3) | Most impactful at trial — captures demeanor |
| Combined steno + video | MCR 2.306(E) | Best of both — higher cost |

### Cost Estimates (Pro Se Budget)
| Item | Estimated Cost | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Court reporter (half day) | $200-$400 | Appearance fee |

| Video recording | $300-$500 | Optional — adds visual |

| Subpoena service | $25-$75 | Non-party witnesses |
| Witness fees | $12/day + mileage | MCL 600.2552 |
| **Total (typical)** | **$500-$1,500** | Per deposition |
