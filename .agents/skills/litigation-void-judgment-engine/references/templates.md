## Motion to Set Aside Void Judgment

```
STATE OF MICHIGAN
IN THE 14TH CIRCUIT COURT FOR THE COUNTY OF MUSKEGON
FAMILY DIVISION

ANDREW JAMES PIGORS,
    Plaintiff,                          Case No. 2024-001507-DC
                                        Hon. Jenny L. McNeill
v.

EMILY A. WATSON,
    Defendant.
_________________________________/

MOTION TO SET ASIDE VOID JUDGMENT
PURSUANT TO MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d)

    Plaintiff moves to set aside the [judgment/order] entered [DATE]
on the ground that it is void:

I. JURISDICTIONAL DEFECT:
    1. The [judgment/order] is void because the Court lacked
       [subject matter jurisdiction / personal jurisdiction /
       acted in excess of jurisdiction].

    2. Specifically: [describe the jurisdictional defect with
       precision — e.g., service of process was defective because
       (describe deficiency), the Court lacked authority to enter
       the specific relief ordered because (cite limitation)]

II. EVIDENCE OF VOID STATUS:
    3. Attached hereto as Exhibit A is [evidence of defective
       service / evidence of jurisdictional defect].
    4. [Additional supporting evidence as Exhibit B, C, etc.]

III. LEGAL STANDARD:
    5. Under Bowie v Arder, 441 Mich 23 (1992), a judgment is void
       when the rendering court lacked jurisdiction.
    6. Void judgments may be attacked at any time with no time limit.
       MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d); MCR 2.612(C)(3).
    7. A void judgment is a legal nullity — it has no legal effect
       and need not be obeyed. Altman v Nelson, 197 Mich App 467.

IV. COLLATERAL CONSEQUENCES:
    8. The following actions were taken in reliance on the void
       judgment: [identify downstream orders, transfers, etc.]
    9. These must be addressed upon vacatur: [proposed remedy]

WHEREFORE, Plaintiff requests:
    a. The [judgment/order] entered [DATE] be set aside as void;
    b. All downstream orders based on the void judgment be vacated;
    c. [Specific additional relief];
    d. Such other relief as justice requires.

Respectfully submitted,

____________________________
Andrew James Pigors, Pro Se
Date: _______________
```

## Independent Action to Vacate Judgment

```
COMPLAINT — INDEPENDENT ACTION FOR RELIEF FROM JUDGMENT
(MCR 2.612(C)(3))

    Plaintiff brings this independent action for relief from the
judgment entered in Case No. [ORIGINAL CASE] on [DATE]:

COUNT I — VOID JUDGMENT
    1. [Describe original case and judgment]
    2. The judgment is void because: [jurisdictional defect]
    3. [Supporting facts]

COUNT II — FRAUD ON THE COURT (if applicable)
    4. The judgment was obtained through fraud on the court because:
       [describe fraudulent conduct]
    5. The fraud prevented fair adjudication of the merits.
    6. Under Averill v Dauterman, 284 Mich App 18 (2009), fraud
       on the court has no time limitation.

RELIEF REQUESTED:
    a. Declaratory judgment that the [judgment/order] is void;
    b. Injunctive relief preventing enforcement of void judgment;
    c. [Damages if applicable];
    d. Costs and fees.
```

## Void Judgment Analysis Worksheet

| Element | Analysis | Evidence | Conclusion |
|---------|---------|----------|-----------|
| **Subject Matter Jurisdiction** | Did court have authority over this type of case? | [Court rules, statutes] | Proper / Lacking |
| **Personal Jurisdiction** | Was defendant properly before the court? | [Service records, proof of service] | Proper / Lacking |
| **Service of Process** | Was service made per MCR 2.105? | [Proof of service, affidavit] | Proper / Defective |
| **Excess of Jurisdiction** | Did court exceed its authority? | [Order vs. statutory limits] | Within / Exceeded |
| **Due Process** | Was adequate notice and opportunity to be heard provided? | [Notices, hearing records] | Satisfied / Violated |
| **CLASSIFICATION** | | | **VOID / VOIDABLE** |

## Workflow: Void Judgment Challenge

```
Step 1: Identify the judgment/order to challenge
    ↓
Step 2: Analyze: void (jurisdictional) or voidable (non-jurisdictional)?
    ↓
Step 3: Gather evidence of jurisdictional defect
    ↓
Step 4: Choose vehicle:
    ├── MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d) motion (within existing case)
    ├── Independent action (MCR 2.612(C)(3)) (separate case)
    ├── Collateral attack (in another proceeding)
    ↓
Step 5: Draft motion/complaint with supporting evidence
    ↓
Step 6: Address all collateral consequences in the motion
    ↓
Step 7: Serve on all parties; file with court
    ↓
Step 8: Attend hearing; present jurisdictional evidence
    ↓
    ├── If GRANTED → Judgment vacated; address downstream orders
    ├── If DENIED → Appeal (de novo review on jurisdictional question)
```

## Common Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Better Approach |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Calling every bad judgment "void" | Court dismisses as meritless; credibility damaged | Only true jurisdictional defects make judgments void |
| Substituting for timely appeal | Court sees through attempt to bypass 21-day deadline | File timely appeal for legal errors; reserve void theory for genuine jurisdictional defects |
| Bare allegations without evidence | Court presumes jurisdiction; you bear the burden of proof | Attach documentary evidence proving jurisdictional defect |
| Ignoring collateral consequences | Vacating judgment creates chaos if downstream orders aren't addressed | Map all downstream orders and request comprehensive relief |
| Waiting decades to challenge | Even void judgments face judicial skepticism for extreme delay | Challenge as soon as the jurisdictional defect is identified |
