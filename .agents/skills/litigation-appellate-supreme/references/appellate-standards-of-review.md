# Appellate Standards of Review — litigation-appellate-supreme

## Overview

The standard of review determines how much deference the appellate court gives to the
trial court's decision. Selecting the wrong standard fundamentally misframes the argument.
This reference covers all Michigan appellate standards with controlling citations.

---

## Standard 1: De Novo Review

**Applies to**: Questions of law, statutory interpretation, constitutional issues,
jurisdictional questions, standing, summary disposition rulings.

**Definition**: The appellate court reviews the issue independently, with no deference
to the trial court's conclusion.

**Key language**: "We review de novo questions of law." "Constitutional issues are
reviewed de novo." "Summary disposition is reviewed de novo."

**Controlling authority**:
- *Dep't of Transp v Tomkins*, 481 Mich 184, 190 (2008) — statutory interpretation
- *Const 1963 art 6, § 30* — jurisdiction of the Court of Appeals
- *Pioneer State Mutual Ins Co v Dells*, 301 Mich App 368, 377 (2013)

**Pigors v Watson application**:
- Whether MCR 2.003 disqualification standard was correctly applied → de novo
- Whether due process requirements of the 14th Amendment were met → de novo
- Statutory interpretation of MCL 722.23 best-interest factors → de novo
- Whether the court had jurisdiction to enter an order → de novo

## Standard 2: Clear Error

**Applies to**: Findings of fact by the trial court (MCR 2.613(C)).

**Definition**: A finding is clearly erroneous when, although there is evidence to
support it, the reviewing court is left with the definite and firm conviction that
a mistake has been made.

**Key language**: "A finding is clearly erroneous when, on the whole record, the
reviewing court is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made."

**Controlling authority**:
- MCR 2.613(C) — clear error standard codified
- *In re Mason*, 486 Mich 142, 152 (2010) — seminal clear error formulation
- *Foskett v Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1, 9 (2001) — family law context

**Pigors v Watson application**:
- Whether a change of established custodial environment occurred → clear error
- Whether proper cause / change of circumstances exists → clear error
- Factual findings underlying best-interest factor determinations → clear error

---

## Standard 3: Abuse of Discretion

**Applies to**: Discretionary rulings — custody decisions, evidentiary rulings,
discovery rulings, sanctions, PPO decisions.

**Definition**: An abuse of discretion occurs when the trial court's decision falls
outside the range of reasonable and principled outcomes.

**Key language**: "An abuse of discretion occurs when the result is so palpably and
grossly violative of fact and logic that it evidences a perversity of will, a defiance
of judgment, or the exercise of passion or bias."

**Controlling authority**:
- *Maldonado v Ford Motor Co*, 476 Mich 372, 388 (2006) — leading formulation
- *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700, 705 (2008) — custody context
- *Hayford v Hayford*, 279 Mich App 324, 325 (2008) — evidentiary rulings

**Pigors v Watson application**:
- PPO issuance or modification decisions → abuse of discretion
- Evidentiary rulings (admissibility of evidence) → abuse of discretion
- Discovery rulings and sanctions → abuse of discretion
- Parenting time schedules → abuse of discretion

---

## Standard 4: Great Weight of the Evidence

**Applies to**: Custody best-interest factor findings under MCL 722.28.

**Definition**: Even greater deference than clear error. The appellate court affirms
unless the evidence clearly preponderates in the opposite direction. This is the
MOST deferential standard in Michigan family law.

**Key language**: "Custody orders will be affirmed unless the trial court's findings
were against the great weight of the evidence."

**Controlling authority**:
- MCL 722.28 — codified standard for custody
- *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499, 507-08 (2003) — seminal custody case
- *Shade v Wright*, 291 Mich App 17, 21 (2010)
- *Fletcher v Fletcher*, 229 Mich App 19, 24 (1998)

**Pigors v Watson application**:
- Individual best-interest factor findings (MCL 722.23(a)-(l)) → great weight
- Ultimate custody determination → great weight
- NOTE: The factual determination of whether an established custodial environment
  exists is reviewed for clear error, not great weight

---

## Standard 5: Plain Error

**Applies to**: Unpreserved issues (no contemporaneous objection at trial).

**Definition**: To prevail under plain error review, the appellant must show:
(1) an error occurred, (2) the error was plain (clear or obvious), (3) the plain
error affected substantial rights, and (4) the error seriously affected the
fairness, integrity, or public reputation of the proceedings.

**Key language**: "Plain error affecting substantial rights."

**Controlling authority**:
- MCR 2.613(C) — plain error preservation
- *People v Carines*, 460 Mich 750, 763 (1999) — four-part test
- *In re Utrera*, 281 Mich App 1, 8-9 (2008) — family law application

**Pigors v Watson application**:
- Any issue not preserved by contemporaneous objection → plain error
- Due process violations may be reviewable even without preservation if they
  constitute structural error
