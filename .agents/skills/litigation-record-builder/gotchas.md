# Gotchas — litigation-record-builder

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Record Designation and Transcripts

Building the record on appeal is one of the most unforgiving procedural tasks. Mistakes here cannot be corrected after briefing begins.

| # | Rationalization (The Lie You Tell Yourself) | Why It Fails | Real Consequence | Correct Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I only need transcripts from the hearings I'm appealing — the other hearings don't matter." | MCR 7.210(B)(1)(b) allows partial transcripts, but the appellee can order additional portions, and the COA will presume omitted portions support the lower court. Incomplete transcripts create negative inferences. | Court of Appeals presumes the missing transcript portions support the trial court's ruling. Your appeal fails because the court fills gaps against you. Lydic v State Farm, unpublished COA opinion (common pattern). | Order ALL transcripts from contested hearings. If cost is prohibitive, order the critical hearings first and file a statement explaining the omission with your partial transcript designation. Seek fee waiver coverage for transcript costs. |
| 2 | "I have 21 days to order transcripts, so I'll figure out what I need later." | The 21-day deadline under MCR 7.204(E) is jurisdictional-adjacent — missing it doesn't automatically kill your appeal, but it triggers dismissal proceedings and requires you to show cause. Court reporters have backlogs; waiting reduces your leverage. | You scramble at day 20, can't reach the court reporter, miss the deadline, receive a show cause order from the COA, and must explain why your appeal shouldn't be dismissed. Even if saved, you've burned credibility. | Contact the court reporter on Day 1 after filing the claim of appeal. Order transcripts immediately. File proof of ordering with the COA clerk well before the 21-day mark. Build in buffer time for reporter unavailability. |
| 3 | "The court clerk will compile the record for me — that's their job." | The clerk prepares and transmits the record, but the clerk only includes what's in the file. If documents were not filed, not stamped, or were misfiled, they won't appear. The clerk does not independently audit the record for completeness. | Critical documents missing from record. Brief references evidence that isn't in the record. COA cannot consider materials outside the record — your argument collapses. | Review the register of actions BEFORE the record is transmitted. Compare it to your personal case file. File a motion to supplement (MCR 7.210(C)) for anything missing. Don't rely on the clerk to catch gaps. |
| 4 | "I can just attach documents to my brief if they're not in the record." | The Court of Appeals can only consider what is in the lower court record. Attaching new documents to your appellate brief is improper and they will be stricken. MCR 7.210(A) defines the record exclusively. | Documents stricken from brief appendix. Arguments relying on non-record materials are waived. Court may sanction for improper filing. | If critical documents are missing from the record, file a motion to supplement under MCR 7.210(C)(1) BEFORE filing your brief. Establish that the documents were part of the lower court proceedings. |
| 5 | "I'll review the transcripts after filing my brief — I know what was said." | Memory is unreliable and adversarial. What you remember being said and what the transcript actually says are often different. Arguments based on incorrect factual recollections are devastating to credibility. | Your brief quotes testimony that doesn't appear in the transcript, or mischaracterizes what was said. Opposing counsel files a response pointing out the discrepancies. Court questions your credibility on everything else. | Read every transcript cover-to-cover before writing a single word of your brief. Create a transcript index with page/line references for every fact you plan to cite. Quote exactly, with page and line numbers. |
| 6 | "The exhibits were admitted at trial, so they're automatically in the record." | Exhibits offered at trial are part of the record, but physical exhibits sometimes get lost, misfiled, or returned to parties. Electronic exhibits may not be properly preserved. You must verify exhibits are actually in the clerk's possession. | Key exhibit is missing from the record. Your brief cites an exhibit the COA doesn't have. Court cannot consider evidence it cannot review. | Verify with the clerk that all exhibits are accounted for and will be transmitted with the record. If exhibits were returned to you, provide copies to the clerk for the record. File motion to supplement if needed. |
| 7 | "A settled statement is just as good as a transcript." | A settled statement (MCR 7.210(B)(3)) is a last resort when transcripts are truly unavailable. Courts view settled statements with skepticism because they're drafted by one party. The opposing party can (and will) challenge your characterization. | Opposing party disputes your settled statement. Trial court modifies it extensively. You end up with a record that's less favorable than the actual proceedings because the settlement process introduced disputes. | Use settled statements ONLY when the transcript is genuinely unavailable (reporter deceased, recording lost). If using one, be scrupulously accurate and fair — include facts that hurt your position. Overreach in a settled statement destroys credibility. |
| 8 | "I don't need a record appendix — the full record speaks for itself." | MCR 7.212(C) requires the appellant's brief to include a record appendix with specified contents (judgment/order appealed, register of actions, relevant findings, jury instructions if applicable). This is mandatory, not optional. | Brief rejected by COA clerk for non-compliance. Must re-file with appendix under time pressure. If past the deadline, need motion for late filing. Signals to the court you don't know the rules. | Prepare the record appendix simultaneously with the brief. Include every item listed in MCR 7.212(C). Tab and index the appendix. Cross-reference appendix page numbers in the brief. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Exhibit Compilation

| # | Rationalization | Why It Fails | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I'll organize my exhibits the day before the hearing." | Exhibit preparation requires authentication planning, numbering coordination, copy preparation, and index creation. Last-minute preparation guarantees errors — wrong page counts, missing copies, unnumbered exhibits. | Exhibits rejected for improper preparation; hearing delayed; judge annoyed; key evidence not admitted because you couldn't lay proper foundation on the fly. | Prepare exhibits at least one week before any hearing. Create the index first, then assemble binders. Do a dry run of foundation questions for each exhibit. Pre-mark all exhibits per local rules. |
| 2 | "Digital copies on my phone/laptop are fine for court." | Most Michigan courts require physical copies of exhibits unless the court has specifically authorized electronic exhibit presentation. Even courts with electronic capabilities often want paper backup. | Judge asks for copies; you don't have them. Opposing counsel objects to exhibit presentation format. Court excludes exhibits you can't properly present. | Prepare physical copies: one for the court, one for opposing counsel, one for the witness, one for yourself. Bring a laptop with electronic copies as backup, not primary. |
| 3 | "I don't need to pre-mark exhibits — I'll identify them at the hearing." | Pre-marking exhibits is standard Michigan practice and expected by most judges. Unmarked exhibits cause confusion, waste hearing time, and create record-keeping problems. | Judge admonishes you for wasting time; hearing goes long; exhibits get confused; record is unclear about which document was admitted as which exhibit number. | Pre-mark all exhibits with sticker labels (Plaintiff's Ex. A, B, C or 1, 2, 3). File an exhibit list with the court before the hearing. Coordinate numbering with opposing counsel if possible. |
| 4 | "Authentication is just a formality — everyone knows these documents are real." | Authentication under MRE 901 is a legal requirement, not a formality. Without proper foundation, exhibits are inadmissible regardless of how "obviously real" they are. Text messages, emails, and social media posts require particular authentication care. | Objection sustained. Critical evidence excluded. You can't un-ring that bell — once excluded, the evidence is out for that hearing. Must lay proper foundation through testimony or stipulation. | Prepare authentication foundation for every exhibit. For documents: who created it, how you obtained it, why it's authentic. For texts/emails: device identification, screenshot timing, metadata. Seek stipulations on authenticity before hearing when possible. |
| 5 | "I'll just hand the judge a stack of documents and explain them." | Unorganized document dumps are the fastest way to lose judicial patience and attention. Judges need to find specific pages quickly. An unindexed stack of papers is functionally useless in a hearing. | Judge cannot follow your argument because they can't find the referenced document. Hearing goes poorly because the judge is frustrated. Critical evidence gets overlooked in the pile. | Organize exhibits in tabbed binders with a cover page and index. Number every page. Use exhibit separator tabs with labels. Cross-reference exhibit numbers in your argument outline so you can direct the court to specific pages instantly. |
| 6 | "The exhibit cover page colors don't matter — that's just tradition." | While color conventions vary by jurisdiction, in Muskegon County and throughout Michigan, exhibit organization signals competence to the court. Professional presentation earns credibility that helps your substantive arguments. | Your exhibits blend together; court has difficulty distinguishing plaintiff's from defendant's exhibits; you appear unprofessional compared to opposing counsel's organized binders. | Follow local conventions (Plaintiff=yellow, Defendant=blue) unless the court directs otherwise. Call the clerk's office to confirm local practice. Even if colors don't matter formally, professional presentation always matters substantively. |
