# Michigan Best Interest Factors — Complete Reference

> All 12 statutory factors under MCL 722.23 with definitions,
> evidence types, relevant case law, and application to Pigors v Watson.

---

## Overview

MCL 722.23 defines "best interests of the child" as the sum total of the following factors to be considered, evaluated, and determined by the court. The court must make specific findings on EACH factor.

**Key principles:**
- No single factor is dispositive
- Court has broad discretion in weighing factors
- Findings must be supported by the record
- Failure to address all factors is reversible error
- Court may consider factors in any order but must address all 12

---

## Factor (a) — Love, Affection, Emotional Ties

**Full text:** "The love, affection, and other emotional ties existing between the parties involved and the child."

**What courts look for:**
- Quality of the parent-child bond, not just frequency of contact
- Child's emotional response to each parent (comfort, anxiety, attachment)
- History of involvement in the child's emotional life
- Parent's ability to recognize and respond to child's emotional needs

**Key case law:**
- *Ireland v. Smith*, 451 Mich 457 (1996): Emotional ties analysis must focus on the child's perspective, not just the parent's feelings
- *Berger v. Berger*, 277 Mich App 700 (2008): Court may consider emotional impact of transitions on the child

**Evidence for Pigors:**
- Document positive interactions during parenting time
- Collect child's artwork, communications expressing affection
- Testimony from family members, neighbors witnessing parent-child interactions
- Compare: Interference with bond by Amy Watson undermines HER factor (a) showing

---

## Factor (b) — Capacity for Love, Guidance, Education

**Full text:** "The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to give the child love, affection, and guidance and to continue the education and raising of the child in his or her religion or creed, if any."

**What courts look for:**
- Proactive involvement in education (attending conferences, helping with homework)
- Consistent discipline and structure
- Supporting child's educational needs (tutoring, special services)
- Religious continuity (if applicable)

**Key case law:**
- *Foskett v. Foskett*, 247 Mich App 1 (2001): Education involvement is a significant subfactor
- *McIntosh v. McIntosh*, 282 Mich App 471 (2009): Capacity includes future ability, not just past conduct

**Evidence for Pigors:**
- School contact logs showing involvement
- Evidence of educational activities during parenting time
- Parenting class completion certificates
- Proof of engagement with school, teachers, tutors

---

## Factor (c) — Material Needs

**Full text:** "The capacity and disposition of the parties involved to provide the child with food, clothing, medical care or other remedial care recognized and permitted under the laws of this state in place of medical care, and other material needs."

**What courts look for:**
- Adequate and stable income
- Appropriate housing (safe, sufficient space, child-appropriate)
- Health insurance and access to medical care
- History of meeting child's material needs

**Key case law:**
- *Mason v. Mason*, unpub (Mich App): Income disparity alone does not determine factor (c); disposition to provide matters equally
- *Fletcher v. Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871 (1994): Court should not punish economic inequality

**Note:** This factor can cut against Pigors if housing is at issue (Lane B Shady Oaks)—address housing quality proactively.

---

## Factor (d) — Stable Environment

**Full text:** "The length of time the child has lived in a stable, satisfactory environment, and the desirability of maintaining continuity."

**What courts look for:**
- Duration in current residence
- Consistency of household composition
- School enrollment stability
- Routine and predictability in daily life

**Key case law:**
- *Foskett*: Stability includes emotional stability, not just physical location
- *Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003): Stability is relevant to both threshold and best interests

**Evidence for Pigors:**
- Document stability of Plaintiff's home environment
- Show children's routine during parenting time
- If Amy Watson's environment is unstable (frequent moves, changing partners), document it
- School records showing consistency or disruption

---

## Factor (e) — Permanence of Family Unit

**Full text:** "The permanence, as a family unit, of the existing or proposed custodial home or homes."

**What courts look for:**
- Stability of the custodial household
- Presence of step-parents, significant others, extended family
- Likelihood the household will remain stable
- Planning for the child's future

**Evidence for Pigors:**
- Document household composition and stability
- If proposing a different custodial arrangement, show the permanence of the proposed home

---

## Factor (f) — Moral Fitness

**Full text:** "The moral fitness of the parties involved."

**What courts look for:**
- Conduct that AFFECTS PARENTING ABILITY
- Criminal history (if relevant to child's welfare)
- Substance abuse
- Domestic violence
- Dishonesty with the court
- Parental alienation (increasingly recognized as moral fitness issue)

**Key case law:**
- *Fletcher v. Fletcher*, 447 Mich 871 (1994): Moral fitness must relate to parenting ability; "almost all conduct has some relevance" but weight depends on nexus to parenting
- *Berger v. Berger*: Parental alienation conduct is relevant to moral fitness

**Evidence for Pigors:**
- Document any dishonesty by Amy Watson in court filings (compare sworn statements to evidence)
- Evidence of alienation behavior goes to BOTH factor (f) and factor (j)
- Any substance abuse or criminal conduct by adverse parties

---

## Factor (g) — Mental and Physical Health

**Full text:** "The mental and physical health of the parties involved."

**What courts look for:**
- Health conditions that affect parenting ability
- Mental health treatment history (not the diagnosis itself, but functional impact)
- Substance abuse
- Capacity for daily caregiving

**Privacy considerations:** Court cannot compel mental health records without good cause showing. Must balance privacy against child's best interests.

---

## Factor (h) — Home, School, Community Record

**Full text:** "The home, school, and community record of the child."

**What courts look for:**
- Academic performance and trends
- School attendance
- Behavioral issues at school
- Extracurricular participation
- Community connections

**Evidence for Pigors:**
- Subpoena school records (grades, attendance, discipline)
- Document community activities during parenting time
- Show positive school performance correlation with parenting time periods

---

## Factor (i) — Child's Reasonable Preference

**Full text:** "The reasonable preference of the child, if the court considers the child to be of sufficient age to express preference."

**What courts look for:**
- Age and maturity of child (generally meaningful at 6+, significant at 12+)
- Whether preference is "reasonable" (not based on permissive parenting or manipulation)
- Whether preference is the child's own or coached/pressured
- Consistency of stated preference over time

**Key case law:**
- *Pierron v. Pierron*, 282 Mich App 222 (2009): In camera interview is the gold standard
- *McIntosh v. McIntosh*: Court not required to follow child's preference but must address it

**Warning:** NEVER coach the child or discuss preferences with the child within earshot of the court process. This destroys credibility and may constitute alienation.

---

## Factor (j) — Willingness to Facilitate Relationship

**Full text:** "The willingness and ability of each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent or the child and the parents. A court may not consider negatively for the purposes of this factor any reasonable action taken by a parent to protect a child or that parent from sexual assault or domestic violence by the child's other parent."

**THE MOST STRATEGICALLY IMPORTANT FACTOR FOR PIGORS.**

**What courts look for:**
- Active encouragement of child's relationship with other parent
- Compliance with parenting time orders
- Flexibility in scheduling
- Speaking positively (or at least neutrally) about other parent
- NOT engaging in gatekeeping, alienation, or interference

**Key case law:**
- *Brown v. Brown*, unpub: Systematic parenting time interference strongly weighs against the interfering parent
- *Demski v. Petlick*, 309 Mich App 404 (2015): Courts take gatekeeping seriously

**Evidence documentation for Pigors:**
1. Calendar log of every denied/interfered parenting time with date, method, excuse
2. Text messages showing denial or last-minute cancellation
3. Witness statements from family members present at exchanges
4. Police reports for denied exchanges
5. Communications showing disparaging remarks about Plaintiff to children
6. Evidence of coaching children to resist parenting time

---

## Factor (k) — Domestic Violence

**Full text:** "Domestic violence, regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child."

**Mandatory consideration.** Court must address even if no party raises it.

**Key case law:**
- *Grew v. Knox*, unpub: PPO history is relevant even if PPO was entered by consent
- Courts must consider domestic violence even from non-party household members

---

## Factor (l) — Catch-All

**Full text:** "Any other factor considered by the court to be relevant to a particular child custody dispute."

**Commonly invoked for:**
- Parental alienation (also relevant to (f) and (j))
- False allegations of abuse
- Cooperation with court processes
- Special needs of the child
- Impact of extended family (Watson family interference)
- Pro se accommodation and access to justice issues

## Factor-by-Factor Litigation Checklist

For each factor, prepare:
```
□ Documentary evidence (3+ pieces)
□ Witness testimony identified (name + expected testimony)
□ Adverse party's likely position anticipated
□ Rebuttal evidence prepared
□ Discovery needed (if evidence gaps exist)
□ Factor weighting argument (why this factor favors/disfavors each party)
```
