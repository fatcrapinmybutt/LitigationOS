# Gotchas — litigation-custody-specialist

> Critical failure modes in Michigan custody litigation. Every row below
> has destroyed a custody case for a pro se father in Michigan family court.

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Best Interest Factor Analysis

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "Factor (j) — willingness to facilitate — is the most important factor and should control the outcome" | Parenting time interference is clearly documented and egregious | Michigan courts weigh ALL 12 factors—no single factor is dispositive, and judges have broad discretion in weighing | MCL 722.23 lists 12 factors; Foskett v. Foskett, 247 Mich App 1 (2001)—no factor is automatically controlling |
| "The child told me she wants to live with me, so factor (i) decides it" | A child's own words should carry the most weight | Judge decides IF the child is of sufficient age/maturity to express preference, and even then it's ONE of twelve factors—courts are skeptical of coached or pressured statements | MCL 722.23(i); judge has total discretion on weight; coached statements actively hurt your case |
| "My income is higher so I win on factor (c) — material needs" | I can provide more financially | Factor (c) is about CAPACITY AND DISPOSITION to provide, not just income—a lower-income parent who consistently meets the child's needs can prevail over a higher earner who doesn't | MCL 722.23(c); "capacity and disposition"—both elements matter equally |
| "Her boyfriend's criminal record wins factor (f) — moral fitness for me" | Criminal record = morally unfit | Moral fitness is evaluated for its EFFECT ON PARENTING—a decades-old conviction with rehabilitation may carry no weight, while recent conduct affecting the child carries heavy weight | Moral fitness must relate to parenting ability; Fletcher v. Fletcher, 447 Mich 871 (1994) |
| "The domestic violence PPO against me is a different case and shouldn't affect custody" | The PPO was in a separate proceeding | Factor (k) requires courts to consider domestic violence REGARDLESS of context—even if the PPO was contested, its existence is relevant and must be addressed head-on | MCL 722.23(k); "regardless of whether the violence was directed against or witnessed by the child" |
| "I just need to show the other parent is worse, not that I'm good" | Custody is comparative | Courts evaluate BOTH parents on ALL factors—merely attacking the other parent without demonstrating your own fitness is a losing strategy that signals alienation | Best practice: Lead with your strengths on each factor, THEN address other parent's deficiencies |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Custody Modification Threshold

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "Things are clearly different now, so the court must consider my motion" | Circumstances have obviously changed | The change must be SIGNIFICANT enough to warrant revisiting custody—minor or routine changes don't meet the Vodvarka threshold | Vodvarka v. Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 499 (2003); change must be significant and affect child's well-being |
| "Accumulating many small changes equals one big change" | The cumulative effect is substantial | Courts are split on cumulative-change arguments—some accept them, but many require at least one individually significant change | Better to identify the single strongest change and lead with it; supplement with cumulative argument |
| "The court must hold a hearing on my modification motion" | Due process requires a hearing | Court only holds a full evidentiary hearing IF you pass the threshold (proper cause/change of circumstances)—if you don't, the motion is denied without hearing on the merits | Vodvarka; threshold is a gatekeeping function; no right to evidentiary hearing until threshold met |
| "Proper cause and change of circumstances are the same thing" | They both lead to the same result | Proper cause = appropriate legal grounds; change of circumstances = factual change since last order—they're alternative paths with different requirements | Vodvarka; either is sufficient, but they're legally distinct; argue both if possible |
| "A change in the child's age is sufficient change of circumstances" | A 6-year-old is different from a 3-year-old | A child growing older, by itself, is NOT a change of circumstances—there must be a specific change in the child's needs or circumstances BECAUSE of age | Growing older is expected, not changed circumstances; but age-related needs changes (school choice, activities) may qualify |
| "My motion should reference everything that's happened since the beginning" | The full history shows the pattern | The threshold analysis focuses on changes SINCE THE LAST CUSTODY ORDER—events predating the current order are not "changed circumstances" (though they may provide context) | Vodvarka; only changes since last order; prior history is background, not basis for modification |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Established Custodial Environment

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "The ECE should be with both parents because I have regular parenting time" | I see my children every other weekend | ECE requires the child "naturally looks to the custodian for guidance, discipline, necessities of life, and parental comfort"—weekend parenting time alone may not establish ECE with non-custodial parent | MCL 722.27(1)(c); ECE is about the child's natural reliance, not just time spent |
| "The ECE was illegally established so it shouldn't count" | The other parent took custody through improper means | Courts generally look at the child's CURRENT situation, not how it was established—even an ECE created through wrongful conduct may be recognized if the child is now settled | Courts prioritize child's current stability; challenge the means but don't assume ECE is voided |
| "Clear and convincing evidence isn't that hard to meet" | My evidence is strong | Clear and convincing is the SECOND HIGHEST standard in law (below beyond reasonable doubt)—it requires the fact-finder to have a firm conviction that the allegations are true | Much harder than preponderance; many modification motions fail at this standard |
| "If the child splits time 50/50, ECE must be with both parents" | Equal time = equal custodial environment | Even with equal time, ECE may exist with only one parent if the child primarily looks to that parent for guidance and comfort—it's about the quality of the custodial relationship, not just time | Quality of custodial relationship matters more than quantity of time |
| "I can avoid the clear-and-convincing standard by asking for more parenting time instead of custody change" | Parenting time modification has a lower threshold | If the parenting time change effectively changes the ECE (e.g., from every-other-weekend to primary custody), the court will apply the clear-and-convincing standard regardless of how you label the motion | Courts look at the substance of what's requested, not the label |
| "The GAL's recommendation establishes which parent has the ECE" | The GAL investigated and concluded | ECE is a legal determination made by the JUDGE, not the GAL—the GAL's recommendation is evidence, but the judge makes the independent legal finding | ECE is the court's finding; GAL recommendation is advisory, not binding |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: FOC and GAL Procedures

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "The FOC recommendation is just a suggestion I can ignore" | It's not a court order | FOC recommendations become court orders unless TIMELY OBJECTED to under MCR 3.218—failure to object = the recommendation becomes binding | MCR 3.218; must file written objection within 21 days; failure to object = waiver |
| "The GAL must interview both parents equally" | Fair process requires equal treatment | While best practice, there is no Michigan rule requiring GALs to spend equal time with each parent—courts give GALs wide latitude in investigation methods | Challenge GAL bias through motion to remove or at best-interest hearing—document the disparity |
| "I can get the GAL removed for bias" | The bias is obvious | Courts are reluctant to remove GALs mid-case—you must show actual bias (not just disagreement with conclusions) and that the bias affected the investigation/recommendation | High bar for removal; better strategy: undermine recommendation with contrary evidence at hearing |
| "The FOC referee's recommendation will be accepted by the judge without question" | Referees are court officers | FOC referee recommendations can be objected to under MCR 3.218, triggering de novo hearing before the judge—the judge makes an independent determination | MCR 3.218; objection triggers de novo hearing; judge is not bound by referee recommendation |
| "I can present new evidence at the FOC objection hearing that I didn't present to the referee" | It's a new hearing | De novo hearings generally consider the same evidence plus any additional evidence—but some courts limit new evidence to what was reasonably available at the referee hearing | Check local practice; generally de novo means fresh review, but prepare your best evidence for both stages |
| "The custody evaluation is scientific and objective" | Evaluators use psychological instruments | Custody evaluations contain subjective judgment regardless of testing—evaluators have biases, and Michigan does not require specific qualifications or methodologies for custody evaluators | Challenge methodology, qualifications, and factual basis of evaluation; not infallible |

## Critical Timing Traps

- **FOC objection deadline:** 21 days from recommendation—JURISDICTIONAL, no extensions
- **Custody modification:** Must file motion BEFORE attempting self-help (changing residence, keeping children)
- **Parenting time makeup:** Must request promptly—courts disfavor delayed claims for makeup time
- **GAL response:** Address GAL concerns in real-time during investigation, not only after report
- **Discovery in custody:** Start immediately—custody cases often have compressed timelines
- **Appeal of custody order:** 21 days from entry of order for claim of appeal (MCR 7.204)

## Michigan Custody Unique Rules

1. **No preference for either parent based on gender** — MCL 722.25(1)
2. **Joint custody is not presumed** — must be requested and evaluated on best interests
3. **Grandparent visitation** — MCL 722.27b (limited after Troxel v. Granville)
4. **100-mile rule** — MCL 722.31: Cannot move child's domicile more than 100 miles without court permission
5. **Parenting time is a RIGHT** — MCL 722.27a: Court shall not deny parenting time unless it would endanger health
6. **Friend of the court opt-out** — MCL 552.505d: Parties may opt out of FOC in limited circumstances
