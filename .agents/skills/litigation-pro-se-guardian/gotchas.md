# Gotchas — litigation-pro-se-guardian

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Procedural Safety

The most dangerous moment for a pro se litigant is when they convince themselves a procedural requirement doesn't apply to them. Every row below represents a real failure pattern.

| # | Rationalization (The Lie You Tell Yourself) | Why It Fails | Real Consequence | Correct Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "The court will give me extra time because I'm pro se — Haines v Kerner protects me." | Haines v Kerner requires courts to liberally construe pro se pleadings, NOT to extend deadlines. Jurisdictional deadlines (statute of limitations, appeal deadlines) apply identically to pro se litigants and attorneys. Marceau v IBEW, 618 F2d 1198 (6th Cir 1980). | You miss the 21-day claim of appeal deadline (MCR 7.204(A)(1)) or the 42-day application deadline, and your appeal is permanently barred. No amount of leniency reopens a jurisdictional deadline. | Calendar every deadline the moment you learn of it. Build in a 3-day personal buffer before every court deadline. Set multiple reminders (phone, calendar, paper). Treat every deadline as if missing it ends the case — because some of them will. |
| 2 | "I served the other party by email — that's good enough." | Email service is only valid in Michigan if the parties have stipulated to email service or the court has ordered it. Default service for most filings is first-class mail or MiFile e-service (if both parties are registered). MCR 2.107(C). | Filing is treated as not properly served. Opposing party claims lack of notice. Court may vacate any order entered based on the improperly served filing. Your motion is dead on arrival. | Use MiFile e-service when possible (automatic proof). For paper service, use first-class mail with a proper Certificate of Service. Only use email if there is a written stipulation or court order authorizing it. Keep proof of every service. |
| 3 | "The clerk rejected my filing because they're biased — I'll complain to the judge." | Clerks reject filings for technical non-compliance (wrong format, missing fee, incomplete caption), not bias. Complaining to the judge about the clerk creates a negative impression and does not fix the technical problem. Ex parte contact with the judge about ministerial matters is inappropriate. | Judge is annoyed by the complaint; still directs you to fix the technical issue; you've wasted time and credibility. If you complained about the clerk in writing, it's now part of the permanent record. | Ask the clerk specifically what's wrong with the filing. Fix the identified issue. Re-file. If you believe the rejection is genuinely improper, file a written objection through the proper process (not an informal complaint to the judge). |
| 4 | "I don't need a fee waiver — I'll just pay the fees as they come." | Filing fees add up quickly: $175 complaint, $20 per motion, $375 COA claim of appeal, $3.50+/page transcripts. Across multiple courts (circuit, COA, possibly federal), total costs can exceed $2,000. Running out of money mid-litigation forces impossible choices. | You can't afford transcript costs for appeal. You can't afford the COA filing fee. You skip a necessary motion because of the fee. Financial pressure forces you to abandon meritorious claims. | File MC 20 fee waiver immediately in every court where you're litigating. Even if you have some resources now, circumstances change. A fee waiver on file prevents financial barriers from blocking access to justice. Renew at each court level. |
| 5 | "I'll figure out the rules as I go — I learn best by doing." | Michigan Court Rules fill hundreds of pages. Federal Rules of Civil Procedure add hundreds more. "Learning by doing" in litigation means learning by losing — procedural errors become waived issues, missed opportunities, and dismissed claims. | You waive an objection by failing to raise it timely. You miss a discovery deadline and face sanctions. You file in the wrong court and lose months. Each procedural error compounds. | Before taking any action, research the governing rule. Use the Procedural Safety Checklist in SKILL.md for every filing. When uncertain, call the clerk's office for procedural guidance (not legal advice). Use SCAO self-help resources. |
| 6 | "I can talk to the judge informally — I'm not a lawyer, so the ex parte rules don't apply." | Ex parte communication rules (MRPC 3.5; MCR 2.003; Mich Canon 2(B)) apply to ALL parties, including pro se litigants. Any communication with the judge outside the presence of the opposing party is prohibited and may result in sanctions or disqualification. | Judge reports the ex parte contact; you face sanctions; any favorable ruling obtained is vacated; opposing counsel moves for sanctions; judge may recuse, delaying the entire case. | NEVER contact the judge outside of court filings and scheduled hearings. All communications go through proper filings served on all parties. If you need emergency relief, file an emergency motion with notice to the other side. |
| 7 | "I should file motions for everything — aggressive litigation shows I'm serious." | Over-filing annoys courts, drains your time and resources, and signals poor judgment. Michigan courts can sanction vexatious filing under MCR 2.114(D) and (E). Strategic restraint is more persuasive than aggression. | Court labels you a vexatious litigant. MCR 2.114(D) sanctions imposed. Judge develops negative perception that colors every subsequent ruling. Your meritorious motions get less attention because they're buried in a flood of filings. | File only motions that advance a concrete strategic objective. Before filing, ask: "What do I gain if I win this motion? What do I lose if I file it and lose?" If the answer to the first question is vague, don't file. Quality over quantity, always. |
| 8 | "The opposing attorney is lying to the court — I need to call them out personally." | Personal attacks on opposing counsel destroy your credibility and violate the professional tone courts expect. Even if counsel is being dishonest, the remedy is factual rebuttal with evidence, not ad hominem attacks. | Judge admonishes you in open court. Written personal attacks in filings become part of the permanent record and undermine your position on appeal. Opposing counsel uses your tone against you. | Respond to misrepresentations with facts and record citations. "Counsel states X; however, the record shows Y (Ex. A, p. 3)" is devastating. "Counsel is a liar" is self-defeating. Let the contrast between your professionalism and their conduct speak for itself. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Court System Navigation

| # | Rationalization | Why It Fails | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I'm in multiple courts, but the rules are basically the same everywhere." | Michigan Circuit Court rules (MCR), Court of Appeals rules (MCR 7.2xx), Michigan Supreme Court rules (MCR 7.3xx), and Federal rules (FRCP + LCivR for W.D. Mich) are significantly different. Formatting, deadlines, service, and filing procedures vary by court. | You use circuit court formatting for a COA brief and it's rejected. You apply state service rules in federal court. You miss a federal deadline because you were tracking state timelines. Cross-court confusion compounds. | Maintain a separate rules reference for each court. Before filing in any court, verify: (1) correct rules apply, (2) correct formatting, (3) correct filing system (MiFile vs. CM/ECF), (4) correct service requirements. Create a court-specific checklist for each. |
| 2 | "The court facilitator can help me with my legal strategy." | Court facilitators (family division) are prohibited from giving legal advice. They can provide forms, explain procedures, and offer general information. They cannot tell you what arguments to make, whether to file a motion, or how to respond to the other party's claims. | You follow a facilitator's general comment as if it were strategic advice and make a poor litigation decision. Or you share case strategy with a facilitator who cannot help you and who may inadvertently mention it to the other party's facilitator session. | Use facilitators for: forms, deadlines, procedural steps, court schedules. Use your own analysis (aided by these skills) for: legal strategy, argument selection, settlement decisions, filing decisions. |
| 3 | "I filed my fee waiver in circuit court, so I'm covered everywhere." | Fee waivers are court-specific. A circuit court fee waiver does not apply at the COA, MSC, or federal court. Each court requires a separate fee waiver application. | You file a claim of appeal at COA without paying or having a fee waiver. Filing is rejected. You scramble to prepare MC 20 for COA, potentially missing deadlines. | File a new MC 20 at every court level when you first appear. For federal court, file an IFP application (28 USC § 1915) — completely different form and standard. Track fee waiver status for each court. |
| 4 | "I backed up all my documents digitally — I don't need paper copies." | Courts require paper copies for many proceedings. Digital files can be corrupted, devices can fail, and courtrooms may not have the technology to display your documents. Paper is the universal backup. | Your laptop dies before a hearing; you have no exhibits. Court requires a paper courtesy copy and you don't have one. Key documents are in a cloud service you can't access from the courthouse. | Maintain BOTH digital and paper copies. Keep a master paper file organized by date and filing type. Bring paper copies of everything to every hearing. Digital is your working copy; paper is your insurance policy. |
| 5 | "The SCAO forms are suggestions — I can draft my own versions." | SCAO forms are standardized for a reason — courts expect them, clerks process them efficiently, and they ensure required information is included. Custom versions may omit required fields or use unfamiliar formatting that confuses court staff. | Custom form is rejected by clerk; or accepted but missing required information that causes processing delays or errors. Judge receives a non-standard form and must spend extra time parsing it. | Use SCAO forms whenever they exist for your filing type. You can add attachments and supplemental pages, but use the standard form as your base. Find forms at courts.michigan.gov/scao-forms. |
| 6 | "I don't need to keep a personal docket — the court tracks everything." | Court records can have errors. Docket entries may be delayed. You may not receive timely notice of orders entered. Relying solely on the court's record means you discover problems after deadlines have passed. | You miss a deadline because you didn't know an order was entered. You discover a hearing was scheduled only when you receive a notice of default. Your personal timeline doesn't match the court's, and the court's controls. | Maintain your own case docket: every filing, every order, every deadline, every hearing. Check MiFile/PACER regularly (at minimum weekly) to compare your records with the court's. Set independent calendar reminders for every deadline. |
