# Gotchas — Post-Judgment Specialist

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Filing modification without threshold showing | Filing motion to modify custody without first establishing proper cause or change of circumstances | MCL 722.27(1)(c) requires a threshold showing BEFORE best interest analysis; skipping this step = instant denial | Always brief the Vodvarka v Grasmeyer threshold first; present specific facts constituting change of circumstances |
| Relitigating settled issues | Raising arguments already decided in the original judgment | Res judicata and collateral estoppel bar relitigation of previously adjudicated issues | Focus on NEW facts and changed circumstances that arose AFTER the original judgment |
| Missing the 21-day appeal window | Failing to file claim of appeal within 21 days of final judgment | MCR 7.204(A)(1) requires claim of appeal within 21 days of entry of judgment; missing this deadline forfeits appeal of right | Calendar the judgment entry date immediately; file claim of appeal on day of entry if possible |
| Confusing modification standards | Applying the wrong legal standard (change in circumstances vs. clear and convincing evidence) | Custody modification requires proper cause/change of circumstances (MCL 722.27); modifying established custodial environment requires clear and convincing evidence | Identify whether an established custodial environment exists first, then apply the correct burden of proof |
| Filing in wrong court | Filing post-judgment motion in a different court than where the judgment was entered | Continuing jurisdiction remains with the court that entered the original judgment under MCL 552.17a | Always file post-judgment motions in the original court; if you moved, file motion for transfer of jurisdiction first |

## Failure Modes
1. **Vodvarka Threshold Failure**: Court denies motion at the threshold without reaching best interest analysis. Recovery: File new motion with stronger factual basis; supplement with additional evidence of changed circumstances; consider whether the "proper cause" alternative (legal defect rather than factual change) applies.
2. **Enforcement vs. Modification Confusion**: Filing modification when enforcement (contempt) is the correct remedy. Recovery: Analyze whether the existing order is adequate but unenforced (contempt) or whether the order itself needs changing (modification). File the correct motion.
3. **Established Custodial Environment Miscalculation**: Failing to address or incorrectly identifying the established custodial environment. Recovery: Present evidence on the ECE question — where the child has lived, for how long, and the stability of the arrangement per MCL 722.27(1)(c).
4. **Judgment Void vs. Voidable Confusion**: Attempting to set aside a voidable judgment as void. Recovery: Void judgments (lack of jurisdiction, due process violation) can be attacked at any time under MCR 2.612(C)(1)(d); voidable judgments must be challenged within 1 year under MCR 2.612(C)(2).

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-motion-practice**, post-judgment motions in family court have unique procedural requirements — MCR 3.213 governs show cause proceedings, not general MCR 2.119 motion practice alone.
- Data flow from **litigation-timeline-forensics** must establish WHEN the change of circumstances occurred — the Vodvarka threshold requires specificity about timing, not just that things are different.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: In the 14th Circuit, post-judgment motions in family cases are typically handled by the same judge who entered the original judgment. If that judge has been reassigned, you may need to confirm which judge has jurisdiction before filing.
