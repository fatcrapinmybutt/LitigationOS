# Gotchas — Parental Alienation Detector

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Using "parental alienation syndrome" terminology | Citing PAS as a clinical diagnosis in filings or expert reports | PAS is not recognized by DSM-5 or APA; Michigan courts do not accept it as a diagnosis; using the term invites credibility attacks | Frame as interference with parent-child relationship; cite MCL 722.23(j) (willingness to facilitate) and document specific behaviors |
| Relying on the child's statements alone | Arguing alienation solely because the child prefers one parent | Children's preferences may reflect genuine experiences, developmental stages, or legitimate concerns — not necessarily alienation | Corroborate with patterns: documented interference, coaching evidence, schedule violations, false allegations pattern |
| Overdiagnosing alienation | Labeling every conflict or resistance as alienation | High-conflict custody involves legitimate grievances on both sides; crying alienation without evidence undermines credibility | Distinguish between alienation, estrangement (justified rejection), and normal adjustment difficulties with documented evidence |
| Failing to document the pattern | Making alienation claims without a documented timeline of specific incidents | Courts require specific evidence, not general allegations; "she's alienating the child" without dates and details will be dismissed | Build a detailed incident log with dates, witnesses, and contemporaneous documentation for each alleged alienating behavior |
| Ignoring the child's legitimate concerns | Dismissing the child's expressed fears or preferences as "brainwashing" | If the child has legitimate safety concerns, dismissing them as alienation harms the child and damages your credibility with the court | Investigate the child's statements seriously; rule out legitimate concerns before alleging alienation |

## Failure Modes
1. **Expert Witness Disqualification**: Expert uses PAS terminology or unvalidated assessment tools. Recovery: Retain a different expert who frames opinions in terms of best interest factors (MCL 722.23) and documented behaviors rather than syndromes; ensure expert is familiar with Michigan evidentiary standards.
2. **Credibility Destruction**: Court finds alienation allegations are exaggerated or retaliatory. Recovery: Refocus on documented specific behaviors; acknowledge complexity of the situation; demonstrate concern for child's wellbeing rather than winning the custody fight.
3. **Backfire — Appearing Controlling**: Court perceives alienation claims as an attempt to control the child and other parent. Recovery: Emphasize child-centered concerns; propose therapeutic interventions rather than punishment; show willingness to cooperate.
4. **Insufficient Evidence Pattern**: Individual incidents, when isolated, appear minor. Recovery: Present the totality of circumstances through a comprehensive timeline; expert testimony can help the court understand how individually minor acts form a pattern of systematic interference.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-timeline-forensics**, the timeline is your single most important evidence tool — build a chronological exhibit showing the pattern of alienating behaviors mapped against parenting time denials, false allegations, and the child's behavioral changes.
- Data flow from **litigation-guardian-ad-litem-specialist** is critical: GAL findings on factor (j) (willingness to facilitate relationship) directly address alienation concerns. If GAL does not investigate factor (j) adequately, file objection.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: Michigan courts analyze alienation through the lens of MCL 722.23(j) — the willingness and ability to facilitate a close and continuing relationship. Do NOT use clinical alienation terminology. Frame everything as factor (j) evidence. The 14th Circuit specifically looks at documented parenting time interference, not theoretical constructs.
