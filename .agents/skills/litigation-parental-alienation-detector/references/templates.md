## Factor (j) Analysis Brief Template

```
BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF FACTOR (j) FINDINGS

    Factor (j) of MCL 722.23 asks: "The willingness and ability of
each of the parties to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing
parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent."

PLAINTIFF'S FACTOR (j) EVIDENCE:
    1. Plaintiff has [specific positive actions demonstrating
       facilitation of relationship with Defendant]
    2. Examples: [encouraged calls, shared information, supported visits]

DEFENDANT'S FACTOR (j) EVIDENCE:
    3. Defendant has [specific documented actions showing interference]
    4. Pattern includes: [list with dates and documentation]

    Under Berger v Berger, 277 Mich App 700 (2008), this factor
requires examination of each parent's actual track record. The
documented evidence shows [conclusion supported by evidence].
```

## Workflow: Documenting and Presenting Alienation

```
Step 1: Begin incident documentation (date, description, evidence)
    ↓
Step 2: Preserve all communications (texts, emails, voicemails)
    ↓
Step 3: Request school/medical records to identify exclusion patterns
    ↓
Step 4: Log all parenting time denials with FOC
    ↓
Step 5: Build comprehensive timeline of alienating behaviors
    ↓
Step 6: Assess Vodvarka threshold — is there enough for modification?
    ↓
Step 7: Retain expert if needed (NOT PAS — behavior-focused only)
    ↓
Step 8: File motion to modify custody citing factor (j)
    ↓
Step 9: Present evidence organized by best interest factor
    ↓
Step 10: Propose remedies: custody change, makeup time, therapy
```

## Communication Preservation Guide

| Source | How to Preserve | Format | Legal Considerations |
|--------|---------------|--------|---------------------|
| Text messages | Screenshot with metadata | PDF/images | Authenticate per MRE 901 |
| Emails | Print with headers | PDF | Include routing information |
| Voicemails | Save audio file + transcript | Audio + text | Michigan one-party consent (MCL 750.539c) |
| Social media | Screenshot with URL and date | PDF/images | May be public or discoverable |
| Witness accounts | Written statements dated and signed | Document | May need affidavit for court |

## Common Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Better Approach |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Using "PAS" or "syndrome" language | Not recognized by DSM-5 or Michigan courts | Frame as factor (j) interference; describe behaviors |
| Unsupported accusations | Courts require specific evidence; vague claims dismissed | Document every incident with date, details, evidence |
| Ignoring child's legitimate fears | Undermines credibility; may harm child | Investigate genuine concerns before alleging alienation |
| Retaliatory filing | Court sees through tit-for-tat motions | File only when pattern is clearly documented |
| All-or-nothing approach | Requesting full custody change may seem extreme | Propose graduated remedies: therapy first, then modification |
| Not filing with FOC | Parenting time violations need official documentation | Report every denial to FOC promptly |
