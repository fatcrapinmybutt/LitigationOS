# Michigan Rules — Parental Alienation Detector

## Michigan Court Rules (MCR)

### MCR 3.210 — Domestic Relations: Referees and Mediation
- Custody evaluations may address alienation dynamics
- Referee hearings may examine parent-child relationship interference

### MCR 3.916 — Guardian Ad Litem
- GAL duty to investigate all best interest factors including factor (j) — willingness to facilitate relationship
- GAL must document patterns of interference or cooperation
- Cross-examination of GAL on factor (j) findings

### MCR 3.218 — Friend of the Court: Parenting Time Enforcement
- **(A)**: Joint meeting when parenting time dispute exists
- **(B)**: Makeup parenting time for wrongful denial
- **(C)**: Civil contempt for repeated parenting time violations
- **(D)**: Court may modify custody if parenting time interference is persistent

### MCR 2.302 — Discovery Scope
- Discovery of communications showing coaching or interference
- Text messages, emails, social media posts as evidence
- Third-party records (therapist, school) via subpoena

### MCR 3.206(D) — Temporary Orders
- Emergency temporary custody modification when alienation creates immediate harm
- Temporary parenting time modifications pending investigation

## Michigan Compiled Laws (MCL)

### MCL 722.23 — Best Interest of the Child Factors
- **(j)**: The willingness and ability of each parent to facilitate and encourage a close and continuing parent-child relationship between the child and the other parent — this is the PRIMARY alienation factor
- **(a)**: Love, affection, emotional ties — alienation damages these ties
- **(f)**: Moral fitness — systematic alienation reflects on moral fitness
- **(i)**: Reasonable preference of child — must distinguish between genuine preference and coached/alienated preference
- **(l)**: Any other relevant factor — pattern of alienating behavior

### MCL 722.27 — Custody Modification
- **(1)(c)**: Proper cause or change of circumstances threshold
- Persistent alienation may constitute change of circumstances
- Court must find change before proceeding to best interest analysis

### MCL 722.27a — Parenting Time
- **(1)**: Parenting time is presumed to be in child's best interest
- **(3)**: Court shall consider frequency and willfulness of interference
- **(6)**: Court may hold an evidentiary hearing on parenting time interference
- **(7)**: Civil contempt for parenting time violation; makeup time required

### MCL 722.27b — Parenting Time: Make-Up Time
- Court must order makeup parenting time for wrongful denial
- Repeated denial triggers potential custody modification

### MCL 750.350a — Parental Kidnapping
- Felony for custodial interference beyond 24 hours
- Relevant when alienation escalates to physical denial of access

### MCL 722.31 — Change of Domicile
- Alienating parent relocating to limit other parent's access
- 100-mile rule: cannot move child's domicile without court approval
- Relocation to interfere may weigh against best interest determination

### MCL 712A.6b — Child's Attorney
- In severe alienation cases, court may appoint attorney for child
- Attorney represents child's expressed wishes
- May differ from GAL best-interest recommendation

## Key Michigan Case Law

### Factor (j) — Willingness to Facilitate Relationship
- **Berger v Berger**, 277 Mich App 700 (2008) — Factor (j) is often the most significant factor in custody disputes involving allegations of alienation; court must examine each parent's track record of facilitating the relationship
- **Shade v Wright**, 291 Mich App 17 (2010) — Persistent interference with parenting time may justify change of custody when it rises to the level of a change of circumstances
- **Pierron v Pierron**, 282 Mich App 222 (2009) — Trial court's findings on factor (j) must be supported by evidence; conclusory findings are insufficient

### Evidence Standards
- **Vodvarka v Grasmeyer**, 259 Mich App 499 (2003) — Standard for establishing proper cause or change of circumstances required before custody modification; alienation allegations must be supported by specific evidence
- **Brown v Brown**, 332 Mich App 1 (2020) — Court must make independent factual findings; cannot delegate to GAL or adopt conclusory expert opinions

### Child's Preference
- **Maier v Maier**, 311 Mich App 218 (2015) — Child's preference under factor (i) must be evaluated in context; court should consider whether preference is the result of coaching or alienation
- **Foskett v Foskett**, 247 Mich App 1 (2001) — Child must be of sufficient age and maturity for preference to be considered; court evaluates independently

### Parenting Time Enforcement
- **Rains v Rains**, 301 Mich App 313 (2013) — Repeated, willful denial of parenting time supports civil contempt and makeup parenting time
- **Porter v Porter**, 285 Mich App 450 (2009) — Contempt is appropriate remedy for parenting time violations; court should consider frequency and willfulness

## Checklists

### Alienation Evidence Documentation Checklist
- [ ] Incident log: Date, time, description, witnesses for each event
- [ ] Parenting time denial log: scheduled vs. actual time
- [ ] Text messages/emails showing interference or coaching
- [ ] School/medical records: which parent is listed, who attends events
- [ ] Social media posts contradicting claims or showing disparagement
- [ ] Third-party witness statements (teachers, coaches, family)
- [ ] Child's behavioral changes correlated with specific events
- [ ] Therapist records (obtained via proper channels)
- [ ] FOC records of parenting time complaints

### Factor (j) Analysis Framework
1. Does parent encourage child's relationship with other parent?
2. Does parent speak positively (or neutrally) about other parent to child?
3. Does parent comply with parenting time schedule?
4. Does parent share information about child's activities, health, school?
5. Does parent facilitate phone/video contact during their parenting time?
6. Does parent attend co-parenting events cooperatively?
7. Does parent support child's expressed desire to see other parent?

### Filing Deadlines
- Parenting time complaint to FOC: No statutory deadline; file promptly
- Motion to modify custody: File when pattern is established (Vodvarka standard)
- Contempt motion: File after each documented violation
- Emergency motion: When child's safety or welfare is at immediate risk
