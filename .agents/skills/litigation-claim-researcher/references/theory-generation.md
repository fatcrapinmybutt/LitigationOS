# Legal Theory Generation — Michigan Reference

> Systematic identification of direct, derivative, and novel claim theories
> Context: Pigors v Watson | 14th Judicial Circuit, Muskegon County

---

## Theory Category Framework

### 1. DIRECT CLAIMS

Direct claims arise from the defendant's own conduct toward the plaintiff.

**Subcategories:**

#### A. Intentional Tort Theories
Claims where defendant's intent is an element:
- Fraud (intentional misrepresentation)
- Silent fraud (intentional concealment)
- IIED (intentional or reckless conduct)
- Conversion (intentional dominion over property)
- Trespass (intentional entry)
- Defamation (knowing or reckless falsity for per se; negligence for per quod)

**Michigan Example:** Landlord knowingly conceals mold infestation before lease signing → fraud + silent fraud + IIED (if extreme) + MCPA § 445.903(1)(s).

#### B. Negligence Theories
Claims based on duty and breach:
- General negligence (Moning v Alfono duty analysis)
- Negligence per se (building code, housing code, fire code, health code violations)
- Premises liability (dangerous condition, failure to warn, failure to repair)
- Professional negligence (if licensed professional involved)

**Michigan Example:** Landlord fails to repair known electrical hazard → negligence + negligence per se (building code) + habitability (MCL 554.139).

#### C. Contract Theories
Claims based on agreement:
- Breach of express contract (lease terms)
- Breach of implied contract (implied terms)
- Breach of implied warranty of habitability (MCL 554.139)
- Breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment
- Unjust enrichment (no contract, but defendant enriched at plaintiff's expense)
- Quantum meruit (value of services/benefits conferred)

**Michigan Example:** Lease requires landlord to maintain HVAC; landlord fails for 3 months → breach of express contract + habitability + rent abatement.

#### D. Statutory Theories
Claims created by statute:
- MCPA (MCL 445.903) — 29 prohibited practices
- Security Deposit Act (MCL 554.601)
- Truth in Renting Act (MCL 554.631)
- Retaliatory Action (MCL 600.5720)
- Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act (MCL 37.2101)
- Manufactured Housing Act (MCL 125.2301)
- Wrongful eviction / forcible entry and detainer (MCL 600.2918)

**Michigan Example:** Landlord fails to return deposit within 30 days AND retaliates after code complaint → Security Deposit Act (double damages) + retaliatory action + MCPA.

### 2. DERIVATIVE CLAIMS

Derivative claims extend liability beyond the direct tortfeasor.

#### A. Vicarious Liability / Respondeat Superior
- Employer liable for employee's torts within scope of employment
- Principal liable for agent's authorized acts
- **Michigan test:** Was the actor acting within the scope of employment/agency? Bagley v Arbor Realty, unpub.

#### B. Civil Conspiracy
- Two or more persons agreed to accomplish unlawful purpose
- Overt act in furtherance
- Requires an underlying actionable tort — not independent
- Extends liability to ALL conspirators for ALL damages
- **Key:** Admiral Ins Co v Columbia Casualty, 194 Mich App 300 (1992)

#### C. Aiding and Abetting
- Knowledge of the wrongful conduct
- Substantial assistance or encouragement
- Michigan recognizes aiding and abetting for intentional torts
- **Application:** Property manager who knows of fraud and facilitates it

#### D. Piercing the Corporate Veil
- Entity is mere instrumentality of the individual
- Entity used to commit fraud or wrong
- Maintaining separate identity would be unjust
- **Michigan factors:** Undercapitalization, commingling funds, failure to observe formalities, using entity as alter ego
- **Key:** MSSC Inc v AMISUB Inc, unpub; Florence Cement Co v Vettraino, 292 Mich App 461 (2011)

#### E. Joint Venture Liability
- Agreement (express or implied)
- Common purpose
- Community of interest in the venture
- Equal right of control
- Each venturer liable for the other's acts within scope

### 3. THIRD-PARTY CLAIMS

Claims that expand the lawsuit beyond the original parties.

#### A. Third-Party Complaint (Impleader) — MCR 2.204
Defendant brings in a non-party who may be liable for:
- Contribution (joint tortfeasor)
- Indemnification (contractual or equitable)
- **Example:** Defendant landlord impleads contractor who performed defective repairs

#### B. Interpleader — MCR 3.603
When multiple parties claim the same fund (e.g., security deposit disputed by landlord, tenant, and third party).

#### C. Intervention — MCR 2.209
Non-party seeks to join the lawsuit:
- Intervention as of right (MCR 2.209(A))
- Permissive intervention (MCR 2.209(B))

---

### 4. CROSS-CLAIMS AND COUNTERCLAIMS

#### A. Cross-Claim — MCR 2.203(B)
Claim by one co-party against another:
- Must arise from same transaction
- **Example:** Co-defendant property manager cross-claims against co-defendant owner for indemnification

#### B. Compulsory Counterclaim — MCR 2.203(A)
MUST be filed if arising from same transaction — waived if not raised.

#### C. Permissive Counterclaim — MCR 2.203(A)
May be filed for claims not arising from same transaction.

---

### 5. CONTRIBUTION AND INDEMNITY

#### A. Contribution — MCL 600.2925a
- Joint tortfeasors share liability
- Right to contribution from other tortfeasors
- Determined by comparative fault percentages
- **Michigan:** Modified comparative fault — MCL 600.2957, 600.2959

#### B. Contractual Indemnification
- Express agreement to indemnify
- Common in: lease provisions, management agreements, vendor contracts
- Must be clearly stated

#### C. Equitable Indemnification
- One party is vicariously liable for another's conduct
- The vicariously liable party can seek full indemnity from the actual wrongdoer
- **Example:** Landlord entity indemnified by individual officer who committed the tort

---

### 6. ALTERNATIVE THEORIES

When the facts support multiple interpretations:

#### A. Alternative Pleading — MCR 2.111(A)(2)
Michigan allows pleading in the alternative:
- "Defendant either knew the representation was false (fraud) OR should have known through reasonable diligence (negligent misrepresentation)"
- Courts do not force election of theories at the pleading stage

#### B. Inconsistent Theories
May plead theories that are technically inconsistent:
- Contract AND unjust enrichment (in the alternative)
- Fraud AND breach of contract (same facts, different theories)
- Intentional AND negligent conduct

**Limitation:** Cannot recover under inconsistent theories — must elect at judgment.

---

### 7. NOVEL AND COMBINATION THEORIES

#### A. Statutory + Common Law Combinations
File both statutory AND common law claims for the same conduct:
- MCPA violation + common law fraud (same misrepresentation)
- Habitability statute + negligence (same condition)
- Security deposit act + unjust enrichment (same deposit)

**Benefit:** Statutory claims carry multipliers; common law claims may have broader damages.

#### B. Pattern and Practice Theory
When defendant's conduct shows a systematic pattern:
- Establishes knowledge/intent for fraud claims
- Supports IIED's "extreme and outrageous" standard
- May support class certification under MCR 3.501
- Discoverable: other complaints, prior lawsuits, inspection records

#### C. Enterprise Liability
When multiple entities are controlled by the same person:
- Each entity as a separate defendant
- Individual as separate defendant
- Conspiracy between entities
- Veil piercing to reach assets

**Michigan Example:** Watson individually + Watson Properties LLC + Watson Management Co → separate counts against each + conspiracy count + veil piercing count.

### 8. THEORY GENERATION CHECKLIST

For every fact pattern, force through this checklist:

| # | Theory Type | Question to Ask | If Yes → |
|---|-----------|----------------|----------|
| 1 | Direct tort | Did defendant intentionally harm plaintiff? | Fraud, IIED, conversion, trespass |
| 2 | Direct tort | Did defendant negligently harm plaintiff? | Negligence, negligence per se |
| 3 | Contract | Was there an agreement? | Breach of contract, warranty |
| 4 | Statutory | Does a statute create a right or prohibition? | Statutory claim + negligence per se |
| 5 | Derivative | Is there a second person/entity involved? | Conspiracy, vicarious liability |
| 6 | Derivative | Did defendant use an entity? | Veil piercing, entity liability |
| 7 | Federal | Was any state actor involved? | § 1983 |
| 8 | Federal | Is there discrimination? | FHA, Elliott-Larsen |
| 9 | Alternative | Can the facts support two theories? | Plead in the alternative |
| 10 | Pattern | Is this conduct repeated? | Pattern evidence, class action |
