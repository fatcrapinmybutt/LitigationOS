# gotchas — litigation-claim-researcher

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Confirmation Bias

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "The facts clearly support fraud — let's focus on that" | Tunnel vision on one strong claim causes you to miss other viable claims. In Pigors v Watson, focusing solely on fraud may cause you to overlook habitability (MCL 554.139), consumer protection (MCPA), security deposit violations, retaliatory action, or building code violations — each carrying separate damages and statutory enhancements. | Map facts to EVERY cause of action on the checklist before prioritizing any single claim. The strongest complaints plead multiple theories. Fraud may be the star, but the supporting counts often carry more money. |
| 2 | "This fact proves the element" | A fact that is consistent with an element does not necessarily PROVE it. Consistency ≠ sufficiency. The fact must affirmatively establish the element, not merely be compatible with it. Courts distinguish between "some evidence" and "sufficient evidence." | For each element, ask: "Does this fact, standing alone, establish this element by preponderance?" If the answer is "it's supportive but not sufficient," you need additional facts or evidence. Mark the element ARGUABLE, not STRONG. |
| 3 | "We have enough claims — no need to keep looking" | Premature closure is the #1 research failure. Even with 5 strong claims, the 6th claim might carry treble damages (MCPA) or attorney fees (§ 1983) that dramatically increase the case value. Every missed claim is missed money and missed leverage. | Complete the ENTIRE checklist in Phase 3 before stopping. Every single cause of action in the reference files must be evaluated and either included with a score or rejected with a stated reason. |
| 4 | "This statutory claim is too technical — skip it" | Statutory claims are often the most valuable because they carry automatic damages, fee-shifting, and multipliers. The MCPA provides treble damages. The Security Deposit Act provides double damages. Skipping them because they require careful statutory analysis is leaving money on the table. | Statutory claims deserve MORE attention, not less. For each statute, read every subsection. The MCPA has 29 prohibited practices — check every single one against the facts. |
| 5 | "The defendant will argue [defense X] so this claim won't work" | Anticipating defenses is important, but DISMISSING claims because of potential defenses is premature. Defenses may fail. Defenses create settlement leverage. Even a claim that might be defeated creates discovery rights and litigation pressure. | Score the defense vulnerability as one factor (15% weight), not a veto. A claim with strong elements (90%) and moderate defense risk (50%) still scores well. Include the claim and note the defense risk — let the litigation strategy decide whether to plead it. |
| 6 | "The damages are too small to bother" | Small actual damages can become large awards through statutory multipliers. $500 in actual MCPA damages becomes $1,500 with treble damages plus attorney fees. $800 in wrongfully withheld security deposit becomes $1,600 plus fees. These "small" claims also establish patterns of misconduct that strengthen larger claims. | Calculate the fully enhanced damages for every claim: actual + multiplier + fees + interest. A claim worth $500 in actual damages may be worth $3,000+ after enhancements. Also consider the claim's value in establishing a pattern of misconduct across multiple counts. |
| 7 | "I already know all the relevant legal theories" | No you don't. Even experienced attorneys miss claims. The systematic checklist exists because human pattern matching is unreliable. Novel theory combinations, rarely-used statutes, and derivative claims are routinely overlooked. | Run the full checklist. Every time. No exceptions. The checklist is not a suggestion — it's a protocol. If the checklist identifies a theory you didn't initially consider, that's the system working as designed. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Premature Dismissal

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "IIED won't work — the standard is too high" | While IIED has a high bar (Roberts v Auto-Owners), dismissing it without analysis is premature. Some conduct patterns — sustained harassment, exploitation of vulnerable persons, deliberate infliction of housing instability — can meet the standard. Michigan has allowed IIED in egregious landlord-tenant contexts. | Score IIED honestly. If the conduct is merely rude or unfair, reject with explanation. But if there's a pattern of extreme behavior targeting a vulnerable person, it may qualify. At minimum, pleading it creates discovery pressure even if it's later dismissed. |
| 2 | "There's no state action for § 1983" | State action is a high bar for private defendants, but it's not impossible. If the defendant used court processes (eviction, garnishment), worked with government inspectors, or operated under government license/regulation in a way that created entanglement, state action may exist. Don't assume — analyze. | Work through each state action test: public function, compulsion, joint action/symbiotic relationship, nexus. If any test arguably applies, include § 1983 as a scored claim. The attorney fees under § 1988 make it worth pursuing even at moderate probability. |
| 3 | "The SOL probably ran — don't bother" | "Probably" is not "definitely." The discovery rule (MCL 600.5855), continuing violations doctrine, equitable tolling, and the saving provision (MCL 600.5856) can all extend time. Never assume SOL is expired without calculating the exact accrual date and checking every applicable tolling/extension doctrine. | Calculate the precise SOL expiration date for each claim. Identify the accrual event. Apply the discovery rule if concealment is alleged. Check for continuing violations. Only mark SOL as failed if the math is unambiguous. |
| 4 | "Civil conspiracy needs a sophisticated agreement — these defendants aren't that organized" | Civil conspiracy doesn't require a formal agreement or written plan. It can be inferred from conduct, circumstantial evidence, and coordinated action. Two people acting in concert toward an unlawful goal is sufficient. Admiral Ins Co. | Look for coordinated behavior between defendants. If multiple actors took complementary harmful actions, conspiracy is viable even without direct evidence of agreement. The "agreement" can be inferred from the pattern of conduct. |
| 5 | "Negligence per se requires a criminal statute" | Negligence per se in Michigan can be based on ANY statute or ordinance — criminal, regulatory, or civil. Building codes, housing codes, fire codes, health codes, and safety regulations all establish standards of care. The statute need not carry criminal penalties. | Identify every statute, ordinance, regulation, and code provision the defendant violated. Each one is a potential negligence per se claim. Housing code violations alone may generate multiple negligence per se counts. |
| 6 | "We can't pierce the corporate veil — that's too hard" | Veil piercing is fact-intensive but not impossible. Michigan courts pierce when: (1) the entity is a mere instrumentality of the individual, (2) the entity was used to commit fraud or wrong, (3) adherence to separate identity would be unjust. If the defendant commingled funds, undercapitalized the entity, or used it as an alter ego, piercing is viable. | Investigate the corporate structure. If individual defendants own/control entity defendants, analyze alter ego factors. Even raising veil piercing in the complaint creates discovery rights into the entity's finances and operations. |

## Cognitive Debiasing Checklist

Before finalizing any claim analysis, the agent must answer:

- [ ] Have I checked EVERY cause of action on the Phase 3 checklist?
- [ ] For each rejected claim, have I stated the specific reason for rejection?
- [ ] Have I considered at least one legal theory I initially thought wouldn't apply?
- [ ] Have I scored every claim with element coverage ≥ 50% rather than just rejecting it?
- [ ] Have I calculated fully-enhanced damages (with multipliers) for every viable claim?
- [ ] Have I identified at least one derivative or combination theory?
- [ ] If all my claims fall in one category, have I forced myself through the other categories?
