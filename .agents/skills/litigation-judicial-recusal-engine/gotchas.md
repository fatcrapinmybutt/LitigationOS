# Gotchas — Judicial Recusal Engine

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Filing recusal motion too late | Waiting until after adverse ruling to seek disqualification | MCR 2.003(D)(1) requires motion at earliest practicable opportunity; delay implies waiver | File immediately upon learning of disqualifying facts; late motions are nearly always denied |
| Confusing disqualification standards | Treating personal bias the same as procedural disqualification | MCR 2.003(C)(1) (personal bias) has different proof requirements than MCR 2.003(C)(2) (personal knowledge of disputed facts) | Identify the specific MCR 2.003(C) subsection that applies and tailor evidence to that standard |
| Relying on adverse rulings as bias evidence | Arguing judge is biased because rulings went against you | Judicial rulings, even erroneous ones, are almost never sufficient grounds for disqualification | Focus on extrajudicial conduct, personal relationships, financial interests, or ex parte communications |
| Not preserving the record | Failing to get a ruling on the recusal motion or accepting oral denial | An unruled-upon or oral-only denial cannot be effectively appealed | Insist on a written order; if denied, file interlocutory appeal to COA under MCR 7.203(A) |
| Attacking the judge personally | Making the motion about the judge's character rather than specific disqualifying facts | Personal attacks destroy credibility and give the judge reason to deny; may result in sanctions | Present objective facts calmly; cite specific MCR 2.003(C) grounds with supporting evidence |

## Failure Modes
1. **Timeliness Waiver**: Motion filed weeks after disqualifying fact discovered. Recovery: Argue the disqualifying fact was not immediately apparent and provide specific timeline of when you learned the full extent; invoke MCR 2.003(D)(1) reasonable diligence standard.
2. **Insufficient Factual Basis**: Motion contains only conclusory allegations of bias. Recovery: Supplement with specific dated incidents, transcripts showing prejudicial comments, documented ex parte contacts, or evidence of personal/financial interest.
3. **Wrong Forum**: Filing recusal motion with the same judge who is the target. Recovery: Under MCR 2.003(D)(2), the challenged judge decides the motion initially; if denied, appeal to chief judge. Know the procedure — this is not a defect, it is the required process.
4. **Mandamus Failure**: Filing mandamus in COA without first exhausting trial-court recusal procedure. Recovery: File the MCR 2.003 motion in circuit court first; obtain denial; then seek leave to appeal or file mandamus in COA.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-motion-practice**, the recusal motion must be the FIRST motion filed — filing substantive motions before recusal may waive the right to seek disqualification for previously-known grounds.
- Data flow from **litigation-timeline-forensics** is critical — build a chronological exhibit of all incidents supporting bias, with transcript references, to attach to the motion as required by MCR 2.003(D)(1).
- Michigan-specific pitfall: In the 14th Circuit, the challenged judge rules on their own recusal motion first (MCR 2.003(D)(2)). If denied, you must request reassignment to the chief judge. Many pro se litigants do not know this two-step process and simply accept the denial.
