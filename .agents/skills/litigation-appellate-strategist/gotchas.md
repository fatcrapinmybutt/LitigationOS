# Gotchas — litigation-appellate-strategist

## Anti-Rationalization Table

| # | Excuse (The Lie) | Reality (The Truth) | Consequence |
|---|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| 1 | "We can raise this issue on appeal even though we didn't object at trial." | Unpreserved issues are reviewed only for plain error affecting substantial rights (MCR 2.613(C), People v Carines). Plain error review is nearly impossible to win — the court presumes the error was harmless. Issue preservation is not optional. | Waived issues are dead issues. In Lane A (custody), failing to object to Judge McNeill's best-interest factor findings at the hearing means those findings get "great weight" deference with no meaningful appellate review. You've lost before the brief is written. |
| 2 | "We should appeal every adverse ruling — throw everything at the wall." | Appellate courts disfavor kitchen-sink appeals. The COA explicitly warns against raising numerous weak issues that dilute strong ones. Pick your 2-4 strongest issues and argue them thoroughly. Weak issues actively harm strong ones by association. | Raising 8+ issues signals desperation and lack of legal judgment. COA judges have limited time per case. If your strong Issue 2 is buried between weak Issues 1 and 3, the panel may give it less attention. Quality over quantity, always. |
| 3 | "The standard of review doesn't matter much — the facts speak for themselves." | Standard of review is the MOST important appellate consideration. It determines whether the COA reviews the issue fresh (de novo) or with heavy deference (abuse of discretion / great weight). Getting the standard wrong means arguing to the wrong legal framework. | Arguing de novo review for a discretionary custody decision (Lane A) gets your brief dismissed as legally unsophisticated. Arguing abuse of discretion for a pure legal question (Lane B contract interpretation) signals you don't understand appellate procedure. |
| 4 | "We don't need record citations for every fact — the court has the whole record." | MCR 7.212(C)(6) requires that every factual statement in the brief reference the record. The COA will not search the record for you. Unverified factual assertions are treated as unsupported and may be stricken. | Briefs with missing record citations get remanded for correction (wasting weeks), or worse, the court disregards the unsupported facts entirely. In the 14th Circuit appeals, this is the #1 reason briefs are rejected. |
| 5 | "We can file the claim of appeal a few days late — the court will grant an extension." | The 21-day deadline for a claim of appeal (MCR 7.204(A)) is jurisdictional in practice. While MCR 7.205(F) allows late applications within 6 months, late applications carry a heavy burden and are frequently denied. There is no automatic extension. | Missing the 21-day deadline converts your appeal of right into a discretionary application for leave. The COA can simply deny leave without explanation. A potentially winning appeal dies because of a calendar error. |
| 6 | "The transcript isn't that important — we have good notes from the hearing." | Appellate review is based on the official record, not counsel's notes. Without the transcript, the COA applies the presumption that the trial court's findings are correct. You cannot challenge factual findings without the transcript. | No transcript = no factual challenge on appeal. In Lane A custody matters, where Judge McNeill's best-interest findings are central, losing the ability to challenge facts is case-ending. Always order the transcript immediately. |
| 7 | "MSC will take our case because the COA got it wrong." | The MSC takes approximately 4-6% of applications. "The COA was wrong" is not a basis for MSC review under MCR 7.305(B). You need a panel conflict, significant public interest, or constitutional question. Most COA losses are final. | Filing a frivolous MSC application wastes client resources and delays finality. Worse, it can result in sanctions. Evaluate MSC criteria honestly before recommending an application. |

## Common Failure Modes

### 1. Missed Claim of Appeal Deadline
- **What happens**: 21-day window from judgment entry passes without filing
- **How to prevent**: Calendar deadline on DAY 1 after adverse judgment; backup calendar system
- **Lane risk**: CRITICAL for all lanes — this is the most common appellate malpractice claim

### 2. Inadequate Record Designation
- **What happens**: Key transcript or exhibit not included in the record on appeal
- **How to prevent**: Use record designation checklist from SKILL.md; verify completeness before brief
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — missing records can't be supplemented easily after briefing

### 3. Wrong Standard of Review
- **What happens**: Brief argues wrong standard, undermining the legal framework
- **How to prevent**: Use lane-specific standard of review map; verify standard for each issue
- **Lane risk**: HIGH for Lane A — custody issues have unique "great weight" standard

### 4. Issue Overloading
- **What happens**: Too many issues raised, diluting strong arguments
- **How to prevent**: Maximum 4 issues per brief; rank by strength; cut ruthlessly
- **Lane risk**: MEDIUM — tempting in complex multi-lane litigation

### 5. Preservation Gap Discovery (Too Late)
- **What happens**: During brief writing, discover key issue was not preserved at trial
- **How to prevent**: Run preservation audit BEFORE filing claim of appeal
- **Lane risk**: HIGH — if discovered after briefing begins, you're stuck with plain error

## Appellate-Specific Citation Gotchas

- Michigan COA opinions cite to Michigan Reports (Mich) and Michigan Appeals Reports (Mich App) — use official reporters, not Westlaw cites
- Unpublished opinions MUST be identified as such (MCR 7.215(C)(1))
- Parallel citations: provide both Mich/Mich App AND NW2d citations
- Record citations use specific format: (Tr at 45) for transcript, (Ex 12) for exhibits
- Do NOT use "Id." for record citations — always give the full reference
