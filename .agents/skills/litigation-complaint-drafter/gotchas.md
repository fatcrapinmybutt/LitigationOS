# gotchas — litigation-complaint-drafter

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Drafting Errors

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "I'll combine multiple elements into one paragraph for readability" | MCR 2.111(A)(1) requires short and plain statements. Each element should be separately alleged so the court can evaluate each independently. Combined paragraphs make it impossible to determine which element is supported by which facts, and invite a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim. | One element per paragraph. Each paragraph should allege one discrete fact or one element of one cause of action. If a paragraph has "and" connecting two unrelated concepts, split it. |
| 2 | "The facts speak for themselves — I don't need to allege each element" | Michigan is a notice pleading state, but you still must allege facts supporting EVERY element of EVERY cause of action. Failure to allege even one element is fatal — the court will grant 12(b)(6) dismissal. You cannot rely on the court inferring an unpled element. | Before finalizing any count, create a checklist of every element from Skill 22's reference files. Map each element to a specific numbered paragraph. If any element has no supporting paragraph, add one or reconsider the count. |
| 3 | "I'll add the fraud count without specifics — we can flesh it out later" | MCR 2.112(B)(1) mandates fraud be pled with PARTICULARITY: time, place, content of the false representation, identity of the person making it, and what was obtained. A vague fraud count will be stricken on motion. There is no "flesh it out later" — the complaint must be specific at filing. | For every fraud allegation: state the exact date, identify the speaker by name, quote the false statement (or describe it precisely), explain why it was false, describe plaintiff's reliance, and quantify the resulting damage. |
| 4 | "I'll skip the verification — it's just a formality" | A verified complaint carries evidentiary weight that an unverified complaint does not. In emergency motions (TRO, preliminary injunction), the verification can serve as the supporting affidavit. For pro se litigants, verification adds credibility and may be required by local court rules. | Always include verification under penalty of perjury. Use the exact language from MCR 2.114(A) and ensure the plaintiff signs and dates it. |
| 5 | "I'll name the business but not the individuals" | Individual liability requires naming individuals as defendants. Corporate officers, managers, and agents can be personally liable for torts they commit or direct. If you only sue the entity, you lose personal liability claims AND risk the entity dissolving or declaring bankruptcy with no recovery. | Name every potentially liable individual defendant separately. Include their capacity (individual, agent, officer, manager). Plead facts showing their personal participation in the wrongful conduct. |
| 6 | "The prayer for relief can be general — the court knows what we want" | Michigan requires specific prayers. If you don't specifically request treble damages, attorney fees, injunctive relief, or declaratory judgment, the court may not award them even if you're entitled. The prayer defines the outer boundary of recovery. | List every specific remedy for every count. Include: compensatory damages (with "in excess of $25,000"), each statutory multiplier by citation, attorney fees with statutory authority, pre/post-judgment interest, costs, and injunctive/declaratory relief. End with the catch-all. |
| 7 | "I'll incorporate all previous paragraphs by reference into every count" | While incorporation by reference is standard, incorporating irrelevant paragraphs into a count muddies the pleading. If Count V (MCPA) incorporates paragraphs about a completely unrelated property dispute, it creates confusion and invites a motion to strike. | Incorporate only the paragraphs relevant to each specific count. Use "Plaintiff incorporates paragraphs 1 through [X] as though fully restated herein" where [X] includes only the relevant general allegations and fact paragraphs. |
| 8 | "I can allege facts 'on information and belief' for everything" | "On information and belief" is appropriate only when facts are outside plaintiff's direct knowledge but plaintiff has a good-faith basis for the allegation. Overusing it signals weakness and invites the court to discount the entire complaint. Facts within plaintiff's personal knowledge must be alleged directly. | Use "on information and belief" sparingly — only for defendant's internal knowledge, third-party conduct plaintiff didn't witness, or corporate structure details. Everything plaintiff personally experienced, saw, heard, or documented should be alleged as direct fact. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Structural Errors

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "I'll put all defendants on every count" | Not every defendant is liable on every claim. Naming a defendant on a count where they have no liability invites sanctions (MCR 2.114) and dilutes the complaint's credibility. Each count must specify which defendant(s) it targets. | Header each count with "(Against Defendant [Name])" or "(Against All Defendants)" and plead facts showing that specific defendant's conduct satisfies the elements of that specific cause of action. |
| 2 | "Venue is obvious — I won't bother alleging it" | Venue is a waivable defense, but failing to properly allege it can delay proceedings if challenged. MCR 2.221-2.226 requires venue in the county where the cause of action arose, where a defendant resides, or where a defendant has a principal place of business. | Include a dedicated venue paragraph citing the specific MCR provision that applies. For Muskegon County: "Venue is proper in this Court pursuant to MCR 2.221 because the events giving rise to this action occurred in Muskegon County." |
| 3 | "I'll file the amendment whenever I want" | MCR 2.118(A)(1) allows amendment as of right only before service of responsive pleading or within 21 days after service of a motion under MCR 2.115 or 2.116. After that window, you need leave of court — and courts consider prejudice, delay, futility, and bad faith. | Track the amendment window carefully. If within the as-of-right period, file immediately. If outside, prepare a motion for leave to amend with a brief explaining why amendment should be allowed, attaching the proposed amended complaint. |
| 4 | "Cross-claims and counterclaims can wait" | Compulsory counterclaims (arising from the same transaction) MUST be filed or they are waived — MCR 2.203(A). If you represent a defendant who has claims against a co-defendant arising from the same events, failing to cross-claim may bar those claims later. | Analyze every party's claims against every other party at the outset. File compulsory counterclaims and cross-claims with the responsive pleading. Permissive claims can wait but should be evaluated early. |
| 5 | "I numbered the paragraphs within each count starting from 1" | Paragraphs must be consecutively numbered THROUGHOUT THE ENTIRE COMPLAINT. Restarting numbering in each count creates confusion when incorporating by reference and when the court needs to cite specific paragraphs. | Number paragraphs sequentially from 1 through the last paragraph of the last count. Count I might be paragraphs 25-40, Count II paragraphs 41-55, etc. Never restart numbering. |
| 6 | "I'll attach all my evidence as exhibits" | Only attach documents specifically referenced in the complaint body AND necessary for the complaint to be understood. Attaching voluminous evidence at the complaint stage is inappropriate — that's what discovery is for. Over-attaching reveals strategy and clutters the record. | Attach only: contracts, written agreements, key correspondence specifically quoted in the complaint, statutory notices, and documents the complaint depends on for its allegations. Save the rest for discovery. |

## Common Pitfalls in Pigors v Watson Context

1. **Pro se formatting matters** — Courts hold pro se litigants to a lower standard on legal theory but the SAME standard on procedural compliance. A sloppy complaint signals incompetence.
2. **Multiple entity defendants** — If Watson operates through LLCs, corporations, or DBAs, each entity must be named separately with its correct legal designation.
3. **Manufactured housing specifics** — If applicable, counts under MCL 125.2301 et seq. require allegations specific to the manufactured housing regulatory scheme — don't just plead generic landlord-tenant.
4. **Federal claims in state court** — If pleading § 1983 in state court, Michigan circuit courts have concurrent jurisdiction but the defendant may remove to federal court under 28 USC § 1441.
