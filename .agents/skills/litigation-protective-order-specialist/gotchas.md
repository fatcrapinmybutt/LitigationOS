# Gotchas — Protective Order Specialist

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Filing PPO without sufficient factual basis | Seeking a PPO based on general fear or discomfort without specific actionable conduct | MCL 600.2950(1) requires showing of "reasonable cause to believe" respondent will engage in specific enumerated conduct (assault, stalking, harassment, etc.) | Document specific incidents with dates, descriptions, and evidence that fit statutory criteria |
| Confusing domestic vs. non-domestic PPO | Filing under the wrong statute because the relationship type was misidentified | MCL 600.2950 (domestic) and MCL 600.2950a (non-domestic/stalking) have different requirements and standards | Verify the relationship qualifies under MCL 600.2950(1) definition of "domestic relationship" before selecting form |
| Violating your own PPO | Initiating contact with the person you obtained the PPO against | A petitioner who initiates contact undermines the PPO's credibility and may face modification or termination; the respondent may use this to dissolve the PPO | Once a PPO is entered, maintain strict no-contact unless the PPO specifically permits certain communications |
| Missing the hearing deadline to challenge | Failing to request a hearing to challenge a PPO within the statutory timeframe | MCL 600.2950(12) gives respondent the right to request a hearing; failure to request waives the opportunity to contest the ex parte PPO | If served with a PPO, calendar the hearing request deadline and file promptly |
| Not addressing PPO in custody context | Treating the PPO and custody case as completely separate proceedings | PPOs directly impact custody under MCL 722.23(k) (domestic violence factor) and may restrict parenting time; courts cross-reference PPO and custody files | Address the PPO's impact on custody in both proceedings; ensure consistency in factual allegations |

## Failure Modes
1. **Ex Parte PPO Dissolved at Hearing**: PPO entered ex parte but dissolved after respondent requests hearing and presents contrary evidence. Recovery: Prepare for the hearing as thoroughly as trial — bring witnesses, documents, and a clear presentation of each statutory element.
2. **PPO Used as Tactical Weapon**: Court determines PPO was filed for litigation advantage rather than genuine safety. Recovery: This destroys credibility in the custody case; if you filed in good faith, prepare evidence of the genuine threat; never use PPO filings strategically.
3. **Contempt for PPO Violation**: Respondent violates PPO but prosecutor declines enforcement. Recovery: File private criminal complaint or pursue civil contempt through the court; document every violation with police reports.
4. **Mutual PPOs**: Both parties file PPOs against each other, creating complicated restrictions. Recovery: Move to consolidate proceedings; present evidence showing which party is the primary aggressor; mutual PPOs often indicate the court needs more information.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-parental-alienation-detector**, be aware that PPO allegations and alienation allegations often intersect — a PPO may be evidence of genuine DV or may be a tool of alienation. Present the factual evidence and let the court evaluate.
- Data flow from **litigation-motion-practice** must account for PPO restrictions — communications and service requirements change when a PPO is in effect; all motions must be served through methods consistent with the PPO.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: In Muskegon County, PPOs in family cases (Case 2023-5907-PP) directly impact the custody proceedings (Case 2024-001507-DC). The family division judge sees both files. Inconsistent factual representations between the PPO and custody proceedings will be noticed and will damage credibility.
