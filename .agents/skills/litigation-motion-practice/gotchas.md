# Gotchas — Motion Practice

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Filing without required brief | Filing a motion without a supporting brief or with a brief that lacks legal authority | MCR 2.119(A)(2) requires a brief citing relevant authority; motions without briefs may be denied without consideration | Always attach a brief with citations to MCR, MCL, and Michigan case law; even simple motions need legal support |
| Missing the notice requirement | Filing a motion without proper notice to all parties or insufficient notice period | MCR 2.119(C) requires 9 days' notice before hearing (14 days if served by mail); insufficient notice = hearing vacated | Calendar notice requirements from the hearing date backward; add 5 days for mail service |
| Filing an improper ex parte motion | Seeking ex parte relief without meeting the stringent requirements | MCR 2.119(B) limits ex parte orders to situations of irreparable injury; most motions require notice and hearing | Reserve ex parte motions for genuine emergencies; document why notice would cause irreparable harm |
| Ignoring the 21-day rule for response | Failing to file a response brief within the required timeline | MCR 2.119(A)(2) allows 21 days for response brief (14 days in some matters); no response = concession of the motion | Calendar response deadlines immediately upon receiving any motion; file timely even if brief |
| Not requesting oral argument | Assuming the court will schedule a hearing automatically | Many Michigan courts decide motions on the briefs without hearing; MCR 2.119(E)(2) allows court to decide without oral argument | Explicitly request oral argument in your motion or response brief; contact the clerk to schedule |

## Failure Modes
1. **Motion Denied on Procedural Grounds**: Motion denied because it lacked proper notice, required brief, or verification. Recovery: File corrected motion immediately; if hearing passed, request new hearing date; procedural deficiencies are curable but cost time.
2. **Response Deadline Missed**: Failed to respond to opposing party's motion within 21 days. Recovery: File late response with motion for leave to file instanter, explaining delay; court has discretion to accept but is not required to.
3. **Improper Motion Type**: Filing a motion for summary disposition (MCR 2.116) when the correct vehicle is motion in limine or vice versa. Recovery: Refile under correct rule; court may treat as filed under the correct rule sua sponte, but do not rely on this.
4. **Sanction Under MCR 1.109(E)(6)**: Motion found to be frivolous or filed for improper purpose. Recovery: Demonstrate good faith basis for the motion; cite specific legal authority; withdraw motion if basis was genuinely mistaken.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-trial-preparation-specialist**, ensure motions in limine are filed with adequate lead time before trial — most courts want them 14+ days before trial date per scheduling order.
- Data flow from **litigation-timeline-forensics** is essential for motions requiring factual chronology — the timeline evidence must be attached as exhibits to the motion, not merely referenced in the brief.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: In the 14th Circuit, motion practice varies between the general civil division and family division. Family division judges often schedule motions on specific motion days. Check with the clerk for the assigned judge's motion schedule and filing requirements — do not assume the general rules apply without local customization.
