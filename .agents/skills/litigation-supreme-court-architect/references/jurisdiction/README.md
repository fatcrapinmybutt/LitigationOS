# MSC JURISDICTION OVERVIEW

## PURPOSE

This document maps every source of Michigan Supreme Court jurisdiction.
Every filing MUST connect to one of these jurisdictional bases or it will be dismissed.

---

## 1. CONSTITUTIONAL GRANT — CONST 1963, ART 6, §4

The Michigan Supreme Court's jurisdiction is constitutionally established:

> "The supreme court shall have general superintending control over all courts;
> power to issue, hear and determine prerogative and remedial writs; and
> appellate jurisdiction as provided by rules of the supreme court."

### Three Constitutional Pillars

**Pillar 1 — General Superintending Control**
- Source: Const 1963 Art 6 §4, first clause
- Scope: Authority over ALL Michigan courts — circuit, probate, district, COA
- Mechanism: May issue orders directing lower courts to act, refrain from acting,
  or correct errors
- Limitation: Not a substitute for ordinary appeal; requires showing that lower
  court exceeded authority or failed to perform duty
- Key Case: Grievance Administrator v Fieger, 476 Mich 231 (2006)
- Application to Pigors: If 14th Circuit judges are exceeding authority or
  systematically failing duties, superintending control is the vehicle

**Pillar 2 — Prerogative and Remedial Writs**
- Source: Const 1963 Art 6 §4, second clause
- Types: Mandamus, habeas corpus, quo warranto, certiorari, prohibition
- Standard: Extraordinary relief — available only when no adequate remedy at law
- Key Case: Pub Health Dept v Rivergate Manor, 452 Mich 495 (1996)
- Application to Pigors: Mandamus may compel specific judicial action;
  habeas for custody restraints

**Pillar 3 — Appellate Jurisdiction**
- Source: Const 1963 Art 6 §4, third clause
- Scope: As defined by MCR 7.301–7.311
- Mechanism: Application for leave to appeal from COA decisions
- Standard: Discretionary — MSC chooses which cases to hear
- Statistics: MSC accepts approximately 3-5% of leave applications annually
- Application to Pigors: Primary vehicle after COA proceedings conclude

## 2. APPELLATE JURISDICTION — MCR 7.301

### MCR 7.301(A) — Jurisdiction of the Supreme Court

The Supreme Court has jurisdiction to:
1. **Review by appeal** — Cases from the Court of Appeals
2. **Review by leave** — Discretionary review of COA decisions
3. **Original proceedings** — Superintending control, writs, constitutional questions
4. **Certified questions** — From federal courts on Michigan law questions
5. **Advisory opinions** — When requested by the Governor (Art 3 §8)

### Mandatory vs. Discretionary

| Type | Mandatory? | Rule |
|------|-----------|------|
| Appeal of right | NO — MSC has almost no mandatory jurisdiction | — |
| Leave to appeal | Discretionary | MCR 7.302, 7.305 |
| Original jurisdiction | Discretionary | MCR 7.303 |
| Extraordinary writs | Discretionary | MCR 7.306 |
| Certified questions | Discretionary (can decline) | MCR 7.308 |
| Advisory opinions | Discretionary | Const Art 3 §8 |

**Critical Implication**: MSC can refuse to hear ANY case. There is no "right"
to MSC review. Every filing must PERSUADE the court to accept jurisdiction.

## 3. SUPERINTENDING CONTROL — CONST 1963, ART 6, §4

### Constitutional Basis

MSC has "general superintending control over all courts." This means:

1. **Administrative Superintending Control** (AO authority)
   - MSC can issue administrative orders governing all courts
   - Implemented through SCAO (State Court Administrative Office)
   - Used for systemic court administration, not individual cases

2. **Judicial Superintending Control** (Case-specific)
   - MCR 7.303 — original proceeding in MSC
   - MCR 7.306 — complaint for superintending control
   - Available when lower court exceeds jurisdiction, fails to act, or
     acts contrary to law
   - Standard: Clear legal duty + no adequate legal remedy

### Requirements for Superintending Control (Judicial)

1. **Clear legal duty** — Lower court has mandatory duty to act
2. **Clear legal right** — Petitioner has right to compel that act
3. **No adequate legal remedy** — Appeal is insufficient or unavailable
4. **Urgency/necessity** — Ordinary proceedings would cause irreparable harm

### Key Cases
- Michigan v Lazar, 349 Mich 377 (1957) — established modern superintending control standard
- Grievance Administrator v Fieger, 476 Mich 231 (2006) — scope of superintending power
- In re Hague, 412 Mich 532 (1982) — limits on superintending control

## 4. ALL GROUNDS FOR ACCEPTING LEAVE — MCR 7.305(B)

### MCR 7.305(B)(1) — Conflict Between Decisions

> The Supreme Court may grant leave if "the Court of Appeals decision conflicts
> with a Supreme Court decision, a decision of another panel of the Court of
> Appeals, or a decision of a federal court."

**Requirements:**
- Must identify SPECIFIC conflicting decisions
- Conflict must be on the SAME legal issue
- Conflict must be real, not superficial
- Must show the conflict matters to the outcome

**Application to Pigors:**
- Lane A: If COA decision conflicts with published custody/due process precedent
- Lane B: If COA decision conflicts with habitability/landlord-tenant precedent
- Lane C: If COA decisions conflict with judicial misconduct/immunity precedent

### MCR 7.305(B)(2) — Significant Question of Public Interest

> The Supreme Court may grant leave if "the issue involves a legal principle
> of major significance to the state's jurisprudence."

**Requirements:**
- Issue must transcend the individual case
- Must affect a significant number of persons or cases
- Must involve an unsettled or developing area of law
- Must show why MSC guidance is needed

**Application to Pigors:**
- Lane A: Parental alienation doctrine in Michigan — unsettled legal framework
- Lane B: Landlord accountability standards — affects all Michigan tenants
- Lane C: Judicial accountability/oversight — systemic significance

### MCR 7.305(B)(3) — Bypass (Extraordinary Circumstances)

> The Supreme Court may grant leave "before decision by the Court of Appeals,
> if the issue is one of major significance to the state's jurisprudence and
> delay in final adjudication is likely to cause substantial harm."

**Requirements (both must be met):**
1. Issue of major significance to Michigan jurisprudence
2. Delay in COA would cause substantial/irreparable harm

**Application to Pigors:**
- Lane A: If custody deprivation is ongoing and COA delay extends harm to child
- Lane C: If systemic judicial misconduct requires immediate MSC intervention

### MCR 7.305(B)(4) — Dispositive Federal Question

> The Supreme Court may grant leave if "the case involves a substantial
> question about the validity of a legislative act, and the issue has
> not been finally determined by the Supreme Court."

**Requirements:**
- Federal constitutional or statutory question must be presented
- Must be DISPOSITIVE — i.e., controlling the outcome
- Question not already settled by MSC precedent

**Application to Pigors:**
- Lane A: 14th Amendment due process/equal protection in custody proceedings
- Lane B: Federal housing law preemption / Fair Housing Act intersection
- Lane C: 42 USC §1983 — federal civil rights claims against state actors

### MCR 7.305(B)(5) — Other Criteria

> The Supreme Court may grant leave if "the decision is clearly erroneous
> and will cause material injustice."

**Requirements:**
- Clear error — not mere disagreement
- Material injustice — real harm, not theoretical
- This is the weakest ground — rarely sufficient alone

**Application to Pigors:**
- Use as supplemental ground alongside (B)(1)–(B)(4)
- Document specific clear errors in lower court proceedings
