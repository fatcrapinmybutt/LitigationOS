# GROUNDS MATRIX — MCR 7.305(B) MAPPED TO PIGORS v WATSON

## PURPOSE

This matrix maps every MCR 7.305(B) ground for granting leave to appeal
to the specific facts and legal issues in the Pigors v Watson litigation.
Each ground is analyzed for each case lane.

---

## MCR 7.305(B)(1) — CONFLICT BETWEEN DECISIONS

### Standard
> MSC may grant leave when "the Court of Appeals decision conflicts with a
> Supreme Court decision, a decision of another panel of the Court of Appeals,
> or a decision of a federal court."

### Requirements for This Ground
1. Identify SPECIFIC published decision(s) that conflict
2. Show conflict is on the SAME legal issue (not merely similar facts)
3. Show the conflict is irreconcilable (not distinguishable)
4. Show the conflict affects the OUTCOME of this case

### Lane A — Watson/Custody: Conflict Analysis

**Potential Conflict Areas:**
- Parental rights presumption — If COA departs from *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000)
  (fundamental right of parents to direct upbringing)
- Best interest factors — If COA misapplies *MCL 722.23* factors in conflict
  with prior MSC interpretation in *Berger v Berger*, 277 Mich App 700 (2008)
- Due process in custody modification — If COA applies wrong standard for
  modification in conflict with *Vodvarka v Grasmeyer*, 259 Mich App 499 (2003)
- Equal protection — If COA treats similarly situated parents differently
  in conflict with established equal protection jurisprudence

**Conflict Strength: MODERATE to STRONG**
- Depends entirely on specific COA holding
- Pre-identify published decisions that support Pigors' position
- Watch for unpublished COA decisions that conflict (persuasive, not binding, but
  evidence of inconsistency in the judiciary)

### Lane B — Shady Oaks/Housing: Conflict Analysis

**Potential Conflict Areas:**
- Implied warranty of habitability — Michigan follows *Rome v Walker*, 38 Mich App 458 (1972);
  if COA departs from this line, conflict exists
- Constructive eviction standard — If COA applies different standard than
  *Automobile Club of Michigan v City of Detroit*, 119 Mich App 610 (1982)
- Landlord duty of repair — If COA conflicts with MCL 554.139 interpretation
  in *Teufel v Watkins*, 267 Mich App 425 (2005)
- Retaliatory eviction — If COA conflicts with MCL 600.5720 protections

**Conflict Strength: MODERATE**
- Landlord-tenant law is well-developed in Michigan — conflict may be subtle
- Focus on specific statutory interpretation conflicts

### Lane C — Convergence/County: Conflict Analysis

**Potential Conflict Areas:**
- Judicial immunity scope — If COA applies broader immunity than *Mireles v Waco*,
  502 US 9 (1991) permits
- Governmental immunity exceptions — If COA misapplies *MCL 691.1407* in conflict
  with *Ross v Consumers Power Co*, 420 Mich 567 (1984)
- Due process in administrative proceedings — If COA conflicts with established
  procedural due process standards
- § 1983 standards for state actors — If COA conflicts with 6th Circuit §1983
  jurisprudence

**Conflict Strength: MODERATE to STRONG**
- Immunity and §1983 law generates significant decisional conflict
- Federal/state conflict is particularly compelling for MSC review

## MCR 7.305(B)(2) — SIGNIFICANT LEGAL QUESTION OF PUBLIC INTEREST

### Standard
> MSC may grant leave when "the issue involves a legal principle of major
> significance to the state's jurisprudence."

### Requirements for This Ground
1. Issue affects persons beyond the parties to this case
2. Issue involves unsettled or developing area of Michigan law
3. Need for uniform statewide rule exists
4. Issue has practical significance for Michigan courts, practitioners, or public

### Lane A — Watson/Custody: Public Significance

**Argument:**
- Parental alienation doctrine in Michigan is UNSETTLED — no MSC decision
  defines standards for identifying, preventing, or remedying parental alienation
- Affects THOUSANDS of Michigan custody cases annually
- Trial courts apply inconsistent standards — some recognize alienation as
  factor, others do not
- Due process standards for custody modification affect every family law
  practitioner and litigant in Michigan
- Child welfare implications extend beyond individual families

**Quantification:**
- Michigan circuit courts handle ~50,000 custody matters annually
- Parental alienation is alleged in an estimated 10-20% of contested cases
- Lack of uniform standard creates inconsistent outcomes across circuits

**Strength: STRONG**
- This is likely the strongest ground for Lane A
- MSC has not addressed parental alienation in a published opinion
- High public interest in child welfare and parental rights

### Lane B — Shady Oaks/Housing: Public Significance

**Argument:**
- Habitability standards and landlord accountability affect ALL Michigan renters
- Michigan has 1.2+ million renter households
- Enforcement of housing codes varies dramatically across jurisdictions
- Low-income tenants disproportionately affected by inconsistent standards
- Intersection of state landlord-tenant law and federal housing law

**Quantification:**
- 1.2+ million renter households in Michigan
- Thousands of habitability complaints filed annually
- Inconsistent enforcement across municipalities

**Strength: MODERATE to STRONG**
- Housing issues have broad public impact
- Developing area at intersection of state and federal law

### Lane C — Convergence/County: Public Significance

**Argument:**
- Judicial accountability and oversight is of FUNDAMENTAL public significance
- Every Michigan citizen interacts with the court system
- Standards for superintending control affect separation of powers
- Systemic judicial misconduct — if proven — undermines public trust in judiciary
- Defines scope of MSC's own constitutional superintending power

**Quantification:**
- Affects all Michigan courts and all Michigan litigants
- Defines the boundaries of judicial power
- Implicates constitutional structure

**Strength: STRONG**
- Judicial accountability is inherently of major public significance
- MSC has institutional interest in defining its own superintending power

## MCR 7.305(B)(3) — BYPASS (EXTRAORDINARY CIRCUMSTANCES)

### Standard
> MSC may grant leave "before decision by the Court of Appeals" when the
> issue is "of major significance to the state's jurisprudence" AND "delay
> in final adjudication is likely to cause substantial harm."

### BOTH PRONGS MUST BE MET

**Prong 1 — Major Significance:** Same analysis as (B)(2) above.

**Prong 2 — Harm From Delay:** Must show specific, concrete, irreparable harm
that will occur while waiting for COA to decide.

### Lane A — Watson/Custody: Bypass Analysis

**Prong 1 (Significance):** STRONG — same as (B)(2) analysis above.

**Prong 2 (Harm from Delay):**
- Every day of wrongful custody deprivation causes irreparable harm to
  parent-child relationship
- Children's developmental windows are time-limited — delay is itself harm
- Parental alienation, if occurring, intensifies with time
- COA backlog means potential 12-18 month delay — unacceptable for child welfare
- Constitutional rights (14th Amendment) violated daily without remedy

**Bypass Strength: MODERATE**
- Strongest bypass case among the three lanes
- Must document specific ongoing harm with evidence (declarations, records)
- MSC grants bypass rarely but custody/child welfare cases have best chance

### Lane B — Shady Oaks/Housing: Bypass Analysis

**Prong 2 (Harm from Delay):**
- Ongoing habitability violations cause daily health/safety risk
- Tenant may face constructive eviction during COA proceedings
- Financial harm accumulates (rent paid for uninhabitable conditions)

**Bypass Strength: WEAK to MODERATE**
- Housing harm is real but may not meet "extraordinary" threshold
- Consider seeking emergency relief in COA instead

### Lane C — Convergence/County: Bypass Analysis

**Prong 2 (Harm from Delay):**
- Systemic issues affect ongoing cases — every day the system operates
  without correction, more litigants are affected
- Constitutional violations compound with time

**Bypass Strength: MODERATE**
- Systemic nature supports urgency argument
- But MSC may prefer to wait for COA to develop the record

## MCR 7.305(B)(4) — DISPOSITIVE FEDERAL QUESTION

### Standard
> MSC may grant leave when the case involves "a substantial question about
> the validity of a legislative act" or a dispositive federal constitutional question.

### Requirements
1. Federal constitutional or statutory question is presented
2. Question must be DISPOSITIVE — controlling the outcome
3. Question not already settled by MSC precedent

### Lane A — Watson/Custody: Federal Question

**14th Amendment Due Process:**
- Parental rights are fundamental under *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000)
- If trial court deprived Pigors of custody without adequate process,
  federal due process question is presented
- Dispositive: if due process was violated, custody orders are void

**14th Amendment Equal Protection:**
- If similarly situated parents treated differently based on
  impermissible classification, equal protection violated
- Dispositive: if equal protection violated, orders must be vacated

**Strength: MODERATE to STRONG**
- Constitutional dimension elevates this ground
- Must show federal question is truly DISPOSITIVE, not just relevant

### Lane B — Shady Oaks/Housing: Federal Question

**Fair Housing Act (42 USC §3601 et seq.):**
- If housing discrimination is at issue, FHA preemption may arise
- Dispositive if federal housing law controls the outcome

**Strength: WEAK to MODERATE**
- Depends on whether federal housing law is actually implicated

### Lane C — Convergence/County: Federal Question

**42 USC §1983:**
- If state actors violated federal constitutional rights under color of law
- State-court §1983 claims present substantial federal questions
- Dispositive: if §1983 claim is valid, it controls remedies

**Strength: STRONG**
- § 1983 claims inherently present federal questions
- Well-developed ground for MSC review

## MCR 7.305(B)(5) — CLEAR ERROR / MATERIAL INJUSTICE

### Standard
> MSC may grant leave when "the decision is clearly erroneous and will
> cause material injustice."

### Lane A: Clear error in custody determination — MODERATE (supplement with other grounds)
### Lane B: Clear error in habitability ruling — MODERATE (supplement with other grounds)
### Lane C: Clear error in immunity application — MODERATE to STRONG

### Strategic Note
**NEVER rely on (B)(5) alone.** Always pair with (B)(1), (2), or (4).
(B)(5) is the "catch-all" and MSC treats it as the weakest ground.
