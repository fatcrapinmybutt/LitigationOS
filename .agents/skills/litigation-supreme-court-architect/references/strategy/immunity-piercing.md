# JUDICIAL IMMUNITY PIERCING — EXCEPTIONS AND STRATEGIES

## PURPOSE

Judicial immunity is NOT absolute. This document maps every recognized exception,
the governing authority, and how each exception applies to the Pigors v Watson
litigation. This is the definitive reference for understanding when judges,
court staff, and government actors CAN be held accountable.

---

## 1. THE IMMUNITY FRAMEWORK — WHAT IT IS AND WHAT IT IS NOT

### General Rule
Judges are generally immune from civil liability for acts performed in their
judicial capacity. This doctrine exists to protect judicial independence, not
to protect individual judges from accountability.

**Source:** *Bradley v Fisher*, 80 US (13 Wall.) 335 (1871) — established doctrine
**Modern Standard:** *Stump v Sparkman*, 435 US 349 (1978) — broadest modern statement

### What Immunity Protects
- Judicial DECISIONS — rulings, orders, judgments
- Judicial ACTS — actions taken in the judge's judicial capacity
- Even if the decision is WRONG — immunity protects incorrect rulings
- Even if the judge acts with malice — immunity protects motive in judicial acts

### What Immunity Does NOT Protect
- Non-judicial acts (administrative, enforcement, personal)
- Acts in clear absence of jurisdiction
- Declaratory and injunctive relief (not damages)
- Acts that are not "functions normally performed by a judge"

---

## 2. EXCEPTION 1 — NON-JUDICIAL ACTS

### Governing Authority
- *Forrester v White*, 484 US 219 (1988) — LANDMARK
- *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9 (1991) — refined the test

### The Test (Two-Part — Mireles/Stump)
A judge's act is "judicial" ONLY if:
1. **The act is a function normally performed by a judge** (nature of the act)
2. **The parties dealt with the judge in a judicial capacity** (expectations of parties)

### Non-Judicial Acts — NO Immunity

| Act | Why Non-Judicial | Authority |
|-----|------------------|-----------|
| Employment decisions (hiring/firing court staff) | Administrative function, not adjudicative | *Forrester v White*, 484 US 219 (1988) |
| Scheduling manipulation for improper purposes | Administrative, not judicial decision-making | *Morrison v Lipscomb*, 877 F2d 463 (6th Cir 1989) |
| Ex parte communications about non-case matters | Not part of adjudicative process | — |
| Physical acts (assault, battery in courtroom) | No judicial function includes violence | *Gregory v Thompson*, 500 F2d 59 (9th Cir 1974) |
| Enforcement of orders by judge personally | Enforcement is executive, not judicial | *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9 (1991) (dicta) |
| Public statements about pending case outside court | Not part of judicial function | — |
| Refusing to file documents presented by clerk | Ministerial/administrative, not adjudicative | — |
| Conspiring with parties outside judicial proceedings | Not a judicial function | *Dennis v Sparks*, 449 US 24 (1980) |

### Application to Pigors v Watson

**Lane A (Custody — Judge McNeill):**
- Any scheduling manipulation to disadvantage Pigors → non-judicial act
- Any ex parte communications with opposing counsel → non-judicial act
- Any refusal to consider properly filed motions → ministerial failure

**Lane C (Convergence):**
- Administrative acts by court personnel → no judicial immunity
- Pattern of non-judicial conduct suggests systemic administrative failure
- Document EACH act and classify: judicial (immune) vs. non-judicial (not immune)

## 3. EXCEPTION 2 — CLEAR ABSENCE OF ALL JURISDICTION

### Governing Authority
- *Stump v Sparkman*, 435 US 349 (1978) — defined the standard
- *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9 (1991) — confirmed the exception

### The Standard
Immunity is LOST when a judge acts in the "clear absence of all jurisdiction."

**Critical Distinction:**
- Acting in EXCESS of jurisdiction ≠ absence of jurisdiction (still immune)
- Acting in CLEAR ABSENCE of ALL jurisdiction = no immunity

### What Constitutes "Clear Absence of All Jurisdiction"

| Scenario | Jurisdiction Status | Immunity |
|----------|-------------------|----------|
| Judge misapplies the law | Excess of jurisdiction | IMMUNE |
| Judge makes wrong factual finding | Within jurisdiction | IMMUNE |
| Judge rules on case not assigned to them | Absence of jurisdiction | NOT IMMUNE |
| Probate judge handles felony criminal case | Clear absence — no subject matter jurisdiction | NOT IMMUNE |
| Judge acts after recusal/disqualification | Absence of jurisdiction | NOT IMMUNE |
| Judge acts on case after removal to federal court | Absence of jurisdiction | NOT IMMUNE |
| Judge enforces void order | Depends — may be absence of jurisdiction | ARGUABLE |

### Application to Pigors v Watson

- If any judge acted on a matter over which they had NO subject matter jurisdiction
- If judge acted after proper recusal/disqualification motion granted
- If judge acted on matters outside the scope of the assigned case type
- NOTE: This is a NARROW exception — most judicial overreach is "excess" not "absence"

## 4. EXCEPTION 3 — DECLARATORY AND INJUNCTIVE RELIEF

### Governing Authority
- *Pulliam v Allen*, 466 US 522 (1984) — LANDMARK
- 42 USC §1983 — statutory basis
- Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1996 — modified but did not eliminate

### The Rule
**Judicial immunity bars DAMAGES but does NOT bar:**
1. **Declaratory relief** — court declares the judge's act unconstitutional
2. **Injunctive relief** — court orders the judge to stop unconstitutional conduct

### Post-1996 Limitation
The Federal Courts Improvement Act (amending 42 USC §1983) provides:
> "Injunctive relief shall not be granted [against a judicial officer]
> unless a declaratory decree was violated or declaratory relief was unavailable."

**Meaning:** You must FIRST seek declaratory relief. Injunctive relief is available
only if declaratory relief is violated or unavailable.

### Practical Pathway
```
Step 1: File §1983 complaint seeking DECLARATORY relief
        → Declare that judge's conduct violates constitutional rights
Step 2: If judge violates declaratory order → seek INJUNCTIVE relief
        → Court orders judge to stop the unconstitutional conduct
Step 3: Attorney's fees available under 42 USC §1988
        → Even against judges when declaratory/injunctive relief granted
```

### Application to Pigors v Watson

**Lane A:**
- If Judge McNeill's custody rulings violate due process → seek declaration
- Declaratory judgment that specific procedures violated 14th Amendment
- If violations continue after declaration → injunctive relief

**Lane C:**
- If pattern of judicial conduct violates constitutional rights → declaration
- Declaratory judgment that systemic practices are unconstitutional
- Injunctive relief ordering specific procedural changes

## 5. EXCEPTION 4 — 42 USC §1983 PATHWAY

### Governing Authority
- *Pulliam v Allen*, 466 US 522 (1984)
- 42 USC §1983 — "Every person who, under color of [state law]..."
- *Monroe v Pape*, 365 US 167 (1961) — judges are "persons" under §1983

### Key Principles
1. Judges ARE "persons" subject to §1983
2. Judges DO act "under color of state law"
3. §1983 does NOT create immunity — common-law immunity is an affirmative defense
4. Damages are barred by judicial immunity (for judicial acts)
5. Declaratory/injunctive relief is NOT barred (per *Pulliam*)

### §1983 Against Non-Judge Court Actors
Court actors OTHER than judges have only QUALIFIED immunity:

| Actor | Immunity Level | Authority |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| Judge | Absolute (for judicial acts) | *Stump v Sparkman* |
| Court clerk | Quasi-judicial (limited) | *Antoine v Byers*, 508 US 429 (1993) |
| Court reporter | None or qualified | Case-specific |
| FOC / Friend of Court | Qualified (for investigative acts) | *Buckley v Fitzsimmons*, 509 US 259 (1993) |
| GAL / Guardian ad Litem | Quasi-judicial (limited) | *Kurzawa v Mueller*, 732 F2d 1456 (6th Cir 1984) |
| Court-appointed evaluator | Qualified | — |
| Prosecutor (advocacy) | Absolute | *Imbler v Pachtman*, 424 US 409 (1976) |
| Prosecutor (investigation) | Qualified | *Buckley v Fitzsimmons* |

### Application to Pigors v Watson

**Lane A:**
- FOC personnel involved in custody evaluation → QUALIFIED immunity only
- GAL if appointed → quasi-judicial but not absolute
- §1983 claims against non-judge actors are viable for DAMAGES

**Lane C:**
- Court clerks refusing to file documents → qualified immunity at best
- County employees involved in court administration → qualified immunity
- Pattern of conduct may overcome qualified immunity (clearly established law)

## 6. EXCEPTION 5 — JUDICIAL TENURE COMMISSION (JTC)

### Governing Authority
- Const 1963 Art 6 §30 — creates JTC
- MCR 9.200 et seq. — JTC procedures
- MCR 9.205 — grounds for discipline

### JTC Is NOT Litigation — It Is Administrative Discipline

**Key Distinction:**
- JTC complaint ≠ lawsuit against a judge
- JTC does NOT award damages to complainant
- JTC imposes DISCIPLINE: censure, suspension, removal from bench
- Immunity does NOT apply — JTC is constitutional oversight, not civil suit

### Grounds for JTC Complaint (MCR 9.205)

| Ground | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Misconduct in office | Acts incompatible with judicial office |
| Failure to perform duties | Neglect of judicial responsibilities |
| Conduct prejudicial to administration of justice | Acts undermining court system |
| Persistent failure to follow law | Repeated legal errors showing incompetence or willfulness |
| Habitual intemperance | Impairment affecting judicial function |
| Conviction of felony | Self-explanatory |
| Physical or mental disability | Preventing performance of duties |

### JTC Process
```
1. Complaint filed (by any person — no standing requirement)
2. JTC investigates (confidential)
3. If probable cause found → formal complaint issued
4. Public hearing before JTC
5. JTC recommends discipline to MSC
6. MSC makes FINAL decision on discipline
```

### Strategic Value of JTC Complaint
- Creates administrative record of judicial misconduct
- JTC investigation may uncover additional evidence
- JTC recommendation to MSC puts misconduct before the very court
  deciding your case (strategic alignment)
- Even if JTC does not act, the complaint is on record

### Application to Pigors v Watson

**Lane A (Judge McNeill):**
- If judicial conduct constitutes "misconduct in office" → file JTC complaint
- Specific acts: ex parte communications, failure to follow law, bias
- JTC complaint runs PARALLEL to MSC application — different track, same facts

**Lane C (Systemic):**
- If multiple judges engaged in coordinated misconduct → multiple JTC complaints
- Pattern evidence strengthens each individual complaint
- JTC complaint against each judge separately

## 7. EXCEPTION 6 — GOVERNMENTAL IMMUNITY (STATE LAW)

### Governing Authority
- MCL 691.1407 — Governmental immunity statute
- *Ross v Consumers Power Co*, 420 Mich 567 (1984) — MSC interpretation

### Michigan Governmental Immunity Exceptions

| Exception | MCL Citation | Application |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Highway exception | MCL 691.1402 | — |
| Defective public building | MCL 691.1406 | If courthouse conditions at issue |
| Proprietary function | MCL 691.1413 | Government acting in non-governmental capacity |
| Constitutional violation | Case law | Governmental immunity does not protect constitutional torts |
| Intentional torts | MCL 691.1407(2) | Individual employees — no immunity for intentional torts |
| Gross negligence | MCL 691.1407(2) | Individual employees — no immunity for gross negligence |

### Individual vs. Governmental Immunity
- GOVERNMENTAL immunity (entity level): broad but has statutory exceptions
- INDIVIDUAL immunity (employee level): only for acts within scope of employment
  AND not grossly negligent AND not intentional torts

### Application to Pigors v Watson (Lane C)
- County government: may invoke governmental immunity for entity-level claims
- Individual county employees: qualified immunity only — must show they acted
  within scope AND without gross negligence or intentional misconduct
- Constitutional violations: governmental immunity is NOT a defense to
  constitutional tort claims (federal or state)

## 8. MASTER IMMUNITY-PIERCING CHECKLIST

```
FOR EVERY CLAIM AGAINST A JUDGE OR GOVERNMENT ACTOR:

1. Is the act JUDICIAL or NON-JUDICIAL?
   Non-judicial → NO immunity (Exception 1)

2. Did the actor have JURISDICTION?
   Clear absence of all jurisdiction → NO immunity (Exception 2)

3. Am I seeking DAMAGES or DECLARATORY/INJUNCTIVE relief?
   Declaratory/injunctive → Immunity does NOT bar (Exception 3)

4. Is the actor a JUDGE or OTHER court actor?
   Non-judge → Qualified immunity only (Exception 4)

5. Does the conduct constitute JUDICIAL MISCONDUCT?
   Yes → File JTC complaint (Exception 5 — parallel track)

6. Is GOVERNMENTAL immunity implicated?
   Check Michigan exceptions (Exception 6)

7. Are FEDERAL civil rights (§1983) claims viable?
   Yes → Immunity is affirmative defense; file claim (Exception 4)

RULE: Immunity is the RESPONDENT'S burden to prove.
      FILE THE CLAIM. Let them raise immunity.
      Then attack immunity with these exceptions.
```
