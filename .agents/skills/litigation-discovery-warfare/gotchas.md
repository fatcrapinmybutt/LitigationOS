# Gotchas — litigation-discovery-warfare

> Critical failure modes in Michigan discovery practice. Pro se litigants face
> asymmetric discovery warfare—these traps are designed to prevent self-sabotage.

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Drafting Discovery Requests

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I can ask for everything even tangentially related" | Discovery scope is broad under MCR 2.302(B) | Overbroad requests get objected to AND make you look unreasonable to the judge—courts impose proportionality limits under MCR 2.302(C) | MCR 2.302(C); proportionality is mandatory, not optional |
| "More interrogatories = more information" | Quantity should produce comprehensive answers | Excessive interrogatories invite protective orders, get cut by the court, and dilute your most important questions among marginal ones | Quality over quantity—20 precise interrogatories beat 100 scattershot ones |
| "I don't need to define terms in my requests" | The meaning seems obvious | Undefined terms create objection opportunities—"vague and ambiguous" objections succeed when terms like "communications" or "relating to" aren't defined | Always include a definitions section; define every key term |
| "RFAs are just like interrogatories" | Both are written discovery to parties | RFAs have AUTOMATIC admission if not responded to within 28 days AND cost-shifting under MCR 2.312(D)—they're weapons, not information-gathering tools | MCR 2.312; deemed admitted if untimely; mandatory cost-shifting for unreasonable denial |
| "I can serve discovery requests any time" | There's no specific deadline mentioned in the rules | Discovery must be completed before the discovery cutoff in the scheduling order—requests served too late may not allow time for responses before cutoff | Check scheduling order; serve early enough for 28-day response + motion to compel time |
| "Subpoenas to non-parties work the same as party requests" | They both seek documents | Non-party subpoenas under MCR 2.305 require formal service, 14-day compliance period, and non-parties can object or move to quash—you can't just mail them a letter | MCR 2.305; formal service required; different objection procedures |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Responding to Discovery

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I can just object to everything I don't want to answer" | Objections are a legitimate response | Blanket/boilerplate objections are deemed waived—you must state specific grounds AND still answer to the extent not objectionable | MCR 2.309(B)(4); objections must be specific; waived if not timely and specifically stated |
| "I have plenty of time to respond—28 days is long" | 28 days sounds like a month | 28 days includes weekends, and late responses to RFAs result in AUTOMATIC DEEMED ADMISSIONS—there is no grace period without court order | MCR 2.312(B)(1); deemed admitted if not timely; must move to withdraw admissions (hard) |
| "My privilege objection protects me without further documentation" | I said it's privileged | Privilege claims require a privilege log identifying each withheld document, its date, author, recipient, and specific privilege claimed—bare assertion is waived | MCR 2.302(C); privilege log required; waiver for undocumented claims |
| "I can supplement my responses later if I find more information" | Supplementation seems natural | You have a duty to supplement under MCR 2.302(E) BUT only for specific types of responses—failure to supplement can result in exclusion of evidence at trial | MCR 2.302(E); duty applies to identity of witnesses, expert opinions, and responses that become incorrect |
| "Objecting to one part protects the whole request" | An objection should block the entire request | You must respond to non-objectionable portions—objecting to one subpart does not excuse failure to answer the rest | MCR 2.309(B)(4); answer to extent not objectionable |
| "I don't need to produce documents in any particular order" | Documents are documents regardless of organization | MCR 2.310(C)(1): Produce as kept in ordinary course of business OR organized to correspond with categories in the request—dumping unsorted boxes is sanctionable | MCR 2.310(C)(1); organized production required |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Motions to Compel and Sanctions

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I can file a motion to compel immediately when they don't respond" | They violated the rules | MCR 2.313(A)(5) requires certification of good-faith meet-and-confer BEFORE filing—courts deny motions without this certification | MCR 2.313(A)(5); good faith conference is a prerequisite; document all attempts |
| "The judge will sanction them because they deserve it" | The discovery abuse is obvious | Judges have broad discretion on sanctions—they may give another chance, impose lesser sanctions, or deny if they find the objection was "substantially justified" | MCR 2.313(A)(5)(b); "substantially justified" defense; judicial discretion |
| "Pro se litigants can't get attorney fee awards" | I'm not an attorney | Courts CAN award reasonable expenses equivalent to attorney fees for pro se litigants in discovery sanctions—document your time and costs | MCR 2.313 authorizes "reasonable expenses including attorney fees"; pro se hourly rate equivalent recognized |
| "One discovery violation is enough for case-dispositive sanctions" | They clearly violated the rules | Courts apply a graduated approach—monetary sanctions first, then evidentiary sanctions, then case-dispositive only for repeated willful violations | MCR 2.313(B)(2); proportionality in sanctions; graduated enforcement |
| "I can wait to file my motion to compel until near trial" | I want to accumulate evidence of their bad faith | Delay in seeking to compel undermines your argument—courts view late motions as tactical rather than genuine need for discovery | File promptly after failed meet-and-confer; stale motions are weak motions |
| "Default judgment is available as a discovery sanction for any violation" | MCR 2.313(B)(2)(c) lists it as an option | Default/dismissal sanctions are reserved for the most egregious cases—willful, repeated, contumacious violation of discovery orders—not first-time failures | MCR 2.313(B)(2); default is a last resort; must show pattern of willful non-compliance |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Depositions

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I can depose anyone I want without limit" | Discovery is supposed to be broad | MCR 2.306(A)(2) limits each side to 10 depositions without leave of court—choose your deponents strategically | MCR 2.306(A)(2); 10-deposition limit; leave required for additional |
| "I can ask anything in a deposition" | Depositions are under oath and scope is broad | Relevance still applies, and opposing counsel can instruct the witness not to answer for privilege or to enforce a court order—but NOT just because the question is embarrassing | MCR 2.306(D)(3); limited grounds for instruction not to answer |
| "A video deposition is automatically usable at trial" | I recorded it under oath | Video depositions require advance notice of recording method AND can only be used at trial under specific conditions (witness unavailability, impeachment, etc.) | MCR 2.306(A)(4); MCR 2.308(A) trial use limitations |
| "Corporate designee depositions bind the company perfectly" | The company designated them | The designee speaks for the company on noticed topics, but gaps in knowledge don't automatically result in sanctions—you may need to follow up with additional requests | MCR 2.306(B)(5); designee must prepare reasonably; but imperfect preparation isn't automatically sanctionable |
| "I don't need to prepare extensively since I'm asking the questions" | I know my case better than anyone | Unprepared deposition questioning wastes your limited 7 hours, produces useless testimony, and may educate opposing counsel about your theory prematurely | 7-hour limit per deponent (MCR 2.306(A)(2)); every minute counts |
| "I can surprise the witness with documents at deposition" | Surprise is a legitimate tactic | While permitted, document ambush often backfires—witnesses claim they need to review, ask for breaks, and the surprise value is lost while creating ill will with the judge | Better practice: Send documents in advance via RFP, then examine on responses at deposition |

## Critical Timing Traps

- **28-day response deadline:** Starts from date of SERVICE, not receipt
- **RFA auto-admission:** Failure to respond in 28 days = deemed admitted; withdrawal requires motion and showing of good cause (MCR 2.312(D))
- **Discovery cutoff:** ALL discovery must be completed (not just served) before cutoff date in scheduling order
- **Supplementation duty:** Ongoing obligation under MCR 2.302(E)—failure to supplement can exclude evidence
- **Motion to compel timing:** File promptly—courts view stale motions skeptically
- **Deposition notice:** Reasonable notice required; what's "reasonable" varies but 14+ days is safe

## Pro Se Discovery Traps

1. **Failure to serve properly:** Must comply with MCR 2.107 service rules—email may not suffice unless agreed
2. **Missing deadlines:** No clerk will remind you—calendar every deadline immediately
3. **Inadequate meet-and-confer:** Courts expect documented good-faith attempts before any discovery motion
4. **Overly emotional discovery requests:** Keep requests factual and professional—inflammatory language hurts credibility
5. **Not keeping copies:** Maintain complete discovery file with date-stamped copies of everything sent and received
6. **Ignoring proportionality:** A pro se plaintiff making 500 document requests from a single defendant triggers protective orders
