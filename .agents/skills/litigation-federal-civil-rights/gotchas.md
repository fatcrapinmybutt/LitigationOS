# Gotchas — litigation-federal-civil-rights

> Critical failure modes for pro se 42 USC § 1983 litigation in W.D. Michigan.
> Every row below represents a real trap that has destroyed federal civil rights cases.

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Immunity Analysis

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "The judge clearly violated my rights, so immunity doesn't apply" | The violation feels egregious and intentional | Judicial immunity protects even malicious and corrupt judicial acts—only non-judicial acts or complete absence of jurisdiction pierces it | Stump v. Sparkman, 435 U.S. 349 (1978); Mireles v. Waco, 502 U.S. 9 (1991) |
| "The GAL works for the court so they have judicial immunity" | GAL was appointed by judge and acts under court authority | GALs receive only qualified immunity in the 6th Circuit, not absolute judicial immunity | Kurzawa v. Mueller, 732 F.2d 1456 (6th Cir. 1984); Cok v. Cosentini, 876 F.2d 1 (1st Cir. 1989) |
| "I can sue the judge for damages because of Pulliam v. Allen" | Pulliam allowed suits against judges | Pulliam only authorizes prospective injunctive and declaratory relief—damages against judges remain barred | Pulliam v. Allen, 466 U.S. 522 (1984); Federal Courts Improvement Act amended § 1983 |
| "Qualified immunity doesn't apply because the right is in the Constitution" | Constitutional rights are clearly established by definition | The right must be clearly established in the SPECIFIC CONTEXT—abstract constitutional principles are insufficient | Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.S. 731 (2011); must show particularized precedent |
| "Filing a § 1983 complaint will stop the state court proceedings" | Federal court is superior to state court | Filing federal does NOT automatically stay state proceedings; you need a separate TRO/preliminary injunction, and Younger abstention may block even that | Younger v. Harris, 401 U.S. 37 (1971); Middlesex County Ethics Comm. v. Garden State Bar Ass'n |
| "I can challenge the custody order in federal court" | The custody order violated my constitutional rights | Rooker-Feldman bars federal review of state court judgments—you must challenge the PROCESS/CONDUCT, not the ORDER itself | Rooker-Feldman doctrine; Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Indus. Corp., 544 U.S. 280 (2005) |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Pleading and Procedure

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "I can just describe what happened and the court will figure out the legal theory" | Courts are supposed to liberally construe pro se filings | Even with liberal construction, Iqbal/Twombly requires plausible factual allegations supporting EACH element of EACH claim against EACH defendant | Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009); Bell Atlantic v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544 (2007) |
| "I can name the county and hold it liable for the judge's actions" | The judge works for the county/circuit | Monell prohibits respondeat superior—you must identify a specific policy, custom, failure to train, or ratification by a final policymaker | Monell v. Dept. of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978) |
| "The conspiracy is obvious from the pattern of conduct" | Multiple actors clearly coordinated against you | § 1985(3) conspiracy requires specific factual allegations of agreement—parallel conduct alone is insufficient, and you need class-based animus | Griffin v. Breckenridge, 403 U.S. 88 (1971); 6th Circuit requires particularity |
| "I have unlimited time to file because the violations are ongoing" | The rights violations continue to affect you | § 1983 borrows state statute of limitations (Michigan: 3 years under MCL 600.5805(2))—each discrete act has its own limitations period | Owens v. Okure, 488 U.S. 235 (1989); Wallace v. Kato, 549 U.S. 384 (2007) |
| "Filing IFP means I don't have to worry about costs" | IFP waives the filing fee | Court can still screen and dismiss under 28 USC § 1915(e)(2) for frivolousness, failure to state a claim, or immunity—IFP complaints get heightened screening | 28 USC § 1915(e)(2)(B); many pro se § 1983 cases dismissed at screening |
| "I can add defendants later if I forget someone" | Rule 15 allows amendments | Adding defendants after the statute of limitations has run requires relation-back under Rule 15(c)(1)(C), which is extremely difficult for new parties | Fed. R. Civ. P. 15(c); Krupski v. Costa Crociere, 560 U.S. 538 (2010) |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Abstention and Jurisdiction

| What you want to believe | Why it feels true | What actually happens | The rule |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|----------|
| "Federal court must hear my constitutional claims" | Federal courts have jurisdiction over § 1983 | Younger abstention may require federal court to decline jurisdiction if state proceedings are ongoing, adequate, and involve important state interests (custody = important state interest) | Younger v. Harris, 401 U.S. 37 (1971); Middlesex factors |
| "Rooker-Feldman only applies to direct appeals of state judgments" | You're not appealing, you're filing a new action | Rooker-Feldman bars claims "inextricably intertwined" with state court judgments—if federal relief would effectively reverse state judgment, it's barred regardless of how you label it | D.C. Court of Appeals v. Feldman, 460 U.S. 462 (1983) |
| "The domestic relations exception doesn't apply to § 1983" | Civil rights is federal, not family law | Federal courts decline jurisdiction over cases at the "core" of domestic relations even when framed as § 1983—custody and visitation determinations are the paradigm case | Ankenbrandt v. Richards, 504 U.S. 689 (1992); Elk Grove v. Newdow, 542 U.S. 1 (2004) |
| "I can get the federal court to change custody" | Federal court can remedy constitutional violations | Federal courts will NOT issue custody orders—the most you can get is declaratory judgment and prospective injunctive relief about the PROCESS | Domestic relations exception; federal courts lack equitable jurisdiction over custody |
| "Colorado River abstention won't apply because my claims are federal" | § 1983 is exclusively federal | Colorado River allows federal courts to abstain when parallel state proceedings involve the same parties and issues—exceptional circumstances test | Colorado River Water Conservation Dist. v. United States, 424 U.S. 800 (1976) |
| "I can file in both state and federal court simultaneously" | Concurrent jurisdiction exists | While technically possible, parallel litigation creates Colorado River abstention risk, inconsistent judgments, and potential sanctions for vexatious litigation | Must coordinate filing strategy carefully |

## Critical Timing Traps

- **Statute of limitations:** 3 years from each discrete violation (Michigan borrowing period)
- **Younger trigger:** Filing federal AFTER state proceedings begin may invoke Younger
- **Rooker-Feldman trigger:** State judgment must be FINAL before doctrine applies—file federal while state case is still pending
- **Heck v. Humphrey:** If success on § 1983 would imply invalidity of criminal conviction, claim is barred until conviction overturned (relevant if contempt findings exist)
- **Exhaustion:** No exhaustion requirement for § 1983 (Patsy v. Board of Regents), but administrative remedies may be relevant to Monell

## W.D. Michigan Specific Traps

- **Pro se screening:** All IFP complaints screened under § 1915—draft complaint anticipating screening dismissal
- **Magistrate consent:** Magistrate judge assigned automatically; you must consent or decline within 14 days
- **ECF registration:** Pro se filers may need to register for electronic filing or get exemption
- **Service requirements:** Must serve each defendant within 90 days (Fed. R. Civ. P. 4(m))
- **Local rules:** W.D. Mich. L. Civ. R. 7.1—motion practice has specific briefing schedule
