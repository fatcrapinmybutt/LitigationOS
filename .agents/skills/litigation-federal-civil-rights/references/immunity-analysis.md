# Immunity Analysis — Judicial, Qualified, and Prosecutorial

## Overview of Immunity Framework

Federal civil rights litigation (42 USC § 1983) faces three immunity barriers. Each defendant must be analyzed independently. A single case may involve defendants with absolute immunity (judge), qualified immunity (GAL, court officer), and no immunity (private parties under state action).

## I. Absolute Judicial Immunity

### Scope
Judges are absolutely immune from damages suits for acts performed in their judicial capacity, even if those acts are:
- Erroneous
- Malicious
- Corrupt
- In excess of jurisdiction

**Pierson v. Ray, 386 U.S. 547 (1967)** — Established judicial immunity under § 1983
**Stump v. Sparkman, 435 U.S. 349 (1978)** — Broadest reading: judge who ordered sterilization of minor without notice was immune

### Two Exceptions to Judicial Immunity

#### Exception 1: Non-Judicial Acts
**Mireles v. Waco, 502 U.S. 9 (1991)** — Test for judicial capacity:
1. Was the act a "function normally performed by a judge"?
2. Did the parties deal with the judge in his judicial capacity?

Acts that are NOT judicial (immunity pierced):
- **Administrative decisions:** Hiring, firing, scheduling manipulation unrelated to case adjudication
- **Ex parte factfinding:** Independent investigation outside the record
- **Enforcement actions:** Personally directing law enforcement without order
- **Legislative acts:** Rulemaking outside adjudicative role

**Application to Judge McNeill:**

| Act | Judicial? | Analysis |
|-----|-----------|----------|
| Custody ruling after hearing | Yes — immune | Core judicial function even if wrong |
| Ex parte communication with GAL outside record | NO — non-judicial | Factfinding outside adversarial process |
| Refusing to schedule hearing on pending motion | Arguable | Administrative scheduling vs. case management |
| Entering order without required statutory findings | Yes — immune | Erroneous judicial act still protected |
| Directing FOC to take specific action outside order | NO — non-judicial | Administrative/enforcement hybrid |
| Retaliating against pro se litigant for filing JTC complaint | NO — non-judicial | Retaliation is not a judicial function |

#### Exception 2: Clear Absence of All Jurisdiction
**Stump v. Sparkman** — Distinction between acting "in excess of jurisdiction" (immune) vs. "in clear absence of all jurisdiction" (not immune).

- **In excess:** Judge makes error about scope of authority → still immune
- **Clear absence:** Judge adjudicates matter entirely outside court's subject matter → not immune
- Example: Probate judge holding criminal trial → clear absence
- Application: 14th Circuit family division acting outside MCL Chapter 722 authority

### Pulliam v. Allen — Injunctive Relief Exception

**Pulliam v. Allen, 466 U.S. 522 (1984):**
- Judicial immunity does NOT bar injunctive or declaratory relief
- Can obtain order directing judge to follow constitutional procedures
- Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1996: Modified to prevent fee awards against judges unless declaratory relief was "unavailable" — but declaratory/injunctive relief itself remains available

**Practical application for Pigors:**
- Cannot get damages from McNeill for judicial acts
- CAN get: Declaration that procedures violated due process
- CAN get: Injunction requiring constitutional hearing procedures
- CAN get: Damages for clearly non-judicial acts

## II. Qualified Immunity

### Standard — Two-Step Analysis

**Saucier v. Katz, 533 U.S. 194 (2001):**
1. Did the official violate a constitutional right?
2. Was the right "clearly established" at the time?

**Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U.S. 223 (2009):**
- Courts may address either step first
- Can dismiss on "not clearly established" without reaching merits

### "Clearly Established" — 6th Circuit Standard

The right must be established with sufficient specificity:
- Not at the "high level of generality" (e.g., "due process is required")
- Must show existing precedent "placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate"
- **Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.S. 731 (2011)**

Sources of clearly established law (6th Circuit hierarchy):
1. U.S. Supreme Court decisions
2. 6th Circuit published decisions
3. Consensus of other circuits (if 6th Circuit silent)
4. State supreme court decisions interpreting federal rights (limited)

#### Court-Appointed Officers / FOC Staff
- FOC referees: Quasi-judicial immunity for recommendation functions
- FOC investigators: Qualified immunity only
- Process servers / clerks: Qualified immunity for ministerial acts

### Overcoming Qualified Immunity — Practical Steps

1. **Plead with particularity:** Specific facts showing what each defendant did
2. **Cite binding precedent:** 6th Circuit or Supreme Court cases with analogous facts
3. **Show obviousness:** Even without case on point, conduct was so egregious any reasonable official would know it was unconstitutional
4. **Allege policy violation:** Defendant violated their own training/policies (not dispositive but relevant)
5. **Discovery strategy:** Request early limited discovery on immunity question

## III. Prosecutorial Immunity

### Absolute (Functional)
- Acts "intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process"
- Imbler v. Pachtman, 424 U.S. 409 (1976)
- Initiating prosecution, presenting evidence at trial

### Qualified Only (Investigative/Administrative)
- Buckley v. Fitzsimmons, 509 U.S. 259 (1993)
- Investigative work before probable cause established
- Administrative duties, press conferences

**Limited applicability in Pigors** — but relevant if Muskegon County prosecutor involved in any enforcement actions related to PPO or contempt proceedings.
