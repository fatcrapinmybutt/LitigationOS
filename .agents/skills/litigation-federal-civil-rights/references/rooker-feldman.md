# Rooker-Feldman Doctrine — Complete Analysis

## Doctrinal Foundation

The Rooker-Feldman doctrine holds that lower federal courts lack subject matter jurisdiction to review state court judgments. It derives from two cases:

1. **District of Columbia Court of Appeals v. Feldman, 460 U.S. 462 (1983)**
2. **Rooker v. Fidelity Trust Co., 263 U.S. 413 (1923)**

Only the U.S. Supreme Court has jurisdiction to review state court judgments (28 USC § 1257).

## Four Requirements (Exxon Mobil Refinement)

**Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp., 544 U.S. 280 (2005)** — narrowed the doctrine to four requirements. ALL must be met for Rooker-Feldman to bar the claim:

1. **State-court loser:** Federal plaintiff lost in state court
2. **Complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgment:** The injury flows from the judgment itself
3. **State judgment rendered before federal proceedings commenced:** Temporal requirement
4. **Federal suit inviting review and rejection of state judgment:** Federal relief would effectively reverse state court

**Critical:** Exxon Mobil significantly narrowed Rooker-Feldman. Many claims previously thought barred are now permitted.

## "Inextricably Intertwined" Test

A federal claim is barred if it is "inextricably intertwined" with a state court judgment, meaning:
- Succeeding on the federal claim would require the federal court to determine the state court decided wrongly
- The federal claim is essentially a de facto appeal of the state judgment

### What IS Barred

| Federal Claim | Why Barred | Example |
|---------------|------------|---------|
| "The custody order is wrong" | Direct challenge to judgment | Asking federal court to reverse custody determination |
| "The court erred in applying MCL 722.23" | Asking federal court to review state law application | Challenging best-interest findings |
| "The PPO should not have been issued" | Direct challenge to state order | Seeking federal reversal of PPO |
| "The eviction was improper" | If challenging the judgment itself | Asking federal court to undo eviction order |

### What IS Permitted

| Federal Claim | Why Permitted | Example |
|---------------|--------------|---------|
| "The judge denied me a hearing before ruling" | Challenges process, not outcome | Due process claim independent of judgment |
| "The GAL conducted a biased investigation" | Conduct independent of judgment | The constitutional violation exists regardless of outcome |
| "The judge retaliated for my filing complaints" | Independent federal wrong | First Amendment claim not tied to judgment |
| "There is a county-wide pattern of denying pro se rights" | Independent § 1983/Monell claim | Systemic challenge, not appeal of one case |
| "Defendants conspired to deprive me of rights" | § 1985 conspiracy is independent | The conspiracy itself is the wrong |

## Avoidance Strategies for Pigors v Watson

### Strategy 1: Challenge Conduct, Not Outcomes

**WRONG framing (Rooker-Feldman barred):**
"Judge McNeill wrongly awarded custody to Amy Watson"

**CORRECT framing (permitted):**
"Judge McNeill deprived Pigors of procedural due process by conducting custody proceedings without adequate notice, opportunity to be heard, and impartial consideration of evidence, in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment"

The key distinction: You're not asking the federal court to decide custody differently. You're asking it to find that the PROCESS was unconstitutional.

### Strategy 2: Identify Independent Federal Injuries

Map each claim to an injury that exists INDEPENDENT of the state court judgment:

| State Court Event | Independent Federal Injury | Constitutional Basis |
|-------------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Custody determination | Denied hearing before deprivation of parental rights | 14th Amend. procedural due process |
| GAL recommendation adopted | Biased investigation deprived fair process | 14th Amend. due process |
| PPO issued/modified | Ex parte deprivation of liberty without adequate process | 14th Amend. procedural due process |
| Motion denied without hearing | Access to courts impeded | 1st Amend. petition clause |
| Pro se filings treated differently | Discrimination against pro se status | 14th Amend. equal protection |
| Contempt finding | Criminal punishment without criminal procedure protections | 5th/6th/14th Amend. |

### Strategy 3: File While State Proceedings Are Pending

Rooker-Feldman requires a FINAL state court judgment rendered BEFORE federal proceedings. If state case is still pending:
- No final judgment exists to "review"
- Federal filing concurrent with state proceedings is NOT barred by Rooker-Feldman
- **Exxon Mobil:** "Rooker-Feldman does not bar federal claims by parties in ongoing state proceedings"

**Timing for Pigors:**
- If any Lane A, B, or C case remains pending → file federal NOW
- Each pending case provides a window before Rooker-Feldman attaches
- Post-judgment motions in state court may keep case "pending" for Rooker-Feldman purposes

### Strategy 4: Seek Only Prospective Relief

Rooker-Feldman primarily bars claims seeking to undo past judgments. Prospective relief is generally permissible:
- Declaratory judgment that procedures are unconstitutional
- Injunction requiring constitutional procedures going forward
- These don't "review" past judgments—they regulate future conduct

### Strategy 5: Frame Conspiracy as Independent Wrong

§ 1985 conspiracy is an independent federal wrong. The agreement to deprive rights is itself the violation, separate from any state court judgment that resulted:
- "Defendants McNeill, Rusco, and Amy Watson conspired to deprive Pigors of equal protection"
- The conspiracy injury is the deprivation of fair process, not the specific custody outcome

## Related Abstention Doctrines

### Younger Abstention
**Younger v. Harris, 401 U.S. 37 (1971)**

Federal court must abstain from interfering with ONGOING state proceedings if:
1. There is an ongoing state judicial proceeding
2. The proceeding implicates important state interests
3. There is an adequate opportunity to raise federal claims in state court

**Custody = important state interest.** If Lane A proceedings are ongoing, Younger may block federal injunctive relief against the state court proceedings specifically.

**Younger exceptions:**
- Bad faith prosecution / extraordinary circumstances
- Patently unconstitutional statute
- No adequate opportunity to raise federal issues in state court

**Application to Pigors:** If McNeill's conduct has denied adequate opportunity to raise constitutional issues in state court, the "inadequate opportunity" exception may apply.

### Colorado River Abstention
**Colorado River Water Conservation District v. United States, 424 U.S. 800 (1976)**

Federal court may abstain in favor of parallel state proceedings based on "exceptional circumstances":
- Assumption of jurisdiction over property (in rem)
- Inconvenience of federal forum
- Desirability of avoiding piecemeal litigation
- Order in which jurisdiction was obtained
- Whether state or federal law controls
- Adequacy of state proceedings to protect federal rights

**Mitigation:** Frame federal claims as exclusively federal (§ 1983, § 1985) with no state analog—reduces Colorado River risk.

### Domestic Relations Exception
**Ankenbrandt v. Richards, 504 U.S. 689 (1992)**

Federal courts lack jurisdiction over cases involving:
- Issuance of divorce decrees
- Child custody determinations
- Alimony awards

**BUT:** Does NOT bar:
- Tort claims between family members
- Constitutional challenges to state domestic relations procedures
- § 1983 claims about how family court conducted proceedings

**Key for Pigors:** Frame claims as constitutional challenges to PROCESS, not requests for federal court to make custody/PPO determinations.

## Rooker-Feldman Checklist for Each Pigors Claim

Before asserting any claim in federal court, run this checklist:

```
□ 1. Is there a final state court judgment on this issue?
     YES → Continue to #2
     NO  → Rooker-Feldman does NOT apply. Proceed.

□ 2. Does the federal claim require finding the state court decided wrongly?
     YES → BARRED. Reframe the claim (Strategy 1).
     NO  → Continue to #3.

□ 3. Would federal relief effectively reverse the state judgment?
     YES → BARRED. Seek only prospective relief (Strategy 4).
     NO  → Continue to #4.

□ 4. Is the federal injury independent of the state judgment?
     YES → PERMITTED. Document the independent injury (Strategy 2).
     NO  → BARRED. Reframe or abandon claim.
```

## Sample Complaint Language (Rooker-Feldman Preemption)

Include in every federal complaint:

> "Plaintiff does not seek review, reversal, or modification of any state court judgment. Rather, Plaintiff challenges the unconstitutional conduct of Defendants that occurred during the course of state proceedings. The constitutional violations alleged herein exist independently of any state court judgment and would constitute actionable deprivations of federal rights regardless of the outcome of state proceedings. This Court's adjudication of Plaintiff's claims does not require review of any state court decision. See Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp., 544 U.S. 280 (2005)."

## Practical Tips for Pro Se Federal Filing

1. **Draft each claim with Rooker-Feldman in mind** — Ask "Am I challenging the judgment or the process?"
2. **Include the preemptive paragraph** above in your complaint
3. **Separate claims clearly** — One count per constitutional violation, per defendant
4. **Anticipate the defense** — Defendants WILL raise Rooker-Feldman; have your response ready
5. **Brief the distinction** — At complaint stage, explain why each claim is independent
6. **Monitor state proceedings** — Keep federal filing window open by filing before final judgment where possible

> "The equal protection violation alleged in this Count challenges the discriminatory TREATMENT Plaintiff received, not the correctness of any state court decision. Whether the state court reached the right result is irrelevant; the claim is that Plaintiff was subjected to [different procedures / heightened burdens / disparate treatment] compared to similarly situated [represented parties / mothers / non-pro-se litigants], in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of equal protection."

### MTD Response Templates — Rooker-Feldman

**Opening Framework:**

```
III. ROOKER-FELDMAN DOES NOT BAR ANY OF PLAINTIFF'S CLAIMS

    A. The Doctrine Is Narrow

    Defendants' reliance on Rooker-Feldman is misplaced. Since Exxon
Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries Corp., 544 U.S. 280 (2005), the
doctrine is "confined to" — and only to — "cases brought by state-court
losers complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments rendered
before the district court proceedings commenced and inviting district court
review and rejection of those judgments." Id. at 284. The Supreme Court
characterized it as a "narrow doctrine." Id. at 291.

    Defendants bear the burden of establishing that ALL FOUR requirements
are met for each challenged claim. Failure on any single requirement
defeats the defense. McCormick v. Braverman, 451 F.3d 382, 393 (6th Cir.
2006).
```

**Requirement-by-Requirement Rebuttal:**

```
    B. Plaintiff's Claims Fail One or More Exxon Mobil Requirements

    Requirement 1 (State-court loser): [If state proceedings are still
pending, there is no final judgment and Plaintiff is not a "loser" within
the meaning of the doctrine.]

    Requirement 2 (Injuries caused by judgment): Plaintiff's injuries
are caused by Defendants' unconstitutional CONDUCT, not by any state court
judgment. The injury of [specific deprivation] exists independently of any
ruling. [Cite specific non-judgment injury for each claim.]

    Requirement 3 (Judgment before federal filing): [If state proceedings
remain pending or post-judgment motions are outstanding, this requirement
is not met.]

    Requirement 4 (Inviting review/rejection): This Court need not review
or reject any state court judgment to adjudicate Plaintiff's claims.
Plaintiff asks this Court to:
    - Declare that certain PROCEDURES violated the Constitution
    - Enjoin future unconstitutional CONDUCT
    - Award damages for past unconstitutional CONDUCT
    None of these remedies requires reviewing any state court decision.
```

**Distinguishing Key Counter-Arguments:**

```
    C. Defendants' "Inextricably Intertwined" Argument Fails

    Defendants may argue Plaintiff's claims are "inextricably intertwined"
with state court judgments. Post-Exxon Mobil, this test has been
significantly narrowed. A claim is intertwined ONLY if succeeding on it
would require the federal court to determine the state court decided
wrongly. McCormick, 451 F.3d at 393.

    Here, Plaintiff can prevail on every claim without this Court ever
determining whether any state court order was correct or incorrect:
    - Due process claims: The Court need only find that Plaintiff was
      denied [notice/hearing/opportunity to be heard] — not that the
      outcome should have been different.
    - Retaliation claims: The Court need only find retaliatory motive —
      not that any underlying ruling was wrong.
    - Conspiracy claims: The Court need only find an agreement to deprive
      rights — not that any state judgment should be reversed.

    D. The Domestic Relations Exception Does Not Apply

    Defendants may invoke the domestic relations exception (Ankenbrandt v.
Richards, 504 U.S. 689 (1992)). The exception bars federal courts from
issuing divorce decrees, custody determinations, and alimony awards. It
does NOT bar constitutional challenges to HOW family courts conduct
proceedings. Plaintiff does not ask this Court to make any custody
determination — only to enforce constitutional procedural requirements.
```
