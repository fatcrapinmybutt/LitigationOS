# 42 USC § 1983 — Complete Elements and Analysis

## Statutory Text (Core)

"Every person who, under color of any statute, ordinance, regulation, custom, or usage, of any State ... subjects, or causes to be subjected, any citizen ... to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws, shall be liable to the party injured in an action at law, suit in equity, or other proper proceeding for redress."

## Essential Elements

1. **Defendant is a "person"**
   - Individuals: yes (officials, employees, private parties under state action)
   - Municipalities: yes (Monell)
   - States/state agencies: NO (11th Amendment sovereign immunity)
   - 14th Judicial Circuit as entity: analyze whether arm of state or local entity

2. **Acting "under color of state law"**
   - State employees acting in official capacity: presumed
   - State employees acting outside authority: still "color of law" if using state power
   - Private parties: only if joint action, nexus, or public function (see below)
   - Test: "misuse of power, possessed by virtue of state law and made possible only because the wrongdoer is clothed with the authority of state law" — United States v. Classic (1941)

3. **Deprivation of federal rights**
   - Constitutional rights (most common)
   - Federal statutory rights (limited—must create enforceable right)
   - Must identify specific constitutional provision violated

4. **Causation**
   - Defendant's conduct must be proximate cause of deprivation
   - For supervisors: personal involvement required (no respondeat superior)
   - For municipalities: policy/custom must be "moving force" behind violation

## Individual Capacity vs. Official Capacity

| Feature | Individual Capacity | Official Capacity |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Who pays | Individual defendant | Government entity |
| Immunity defense | Qualified immunity | Sovereign immunity (11th Amend. for state; no immunity for municipality) |
| Damages available | Compensatory + punitive | Compensatory only (no punitive against govt) |
| Must show | Personal involvement | Policy/custom (Monell) |
| Practical effect | Defendant personally liable | Suit against the entity itself |

**Strategy for Pigors:** Sue individual defendants in BOTH capacities. Individual capacity for damages + punitive. Official capacity for injunctive/declaratory relief.

## Color of State Law — Private Party Liability

### Dennis v. Sparks (1980) — Corruption of Judicial Process
- Private parties who conspire with judge to corrupt judicial proceedings act under color of state law
- Application: If Amy Watson or Rusco conspired to influence McNeill's rulings through improper channels

### Joint Action Test
- "Willful participant in joint activity with the State or its agents"
- Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co. (1982)
- Evidence needed: communications, coordinated timing, shared objectives

### Symbiotic Relationship / Nexus Test
- State "so far insinuated itself into a position of interdependence"
- Burton v. Wilmington Parking Auth. (1961)
- Application: GAL-attorney-judge relationship in custody proceedings

## Monell Municipal Liability — Deep Dive

### Policy
- Formally adopted rule, regulation, or decision
- Must be adopted by entity's legislative body or authorized decisionmaker
- Example: Written court administration policy on pro se hearings

### Custom
- Persistent, widespread practice not formally authorized
- "So common and well-settled as to constitute a custom that fairly represents municipal policy"
- Doe v. Claiborne County (6th Cir. 2000)
- **Pigors application:** Pattern of denying fathers meaningful custody hearings, pattern of rubber-stamping GAL recommendations without scrutiny

### Failure to Train
- City of Canton v. Harris (1989)
- Must show: (1) training program inadequate; (2) inadequacy results from deliberate indifference; (3) inadequacy actually caused the violation
- "Deliberate indifference" = city aware of need and chose not to act
- Single-incident theory: If violation is "highly predictable" consequence of failure to train

### Ratification
- Final policymaker with authority over decision approves the specific unconstitutional action
- Pembaur v. City of Cincinnati (1986)
- Must identify WHO is the final policymaker (state law determines this)

### Policymaker Identification in Michigan Courts
- Chief Judge: administrative authority over court operations
- State Court Administrative Office (SCAO): statewide policies
- County Board of Commissioners: funding and facilities
- Family Division presiding judge: division-specific procedures

## Damages Under § 1983

### Compensatory Damages
- Out-of-pocket losses (filing fees, travel, lost wages)
- Emotional distress (must prove more than de minimis injury)
- Loss of companionship with children (recognized in 6th Cir.)
- Carey v. Piphus (1978): Procedural due process violation—entitled to nominal damages even without proof of actual injury

### Punitive Damages
- Available against individual defendants
- NOT available against municipalities (City of Newport v. Fact Concerts, 1981)
- Standard: Defendant acted with "evil motive or intent" or "reckless or callous indifference"
- Smith v. Wade (1983)

### Nominal Damages
- $1 for established violation without proven compensatory damages
- Still valuable: establishes the violation and supports fee petition

### Attorney's Fees (42 USC § 1988)
- Prevailing plaintiff: fees are the norm
- **Pro se exception:** Kay v. Ehrler (1991)—pro se litigants who are NOT attorneys cannot recover § 1988 fees
- BUT: Pro se can recover costs under 28 USC § 1920

## § 1985(3) Conspiracy — Elements

1. **Conspiracy:** Agreement between two or more persons
   - Cannot conspire with yourself—intracorporate conspiracy doctrine may apply
   - BUT: Exception when officials act outside scope of employment
   
2. **Purpose:** Deprive person of equal protection or privileges/immunities
   - Must allege class-based animus (race, gender, etc.)
   - 6th Circuit: "pro se litigant" status alone may not suffice as a class
   - Consider: gender (father in custody), economic class (tenant)

3. **Overt act:** At least one act in furtherance of conspiracy

4. **Injury:** Deprivation of right or privilege of citizen

## § 1986 — Neglect to Prevent

- Knowledge of § 1985 conspiracy
- Power to prevent it
- Neglects or refuses to prevent
- 1-year statute of limitations (shorter than § 1983)
- Application: County administrators who knew of judicial misconduct patterns

## 6th Circuit Specific Standards

### Pleading
- Iqbal plausibility standard strictly enforced
- Conspiracy claims require "specific factual allegations"
- Immunity must be addressed in complaint (don't wait for motion)

### Discovery
- Qualified immunity is "immunity from suit, not just liability"
- Discovery may be limited until immunity resolved
- Ashcroft v. Iqbal allows narrow discovery on immunity question

### Summary Judgment
- Court must view facts in light most favorable to non-moving party
- But once qualified immunity raised, burden shifts to plaintiff
- Must show "clearly established" with sufficient specificity
