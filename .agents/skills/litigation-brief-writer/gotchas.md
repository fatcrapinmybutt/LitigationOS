# Gotchas — litigation-brief-writer

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Argument Structure

Common ways agents (and pro se litigants) rationalize sloppy legal writing. Each row is a failure mode observed in real Michigan filings.

| # | Rationalization (The Lie You Tell Yourself) | Why It Fails | Real Consequence | Correct Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "The judge will understand what I mean even if I don't say it precisely." | Judges read dozens of filings per week. They will not fill in gaps in your argument. Michigan courts have explicitly stated they will not "search for arguments not briefed" — Derderian v Genesys Health Care Sys, 263 Mich App 68 (2004). | Your best argument gets overlooked because the judge couldn't find it in your rambling prose. Opposing counsel capitalizes on the gap. You lose a winnable motion. | State every argument with a clear thesis sentence in the heading, supported by rule + application + conclusion. If the judge skims only your headings, they should understand your entire argument. |
| 2 | "I should include every possible argument — more arguments = better chance of winning." | The kitchen-sink approach dilutes your strong arguments with weak ones. Courts evaluate your judgment by what you include. Three strong arguments beat ten mixed ones. Weak arguments actually undermine your credibility. | Judge perceives the filing as unserious, scattershot, or desperate. Strong arguments get lost in the noise. Court may sanction under MCR 2.114(D) for frivolous arguments. | Select your 2–4 strongest arguments. Cut everything else ruthlessly. If an argument wouldn't survive a motion to dismiss on its own, it probably shouldn't be in your brief. Save marginal arguments for reply briefs if needed. |
| 3 | "I don't need to address the opponent's arguments — mine are stronger." | Failing to address counterarguments tells the court you either don't understand them or can't refute them. Michigan courts routinely note when a party "failed to address" an argument, treating it as conceded. | Court treats unaddressed arguments as conceded. You lose on points you could have won simply because you didn't engage with the opponent's position. | Use the concession-and-refute technique. Acknowledge the strongest counterargument, then explain precisely why it fails under the law and facts. This builds credibility and eliminates the court's doubt. |
| 4 | "Block quotes from cases are more persuasive than my own words." | Block quotes signal lazy briefing. Judges skip long block quotes. The quote may contain language that helps the other side. Your job is to synthesize authority, not copy-paste it. | Court skims past your block quotes, misses the relevant language, or worse — opposing counsel points out that the same quote contains language that actually supports their position. | Paraphrase holdings in your own words with pinpoint citations. Use short in-line quotes (under 50 words) for key phrases. If you must block-quote, bold the critical sentence and explain its significance immediately after. |
| 5 | "Formatting doesn't matter — substance wins." | Formatting IS substance for pro se litigants. A professionally formatted brief signals competence, earns judicial respect, and makes arguments accessible. A poorly formatted brief signals chaos and invites dismissal. | Judge struggles to read your brief, misses arguments, assumes incompetence. Clerk may reject filing for non-compliance with local rules. Opposing counsel uses your formatting against you. | Follow Michigan format rules exactly: TNR 12pt, double-spaced, 1" margins, proper caption (MCR 2.113), numbered pages, proper heading hierarchy. See references/style-guide.md. |
| 6 | "I can write emotionally because what happened to me was outrageous." | Emotional writing destroys credibility in court. Judges are trained to distrust emotional appeals and look for rational, fact-based arguments. Anger in a brief makes you look unhinged, not sympathetic. | Judge discounts your filing as a "rant," not a legal argument. May trigger MCR 2.114(D) sanctions for improper purpose. Opposing counsel quotes your emotional language to paint you as unreasonable. | Channel emotion into facts. Instead of "Defendant cruelly and maliciously destroyed my relationship with my children," write: "Between March and August 2025, Defendant denied Plaintiff's court-ordered parenting time on 14 documented occasions. (Ex. D, Parenting Time Log.)" Let the facts carry the emotional weight. |
| 7 | "I'll add the citations later — let me get the argument down first." | Arguments without citations are assertions. Courts do not credit unsupported legal assertions. If you draft without citations, you often discover the law doesn't actually support your argument — after you've committed to it. | Filing goes out with missing or incorrect citations. Court treats unsupported legal claims as waived. Opposing counsel moves to strike for failure to cite authority. | Research first, write second. For every legal proposition, find the authority BEFORE writing the argument paragraph. If you can't find authority, the argument may not be viable. Pivot to an argument you can support. |
| 8 | "The Statement of Facts is just background — the Argument section is what matters." | The Statement of Facts is where you win or lose. Judges form impressions during the facts; the argument section merely confirms or challenges those impressions. A weak fact section undermines even the best legal argument. | Judge enters the Argument section already leaning toward the opponent because their fact section was more compelling. Your legal analysis can't overcome the factual framing disadvantage. | Draft the Statement of Facts with the same care as the Argument. Use the TEC structure (Theme-Evidence-Conclusion). Every sentence should be factual, cited to the record, and arranged to build toward your legal theory. The facts should make your legal conclusion feel inevitable. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Michigan-Specific Style

| # | Rationalization | Why It Fails | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Citation format doesn't matter as long as the court can find the case." | Michigan uses a specific citation format. Incorrect citations signal unfamiliarity with practice. Judges and clerks expect standard Michigan citation form. | Court clerk cannot verify your authorities; judge assumes you're citing cases you haven't read; opposing counsel objects to improper citations. | Use Michigan citation format: Case Name, Volume Mich/Mich App Page (Year). Example: Vodvarka v Grasmeyer, 259 Mich App 499, 508 (2003). Include pinpoint page numbers always. |
| 2 | "I don't need a Table of Contents for a 15-page brief." | MCR 7.212(B) requires a TOC for appellate briefs, and local practice expects them for any filing over 10 pages. A TOC makes your brief navigable and signals professionalism. | Judge cannot quickly find your arguments; brief appears disorganized; may be rejected by COA clerk for non-compliance. | Include a TOC for any filing over 10 pages. Format with heading text and page numbers. Update page numbers after final formatting. The TOC is often the first (and sometimes only) thing a judge reads. |
| 3 | "The caption is just a formality — I'll copy it from an old filing." | Captions must be current and accurate under MCR 2.113. Case numbers, party names, and court designations change as cases progress. An incorrect caption can result in filing in the wrong case. | Filing is rejected, entered in wrong case, or not properly linked in court system. Opposing party argues improper notice. | Create fresh captions for every filing. Verify case number, party names (check for any name changes or added parties), court name, and judge assignment. Include "Pro Se" designation under your name. |
| 4 | "Michigan courts don't care about page limits — they want thorough briefing." | MCR 7.212(B) strictly enforces page limits (50 pages/16,000 words for COA briefs). Circuit courts have local rules or judge-specific limits. Exceeding limits without leave is grounds for rejection. | Brief rejected by clerk; must re-file shortened version under time pressure; court grants leave to file overlength brief but signals annoyance. | Draft within page limits from the start. If you cannot fit your argument, you need to cut, not request more pages. Tight writing is more persuasive than exhaustive writing. |
| 5 | "I can use footnotes for important arguments — they save space." | Michigan courts have expressed skepticism toward substantive footnotes. Judge Sutton (6th Circuit) famously noted that if an argument is important enough to make, it belongs in the text. | Important arguments buried in footnotes get ignored. Courts may treat footnoted arguments as waived or insufficiently briefed. | Put all substantive arguments in the main text. Use footnotes only for: (1) citation strings too long for text, (2) tangential clarifications, (3) procedural background. Never put a holding or key fact in a footnote. |
| 6 | "I'll use legal jargon to sound more like a lawyer." | Pro se litigants who misuse legal jargon undermine their credibility worse than those who write in plain English. Judges respect clarity; they do not respect pretension. Misused terms actively confuse the court. | Judge misunderstands your argument because you used a term of art incorrectly (e.g., "estoppel" when you mean "waiver," or "jurisdiction" when you mean "venue"). Opposing counsel exploits the error. | Write in clear, plain English. Use legal terms only when you understand their precise meaning and they're necessary. Define terms if there's any ambiguity. Your authentic pro se voice is an asset, not a liability. |
