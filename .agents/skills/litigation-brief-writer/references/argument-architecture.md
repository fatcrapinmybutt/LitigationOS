# Argument Architecture — IRAC / CREAC / TEC Frameworks

Complete structural reference for legal argument construction in Michigan courts. Each framework includes templates, Michigan court preferences, section ordering, and heading hierarchy standards.

## I. IRAC Framework — Detailed

### Structure

```
ISSUE:       One sentence framing the precise legal question.
RULE:        The governing legal standard with full citation chain.
APPLICATION: Fact-to-law mapping with record citations.
CONCLUSION:  The result the court should reach + specific relief.
```

### IRAC Template

```
[HEADING: Persuasive thesis sentence in bold]

The issue before this Court is whether [precise legal question]. [Optional:
This issue is [dispositive / critical / threshold] because [why it matters].]

Under Michigan law, [rule statement with primary authority]. [Case name],
[volume] Mich [App] [page], [pinpoint] ([year]). [Elaboration of rule or
elements if multi-factor test.] See also [secondary authority].

Here, [application of rule to facts of Pigors v Watson]. [Plaintiff/
Defendant] [specific conduct with date] constitutes [legal conclusion]
because [reasoning connecting facts to rule]. ([Record citation: Ex. [X],
[description]]; see also [additional record cite].) [Address each element
of the rule with specific facts.]

[If needed: counter-argument and refutation paragraph.]
Defendant may argue [opposing position]. This argument fails because
[reason with authority]. [Distinguishing case or factual distinction.]

Therefore, [conclusion]. This Court should [specific relief requested].
```

### When to Use IRAC

- Single-issue procedural motions (motions to compel, motions to extend time)
- Individual elements within a larger CREAC argument
- Response briefs addressing each of the opponent's arguments point-by-point
- Any argument that can be stated in 1–3 pages

## II. CREAC Framework — Detailed

### Structure

```
CONCLUSION:    Your position stated upfront — the thesis.
RULE:          The complete legal standard with authority chain.
EXPLANATION:   How courts have applied this rule in analogous cases.
APPLICATION:   Specific application to Pigors v Watson facts.
CONCLUSION:    Restatement + specific relief requested.
```

### CREAC Template

```
[HEADING: Major argument — persuasive thesis sentence]

[Opening Conclusion]
[Defendant's] [specific conduct] [violates / satisfies / requires]
[legal standard] because [reason]. This Court should [relief requested].

[Rule Statement]
Under [statute/rule/common law], [complete rule statement]. [Primary
authority with pinpoint cite]. [If multi-factor test, list all factors.]
The [Michigan Supreme Court / Court of Appeals] has held that
[refinement of rule]. [Authority].

[Explanation — Analogous Case Application]
In [Case Name], [volume] Mich [App] [page] ([year]), the court
[held/found] that [brief facts of analogous case]. There, the
[party] had [specific conduct similar to current case], and the
court [concluded] that [holding]. [Pinpoint cite].

[If distinguishing case:] By contrast, in [Case Name], [cite], the
court reached a different result because [distinguishing facts].
[Explain why that case is different from Pigors v Watson.]

[Application to Pigors v Watson]
Here, the facts are [more compelling than / analogous to / even
stronger than] those in [analogous case]. Specifically, [Defendant]
[specific conduct with date and record cite], which [satisfies /
violates] the [element/factor] because [reasoning]. (Ex. [X],
[description]; [additional record cites].)

[Address each element/factor with specific facts and record cites.]

[If opponent's arguments need addressing:]
[Defendant's] reliance on [opposing authority/argument] is misplaced
because [reason]. [Distinguish or refute with specific factual or
legal distinction.]

[Closing Conclusion]
Because [brief summary of application], this Court should [specific
relief]. [Optional: urgency or equity statement.]
```

### When to Use CREAC

- Dispositive motions (summary judgment, dismissal, default judgment)
- Complex motions involving multi-factor tests
- Appellate briefs (argument section)
- Any argument requiring analogical reasoning from case law
- Arguments exceeding 3 pages

## III. TEC Framework — Detailed

### Structure

```
THEME:      Establish the narrative frame — what this case is really about.
EVIDENCE:   Marshal facts in thematic order with record citations.
CONCLUSION: Draw the factual inference supporting your legal theory.
```

### TEC Template (Statement of Facts)

```
[HEADING: STATEMENT OF FACTS]

[Theme Paragraph — Sets the Narrative Frame]
This case arises from [thematic description — not just "a custody
dispute" but "a systematic pattern of parental alienation and custody
interference that has deprived a father of his relationship with his
children"]. [1-2 sentences establishing the moral frame.]

[Background Facts — Essential Context]
[Who, what, where, when — in narrative order]
Andrew Pigors and [Watson] [relationship context]. (Ex. [X].)
[Chronological development of relevant facts leading to the dispute.]

[Core Facts — The Heart of the Dispute]
[Present facts in thematic clusters, not strict chronology]
[Each fact: What happened + when + record citation]
[Build from background to crisis to current situation]

[Impact Facts — Why This Matters]
[Consequences of defendant's conduct on plaintiff and children]
[Documented harms with record citations]

[Conclusion — Factual Inference]
[The facts] demonstrate that [factual conclusion supporting legal
theory]. [Bridge sentence connecting to the Argument section.]
```

### When to Use TEC

- Statement of Facts in any brief or motion
- Narrative sections of complaints
- Equity arguments (appeals to fairness)
- Trial briefs (narrative framing)
- Any section where storytelling is primary

## IV. Section Ordering — Michigan Practice

### Standard Motion Structure

```
1. CAPTION (MCR 2.113)
2. INTRODUCTION / SUMMARY (1 page max — thesis + relief requested)
3. STATEMENT OF FACTS (TEC structure)
4. LEGAL STANDARD (1-2 paragraphs — the threshold rule)
5. ARGUMENT
   I.   [Strongest argument — CREAC]
   II.  [Second argument — CREAC or IRAC]
   III. [Third argument — IRAC]
   [Order: strongest first, second-strongest last, weakest middle]
6. CONCLUSION / RELIEF REQUESTED (specific, itemized)
7. SIGNATURE BLOCK
8. CERTIFICATE OF SERVICE
9. EXHIBITS (tabbed, indexed)
```

### Appellate Brief Structure (MCR 7.212)

```
1. CAPTION / COVER PAGE
2. TABLE OF CONTENTS
3. TABLE OF AUTHORITIES
4. JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT
5. STATEMENT OF QUESTIONS PRESENTED
   [Frame questions to suggest your answer]
6. STATEMENT OF FACTS (TEC)
7. STANDARD OF REVIEW
   [Critical section — sets the lens through which court evaluates]
8. ARGUMENT (CREAC for each major point)
9. CONCLUSION / RELIEF REQUESTED
10. SIGNATURE BLOCK
11. CERTIFICATE OF SERVICE
12. APPENDIX (MCR 7.212(C) required contents)
```

### Michigan Court Preferences

| Court | Preference | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 14th Circuit Court | Clear, concise motions; local judges prefer brevity | Check for judge-specific practice requirements |
| Michigan COA | Strict compliance with MCR 7.212; issue-focused arguments | Standard of review is dispositive — get it right |
| Michigan Supreme Court | Questions of statewide significance; policy arguments | Different tone — more academic, more policy |
| W.D. Michigan (Federal) | LCivR 7.1 — brief + concise statement of issues + response format | Federal judges expect extremely tight writing |

## V. Heading Hierarchy Standards

### Level 1: Major Argument Sections
```
I.  THE TRIAL COURT ABUSED ITS DISCRETION BY MODIFYING CUSTODY
    WITHOUT FINDING PROPER CAUSE OR A CHANGE OF CIRCUMSTANCES
```
- ALL CAPS, BOLD, CENTERED (or left-aligned per local practice)
- Complete persuasive sentence — not just a topic
- Numbered with Roman numerals

### Level 2: Sub-Arguments
```
    A.  Defendant Failed to Demonstrate a Change of Circumstances
        Under MCL 722.27(1)(c) and Vodvarka
```
- Title Case, Bold, Left-Aligned
- Indented under Level 1
- Lettered with capitals

### Level 3: Supporting Points
```
        1.  The alleged change occurred before the last custody order
            and therefore cannot constitute a "new" circumstance.
```
- Sentence case, bold or italic, left-aligned
- Further indented
- Numbered with Arabic numerals

### Level 4: Sub-Points (Rare)
```
            a.  The April 2024 incident was fully litigated in the
                prior proceedings.
```
- Sentence case, italic, left-aligned
- Lettered with lowercase
- Use only when absolutely necessary — prefer restructuring to avoid Level 4

## VI. Thesis Sentence Construction

Every section heading should be a thesis sentence — a complete argument in miniature.

**Bad (Topic Labels):**
- "Standard of Review" ✗
- "The Security Deposit" ✗
- "Emotional Distress" ✗

**Good (Thesis Sentences):**
- "This Court Reviews Custody Modifications for Abuse of Discretion, Requiring Proper Cause or Changed Circumstances" ✓
- "Defendant Forfeited the Right to Retain Any Security Deposit by Failing to Provide an Itemized Statement Within 30 Days Under MCL 554.609" ✓
- "Defendant's Extreme and Outrageous Conduct Caused Severe Emotional Distress Requiring Ongoing Therapeutic Treatment" ✓

A judge who reads only your headings should understand your entire argument.
