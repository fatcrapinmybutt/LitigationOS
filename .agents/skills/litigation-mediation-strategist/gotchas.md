# Gotchas — Mediation Strategist

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Treating mediation as adversarial | Approaching mediation like trial — aggressive posturing, withholding information, making demands | Mediation requires collaborative problem-solving; adversarial approach causes impasse and wastes the session | Prepare interests-based positions; identify BATNA and ZOPA; focus on creative solutions both parties can accept |
| Conceding too early to "be cooperative" | Making significant concessions in the first session to show good faith | Early concessions anchor expectations low and signal desperation; mediator and other side will expect continued concessions | Start with justified positions supported by evidence; make strategic concessions tied to reciprocal movement |
| Ignoring the mediator's role | Treating the mediator as a judge who will decide the case | Mediators facilitate agreement; they do not decide. Expecting a ruling wastes the opportunity for self-determination | Use the mediator as a communication channel; ask mediator to reality-test both sides' positions |
| Failing to prepare a mediation summary | Showing up without a written summary of positions, evidence, and proposals | MCR 3.216(H)(2) requires good faith participation; arriving unprepared may constitute bad faith and invite sanctions | Prepare a concise mediation summary with key facts, legal positions, evidence highlights, and settlement proposals |
| Agreeing to terms without understanding enforcement | Signing a mediation agreement without understanding it becomes a binding court order | MCR 3.216(H)(7) — mediation agreements are enforceable as contracts; once entered as a court order, modification requires showing changed circumstances | Review every term carefully before signing; ensure you understand enforcement mechanisms and modification standards |

## Failure Modes
1. **Impasse Without Documented Position**: Mediation fails but you have no written record of your proposals and concessions. Recovery: Prepare a post-mediation position statement for the court documenting your good faith efforts and proposals; this helps at trial when the court considers each party's reasonableness.
2. **Binding Agreement Regret**: Signing agreement under pressure, later realizing terms are unfavorable. Recovery: Very limited options — may seek to set aside under MCR 2.612(C) for fraud, duress, or mutual mistake, but courts strongly enforce mediation agreements. Prevention: never sign at the table; request 24-48 hours to review.
3. **Confidentiality Breach**: Disclosing mediation communications in subsequent court filings. Recovery: Opposing party may move to strike; MCR 2.412 protects mediation confidentiality. Ensure all filings avoid referencing what was said in mediation sessions.
4. **Bad Faith Finding**: Court determines you did not participate in good faith. Recovery: File responsive brief demonstrating good faith efforts with documentation; bad faith findings may result in fee-shifting under MCR 3.216(I).

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-settlement-analyzer**, ensure settlement analysis is completed BEFORE mediation — you need to know your walk-away number and what terms you can and cannot accept before entering the room.
- Data flow from **litigation-mandatory-disclosure-specialist** is prerequisite — incomplete financial disclosure before mediation means you cannot make informed settlement decisions and any agreement may be voidable for fraud.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: In Muskegon County 14th Circuit, the court frequently orders mediation in custody cases under MCR 3.216. Failure to attend or participate in good faith can result in sanctions, including adverse inferences on best interest factors. A pro se litigant MUST attend even if settlement seems unlikely.
