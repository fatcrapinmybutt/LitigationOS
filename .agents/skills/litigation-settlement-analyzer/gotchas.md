# Gotchas — Settlement Analyzer

## Anti-Rationalization Matrix

| Trap | What It Looks Like | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|
| Settling without complete financial discovery | Agreeing to terms before receiving opposing party's full financial disclosure | You cannot make informed settlement decisions without knowing the full financial picture; agreements based on incomplete information may be voidable but are hard to undo | Insist on complete MCR 3.206(B) disclosures before any settlement discussion; verify independently |
| Ignoring tax consequences | Focusing on gross asset values without considering tax implications of division | Asset transfers, retirement account division (QDRO), and property transfers have different tax treatments that affect real value | Analyze after-tax value of every asset; $100K in a 401(k) is not equivalent to $100K in home equity |
| Undervaluing future obligations | Discounting future child support or spousal support obligations in present value analysis | Long-term support obligations represent significant financial exposure; failing to calculate present value leads to lopsided settlements | Calculate present value of all future payment streams using appropriate discount rate |
| Accepting lump-sum child support | Agreeing to a single payment in lieu of ongoing child support | Michigan disfavors lump-sum child support; FOC may not approve; it cannot be modified if circumstances change | Maintain monthly support per the Michigan Child Support Formula with periodic review provisions |
| Not considering non-monetary terms | Focusing exclusively on dollars while ignoring parenting time, decision-making authority, and relocation restrictions | Custody and parenting time terms have enormous practical and financial value; a "good financial deal" with bad custody terms is not a good deal | Evaluate all terms holistically — financial, custodial, and practical — using a weighted analysis |

## Failure Modes
1. **Settlement Below Formula Minimums**: Agreeing to child support below the Michigan Child Support Formula. Recovery: FOC may reject the agreement; if entered, either party can move to modify to formula amounts. Prevention: always run the formula calculation before agreeing to support terms.
2. **Unenforceable Provisions**: Settlement includes terms the court cannot enforce (e.g., requiring a party to maintain certain employment). Recovery: Identify unenforceable terms before signing; if already signed, argue the provision is severable and does not invalidate the entire agreement.
3. **Waiving Unknown Claims**: Settlement releases claims the party did not know existed at the time. Recovery: Narrow the release language to known, identified claims; never agree to a general release in family law — hidden assets may be discovered later.
4. **Failure to Account for Marital Debt**: Agreeing to asset division without addressing joint debt allocation. Recovery: File supplemental motion to address undivided debts; joint debts remain joint regardless of the settlement agreement as to creditors.

## Integration Gotchas
- When combining with **litigation-mandatory-disclosure-specialist**, settlement analysis MUST wait until all disclosures are verified — running analysis on incomplete data produces unreliable results.
- Data flow from **litigation-mediation-strategist** requires settlement parameters to be calculated BEFORE entering mediation — your BATNA and walk-away numbers must be pre-computed.
- Michigan-specific pitfall: Michigan Child Support Formula deviations require specific written findings by the court that the formula amount would be "unjust or inappropriate" under MCL 552.605(2). Settlement agreements that deviate from the formula without these findings may be rejected by FOC or modified on motion.
