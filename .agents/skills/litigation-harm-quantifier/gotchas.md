# Gotchas — litigation-harm-quantifier

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Damages Calculation

Common ways agents (and litigants) rationalize skipping critical damages requirements. Every row represents a failure mode that has destroyed real cases.

| # | Rationalization (The Lie You Tell Yourself) | Why It Fails | Real Consequence | Correct Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "The damages are obvious — the judge will figure out the number." | Michigan law requires the plaintiff to prove damages with reasonable certainty. Brinker v Hobbs, 388 Mich 655 (1972). Judges do not calculate damages for you; they rule on what you present. | Court awards $0 or nominal damages only. You proved liability but get nothing because you failed to quantify harm. | Calculate every damages category with a specific dollar amount, method, and supporting evidence. Present a damages summary table in every filing that requests money. |
| 2 | "I'll just use a big round number and let the jury sort it out." | Round numbers without supporting methodology signal guesswork, not evidence. Michigan juries are instructed they must base damages on evidence, not speculation. M Civ JI 50.01. | Judge may strike damages claim as speculative; jury may award nothing; appellate court reverses as unsupported. | Use a named calculation method (per diem, multiplier, comparable verdict) with inputs shown. Show the math. Even non-economic damages need a framework. |
| 3 | "Emotional distress is real so I don't need documentation." | Michigan requires evidence of emotional distress severity — not just the plaintiff's say-so. Physical manifestation or corroborating evidence strengthens the claim enormously. | Defendant moves to dismiss emotional distress claim for insufficient evidence; court grants because plaintiff offered only conclusory allegations. | Document every symptom: insomnia dates, medical visits, therapy records, medications, weight changes, witness observations of behavioral changes. Build the paper trail. |
| 4 | "I can claim the same damage under multiple legal theories — more theories = more money." | Anti-double-counting doctrine. You cannot recover twice for the same actual harm even if multiple legal theories support liability. A dollar of rent overpayment is one dollar regardless of how many claims cover it. | Court reduces award or defendant successfully argues for setoff/reduction. May undermine credibility if it appears you're inflating damages. | Create a damages deduplication matrix. Map each dollar amount to exactly one claim category. Show the court you're not double-counting. Separate economic and non-economic items clearly. |
| 5 | "Punitive damages will make up for whatever I can't prove in compensatory damages." | Punitive damages require a compensatory baseline. State Farm v Campbell, 538 US 408 (2003), limits the ratio to roughly 9:1 (single-digit multiplier). Michigan requires malicious/willful/wanton conduct under MCL 600.6304. | Court strikes punitive claim for failure to establish compensatory baseline; or awards punitive damages but appellate court reduces under constitutional limits. | Prove compensatory damages first. Punitive damages supplement, they do not substitute. Document the defendant's state of mind (malice, willfulness, wantonness) with specific evidence. |
| 6 | "Future damages are speculative so I'll just skip them." | Michigan allows future damages when proven with reasonable certainty. Skipping them means you're leaving real money on the table — ongoing harm, future therapy, future lost parenting time. | You recover only past damages. Ongoing harm continues but you've waived the right to claim future compensation because you didn't ask for it. | Project future damages with a reasonable methodology. Use present value calculations. State assumptions explicitly. Courts accept reasonable projections even if not perfectly certain. |
| 7 | "The statutory treble damages under MCPA automatically apply, so I just triple my number." | MCPA treble damages (MCL 445.911(2)) require you to first prove actual damages AND prove a violation of the MCPA. The trebling is at the court's discretion for knowing violations. The $250 minimum is automatic, trebling is not. | Court applies $250 minimum instead of treble damages because plaintiff assumed trebling was automatic and didn't present the evidentiary foundation for knowing violation. | Prove actual damages first, then argue the violation was knowing/willful, then request treble damages as a separate calculation. Present the base amount and the trebled amount separately. |
| 8 | "I don't need expert testimony for damages — I lived through it, I know what it cost me." | Expert testimony is required in Michigan for: future damages projections, complex causation, medical/psychological harm quantification, and lost earning capacity calculations. Lay testimony on these topics may be excluded. | Damages evidence excluded at trial; court grants motion in limine preventing plaintiff from testifying about future harm value or causation of psychological injuries. | Identify which damages categories require expert support. For categories where you cannot afford experts, use documentary evidence (medical records, bills) and limit claims to documented past losses. Acknowledge limitations transparently. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Evidence for Damages

| # | Rationalization | Why It Fails | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "My testimony alone is enough to prove damages." | For many damages categories, plaintiff testimony is necessary but not sufficient. Courts require corroboration for non-economic damages and documentation for economic damages. | Damages claim is attacked as self-serving and unsubstantiated; court gives it minimal weight. | Corroborate with documents (receipts, records, photos), third-party witnesses, expert opinions. Your testimony provides the narrative; documents provide the proof. |
| 2 | "I don't need to keep receipts — I remember what I spent." | Michigan courts require reasonable certainty in economic damages. Memory-based estimates without documentation are easily attacked on cross-examination and may be excluded. | Economic damages reduced or eliminated because plaintiff cannot produce receipts, bills, or bank statements supporting claimed amounts. | Keep every receipt, bill, bank statement, and cost record. Create a running damages log with dates, amounts, and descriptions. Photograph receipts before they fade. |
| 3 | "The mitigation defense doesn't apply to me — I'm the victim." | Michigan requires all plaintiffs to mitigate damages. MCL 600.6304; general tort principle. Failure to take reasonable steps to reduce harm gives defendant a damages reduction argument. | Defendant argues successfully that plaintiff's damages are inflated because plaintiff failed to mitigate (e.g., didn't seek alternative housing, didn't pursue therapy, didn't look for replacement work). | Document every mitigation step taken. If you couldn't mitigate, document why (cost, unavailability, danger). Show the court you acted reasonably to minimize your own harm. |
| 4 | "Damages from before the statute of limitations still count." | Economic damages tied to conduct outside the statute of limitations may be barred. The discovery rule may extend some claims, but damages accruing before the limitations period are generally not recoverable. | Court limits damages calculation to the limitations period; plaintiff loses significant claimed damages for earlier harms. | Calculate damages within the statute of limitations period. For continuing torts, argue continuing violation doctrine but calculate primary damages within the safe period. |
| 5 | "I can claim lost wages without showing what I actually earn." | Lost wage claims require proof of actual earnings or earning capacity — pay stubs, tax returns, employer verification. Bare assertions of income are insufficient. | Court excludes lost wage claim; or awards minimal amount based on minimum wage rather than plaintiff's actual (higher) wage. | Gather W-2s, tax returns, pay stubs, employment verification letters. Calculate missed work days tied to specific case events (court appearances, moving, recovery). |
| 6 | "The children's damages are automatically part of my damages." | Children's damages are separate claims requiring separate standing. A parent cannot recover for a child's emotional distress as part of their own claim without specific authority (next friend action). | Court strikes children's damages from parent's claim; damages are significantly reduced. | If claiming children's harms, file as next friend under MCR 2.201(E). Distinguish parent's harms (loss of companionship) from children's harms (emotional distress, therapy costs). |
