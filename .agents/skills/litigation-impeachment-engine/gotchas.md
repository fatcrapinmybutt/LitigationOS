# Gotchas — litigation-impeachment-engine

## Anti-Rationalization Table

This table prevents the most common impeachment failures — moments where
a litigant believes they have a "gotcha" but actually has nothing, or
worse, damages their own credibility.

| # | Excuse (Rationalization) | Reality (What Actually Happens) | Consequence |
|---|--------------------------|--------------------------------|-------------|
| 1 | "They said X before and now they're saying Y — that's perjury!" | Inconsistency is not automatically perjury. Perjury requires proof of willful, material false statement under oath. Witnesses legitimately forget details, refine recollections, and correct errors. Michigan courts distinguish between inconsistency (impeachment) and perjury (criminal). | Accusation of perjury without meeting elements backfires. Judge views you as overreaching. Credibility damaged. |
| 2 | "The contradiction is obvious — I don't need to lay a foundation." | MRE 613(a) requires you to disclose the contents of the prior statement to the witness and give them an opportunity to explain or deny. Skipping foundation means the impeachment evidence is inadmissible. Even if the judge allows it, opposing counsel objects and it's stricken. | Devastating impeachment evidence excluded for failure to follow MRE 613 procedure. The jury/judge never hears it. |
| 3 | "I'll use the email to prove they lied — emails are evidence." | Emails require authentication under MRE 901. The author must be identified, the email must be shown to be what it claims to be, and hearsay exceptions must be established. Unsworn emails used to impeach sworn testimony have limited admissibility. | Email excluded or admitted only for limited impeachment purpose (not substantive truth). Impact reduced or eliminated. |
| 4 | "Any inconsistency is impeachment material." | Trivial inconsistencies (wrong day of the week, minor time discrepancy) annoy judges and waste credibility. Effective impeachment requires *material* inconsistency — a contradiction that affects the outcome of the case. Judges in the 14th Circuit are particularly intolerant of trivial impeachment. | Judge interrupts: "Counsel, is this going somewhere?" Credibility spent on immaterial point. Real impeachment opportunities overshadowed. |
| 5 | "I'll impeach them with their social media posts." | Social media requires authentication (MRE 901), relevance (MRE 401), and clearing hearsay hurdles. Screenshots can be challenged as fabricated. Metadata may be needed. Michigan courts are increasingly strict about social media evidence authentication. | Social media evidence excluded. If admitted, opposing counsel challenges authenticity and creates reasonable doubt about the evidence itself. |
| 6 | "I don't need to prepare — I'll catch them in the lie during cross." | Unprepared impeachment attempts fail catastrophically. Without the exact page, line number, and exhibit ready, you fumble while the witness collects themselves. The moment is lost. Prepared impeachment is devastating; unprepared impeachment is embarrassing. | Witness recovers. Explanation sounds plausible because you didn't have the follow-up ready. Judge perceives you as fishing. |
| 7 | "The inconsistency between the FOC report and their testimony is enough." | FOC reports are written by third parties summarizing what someone said. The FOC worker's characterization may differ from what the witness actually said. Always compare primary sources (what the witness actually said/wrote) rather than third-party summaries. | Opposing counsel demonstrates FOC report was the caseworker's interpretation, not the witness's actual words. Impeachment collapses. |
| 8 | "MRE 801(d)(1) makes all prior inconsistent statements admissible as substantive evidence." | MRE 801(d)(1)(A) only applies to prior inconsistent statements given *under oath* at a *trial, hearing, other proceeding, or deposition*. Unsworn prior statements are admissible for impeachment under MRE 613 but NOT as substantive evidence. This distinction is critical. | You offer a letter as substantive evidence of truth under 801(d)(1). Judge sustains objection — it's an unsworn statement. You can only use it for impeachment, severely limiting its value. |

## Common Impeachment Failures

### 1. Premature Confrontation
**Symptom**: Witness is confronted with inconsistency before being locked into current testimony.
**Cause**: Impeacher skips "commit" phase and jumps directly to "confront."
**Fix**: Always follow the four-step protocol: COMMIT → CREDIT → CONFRONT → CONTRAST. Lock the witness in before revealing the trap.

### 2. Inadequate Exhibit Preparation
**Symptom**: During cross-examination, impeacher cannot locate the prior statement quickly.
**Cause**: Exhibits not pre-marked, not organized by topic, not tabbed to specific page/line.
**Fix**: Pre-mark all impeachment exhibits. Tab to exact page. Highlight exact language. Have copies for witness, court, and opposing counsel.

### 3. Overloading Impeachment
**Symptom**: Ten contradictions presented; judge remembers none.
**Cause**: Quantity over quality. Too many impeachment points dilute each one.
**Fix**: Select top 3–5 contradictions ranked by composite score. Lead with the strongest. End with the second strongest.

### 4. Failing to Close
**Symptom**: Contradiction presented but witness provides plausible explanation that goes unchallenged.
**Cause**: No prepared rebuttal to anticipated explanations.
**Fix**: For each impeachment target, prepare the anticipated explanation and the rebuttal. Never ask a question you don't have a follow-up for.

### 5. Missing the Substantive/Impeachment Distinction
**Symptom**: Unsworn prior statement offered as substantive evidence.
**Cause**: Failure to distinguish MRE 613 (impeachment) from MRE 801(d)(1) (substantive).
**Fix**: Before offering any prior statement, classify it: sworn at proceeding → 801(d)(1)(A) substantive. Otherwise → 613 impeachment only.

## Michigan-Specific Impeachment Traps

- **MRE 613 vs. FRE 613**: Michigan's MRE 613 is similar to the federal rule but Michigan courts may apply it differently. Always cite MRE, not FRE, in state court.
- **Deposition use at trial**: MCR 2.308 governs use of depositions at trial. Deposition of a party is admissible for any purpose. Deposition of a non-party witness has additional requirements.
- **FOC recommendations**: Friend of the Court recommendations in Lane A cases are not sworn testimony. They cannot be used for 801(d)(1)(A) substantive impeachment. They may be used for 613 impeachment of the FOC worker.
- **Transcript availability**: Michigan courts may take weeks to produce hearing transcripts. Request expedited transcripts when impeachment is anticipated. Budget for transcript costs.
- **Judicial notice**: If impeaching with public records, consider requesting judicial notice under MRE 201 rather than formal exhibit introduction. Faster and avoids authentication challenges.
