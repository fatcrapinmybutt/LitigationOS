# Deviation Factors — Michigan Child Support Reference

## MCL 552.605(2) Deviation Analysis Framework

### When Deviation Is Appropriate

The MCSF is presumed correct, but may be deviated from when application would be "unjust or inappropriate." The burden is on the party requesting deviation.

### Factor-by-Factor Analysis

#### Factor 1: Special Needs of the Child
**Applicable when**: L.D.W. has medical, therapeutic, educational, or developmental needs that exceed what the base formula accounts for.

**Evidence required**:
- Medical records documenting condition
- Treatment plans with cost estimates
- Insurance coverage gap documentation
- Specialist recommendations
- Comparison to formula's base provision

**Michigan case law**:
- *Stallworth v Stallworth*, 275 Mich App 282 (2007) — special needs child deviation
- *Loutts v Loutts*, 298 Mich App 21 (2012) — deviation factors analysis

**Documentation template**:
```
Special Need: _______________
Monthly Cost: $______________
Insurance Coverage: $________
Out-of-Pocket Gap: $_________
Duration: ___________________
Formula Provision: $_________
Additional Amount Needed: $__
```

#### Factor 2: Extraordinary Educational Expenses
**Applicable when**: Child attends private school, requires tutoring, or has specialized educational needs not covered by public education.

**Evidence required**:
- Tuition bills or enrollment documentation
- History of attendance (if established lifestyle)
- Educational testing showing need
- Comparison to public school alternatives
- Both parents' positions on educational choice

#### Factor 3: Extraordinary Medical Expenses
**Applicable when**: Either parent has significant medical costs that affect ability to pay support.

**Evidence required**:
- Medical bills and insurance EOBs
- Out-of-pocket cost documentation
- Impact on disposable income
- Duration of medical condition
- Whether condition affects earning capacity

#### Factor 4: High-Income Situations
**Applicable when**: Combined parental income exceeds the MCSF income tables (currently ~$300,000/year combined).

**Analysis**:
```
If combined income > MCSF table maximum:
  1. Calculate support at table maximum
  2. Determine if additional support appropriate
  3. Consider child's established lifestyle
  4. Consider reasonable needs of the child
  5. Avoid support as wealth transfer mechanism
```

**Key case**: *Ghidotti v Barber*, 459 Mich 189 (1998) — high-income support analysis

#### Factor 5: Non-Income Assets
**Applicable when**: A parent has significant assets (trust funds, inheritance, property equity) but limited income.

**Evidence required**:
- Asset documentation (trust statements, property appraisals)
- Income generated by assets
- Liquidity analysis
- Whether assets are marital or separate
- Impact on parent's actual ability to pay

#### Factor 6: Prior Support Orders
**Applicable when**: Either parent pays support for children from a prior relationship.

**MCSF treatment**:
- Prior-born children's support is deducted before calculating current obligation
- Only COURT-ORDERED support for prior-born children qualifies
- Voluntary support for stepchildren does NOT qualify
- Documentation: copy of prior support order + proof of payment

#### Factor 7: Child Care Cost Variance
**Applicable when**: Actual child care costs significantly differ from MCSF assumptions.

**Evidence required**:
- Child care provider invoices
- Work schedule requiring care
- Comparison to formula's assumed cost
- Geographic cost variation documentation

#### Factor 8: Established Lifestyle
**Applicable when**: Child enjoyed a lifestyle during the marriage that the formula amount cannot maintain.

**Evidence required**:
- Documentation of lifestyle during marriage (activities, housing, travel)
- Current cost to maintain lifestyle elements
- Formula amount vs. lifestyle maintenance cost
- Which elements serve the child's best interest

### Deviation Motion Template

```
MOTION FOR DEVIATION FROM CHILD SUPPORT FORMULA

Pursuant to MCL 552.605(2), [Party] moves this Court to deviate from
the Michigan Child Support Formula because application of the formula
would be unjust or inappropriate for the following reasons:

1. FORMULA CALCULATION
   The MCSF produces a support obligation of $______/month.

2. DEVIATION FACTOR(S)
   [Identify specific factor(s) from MCL 552.605(2)]

3. FACTUAL BASIS
   [Detailed facts supporting deviation with evidence citations]

4. REQUESTED AMOUNT
   [Party] requests support of $______/month, which represents
   a [increase/decrease] of $______/month from the formula.

5. JUSTIFICATION
   [Explain why the requested amount is more appropriate than
   the formula amount for L.D.W.'s needs]

EXHIBITS:
A. MCSF Calculation Worksheet (current)
B. MCSF Calculation Worksheet (proposed deviation)
C. [Supporting evidence — medical records, bills, etc.]
D. [Additional documentation]
```

### Common Deviation Arguments in Custody Cases

| Argument | For Higher Support | For Lower Support |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Child's medical needs | Child requires ongoing therapy | Parent has disabling medical condition |
| Education | Child in established private school | Parent paying student loans for career advancement |
| Income volatility | Parent's income is declining | Overtime is not guaranteed |
| Housing costs | Custodial parent needs larger home | Non-custodial in high-cost area |
| Transportation | Long-distance parenting time costs | Both parents in same community |

### Judicial Discretion Notes

Michigan courts have BROAD discretion in deviation decisions:
- Trial court findings on deviation are reviewed for abuse of discretion
- Written findings are MANDATORY when deviating (MCL 552.605(2))
- Failure to make written findings is reversible error
- Appellate courts will not substitute their judgment for the trial court's
- But deviation without adequate factual basis IS abuse of discretion

### Impact on Pigors v Watson

Relevant deviation considerations:
1. L.D.W.'s specific needs and circumstances
2. Both parents' actual earning capacity
3. Historical lifestyle during the parents' relationship
4. Overnight parenting time schedule and its impact on costs
5. Any extraordinary expenses for L.D.W.'s welfare
6. Cross-lane financial impacts (Lane B housing costs may affect ability to pay)
