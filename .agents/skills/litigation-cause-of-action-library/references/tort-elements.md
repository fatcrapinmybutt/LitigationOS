# Michigan Tort Claims — Complete Elements Reference

> Jurisdiction: Michigan | Context: Pigors v Watson | 14th Judicial Circuit, Muskegon County

---

## 1. FRAUD (Common Law)

**Elements (6):**
1. Defendant made a material representation
2. The representation was false
3. Defendant knew the representation was false when made, or made it recklessly without knowledge of its truth
4. Defendant made the representation with the intent that plaintiff would act on it
5. Plaintiff actually relied on the representation
6. Plaintiff suffered damages as a result of the reliance

**Burden:** Clear and convincing evidence
**SOL:** 6 years from discovery — MCL 600.5813; MCL 600.5855 (discovery rule)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, rescission, benefit-of-the-bargain or out-of-pocket measure
**Key Case:** Hi-Way Motor Co v Int'l Harvester Co, 398 Mich 330 (1976)
**Note:** Fraud must be pled with particularity — MCR 2.112(B)(1). State the time, place, content of false representation, identity of person making it, and what was obtained.

---

## 2. SILENT FRAUD (Fraudulent Concealment)

**Elements (4):**
1. Defendant had a duty to disclose a material fact
2. Defendant suppressed or concealed that fact
3. Plaintiff would have acted differently had the fact been disclosed
4. Plaintiff suffered damages from the nondisclosure

**Burden:** Clear and convincing evidence
**SOL:** 6 years from discovery — MCL 600.5855
**Remedies:** Same as fraud; additionally may toll SOL on other claims
**Key Case:** M&D, Inc v McConkey, 231 Mich App 22 (1998)
**Note:** Duty to disclose arises from: fiduciary relationship, partial disclosure (creating duty to complete), active concealment, or statutory duty.

---

## 3. NEGLIGENCE

**Elements (4):**
1. Defendant owed plaintiff a duty of care
2. Defendant breached that duty
3. The breach was the proximate cause of plaintiff's injuries
4. Plaintiff suffered actual damages

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(10)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages (economic + non-economic), medical expenses, lost wages
**Key Case:** Moning v Alfono, 400 Mich 425 (1977) (duty analysis); Case v Consumers Power Co, 463 Mich 1 (2000) (proximate cause)
**Note:** Duty is a question of law. Michigan uses the Moning factors: foreseeability, severity of risk, burden of alternative conduct, relationship of parties.

---

## 4. NEGLIGENCE PER SE

**Elements (4):**
1. Defendant violated a specific statute or ordinance
2. Plaintiff is within the class of persons the statute was designed to protect
3. The harm suffered is the type the statute was designed to prevent
4. The statutory violation was a proximate cause of plaintiff's injuries

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(10)
**Remedies:** Same as negligence
**Key Case:** Klatt v City of Royal Oak, 234 Mich App 1 (1999)
**Note:** The violated statute establishes the standard of care. Building code, housing code, and safety statute violations are common bases.

---

## 5. INTENTIONAL INFLICTION OF EMOTIONAL DISTRESS (IIED)

**Elements (4):**
1. Defendant's conduct was extreme and outrageous
2. Defendant intended to cause or recklessly disregarded the probability of causing emotional distress
3. Plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress
4. Defendant's conduct was the proximate cause of the distress

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence (but "extreme and outrageous" is a very high bar)
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages for emotional suffering, consequential physical harm damages
**Key Case:** Roberts v Auto-Owners Ins Co, 422 Mich 594 (1985) ("so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community")
**Note:** Michigan courts RARELY sustain IIED claims. The conduct must be truly extraordinary. Insults, annoyances, threats of litigation, and hard bargaining do not qualify.

---

## 6. NEGLIGENT INFLICTION OF EMOTIONAL DISTRESS (NIED)

**Elements (4):**
1. Defendant owed plaintiff a duty of care
2. Defendant breached that duty through negligent conduct
3. Plaintiff suffered serious emotional distress with physical manifestation
4. The breach was the proximate cause of the distress

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages for emotional and physical consequences
**Key Case:** Daley v LaCroix, 384 Mich 4 (1970) (requiring physical manifestation or definite and objective physical injury)
**Note:** Without physical manifestation, NIED fails in Michigan. "Physical manifestation" means objectively verifiable symptoms — not merely subjective distress.

---

## 7. CONVERSION

**Elements (4):**
1. Plaintiff had an ownership interest or right to possession of the property
2. Defendant took or detained the property
3. The taking or detention was unauthorized
4. Plaintiff demanded return or defendant exercised dominion inconsistent with plaintiff's rights

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Fair market value of property at time of conversion, or return of property + consequential damages
**Key Case:** Foremost Ins Co v Allstate Ins Co, 439 Mich 378 (1992)
**Note:** Applies to personal property, not real property. For real property, use trespass or ejectment.

---

## 8. TRESPASS TO LAND

**Elements (3):**
1. Plaintiff had possessory interest in the property
2. Defendant intentionally entered or caused entry onto the property
3. The entry was unauthorized

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Nominal damages (trespass itself), compensatory for actual harm, injunctive relief, treble damages for timber trespass MCL 600.2919
**Key Case:** Adams v Cleveland-Cliffs Iron Co, 237 Mich App 51 (1999)
**Note:** Intent to enter is sufficient — defendant need not intend to trespass. Mistake of boundary is no defense.

---

## 9. DEFAMATION — PER SE

**Elements (4):**
1. Defendant made a false statement of fact about plaintiff
2. The statement was published (communicated to a third party)
3. The statement was defamatory per se (imputes: crime, loathsome disease, business/professional incompetence, serious sexual misconduct)
4. The statement was not privileged

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence; damages presumed for per se defamation
**SOL:** 1 year — MCL 600.5805(9)
**Remedies:** Presumed general damages (no proof of actual harm required), special damages, punitive where actual malice is shown
**Key Case:** Rouch v Enquirer & News of Battle Creek, 440 Mich 238 (1992)

---

## 10. DEFAMATION — PER QUOD

**Elements (5):**
1. Defendant made a false statement of fact about plaintiff
2. The statement was published to a third party
3. The statement was defamatory (but requires extrinsic facts to understand the defamatory meaning)
4. The statement was not privileged
5. Plaintiff suffered special damages (specific economic loss)

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence; plaintiff must prove special damages
**SOL:** 1 year — MCL 600.5805(9)
**Remedies:** Special damages (must be specifically pled and proven), general damages, punitive where actual malice is shown
**Key Case:** Burden v Elias Bros Big Boy Restaurants, 240 Mich App 723 (2000)

---

## 11. INVASION OF PRIVACY — Four Types

### 11a. Intrusion Upon Seclusion
**Elements:** (1) Intentional intrusion (2) into a matter plaintiff has a right to keep private (3) that would be highly offensive to a reasonable person.
**Key Case:** Doe v Mills, 212 Mich App 73 (1995)

### 11b. Public Disclosure of Private Facts
**Elements:** (1) Public disclosure (2) of private facts (3) that would be highly offensive to a reasonable person (4) and are not of legitimate public concern.
**Key Case:** Doe v Mills, 212 Mich App 73 (1995)

### 11c. False Light
**Elements:** (1) Defendant publicized a matter (2) placing plaintiff in a false light (3) that would be highly offensive to a reasonable person (4) defendant knew or recklessly disregarded the falsity.
**Key Case:** Porter v Royal Oak, 214 Mich App 478 (1995)

### 11d. Appropriation of Name or Likeness
**Elements:** (1) Defendant used plaintiff's name or likeness (2) for defendant's commercial advantage (3) without plaintiff's consent.
**Key Case:** Doe v Mills, 212 Mich App 73 (1995)

**SOL (all four):** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, injunctive relief, disgorgement of profits (appropriation)

## 12. CIVIL CONSPIRACY

**Elements (4):**
1. Two or more persons combined or agreed
2. By concerted action to accomplish a criminal or unlawful purpose, or a lawful purpose by criminal or unlawful means
3. An overt act was committed in furtherance of the conspiracy
4. Plaintiff suffered damages as a result

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** Borrowed from the underlying tort — typically 3 years
**Remedies:** Joint and several liability for all damages caused by the conspiracy
**Key Case:** Admiral Ins Co v Columbia Casualty Ins Co, 194 Mich App 300 (1992)
**Note:** Civil conspiracy is not an independent cause of action in Michigan — it requires an underlying actionable tort. The conspiracy merely extends liability to all conspirators.

---

## 13. ABUSE OF PROCESS

**Elements (3):**
1. Defendant made an illegal, improper, or perverted use of process
2. Defendant had an ulterior motive or purpose in exercising the process
3. Plaintiff suffered damages as a result

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, attorney fees in egregious cases
**Key Case:** Friedman v Dozorc, 412 Mich 1 (1981)
**Note:** The "process" must have been used for a purpose other than that for which it was designed. Filing a lawsuit to harass (rather than to obtain judgment) qualifies.

---

## 14. MALICIOUS PROSECUTION

**Elements (5):**
1. Defendant initiated a criminal proceeding or civil action against plaintiff
2. The proceeding was terminated in plaintiff's favor
3. Defendant lacked probable cause to initiate the proceeding
4. Defendant acted with malice (improper purpose)
5. Plaintiff suffered damages (including attorney fees, reputation, distress)

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years — MCL 600.5805(2)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages, attorney fees, emotional distress damages, punitive damages in extreme cases
**Key Case:** Friedman v Dozorc, 412 Mich 1 (1981)
**Note:** For civil malicious prosecution, Michigan requires "special injury" beyond the ordinary burden of defending a lawsuit — e.g., arrest, seizure of property, injunction.

---

## 15. WRONGFUL EVICTION

**Elements (4):**
1. Landlord-tenant relationship existed
2. Landlord took action to remove tenant from possession
3. The removal was accomplished without following statutory procedures (MCL 600.5714 summary proceedings)
4. Tenant suffered damages from the wrongful removal

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 3 years (tort theory) or 6 years (contract theory)
**Remedies:** Compensatory damages (moving costs, storage, temporary housing, emotional distress), injunctive relief (reinstatement), statutory damages under MCL 600.2918
**Key Case:** Ypsilanti v Appletree Assoc, unpub; Hilsendegen v Scheich, 21 Mich 164 (1870)
**Note:** Self-help eviction is illegal in Michigan. A landlord cannot change locks, remove belongings, shut off utilities, or physically remove a tenant without a court order. MCL 600.2918 provides triple damages for forcible entry and detainer.

---

## 16. BREACH OF CONTRACT

**Elements (4):**
1. A valid contract existed between the parties
2. The terms of the contract
3. Defendant breached one or more terms
4. Plaintiff suffered damages as a result of the breach

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 6 years — MCL 600.5807(8)
**Remedies:** Expectation damages, reliance damages, consequential damages (if foreseeable at formation), specific performance (extraordinary circumstances)
**Key Case:** Miller-Davis Co v Ahrens Constr, Inc, 495 Mich 161 (2014)

---

## 17. BREACH OF IMPLIED WARRANTY OF HABITABILITY

**Elements (4):**
1. A lease agreement existed (residential)
2. The premises had a defect affecting habitability
3. The landlord had notice of the defect (actual or constructive)
4. The landlord failed to repair within a reasonable time

**Burden:** Preponderance of the evidence
**SOL:** 6 years (contract theory) — MCL 600.5807(8)
**Remedies:** Rent abatement, repair costs, consequential damages, lease termination
**Key Case:** Trentadue v Gorton, 479 Mich 378 (2007); MCL 554.139
**Note:** MCL 554.139 codifies the warranty — landlord must maintain premises fit for intended use and keep in reasonable repair. Cannot be waived by lease provision.
