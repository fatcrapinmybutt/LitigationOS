# gotchas — litigation-cause-of-action-library

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Element Conflation

The most dangerous error is treating related-but-distinct elements as interchangeable.

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "Fraud and misrepresentation are the same claim" | Michigan distinguishes fraud (6 elements including intent to deceive and reliance) from innocent misrepresentation (no scienter required) and negligent misrepresentation (duty-based). Each has different elements, different burdens, different remedies. | Always specify which type of misrepresentation: fraudulent (Hi-Way Motor Co v Int'l Harvester), negligent (Williams v Polgar), or innocent (Clemens v Lesnek). List elements for the specific subtype. |
| 2 | "The defendant acted wrongfully so IIED is met" | IIED requires conduct "so outrageous in character and so extreme in degree as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency" — Roberts v Auto-Owners Ins Co. General wrongfulness is nowhere close to this standard. Most bad behavior doesn't qualify. | Quote the Roberts v Auto-Owners standard verbatim. Apply it honestly — if the conduct is merely rude, unfair, or annoying, IIED fails. Note that Michigan courts rarely uphold IIED claims. |
| 3 | "They caused harm so negligence applies" | Negligence requires DUTY → BREACH → CAUSATION → DAMAGES, and duty is the threshold question. No duty = no negligence, regardless of how much harm occurred. Duty is a question of law for the court. Moning v Alfono. | Start every negligence analysis with the duty question. Identify the specific legal relationship creating the duty. If no recognized duty exists, stop the analysis and say so. |
| 4 | "There was a contract so any breach gives rise to damages" | Not all breaches are actionable. The breach must be material (first material breach doctrine), and the non-breaching party must have performed or tendered performance. Nominal damages may be all that's available for technical breaches. | Analyze materiality of the breach. Check whether plaintiff performed their obligations. Quantify actual damages flowing from the specific breach — not from the entire relationship souring. |
| 5 | "They violated the statute so statutory damages are automatic" | Many Michigan statutes require specific procedural prerequisites: MCPA requires pre-suit notice for some remedies, Truth in Renting requires specific lease provisions to trigger, security deposit statute requires specific timeline violations. | Check every procedural prerequisite for the specific statute. Verify that the exact subsection applies. Read the remedies provision — it often has conditions or caps the agent skips. |
| 6 | "This is clearly a Section 1983 case" | Section 1983 requires state action — private parties cannot violate constitutional rights unless acting under color of state law. The state action doctrine (Lugar v Edmondson Oil) is a high bar that eliminates most private-party claims. | Always check the state action element first. For private defendants, the only paths are: public function doctrine, entanglement/joint action, compulsion, or nexus test. If none apply, 1983 fails regardless of how egregious the conduct. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Wrong Statute of Limitations

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "The SOL runs from when the harm occurred" | For fraud and concealment-based claims, MCL 600.5855 applies the discovery rule — the SOL runs from when plaintiff discovered or should have discovered the claim. For continuing torts, each new act restarts the clock. | Identify the exact accrual date. Apply the discovery rule where applicable. For continuing wrongs, identify the last actionable event and calculate from there. |
| 2 | "We have 6 years for everything" | Michigan has different SOL periods for different claims: 3 years for personal injury/torts (MCL 600.5805), 6 years for contract and fraud (MCL 600.5807/5813), 2 years for medical malpractice (MCL 600.5805(6)). Using the wrong period is malpractice-level error. | Look up the specific SOL for each individual cause of action. Never assume. Create a per-claim SOL chart with authority citations. |
| 3 | "The statute tolled because plaintiff didn't know" | Michigan tolling is narrow. Infancy (MCL 600.5851) and insanity (MCL 600.5851) toll, but ignorance of legal rights does not. The discovery rule (MCL 600.5855) is different from tolling — it shifts the accrual date, it doesn't pause the clock. | Distinguish between the discovery rule (shifts accrual) and tolling (pauses running). Identify which specific tolling doctrine applies and cite the statute. |
| 4 | "Filing the complaint stops the SOL" | In Michigan, the SOL is satisfied by filing the complaint, but only if service is completed within the service period. If service lapses and the complaint is dismissed, the saving provision MCL 600.5856 gives only 6 months to refile. | Verify both filing date AND service date. If relying on the saving provision, count the 6-month window precisely. |
| 5 | "The continuing violations doctrine saves us" | Michigan's continuing violations doctrine is narrower than federal. It applies only when each act independently violates the law — not when a single violation has continuing effects. Sumner v Goodyear Tire. | Analyze whether each complained-of act is independently actionable. If the complaint is about lingering effects of a single past act, the continuing violations doctrine does not apply. |
| 6 | "We can use equitable tolling" | Michigan courts rarely apply equitable tolling in civil cases. It requires active concealment by defendant or extraordinary circumstances preventing filing. Mere lack of knowledge or litigation difficulty is insufficient. | Only invoke equitable tolling if defendant actively concealed the cause of action. Cite specific concealment acts with dates. |

## Anti-Rationalization Table: Remedy Mismatch

| # | The Agent Wants To Say | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | "We'll get punitive damages" | Michigan abolished punitive damages for most claims. They survive only in narrowly defined statutory contexts (e.g., exemplary damages under specific statutes). Common law punitive damages are generally unavailable. Veselenak v Smith. | Never promise punitive damages in Michigan common law claims. Check the specific statute for exemplary or enhanced damages. MCPA treble damages are the closest equivalent for consumer claims. |
| 2 | "We'll get attorney fees because we won" | Michigan follows the American Rule — each party pays their own fees unless a statute, court rule, or contract provides otherwise. MCR 2.625 allows costs but not fees absent specific authority. | Identify the specific statutory or contractual basis for fee-shifting. MCPA § 445.911 allows fees. Section 1983 has 42 USC § 1988. If no basis exists, do not claim attorney fees. |
| 3 | "Treble damages apply to all our claims" | Treble damages are statutory and claim-specific. MCPA provides treble damages only for knowing violations (MCL 445.911(2)). They do not automatically extend to pendant claims. | Identify which specific claims carry treble damage provisions. Calculate treble damages only on those claims. Present separate damage calculations per count. |
| 4 | "Injunctive relief is available for past harm" | Injunctions are prospective — they prevent future harm. To get a TRO or preliminary injunction, plaintiff must show irreparable harm that money damages cannot remedy, likelihood of success, and balance of equities. Thermatool Corp v Borzym. | Only seek injunctive relief for ongoing or threatened future conduct. Address all four injunction factors. If the harm is solely past and monetary, injunctive relief is improper. |
| 5 | "We'll get specific performance on the lease" | Specific performance is an extraordinary remedy available only when money damages are inadequate and the contract terms are sufficiently definite. For real property it's more available, but for personal property or services, courts strongly disfavor it. | Analyze whether money damages are truly inadequate. For lease disputes, check whether the specific premises are unique or whether substitute housing is available. |
| 6 | "Emotional distress damages are automatic" | Emotional distress damages in Michigan require either physical manifestation of distress or an independent tort (IIED/NIED). They are not automatically available as consequential damages on contract or property claims. Daley v LaCroix. | Verify the legal basis for emotional distress damages on each specific claim. If relying on Daley v LaCroix, establish the physical manifestation. If no qualifying tort exists, emotional distress damages may be unavailable. |

## Common Traps in Pigors v Watson Context

1. **Manufactured home law is specialized** — MCL 125.2301 et seq. and federal HUD standards create a separate regulatory framework. Don't assume general landlord-tenant law covers everything.
2. **Multiple defendant types require different claim theories** — Individual defendants, corporate entities, and LLCs each have different exposure profiles and may require separate counts.
3. **Federal-state claim interaction** — When pleading both § 1983 and state claims, supplemental jurisdiction (28 USC § 1367) must be properly invoked, and Eleventh Amendment immunity must be addressed for any government defendants.
