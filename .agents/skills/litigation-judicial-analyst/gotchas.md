# Gotchas — litigation-judicial-analyst

## Anti-Rationalization Table

This table prevents the most dangerous error in judicial analysis: conflating
legitimate judicial discretion with misconduct. Every row addresses a real
trap in evaluating judges in the 14th Judicial Circuit.

| # | Excuse (Rationalization) | Reality (What Actually Happens) | Consequence |
|---|--------------------------|--------------------------------|-------------|
| 1 | "The judge ruled against me, so they must be biased." | Adverse rulings alone do not establish bias. Michigan law requires a pattern of conduct or specific evidence of prejudgment. MCR 2.003(C)(1) requires showing bias stems from an extrajudicial source or demonstrates such deep-seated antagonism that fair judgment is impossible. | Frivolous recusal motion denied. Credibility damaged with the very judge you must continue appearing before. |
| 2 | "I don't need specific evidence — the pattern speaks for itself." | JTC complaints and recusal motions require specific, documented instances. Vague allegations of "unfairness" are insufficient. Michigan's Judicial Tenure Commission requires concrete factual allegations per MCR 9.220. | Complaint dismissed without investigation. Time and effort wasted. No record of misconduct created. |
| 3 | "Filing a JTC complaint will pressure the judge to recuse." | JTC complaints are confidential until formal charges are filed. The judge may never know about the complaint. Even if they learn of it, filing a complaint does not create automatic recusal grounds. It may provoke judicial hostility if perceived as tactical. | No recusal obtained. Potential negative inference from judge if learned indirectly. JTC complaint proceeds (or doesn't) independently of case. |
| 4 | "Judge McNeill always favors mothers in custody cases — everyone knows it." | Anecdotal reputation is not evidence. The analysis must be based on documented ruling patterns, transcript evidence, and comparable case outcomes. Confirmation bias causes litigants to see patterns that statistical analysis doesn't support. | Baseless gender bias claim damages credibility. Motion denied with prejudice. May trigger sanctions for unsupported allegations. |
| 5 | "The judge's off-the-record comments aren't usable." | While off-record comments may not appear in official transcripts, they can be documented in contemporaneous notes, witness statements, and subsequent filings. They are relevant to bias analysis even if not directly quotable from the record. | Critical evidence of bias lost because it wasn't documented when it occurred. Build the habit of contemporaneous notes after every hearing. |
| 6 | "Hoopes is new to the bench so there's no pattern to analyze." | Newer judges still bring pre-bench patterns: prior practice areas, political affiliations, published writings, bar association involvement. Early rulings in a case are particularly revealing because the judge hasn't yet been influenced by case familiarity. | Missed opportunity to understand judicial tendencies early. Strategy not tailored to this specific judge. |
| 7 | "The Michigan Canons are aspirational — judges don't actually get disciplined for violations." | The Michigan JTC investigates hundreds of complaints annually and has imposed formal discipline (censure, suspension, removal) multiple times in recent years. Canon violations, particularly Canon 3 (impartiality) and Canon 2 (appearance of impropriety), are the most common basis for discipline. | Real misconduct goes unreported because filer assumes nothing will happen. No paper trail created for future complaints or appeals. |
| 8 | "I can file for disqualification at any time." | MCR 2.003(D)(1) requires disqualification motions to be filed "within 14 days of discovery of the grounds." Delay can waive the right. Also, disqualification for cause (vs. peremptory) requires specific legal grounds. Michigan allows only one peremptory disqualification per case. | Disqualification motion denied as untimely. Peremptory challenge already used. No path to remove judge. |

## Common Analytical Failures

### 1. Outcome Bias
**Symptom**: Analysis concludes bias exists because outcomes are unfavorable.
**Cause**: Confusing unfavorable outcomes with biased process. A judge can rule correctly against you.
**Fix**: Focus analysis on *process* indicators (tone, time allocation, procedural fairness), not just *outcome* indicators.

### 2. Small Sample Size
**Symptom**: Bias conclusion drawn from 2–3 rulings.
**Cause**: Insufficient data for pattern analysis. Random variation can produce apparent patterns.
**Fix**: Require minimum 5+ data points across different motion types before concluding bias pattern.

### 3. Missing Baseline
**Symptom**: Judge's behavior described as unusual without comparison data.
**Cause**: No baseline established for what "normal" judicial behavior looks like in the 14th Circuit.
**Fix**: Compare target judge's behavior against other 14th Circuit judges on similar case types.

### 4. Attribution Error
**Symptom**: Negative outcome attributed to bias when it resulted from legitimate legal weakness.
**Cause**: Failure to objectively assess the merits of one's own position before attributing loss to bias.
**Fix**: Red team your own filing first. If the filing has genuine weaknesses, address those before analyzing judicial conduct.

### 5. Canon Misapplication
**Symptom**: Canon violation alleged for conduct that falls within legitimate judicial discretion.
**Cause**: Over-reading Canon requirements. Judges have broad discretion in case management, courtroom conduct, and evidentiary rulings.
**Fix**: Consult Canon commentary and JTC precedent before alleging violation. Focus on clear violations, not judgment calls.

## Michigan-Specific Judicial Analysis Traps

- **Peremptory disqualification limit**: Michigan allows only ONE peremptory disqualification per case under MCR 2.003(B). Use it strategically — once it's gone, you need cause.
- **14th Circuit chief judge**: Disqualification motions in the 14th Circuit go to the chief judge for ruling. Know who the chief judge is and their tendencies.
- **Judicial immunity**: Judges have absolute immunity for judicial acts. Analysis should focus on documentation for appellate review and JTC complaints, not civil liability.
- **Recusal standard**: Michigan uses the "reasonable person" standard — would a reasonable person, knowing all the facts, question the judge's impartiality? This is lower than "actual bias."
- **JTC timeline**: JTC investigations can take 6–18 months. Filing a complaint is a long-term strategy, not an immediate remedy. Plan accordingly.
